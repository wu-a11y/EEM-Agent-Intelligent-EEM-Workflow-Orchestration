Using fluorescence spectroscopy and parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) we characterized and modeled the fluorescence properties of dissolved organic matter (DOM) in samples from the Penobscot River, Androscoggin River, Penobscot Bay, and the Gulf of Maine (GoM). We analyzed excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) using an existing PARAFAC model (Cory and McKnight, 2005) and created a system-specific model with seven components (GoM PARAFAC). The GoM PARAFAC model contained six components similar to those in other PARAFAC models and one unique component with a spectrum similar to a residual found using the Cory and McKnight (2005) model. The unique component was abundant in samples from the Androscoggin River immediately downstream of a pulp mill effluent release site. The detection of a PARAFAC component associated with an anthropogenic source of DOM, such as pulp mill effluent, demonstrates the importance for rigorously analyzing PARAFAC residuals and developing system-specific models.

# 1. Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is ubiquitous to all natural waters and contains chemical compounds derived from both natural sources, such as terrestrial vegetation, soils, and microorganisms, and anthropogenic sources, such as municipal and industrial wastewater discharge. Understanding DOM dynamics provides insight into the potential ecological effects of climate and land-use changes on coastal ecosystems (Bianchi and Allison, 2009). Changes in land use that increase DOM export may alter the chemistry of coastal ocean ecosystems, increase the extent of eutrophication and hypoxia (Cloern, 2001), or exacerbate harmful algal blooms (Anderson et al., 2002). For example, understanding mixing and transformation of DOM from anthropogenic sources in the ocean has potential benefits for detecting and understanding ecological factors leading to blooms of the ‘‘red tide’’ Alexandrium species common to the Gulf of Maine. Another important impact of DOM from anthropogenic sources in the Gulf of Maine may be associated with pulp mill effluent, which contains lignin degradation products and can alter fish reproduction and sexual characteristics (Sandstrom et al., 1988). Therefore, detecting and monitoring terrestrial inputs to the coastal ocean is important for protecting marine resources.

Recent advances in fluorescence spectroscopy have demonstrated the effectiveness of fluorescence for characterizing and tracking DOM from diverse sources, including terrestrial plants and soils, microbial communities in freshwater and marine ecosystems, and from anthropogenic wastewaters (Stedmon et al., 2003; Cory and McKnight, 2005; Cory et al., 2007; Yamashita et al., 2008; Murphy et al., 2008; Jaffe et al., 2008; Fellman et al., 2010; Larsen et al., 2010). Fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) collected from diverse natural waters have consistent major fluorophores that can be characterized as humic-like and amino acid-like (Coble et al., 1998; Stedmon et al., 2003; Cory and McKnight, 2005; Yamashita et al., 2008). Differences in fluorescence between sources have been employed as forensic tools to trace water flow and assess the impacts of anthropogenic activities on water quality. For instance, the strong protein-like fluorescence signals exhibited by DOM in municipal wastewater effluents have been used to monitor the effects of wastewater on rivers and streams (Westerhoff et al., 2001; Baker, 2002; Henderson et al., 2009). In marine systems, fluorescence analysis has been used to trace sources of ship ballast water (Murphy et al., 2006; Hall and Kenny, 2007), to monitor wastewater effluent plumes with in situ sensors (Tedetti et al., 2010), and for remote sensing applications (Vodacek, 1989; Vodacek et al., 1995; Siegel et al., 2005). Fluorescence has also been used to track the presence of pulp mill effluents containing compounds such as lignin sulfonates and other lignin degradation products in coastal marine waters (Nyquist, 1979; Santos et al., 2000). Nyquist (1979) measured 3- dimensional fluorescence characteristics of common pulp mill effluent compounds such as lignin sulfonates, lignin, and degraded lignin, which show similar fluorescence maxima near excitation (ex) $2 8 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and emission (em) $3 5 8 \mathrm { n m }$ . Fluorescence scans of Swedish coastal waters showed similarities to those of lignin compounds, indicating transport of terrestrial, lignin-derived compounds to the sea (Nyquist, 1979). Santos et al. (2000) identified a similar fluorescence peak at an excitation wavelength of $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and an emission wavelength of $3 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ as characteristic of pulp mill effluent in river water samples.

Statistical decomposition of EEMs collected on filtered samples in a laboratory can elucidate changes in overlapping fluorophores that are not easily discerned using visual interpretation. For instance, with a dataset containing substantial variation and many EEMs (more than ${ \sim } 5 0$ ), one can use parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC), a multivariate statistical method, to develop models to decompose individual EEMs (Bro, 1997; Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Stedmon et al. (2003) applied PARAFAC to EEMs from a Danish estuary and developed a 5-component model to trace fluorescent DOM dynamics. Further application of PARAFAC has shown that this approach can distinguish fluorescent components that overlap and have subtle differences. Cory and McKnight (2005) created two subsequent PARAFAC models: a microbial-only model based on Antarctic samples (microbial model) and a geographically global model containing 13 components (Cory and McKnight, 2005). Both models were based on spectra from oxic and anoxic surface waters, and neither contained marine or groundwater samples. The microbial model contained seven total components, all of which were found in the Cory and McKnight (2005) global model as well. Yamashita et al. (2008) developed a seven-component model to represent terrestrial and microbial sources and sinks of DOM in a complex estuarine system. Murphy et al. (2008) developed an eight-component and a nine-component PARAFAC model, which were useful in distinguishing ballast water source (coastal or open ocean) on ships in the Pacific and Atlantic Oceans, respectively. Many of the fluorescent components resolved across different PARAFAC models have consistent spectra, despite the large variation in source waters and number of model components. This consistency allows for meaningful comparison of new models with those already developed.

In this study we applied fluorescence analysis to samples obtained from the Gulf of Maine, Penobscot Bay, and rivers within its watershed. We developed a PARAFAC model for the Gulf of Maine system (GoM PARAFAC model) and also applied an existing 13-component model (Cory and McKnight, 2005). The dataset used for the GoM PARAFAC model is comprised of river samples including the Kennebec River, Penobscot River and tributaries, pulp millimpacted water from the Androscoggin River, mixed samples from Penobscot Bay, and marine samples from the Gulf of Maine. The results suggest the presence of pulp mill effluents in the waters of the Gulf of Maine. We also present a novel approach to analyzing residuals from established PARAFAC models to better characterize DOM.

# 2. Methods

# 2.1. Site description

The Gulf of Maine receives freshwater from rivers in Maine, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, and the Canadian provinces of New Brunswick and Nova Scotia. The Penobscot River is the second-largest river in New England, USA. It drains 8570 sq. miles and enters Penobscot Bay at the City of Bucksport, Maine. The Penobscot watershed is sparsely populated, mainly forested, and contains abundant wetland areas and numerous reservoirs and lakes (Bohlen and Lewis, 2009). The Penobscot River empties into Penobscot Bay, which flows into the Gulf of Maine (Fig. 1). The Androscoggin River flows into the Gulf of Maine southwest of Penobscot Bay (Fig. 1). The Androscoggin River is heavily impacted by industrial activities, including pulp mill effluent from several paper mills (Hunt et al., 2005; Salisbury et al., 2008). The Kennebec River also has a history of being impacted by pulp mill effluent (Salisbury et al., 2008). However, it has higher flow rates and potentially greater dilution of the pulp mill effluent. Effluent from the numerous paper and pulp mills has been regulated in Maine since environmental legislation was adopted in the 1970’s (Koster et al., 2007). Even with environmental protections, the Maine Department of Environmental Protection has estimated that the combined permitted pulp mill effluent could account for up to $1 /$ 8 of the total flow of the Androscoggin River during summer low-flow conditions (document: DEPLW-1119, Department of Environmental Protection, Bureau of Land and Water Quality, State of Maine). Therefore, pulp mill effluent is a potentially important anthropogenic input to the Gulf of Maine ecosystem that may vary temporally.

# 2.2. Sample collection and analysis

Water samples from the Gulf of Maine, Penobscot Bay, Penobscot River, Kennebec River, Androscoggin River, and many tributaries were collected seasonally over the period 2004–2007 through the GoMOOS (Gulf of Maine Ocean Observing System) and NASA interdisciplinary research studies (www.GoMOOS.org). Samples were collected in May–July 2010 just downstream of the Verso pulp mill on the Androscoggin River, about $2 0 \mathrm { k m }$ upstream of Lewiston, in order to compare them to the previous sample set from the Gulf of Maine system. The water samples were filtered through a $0 . 4 5 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ cartridge filter in the field into pre-combusted amber glass bottles with Teflon lined caps. Samples were kept cold and in the dark while being shipped overnight to the US Geological Survey lab in Boulder, CO. Within 2 working days of arriving in

Two instruments, employing different methods of oxidation, were used to measure DOC concentration. The platinum catalyzed persulfate wet oxidation method of the OI Analytical Model 700 TOC analyzer yields higher DOC concentration estimates than the high-temperature combustion method (Kaplan, 2000), and was used to analyze samples with chloride concentrations ${ < } 0 . 0 2 \mathrm { ~ M ~ }$ (Aiken, 1992). Since chloride interferes with the wet oxidation method (Aiken, 1992), the high temperature combustion method of the Shimadzu TOC-VCPH was employed to analyze samples that contained ${ \tt > } 0 . 0 2$ M chloride. $\mathsf { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were calculated as the ratio of the UV absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ to DOC concentration $( \mathrm { m g \mathrm { - } C L ^ { - 1 } } .$ ) according to Weishaar et al. (2003).

EEMs were collected on a Jobin-Yvon Horiba Fluoromax-3 fluorometer using a $5 \mathrm { - n m }$ bandwidth for both excitation and emission. EEMs were collected over an excitation range of $2 4 0 { - } 4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ every $5 \mathrm { n m }$ , with an emission range of $3 0 0 { - } 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ every $2 \mathrm { n m }$ in a 1-cm quartz cuvette. If necessary, samples were diluted to an absorbance of $< 0 . 2 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ at $\lambda = 2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ prior to fluorescence analysis to ensure that samples were within the absorbance range optimal for correction of inner-filter effects. EEMs were blank-subtracted with a deionized water EEM using the instrument software (Datamax), corrected for excitation and emission instrument optics using factors supplied by the manufacturer, Raman area normalized from a Raman scan ( $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ excitation wavelength, $3 8 1 - 4 2 6 \mathrm { n m }$ emission range) and then inner-filter corrected (Ohno, 2002).

The fluorescence index (FI) is reported as the ratio of emission intensities at $4 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $5 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at an excitation wavelength of $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Cory and McKnight, 2005). All fluorescence data and indices were calculated and reported on the same corrected EEM spectra. A high FI value (1.8) generally indicates autochthonous, microbial fluorescence character, whereas a low FI (1.2) signifies an allochthonous, terrestrial fluorescence signature. Intermediate FI values are found in waters with DOM from blended sources (McKnight et al., 2001; Cory and McKnight, 2005).

The humification index (HIX) was calculated using the methods of Ohno (2002). EEMs were interpolated from the emission intensities at an excitation of $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $2 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ using a linear interpolation function in Matlab in order to determine the emission intensities at an excitation of $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ , which were not directly measured. The HIX is the ratio between the sum of fluorescence intensity at $4 3 5 { - } 4 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and that at $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . HIX values ranged from 0 to 1, with larger values indicating higher degree of humification (Ohno, 2002). Ohno (2002) measured HIX values for soil fulvic acid extracts and a subsequent study (Chen et al., 2011) found that HIX values for seawater were distinctively lower than for river or soil extract samples and may be useful for determining DOM source and character in aquatic environments.

The biological index (BIX) was calculated using the methods of Huguet et al. (2009). The BIX is the ratio of the fluorescence intensity at $e x = 3 1 0 \mathrm { n m } / e m = 3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $e x = 3 1 0 \mathrm { n m } / e m = 4 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and increases with increasing autochthonous DOM inputs. A $\mathsf { B I X } > 1$ corresponds to a dominant autochthonous DOM source, while BIX values $0 . 6 \mathrm { - } 0 . 7$ or lower indicate lower amounts of autochthonous production (Huguet et al., 2009).

# 2.3. PARAFAC modeling

Because of differences in intensity greater than an order of magnitude among the sample EEMs, EEMs were normalized to a maximum intensity of unity after being trimmed to remove first- and second-order Rayleigh scattering. The GoM PARAFAC model was created using 270 EEMs in accordance with Stedmon and Bro (2008). The number of EEMs from the rivers, Penobscot Bay, and the Gulf of Maine were approximately equal. A seven-component model was validated using the split-half technique with excitation and emission ranges of $2 5 0 { - } 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 5 0 \mathrm { - } 5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. The seven-component model showed a lower sum of squared error than validated models with fewer components. We were unable to validate a model containing more than seven components. After validation of the GoM PARAFAC model, the original non-normalized EEMs were modeled with it. The Cory and McKnight (2005) model was also applied to the 270 EEMs used to create the GoM PARAFAC model. Residuals were determined as the difference between the measured and the modeled EEMs. A significant residual was determined as $1 0 \%$ of the measured EEM intensity at a specific excitation/emission pair. We also created an unvalidated 14-component model by combining the Cory and McKnight (2005) model with GoM component 6 in order to confirm that component 6 (C6) was the component consistently appearing in the residuals of the Cory and McKnight (2005) model. We used Tucker’s congruence coefficients (TCC) calculated using a Matlab function to determine if components from the GoM model were statistically similar to components from the Cory and McKnight (2005) model (Lorenzo-Seva and Berge, 2006). In order to prevent bias, all seven of the GoM components were compared to each of the 13 components in the Cory and McKnight (2005) model. We set the minimum congruence coefficient to 0.85 for a match. TCC values of 0.95 or greater are considered by Lorenzo-Seva and Berge (2006) significant enough to be ‘‘equal’’, with values between 0.85 and 0.95 considered ‘‘fair’’.

SPSS software was used for ANOVA with Tukey post hoc tests and analysis of means to determine statistical differences between river, Penobscot Bay, and Gulf of Maine sample DOM character.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. DOM distribution and character within rivers, Penobscot Bay, and the Gulf of Maine

The DOC concentration varied widely in the GoM system, ranging from $0 . 9 \ : \mathrm { m g \mathrm { - } C / L }$ in the Gulf of Maine to $2 5 \mathrm { m g - C / L }$ in the Penobscot River. DOC values for the Androscoggin River fell within that range and varied from 5 to $8 \mathrm { m g - } C / \mathrm { L }$ . The DOC concentrations of the 2010 pulp mill-impacted Androscoggin River samples were 6.7 and $7 . 7 \ : \mathrm { m g - C / L }$ . The average DOC values in the rivers, Bay, and Gulf (Fig. 2) are all significantly different $p < 0 . 0 5 ,$ . DOM concentration was the highest in the rivers, followed by Penobscot Bay, and finally by the Gulf of Maine.

EEMs from representative samples in the Penobscot River, Androscoggin River, and Gulf of Maine (Fig. 3) show that the maximum fluorescence intensity is much lower in the Gulf of Maine samples than the river samples. This is consistent with the lower DOC values measured in the Gulf than in the rivers. DOC values were significantly correlated to fluorescence intensity ${ \mathit { p } } = 0 . 0 1 6$ , $r = 0 . 1 4 6$ ).

Similar to the patterns observed by Yamashita et al. (2008), average FI values increased from the rivers (1.28), to Penobscot Bay (1.50), to the Gulf of Maine (1.52) (Fig. 2). The average FI for river samples was significantly lower $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ than samples from Penobscot Bay or the Gulf or Maine. However, Penobscot Bay and Gulf of Maine FI values were not significantly different from each other $( p = 0 . 2 2 4 )$ . The low FI value for river samples indicates a terrestrially derived fluorescence signature in the rivers, and the higher FI combined with lower DOC concentrations in Penobscot Bay and the Gulf of Maine indicate a dilution of the terrestrial, riverine DOM with autochthonous, microbially-produced DOM from the coastal areas.

Chen et al. (2011) found that soil extracts had higher HIX values than river samples, which had higher HIX values than saline bay samples. Similarly, we measured a high average HIX value (0.92) in the rivers and lower HIX values in Penobscot Bay and the Gulf of Maine (0.65 and 0.73, respectively) (Fig. 2). The average HIX measurements suggest that there are likely more inputs of microbially-produced DOM in the Bay and Gulf compared to the rivers (Fig. 2). Average HIX values were significantly different $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ .

The average BIX values showed the opposite trend as HIX, with a low value (0.47) in the rivers, which is below the values reported for rivers by Huguet et al. (2009) indicating the rivers in the Gulf of Maine system have extremely low autochthonous inputs. The BIX was highest for Penobscot Bay (0.77) and slightly lower for the Gulf of Maine (0.74), both of which are in the range of intermediate autochthonous inputs according to Huguet et al. (2009). Based on the BIX values it is evident that terrestrial DOM sources continue to be important even in the estuarine and marine areas and that autochthonous sources increase in abundance in the Gulf of Maine and Penobscot Bay. The average BIX for the rivers was significantly lower $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ than either Penobscot Bay or the Gulf of Maine, which were not statistically different from each other $( p = 0 . 3 5 7$ ).

# 3.2. Fluorescence EEMs and PARAFAC modeling

We validated seven components when applying PARAFAC to our 270-EEM Gulf of Maine system data set (Table 1). The magnitude of the residuals and sum of squared error was lower with the seven-component model compared to models with fewer components. Each validated component (Fig. 4) had one or two excitation maxima and only one emission maximum. We classified components 1–3 as terrestrial humic-like components based on their excitation/emission maxima and similarity to terrestrial, humic components found in other studies (Cory and McKnight, 2005; Yamashita et al., 2008). Component 4 shows similarities to marine humic-like fluorophores, although this component was important in the river samples as well as the Bay and Gulf of Maine (Fig. 5). Component 5 was categorized as a protein-like component similar to the amino acid tryptophan. Generally, the GoM model components were similar to those observed in other models (Coble et al., 1998; Cory and McKnight, 2005; Yamashita et al., 2008) except for component 6, which was associated with samples collected in the Androscoggin River throughout the study and the Gulf of Maine in 2004 and 2005. Component 7 has spectral characteristics of a humic-like fluorophore and was found with low intensities in all samples (Fig. 5).

Component 6 has an excitation maximum at $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , a less intense maximum at $3 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and an emission maximum at $4 3 6 \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. 4). Samples containing the largest relative amounts of component 6 were from the Gulf of Maine in 2004 and 2005 and the heavily pulp mill-impacted Androscoggin River in all years sampled for this study (Fig. 5). Emission spectra at the peak excitation wavelength for component 6 show similarities in spectral shape but dramatic differences in intensity (i.e., concentration) between the pulp mill effluent in the Androscoggin River and the Gulf of Maine sample collected in 2005 (Fig. 6). The Gulf of Maine samples collected in 2007 do not show the same peak emission at $4 3 6 \mathrm { n m }$ . The observation of a fluorescence signature associated with pulp mill effluent in the Gulf is consistent with results of Nyquist (1979) who calculated a half-life of 3.8 years for lignin sulfonates from the wood-processing industry that were found in Swedish coastal waters.

Lignosulfonic acid, the dotted line in Fig. 4 (on the same plot as component 6), is likely to be one of many lignin degradation products found in pulp mill effluent. Although the model compound does not exhibit a second excitation maximum at $3 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , it is likely that component 6, like other PARAFAC components, is a mixture of fluorophores, with different excitation maxima that co-vary. The emission maxima for component 6 $( 4 3 6 \mathrm { n m } )$ ), lignin sulfonates $3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ; Nyquist, 1979), pulp mill effluent $3 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ; Santos et al., 2000), and lignosulfonic acid $( 3 9 2 \mathrm { n m } )$ vary more than the excitation maxima. The larger variation in emission maxima may be due to variations in the actual chemical moieties present in each sample. Also, emission spectra are more sensitive in general to the local chemical environment surrounding the fluorophore (Cory and McKnight, 2005).

Component 1, associated with a terrestrial DOM source, has an excitation maximum at $< 2 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and an emission maximum at

$4 3 6 \mathrm { n m }$ . This component is within the range described by Coble et al. (1998) for peak A, also called the UV humic-like peak. Component 1 is visually similar to a combination of components Q1, Q2, and Q3 of the Cory and McKnight (2005) PARAFAC model and matches the sum of these components with a Tucker’s congruence coefficient (TCC) value of 0.86 (Fig. 4). Component 1 shows broad excitation in the $3 0 0 { - } 3 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ range, possibly corresponding to the small secondary maximum of Q3. It has an emission curve most similar to the terrestrial quinone-like component Q2 but with some emission intensity in the $3 0 0 { - } 3 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ region similar to the terrestrial Q1 component. This component is very similar, but $< 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ blue-shifted, to C1 found by Yamashita et al. (2008) in their coastal PARAFAC model.

The second terrestrial component in the GoM model, component 2, has an emission maximum at $4 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ and two excitation maxima: one at $3 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and a lower-intensity one at $< 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The peak at the major excitation wavelength is within the range of peak C in Coble et al. (1998). Component 2 is visually similar to C1 in the Cory and McKnight (2005) model (associated with terrestrially-derived DOM) and matches with a TCC value of 0.85. Similarly, the patterns in this dataset support the conclusion that component 2 is associated with terrestrial DOM because it has a higher relative abundance in the river samples than in Penobscot Bay and the Gulf of Maine (Fig. 5). Component 2 has a similar maximum excitation wavelength to C2 in Yamashita et al. (2008) and has an emission maximum that is ${ \sim } 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ blue-shifted.

The third component identified as terrestrial in the GoM model, component 3, has two excitation maxima and a single emission maximum. The higher-intensity excitation maximum is at $2 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and the lower intensity maximum at $3 9 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ; the emission maximum is at $5 1 2 \mathrm { n m }$ . Component 3 is visually similar to terrestrial SQ1 resolved in the Cory and McKnight (2005) model, with a TCC value of 0.85 (Fig. 4). Component 3 is visually similar to C3 in Yamashita et al. (2008), which is attributed to terrestrial origins and the degradation of particulate organic matter (POM). However, the relative intensities of the two excitation maxima are switched, such that C3 in the Yamashita et al. (2008) model has a higher intensity at $3 9 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and a lower intensity at $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . Also, the emission maximum of component 3 is red-shifted by $3 3 \mathrm { n m }$ relative to C3 in the Yamashita et al. (2008) model.

The first marine humic-like component resolved in the GoM model, component 4, has a major excitation maximum at $3 0 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and a less intense excitation maximum at $< 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , with an emission maximum at $3 9 6 \mathrm { n m }$ . The dominant peak of component 4 is within the range of peak M from Coble et al. (1998). Further, component 4 shows similarities to the Cory and McKnight (2005) C3 that was found in the microbial-only model (TCC value of 0.9; Fig. 4). The Cory and McKnight C3 only had one excitation maximum, but it is very similar to the location of the highest-intensity excitation maximum in component 4. The major excitation maximum for component 4 is slightly blue-shifted and the emission maximum is slightly red-shifted, but they both show excitation maxima at similar locations. Component 4 also shows similarities to C6 in Yamashita et al. (2008).

Component 7, a ubiquitous humic-like component in the GoM model, shares similarities with SQ2 in the Cory and McKnight (2005) model, with a TCC value of 0.85 (Fig. 4). Component 7 has two excitation maxima, the higher-intensity at $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and the lower-intensity at $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and a single emission maximum at $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . Compared to the Cory and McKnight (2005) SQ2, component 7 has slightly blue-shifted excitation maxima. SQ2 was found in both the microbial model and their total model and was classified as semiquinone-like based on its spectral similarities to reduced anthraquinone disulfonate (AQDS) (Cory and McKnight, 2005). Component 7 was present in similar relative abundance throughout the GoM system. We did not see a component similar to component 7 in the Yamashita et al. (2008) model.

Component 5 shows protein-like fluorescence at an excitation maximum of $2 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and an emission maximum of $< 3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. 4). Coble et al. (1998) saw fluorescence in this region for the T peak associated with tryptophan-like (trp) fluorescence. Cory and McKnight (2005) found a tryptophan-like component $e x = 2 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , $e m = 3 6 0 \mathrm { n m } .$ ) in the microbial-only dataset and the global model, but only a tyrosine-like (tyr) component $e x = 2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , $e m < 3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ) was resolved in the global model. GoM component 5 was statistically equivalent (Lorenzo-Seva and Berge, 2006) to the tryptophan-like component in the Cory and McKnight (2005) model, with a TCC value of 0.95, the highest in this study. However, it is likely that fluorescence modeled by component 5 is not a result solely of tryptophan-like compounds in the samples. It is possible that in the Penobscot watershed and Gulf of Maine the tyrosine fluorescence was quenched by energy transfer to nearby tryptophan in protein-like structures (Lakowicz, 2006), explaining why they were not resolved separately in the GoM model. While often associated with protein-like fluorophores, fluorescence in this region could also be due to simple phenols, such as tannins and gallic acid (see Maie et al., 2007), and lignin phenols (Hernes et al., 2009), especially in the river samples which are influenced heavily by terrestrial vegetation such as the Penobscot and Androscoggin rivers.

# 3.3. Distribution of fluorescent components using the Cory and McKnight (2005) model

To further evaluate whether component 6 is a unique fluorescent component not found in other PARAFAC models, we applied the Cory and McKnight (2005) model to our GoM system dataset and found significant residuals in $1 8 \%$ of the samples (an example is shown in Fig. 7). The significant residuals had consistent peaks and similar fluorescence spectra to GoM component 6 (Fig. 8).

As a final step to evaluate whether the unexplained portion of sample spectra modeled with the Cory and McKnight (2005) model can be accounted for by component 6, we modeled the GoM system samples with a composite model constructed by adding GoM component 6 as a 14th component to the Cory and McKnight (2005) model. We used two different components as the 14th component: (1) the averaged Cory and McKnight model residuals of all GoM samples with ${ > } 1 0 \%$ component 6 according to the GoM PARAFAC model and (2) component 6 from the GoM model. In the first composite model, using the averaged residual, we obtained results with noisier and lower intensity residuals compared to the original Cory and McKnight (2005) model (Fig. 7). In the second composite model, with the addition of component 6 from the GoM PARAFAC model, residuals were much lower in intensity than the first composite model but similarly noisy (Fig. 7). Summarizing, we saw good results (low intensity and noisy residuals) for the second composite model (Cory and McKnight $( 2 0 0 5 ) + \mathsf { G o M }$ component 6) and the original seven-component GoM model (Fig. 7).

The results from the Cory and McKnight $( 2 0 0 5 ) + \mathsf { G o M }$ component 6 and our GoM PARAFAC model both show that the distribution of components changes throughout the GoM system (Fig. 5). The protein-like fluorescent components, GoM component 5 and Cory and McKnight (2005) Tyr and Trp, are found in much higher percentages in the Gulf and Bay compared to the river, but they do not show identical distributions in the Penobscot Bay and GoM samples (Fig. 5). Component 6 is only found in large percentage $5 1 0 \%$ total fluorescence) in the Androscoggin River samples and Gulf of Maine samples collected in 2004 and 2005. Component 2 and Cory and McKnight (2005) C1, both identified as fluorescent components of terrestrial origin, are found in higher percentages in the Penobscot River compared to Penobscot Bay and the Gulf of Maine.

# 4. Conclusions

In this study we were able to identify trends in DOM source and character that were captured in the DOC concentration, $\mathrm { S U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 } .$ and FI and HIX, which were consistent with changes in PARAFAC component distributions. We developed a new fluorescence PARAFAC model from samples collected in the Gulf of Maine system. The GoM model contains several components with similarities to peaks identified by the ‘‘peak picking’’ method of Coble et al. (1998) and PARAFAC models created by Cory and McKnight (2005) and Yamashita et al. (2008). However, component 6 of the GoM model was unique to the Gulf of Maine and pulp mill effluent-impacted rivers and tributaries.

Component 6 was spectrally similar to the residuals found in several GoM samples modeled with the Cory and McKnight (2005) model, confirming that this unique component could not be represented by the Cory and McKnight (2005) model, which included a broad range of samples from diverse environments, none of which receive pulp mill effluent. We identified component 6 as being associated with pulp mill effluent due to its spectral similarity to Androscoggin River water near pulp mill effluent discharge and lignosulfonic acid. These results show that system-specific PARAFAC models are particularly useful in systems with anthropogenic impacts, like the pulp mill effluent in the Androscoggin River, which feeds into the Gulf of Maine. Our results also show that fluorescence is a useful tool for monitoring pulp mill effluent as it is transported throughout a watershed into the marine system.

While we are confident about the source of component 6 in the Androscoggin River samples, we are not able to fully explain the differences in the percentage of component 6 in the Gulf of Maine samples between those samples collected in 2004 and 2005 compared to subsequent years. Variation in pulp mill effluent discharge and treatment protocols may have been a factor. Major changes in pulp mill effluent treatment protocols designed to improve effluent quality implemented in 2005 may have influenced the amount of fluorescent effluent reaching the Gulf of Maine sampling sites after 2005 (Darby, 2009). Additionally, fewer pulp mills are operational in the Gulf of Maine watershed than in previous years. Ultimately, changes to the number of sources of pulp mill effluent and to treatment protocols may have a large impact on its fate and transport within the Gulf of Maine system.
