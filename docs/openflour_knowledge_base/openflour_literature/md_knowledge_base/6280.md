# ORIGINAL ARTICLE

# Large differences in bacterial community composition of nearby shallow lakes surrounded by Nothofagus pumilio forest in Patagonia (Argentina)

MARCELA BASTIDAS NAVARRO

laboratorio de limnología, inibioma, conicet-universidad nacional del comahue, quintral 1250, bariloche 8400, argentina

\*corresponding author: mbastidas@comahue-conicet.gob.ar

Received December 18, 2021; accepted March 21, 2022

Corresponding editor: Beatrix E. Beisner

Freshwater ecosystems are integrated in a terrestrial landscape, receiving continuously soil and plant-derived material. In Patagonia, the deciduous beech Nothofagus pumilio constitutes an important source of organic matter for aquatic environments. Here, I attempted to analyze bacterial communities, including genetic diversity and metabolic functioning, in four nearby lakes located under a ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio forest (Chall-Huaco Mountain, Argentina). I combined fieldwork and experiments to assess physicochemical characteristics, bacterial community composition (BCC) and Carbon (C)-consumption. Physicochemical variables analyzed with NMDS showed a clear segregation of all the studied lakes. Similarly, BCC differed among lakes, maintaining its differences regardless of the seasons. Variables that significantly affected bacterial community structuring were dissolved $\mathrm { P }$ concentration and protein-like DOM component. The number of specialist operational taxonomic units was higher than that of generalists, but the latter doubled specialists in number of sequences. The last suggests that generalists may be responsible for the differences in BCC among lakes, and species sorting appears to be the main mechanism structuring the metacommunity. These results showed that even at a small geographic scale distinctive bacterial communities can be developed in lakes under the influence of ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio forest.

KEYWORDS: Nothofagus pumilio forest; Patagonian shallow lakes; dissolved organic matter; bacterial community composition; niche breadth

# INTRODUCTION

Inland waters usually display high diversity of water bodies with different degrees of connectivity between each other and often surrounded by different terrestrial environments (Ruiz-González et al., 2015a). In these complex networks, microbial communities form part of a larger metacommunity, which is a group of local communities connected by dispersal of species that within each local habitat interact with each other and respond to local environmental conditions (Leibold et al., 2004, Nelson et al., 2009, Ruiz-González et al., 2015b, Niño-García et al., 2016a, Comte et al., 2018). The metacommunity framework establishes that differences between communities can result from processes, such as the selection of species by the environmental and biological conditions (species sorting, niche theory), and its dispersion (neutral theory). The relative importance of species sorting usually depends on the spatial scale at which processes are linked to differences in environmental heterogeneity and dispersal rates within a metacommunity (Martiny et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2013; Zinger et al., 2014; Comte et al., 2016). Dispersion in freshwater bacteria communities is related to ambient connectivity, so intense immigration or transport of species among environments could render similar communities (mass effect) despite different environmental conditions. On the contrary, dispersal limitation (due to spatially isolated environments) could lead to different communities, regardless of similar environmental conditions (Leibold et al., 2004). In this sense, habitats at the terrestrial-freshwater interface are ideal for addressing questions about metacommunity ecology.

Freshwater ecosystems are integrated into a terrestrial landscape, receiving continuously soil and plant-derived material, including particulate and dissolved organic matter (DOM), and microorganisms (Crump et al., 2012; Ruiz-González et al., 2015b; Comte et al., 2018). Differences in forest types (monospecific or mixed) or diversity of tree species, can develop distinct microbial communities that inhabit soil by altering taxonomic composition and activity (Xu et al., 2009; Kembel et al., 2014; Rivest et al., 2015; Ruiz-González et al., 2018). The soil under different tree species can show differences in physical and chemical properties, but also can be inoculated by a specific bacterial community, since phyllosphere bacterial assemblages are speciesspecific (Kim et al., 2012). It can be thus expected that freshwater environments located under the influence of a monospecific forest would receive similar bacterial communities transported from the surrounding catchment. Moreover, nutrients derived from soil and leaf litter that enters the aquatic environment would be similar, so this should not trigger major changes in taxonomic composition and functioning of the local lake bacterial communities.

In Patagonia, the deciduous tree Nothofagus pumilio (Poepp. et Endl.) Krasser, locally known as “lenga”, dominates high-elevation environments of Argentina and Chile occurring as monospecific forests from $3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { ~ S ~ }$ down to $5 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { ~ S ~ }$ on the Cordillera de los Andes, constituting part of the world’s most austral forests (Hildebrand-Vogel et al., 1990). It usually forms the upper altitudinal boundary of the forest displaying an abrupt transition between forests and subalpine vegetation, the treeline (Veblen et al., 1996). Particularly, in the North Patagonian Andes $( { \sim } 4 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { S } )$ , the deciduous austral beech grows as erect trees, decreasing in importance toward higher altitude, to krummholz (upright trees taller than $3 ~ \mathrm { m }$ ) that dominate the treeline ecotone up to $1 6 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ a.s.l. (Daniels and Veblen, 2004). In lakes and rivers located below the treeline, ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio constitutes the major source of organic matter, since it presents an autumn pulse of leaf fall that enters directly into aquatic environments; thus, the input and breakdown timing of this leaf litter are key factors of mountain ecosystem functioning (Bastidas Navarro et al., 2014; Bastidas Navarro et al., 2019; Villanueva and Albariño, 2021).

Previous studies performed in the Northern Patagonian Andes have shown that the quality of the DOM pool and Phosphorus $( \mathrm { P } )$ concentrations, as well as the bacterial community diversity, change along an altitudinal gradient in mountain lakes, decreasing toward higher altitude environments (Bastidas Navarro et al., 2014). This highlights the importance of the terrestrial vegetation (N. pumilio) at and below the treeline for the structure and functioning of aquatic bacterial assemblages. Moreover, an experimental study showed that bacterial C-consumption increased when an allochthonous source of DOM such as ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio leachates were added to lake water because of an increase in $\mathrm { P }$ concentration (lower C:P ratio) (Bastidas Navarro et al., 2019). ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio recovers the lowest P content from its leaves before abscission regarding the other Nothofagus species from the Andean Patagonian region, (Diehl et al., 2003); thus, this monospecific forest constitutes an important P contributor for aquatic environments and a key factor driving bacterial C-consumption and diversity. Bastidas Navarro et al. (2019) also showed differences in the dominant bacterial classes among the different seasons, likely associated with the input of ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio litter in autumn.

In recent years, several studies have paid attention to bacterial communities structure in alpine lakes (Rofner et al., 2017; Su et al., 2021; Vincent et al., 2021), but studies in mountain lakes of forested areas (specially with natural forest) are scarce or completely missing. Thus, the response of bacterial communities under different features of forests (e.g. dominant tree species, senescence) is largely unknown. Here, I aimed to assess the following questions: Do lakes under the influence of a specific species tree, such as ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio forest share the same bacterial community? Are communities from disconnected patches different regardless of similar organic matter and nutrient resources?, and, are there differences in bacterial communities in the different seasons? My first hypothesis proposed that ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio forest supplies a similar source of resources to the environments under its influence, resulting in similar bacterial communities, particularly just after leaves fall (Autumn), and communities from lakes more close or connected would be more similar. A second hypothesis was that the lack of variation in supporting inputs would imply a high proportion of taxa with a wide niche breadth (generalists), over taxa with limited niche breadth (specialists). To test these hypotheses, I combined fieldwork in three different seasons (Autumn, Spring and Summer) and experiments to assess if there are differences in freshwater bacterial communities assembly, including genetic diversity (through hierarchical clustering and Bray Curtis dissimilarity) and metabolic functioning (C-consumption rate), from four nearby environments ( $\mathrm { < } 5 0 \mathrm { m }$ of altitude range, in $3 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ range distance) located at $1 5 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ a.s.l. in a ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio forest.

# METHODS

# Study site and sampling

Sampling was performed in four shallow permanent lakes $( Z _ { \mathrm { m a x } } < 3 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ located in the Northern Patagonian Andes on the eastern slope of Chall-Huaco Mountain $( 4 1 ^ { \circ } 1 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { S } .$ , $7 1 ^ { \circ } 2 0 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { W } )$ in Nahuel Huapi National Park (Patagonia, Argentina). Lakes were located within a distance of $3 3 0 \mathrm { m }$ (geographical distance) and in a $4 0 \mathrm { m } \cdot$ - altitude range (1507–1545 m a.s.l.) (Fig. 1). Lakes Verde and Sol, are isolated from lakes Los Patos, and Mallín. Each pair of lakes could be temporarily connected in winter or spring because of high precipitation (rain and snow) or thaw (personal observation). All the environments included in this study are located in the same forest of pure stands of ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio erected trees (Fig. 1), which constitutes almost $1 0 0 \%$ of the material that enters the aquatic environments (Albariño et al., 2009).

Environments were sampled on three different dates: in austral autumn (16 May 2017) just after leaves began

On each sampling occasion, I determined DOC, TDP, and chlorophyll a concentration, and I performed DOM optical characterization. DOC concentration was determined with a high-temperature combustion analyzer (Shimadzu TOC V-CSH), using potassium hydrogen phthalate as the standard (detection limits: $0 . 5 \mu \mathrm { g } \ L \mathrm { - } 1$ ). TDP was determined by digestion with potassium persulfate at $1 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 1.5 atm for $^ { 1 \mathrm { h } }$ . P concentrations were obtained using the ascorbate-reduced molybdenum method (Eaton et al., 2005) with a Shimadzu UV2450 dual-beam spectrophotometer (detection limits: $0 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { g L } \mathrm { - } 1$ ). The chlorophyll a concentration (Chl a) was determined by extraction with $9 0 \%$ ethanol, according to Nusch (1980), using a fluorometer (Turner Designs, 10-AU).

For each sample, single measurements at specific excitation–emission wavelengths and excitation–emission matrices (EEMs) were performed with a Perkin-Elmer LS 45 fluorescence spectrometer equipped with a xenon discharge lamp. The excitation wavelength intervals were $2 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , between 240 and $4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , and the emission ranged between 300 and $5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , with $5 \mathrm { - n m }$ increments. The measurements were performed at a constant room temperature of $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a 1-cm quartz fluorescence cell. To characterize DOM, fluorescence data were normalized according to Murphy et al. (2013) by dividing the obtained fluorescence values (relative Units) by the Raman peak of MilliQ water at $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ excitation on the same day as the measurement. Fluorescence was then expressed as Raman Units (RU) (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009).

# Genetic diversity, DNA extraction and sequencing

Samples for bacterial assemblage identification were filtered through Nucleopore® filters $( 0 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size, $2 5 \mathrm { \ m m }$ diameter). The Nucleopore filters were stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until further processing. DNA was extracted from the filters using a DNeasy PowerSoil Kit MoBio (Qiagen GMBH) following the manufacturer’s protocol. Purity of DNA was determined by PCR amplification with universal bacterial primers (8F/1492R) and quantified using QuantiT ™ PicoGreen® dsDNA Assay Kit (Invitrogen) following the manufacturer’s protocol (high range curve—half area plate). Unfortunately, the Autumn sample from lake Mallín did not contain enough DNA for sequencing, thus, this sample was absent in the forward analysis.

Purified DNA samples were sent to INDEAR (Rosario University, Argentina). Amplicon sequencing libraries were constructed by PCR amplification of the V1–V3 variable region in the 16S rRNA gene. Amplicon and shotgun libraries were sequenced using 454-FLXPlusTitanium chemistry (Roche, Switzerland). Raw data processing were performed following standard procedures

# Metabolic functioning: Bacterial C-consumption

I applied a dual approach to assess DOM lability in each sampling occasion by standardized laboratory experiments, in darkness at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 28 days. The shortterm dynamic of DOC was analyzed through respiration experiments lasting 2 days, and the long-term dynamic was evaluated by determining the DOC decay over 28 days of incubation. Respiration experiments consisted of the incubation of lake water filtered through sterile polycarbonate filters $2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ Nucleopore pore size) to exclude nanoflagellates. The incubations were performed in duplicate using $5 0 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ ground stoppered Erlenmeyer flasks in an incubation chamber in the dark at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The concentration of dissolved oxygen was measured every $^ { \mathrm { ~ 6 ~ h ~ } }$ with an optical-oxymeter (ODO, Fibox 4 Presens). The oxygen consumption rates were converted to $\mathrm { C O _ { 2 } }$ production to provide short-term estimates of organic carbon consumption using a respiratory quotient of 1 (Mccallister et al., 2006). Afterwards, the C-decay analysis was performed through sampling water every 2–4 days up to 28 days, by collecting $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of sample in precombusted vials $( 4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ to which $4 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $5 ~ \mathrm { M }$ sulfuric acid had been added (Guillemette and Del Giorgio, 2011).

# Statistical analysis and calculations

I tested for significant differences in DOC, TDP, chlorophyll $a$ concentrations, DOC: TDP ratio and k values with two-way ANOVA with season and lakes as factors. When these tests resulted in significant differences I did the posthoc multiple comparison Holm-Sidak test. I checked for normality and homoscedasticity before these analyses. These statistical analyses were done with SigmaPlot 12.5 (Systat Software Inc., San Jose, CA, USA).

Physicochemical characteristics and bacterial community dissimilarities were visualized using nonmetric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) analyses based on Bray Curtis distance. ANOSIM was used to test for significant differences among lakes in the ordinations (Clarke, 1993). The comparison of the distance matrices of BCC and environmental variables for the studied lakes was performed applying a Mantel test. I also performed a redundancy analysis (RDA), on which environmental variables with a strong intercorrelation were eliminated (Ricart et al., 2010), and manual forward selection with Monte Carlo permutation tests $( P \mathrm { ~ < 0 . 0 5 ) }$ was performed to determine which environmental variables should be included. In addition, a Pearson correlation analysis (significance level $P < 0 . 0 5$ ) was used to test for relationship between the dominant bacterial groups and environmental factors/C-consumption rate $( k )$ . These analyses were performed using the R package Vegan (Oksanen, 2013).

Alpha-diversity metrics such as richness, Chao1, and Shannon Index, were calculated with Ampvis2 package in R (v2.5.1) (Andersen et al., 2018). This package was also used to draw heatmaps and clustering of dominant bacterial classes and genera.

i. OTUs with mean relative abundances $< 2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 5 }$ were not considered, as they could erroneously indicate specialized taxa (Pandit et al., 2009). The considered OTUs after the removal of rare taxa were 2259. OTUs with a B-value $> 3$ were considered as habitat generalists and evenly distributed along with a wide range of habitats, whereas OTUs with a B-value $< 1 . 5$ were defined as habitat specialists and unevenly distributed among sampling sites (Logares et al., 2013). B-values $> 3$ was chosen because these values were within the outlier area of the B distribution (Supplementary Fig. Fig. 1). In order to identify which taxa are associated with each investigated environment, it means as an indicator species or strict specialist (Legendre and Legendre, 2012), I used the INDVAL (INDicator VALues) (Dufrêne and Legendre, 1997) analysis, which identifies indicator species based on OTU fidelity and relative abundance. Analyses were performed using the labdsv package within the R program (https://www.r-project.org). Only OTUs with significant $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ INDVAL values that were $> 0 . 3$ were considered (Dufrêne and Legendre, 1997).

The fluorometric EEMs were analyzed with a Parallel Factor analysis (PARAFAC) with the DOMFluor toolbox in MatLab R2015a (The Math-Works, Inc., Natick, MA, USA) (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). The PARAFAC modeling was validated using split-half method analysis (Murphy et al., 2013). I cross-referenced the values of the obtained PARAFAC components with the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al., 2014) by comparing spectra from other studies.

Respiration and C-decay experiments were combined to generate a single decay curve to reconstruct the complete dynamics of C consumption. A first-order decay model based on the multiG model (Westrich and Berner, 1984) with only one reactive member was applied to the single decay curve. The equation was as follows:

$$
G _ { T } ( t ) = G _ { L a b } \left[ \exp \left( - k t \right) \right] + G _ { \mathrm { R e } s }
$$

According to the OTUs relative abundance, they were classified as abundant $> 1 \%$ , uncommon ${ > } 0 . 1 \%$ to ${ < } 1 \%$ , and rare ${ < } 0 . 1 \%$ (Magurran and Henderson, 2003; Pedrós-Alió, 2006; Fuhrman, 2009).

The niche breadth of each identified taxa was calculated according to Pandit et al. (2009) using Levins’ niche breadth index (Levins, 1968):

$$
B _ { j } = 1 / \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { n } P _ { i j } ^ { 2 }
$$

where $\mathrm { B _ { j } }$ indicates the habitat niche breadth of OTUj and $\mathrm { P _ { i j } }$ is the relative abundance of OTUj in a given habitat where GT is the total DOC concentration at time $t$ ; GLab and GRes are the labile and residual pools, respectively; $k$ is the first-order decay constant, and $t$ is the time of decomposition. The $k$ value represents the shape of the overall DOC decline, thereby indicating the evolution of the initial consumption over time. High values of $k$ represent a strong inflection in the DOC vs time curve, suggesting the rapid exhaustion of a highly reactive pool and a rapid transition to refractory carbon. Conversely, low values of $k$ imply relatively constant rates of DOC consumption over time, suggesting a more homogenous composition of the labile pool (Guillemette and Del Giorgio, 2011).

# RESULTS

# The lakes: Nutrients and DOM characteristics

Nutrients (DOC, TDP, and DOC:TDP ratio) and chlorophyll $a$ concentrations showed changes according to the season and the lake sampled (Two-way ANOVA, Season x Lake $P < 0 . 0 0 I$ ) (Table I, Supplementary Fig. Fig. 2). The highest values of DOC, TDP, and chlorophyll $a$ were observed in lake Sol and Verde (for statistical significance, see Table I, Supplementary Fig. Fig. 2).

Based on fluorometric matrices, PARAFAC analysis detected three components (Fig. 2A). The detected components could be grouped into two types according to their emission properties. C1 and C2 had fluorescent properties that are often referred to as humic-like, with emission maxima above $3 8 0 ~ \mathrm { { \ n m } }$ (Supplementary Fig. Fig. 3), typically of terrigenous origin. C3 showed a fluorescence emission $< 3 6 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ which resembles protein or amino-acid-like fluorescence. In particular, C3 had spectral properties similar to those of free dissolved tryptophan. All the components matched aquatic fluorescent components found in the online OpenFluor database depository $9 5 \%$ minimum similarity score; Murphy et al., 2014). For a deeper description of the PARAFAC components identified in this study please see supporting information (Supplementary Fig. Fig. 3). In terms of DOM composition distribution, humic-like components (C1 and C2), constituted ${ > } 6 0 \%$ in lakes Los Patos, Mallín and Sol. In lake Verde, the proteinlike component (C3) showed the highest contribution, around $4 8 \%$ (Fig. 2B) (for statistical significance, see Supplementary Fig. Fig. 2).

# Taxonomy and abundance patterns of Bacterial Communities

For community analysis using the 16S rRNA gene V3- V4 region, a total of 56 475 sequences were classified into four 150 operational taxonomic units (OTUs) after quality analysis and trimming of the original sequences at $9 7 \%$ similarity. The highest OTU richness was observed in Lake Mallín, followed by Lake Los Patos (Table II). Lake Mallín showed also the highest alpha-diversity values, whereas the lowest values were observed in lakes Verde and Los Patos (Table II).

Including all the OTUs identified in the study, an ordination (NMDS) analysis showed major differences among lakes (ANOSIM, Global $R = 0 . 8 2 7$ , $P _ { \mathrm { \ell } } < 0 . 0 0 1$ ), with clear segregation of all the studied environments (pairwise test, $P = 0 . 0 1$ for all comparisons; Fig. 3B). Thus, the differences in BCC among lakes were maintained regardless of the seasons, suggesting that the intralake seasonal variability was smaller than the differences across lakes.

The percentage of abundant OTUs $( > 1 \%$ relative abundance) in all lakes was generally low; however, lake Verde showed a significantly higher proportion of this fraction, reaching $8 . 8 \%$ (332 reads) (ANOVA, $P = 0 . 0 0 1$ , a posterior Holm-Sidak test, $P < 0 . 0 1 7$ , for Lake Mallín vs Los Patos/Sol/Verde) (Table II). Consequently, rare OTUs $( < 0 . 1 \%$ relative abundance) were dominant $( 6 6 . 8 \% - 9 4 . 6 \%$ of the total OTU number), particularly in Lake Mallín.

Comparing the number of shared OTUs among lakes, only $2 \%$ of the total OTUs identified (83 OTUs) were common to all environments (Fig. 4). The shared OTUs accounted for $1 8 \ 0 5 1$ reads, and constituted $2 8 . 8 \%$ of the abundance of the dataset. The most abundant shared OTUs belonged to the genera: Candidatus Rhodoluna (6526 reads), Flavobacterium (1320 reads), Mycobacterium (986 reads) and Limnohabitans (729 reads), among others. The number of unique OTUs in each lake accounted for values between 239 and 185; the highest value of unique OTUs was observed in Lake Mallín, that constituted nearly half of the total identified OTUs $( 4 4 . 7 \%$ ; Fig. 4).

# DISCUSSION

In Patagonia, ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio dominates the Cordillera de los Andes forming a monospecific forest, that constitutes a key source of nutrients and organic matter for aquatic environments (Bastidas Navarro et al., 2014; Bastidas Navarro et al., 2019). Microbial communities from these environments, particularly lakes, have been scarcely studied, and there are no data about dominant bacterial taxa. Contrary to my hypothesis that communities from these lakes should be quite homogeneous since nutrients derived from soil and leaf litter leachates that enter the aquatic environment would be similar, I found relatively large differences in nutrient concentrations, C- consumption rate and BCC.

Differences in physicochemical variables, particularly in TDP and chlorophyll concentrations, among lakes, were unexpected considering that lakes are included in a radius of $3 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ . Previous studies performed in streams from the area have shown seasonal changes in nutrients (TDP, TDN and DOC), associated mainly with ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio leaf fall (Díaz Villanueva et al., 2016; Diaz Villanueva, 2019). However, in this study, the NMDS results did not show seasonal differences in nutrients, chlorophyll $a$ concentration, or DOM composition, probably due to the low frequency of sampling. The largest differences were observed in autumn, which showed the highest values of DOC and TDP, but the majority of the measured variables were relatively constant among seasons. Differences can be attributed to particular in-pond processes that trigger physicochemical variability among lakes, as was previously described in other small ponds in the Andean Altiplane (Aguilar et al., 2016). Lake Sol showed the highest values of TDP, DOC, chlorophyll and humiclike components, likely because this lake is the shallowest sampled and it presents a profuse layer of benthic algae that covers the bottom. An experimental study performed in the area has shown that benthic algae exudate both humic and protein-like compounds, and more C than P, generating an increase in the C:P ratio of DOM corresponding to low DOM quality, and a consequent decrease in bacterial C-consumption rates (Bastidas Navarro et al., 2019). Accordingly, in the present field study, the lowest values of C-consumption rates were observed in lakes Sol and Verde that exhibited the highest DOC: TDP ratios. Differences in C-consumption rates among lakes would be linked to BCC since it is known that bacterial degradation of different DOM components is carried out by phylogenetically different communities (Jones et al., 2009; Logue and Lindström, 2008; Zhou et al., 2021). Correlation analysis between the C-consumption rate (k) and the dominant bacterial groups, showed a significant positive relationship with Gammaproteobacteria, a class with low relative abundance in general. This highlights the key role of low abundance bacterial groups in important metabolic processes such as C-consumption. However, the link between the cycling of C (DOM quality) and the composition of bacterial communities needs more studies, since studies have yielded contrasting results (Ruiz-González et al., 2015b; Logue et al., 2016).

The highest OTU richness, diversity, and the number of exclusive OTUs were detected in the lakes Los Patos and Mallín, and only $2 \%$ (83 OTUs) of the total identified OTUs were common to all lakes. However, in agreement with previous studies (Niño-García et al., 2016b,

Ruiz-González et al., 2017), the shared fraction constituted a relatively high proportion of the total sequences recorded in this study $( 2 8 . 8 \% )$ , since it includes abundant genus such as Limnohabitans, Flavobacterium and Candidatus Rhodoluna. Nevertheless, the low proportion of shared OTUs in terms of number of OTUs, and even the proportion of strict specialist, supports the idea that distinctive bacterial communities inhabit the studied environments, despite being close to each other and under the influence of the same forest.

Several studies have pointed out the key role of local environmental factors shaping BCC, despite the small spatial scale (Langenheder and Ragnarsson, 2007; Székely et al., 2013; Aguilar et al., 2016). In this study, BCC significantly differed among the four studied lakes, with differences in the abundance, and taxonomic level (class and genus). As in the majority of bacterial communities’ studies, the total abundance of rare OTUs was the dominant fraction. However, in lake Verde, abundant OTUs reached $8 . 8 \%$ of total abundance (332 reads). This could be related to a higher stability of lake Verde, considering that lakes Los Patos and Mallín are more affected by streams and lake Sol is a shallow pond that suffers high evaporation during summer. Ordination analysis (NMDS) of physicochemical variables supported this idea, since in lake Verde it can be seen the lowest seasonal variability. In this sense, in a complex boreal network, it was demonstrated that hydrology explained a much larger fraction of the variance in bacterial taxonomic composition in systems below 10 days of water residence time, whereas in ecosystems that exceeded this threshold environmental factors were responsible for most of the variability (Niño-García et al., 2016a). Considering that lake Verde has the highest surface area/maximum depth ratio, so its water residence time and stability should be higher than in the other lakes.

The dominant classes in the studied lakes were Actinobacteria, Flavobacteria, Betaproteobacteria, Alphaproteobacteria and Cytophagia. Previous studies performed in piedmont North-Patagonian lakes, including deep and shallow lakes, agree that Beta and Alphaproteobacteria are dominant bacterial classes in the area, however, also Gammaproteobacteria and Bacteroidea reach high values of relative abundance (Corno et al., 2009; Modenutti et al., 2016; Modenutti et al., 2018). Actinobacteria was also important in the previously mentioned studies, however, never exceeded $2 0 \%$ of the total bacterial abundance, whereas in the present study it constituted around $7 8 \%$ . This can indicate the high influence of soil from the drainage area on the bacterial community structure of these small lakes since Actinobacteria usually dominates soil bacterial communities (Warnecke et al., 2005; Bull et al., 2018; Ruiz-González et al., 2018). In a complex boreal network, it was observed most terrestrial OTUs (from soil samples) are still found in the smallest headwater streams, since even if the land– water interface is an important ecotone that affects the structure of a network metacommunity, it did not act as a dispersal barrier (Ruiz-González et al., 2015a), since. This seems to be the case of Actinobacteria, since in this study are still present in lakes and even dominate the bacterial community, particularly in Lake Verde. Unlike Betaproteobacteria, which exhibit the highest maximum growth rates among freshwater bacteria, Actinobacteria have relatively low growth potential (Simek et al., 2006), and this has been linked with previous observations of low RNA:DNA ratios for members of this phylum (Jones and Lennon, 2010; Campbell and Kirchman, 2013). Rofner et al. (2016) pointed that Actinobacteria have a proportionally lower contribution to P uptake, suggesting that these taxa might have high cellular C:P ratios and thus, lower P requirements, explaining their prevalence in P-limited systems.

As in many species-rich communities, this study showed few habitat generalists and many specialists, in terms of number of OTUs (Romanuk and Kolasa, 2005; Van Der Gast et al., 2011; Logares et al., 2013; Monard et al., 2016; Luo et al., 2019), considering the entire dataset, hence my second hypothesis, which expected a higher proportion of generalist OTUs, over specialists, should be rejected. However, in terms of number of sequences, generalists reached 27 832 reads against less than half for specialists (12 307 reads). This is because the low number of generalist OTUs includes genera with high abundance such as Candidatus Rhodoluna, Limnohabitans, Rhodoferax and Flavobacterium. In this regard, Székely and Langenheder (2014), among other studies (Lennon and Jones, 2011; Lindh et al., 2016), have pointed out for marine bacterial communities that species sorting (BCC regulated by environmental factors) is stronger for the generalist most common taxa, indicating that beta-diversity along environmental gradients can be adequately described by this group. Thus, the high abundance of generalists in our dataset could explain the important differences in BCC among lakes. On the other hand, specialist OTUs are numerous in number of taxa but rare in terms of abundance, suggesting that they may just not be adapted to local environment, and are only transported, without playing a role in ecosystems (Niño-García et al., 2017) since they are inactive or dormant (Lennon and Jones, 2011). Due to biases induced by varying sequencing depth between samples, rare OTUs usually exhibit a substantial stochastic variation in their distribution across time and space, and its variation may be more influenced than that of abundant OTUs (Lindh et al., 2016). However, they are contituents of the seed bank in the environments, and could be vital in maintaining ecosystem stability and function after a disturbance due to their capability to awaken from a dormant state to active one (Lennon and Jones, 2011; Lynch and Neufeld, 2015; Nyirabuhoro et al., 2020), and usually dominate the new communities (Li et al., 2017).

# CONCLUSIONS

In this study distinctive bacterial assemblages were identified in lakes located under the influence of the same ${ \mathcal { N } } .$ pumilio forest, and at a small spatial scale. BCC was not related with seasonal changes in physicochemical variables, however, the main variables that appeared to explain the observed differences among the studied communities were TDP concentration and the protein-like DOM component (C3). As in many previous studies, habitat specialist OTUs showed the highest numbers, with respect to generalists, however, generalists included highly abundant OTUs that could be associated with the differences in BCC among lakes. Nevertheless, we must not take our attention away from specialist rare taxa (specially from the strict ones), because they could play a key role maintaining ecosystem stability and function, particularly after disturbs.
