# Abstract

Melting glaciers play a substantial role in the Arctic hydrography of Svalbard and the associated bacterial processes. In this study, we surveyed the spatial variability in dissolved organic carbon (DOC), fuorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM), and bacterial biogeography (diversity and community composition) using 16S rRNA gene metabarcoding at four fords in Svalbard in August 2019. The environmental heterogeneity of Arctic ford seawater was represented by the water mass balance parameters of surface water (SW), subsurface water (SSW), and intermediate water (IW). The DOC concentrations in the SW (avg. $6 7 \pm 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ , IW (avg. $6 8 \pm 8 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ), and SSW (avg. $6 8 \pm 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ of the surveyed Arctic fords did not difer signifcantly $( P > 0 . 0 1 )$ ). We found that the DOC concentration depended on the microbial community because of the strong association between DOC and marine humic-like FDOM $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 4 8$ for SW and $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 4 5$ for SSW), along with the humifcation, biological, and fuorescence indices. Metabarcoding results revealed that six heterotrophic bacteria (Alphaproteobacteria, Gammaproteobacteria, Bacteroidia, Verrucomicrobiae, Actinobacteria, and Acidimicrobiia) were dominant, and their population dynamics represented the water mass balance. Furthermore, our statistical results indicated that Actinobacteria may signifcantly afect the DOC distribution during the glacier melt season. Our results confrm that the water mass balance shapes the bacterial community composition and suggest that DOC derived from microbial activities warrants signifcant attention considering the efects of climate change during the glacier melt season in the Arctic fords around Svalbard.

# Introduction

Svalbard consists of various fords formed by glacial erosion. Among the Svalbard fords, Van Mijenforden, Van Keulenfjorden, and Hornsund open on the Nordic Sea near the North Atlantic Ocean, which is connected to the Arctic Ocean. Most glaciers in Svalbard fow along a ford or extend into the sea (tidewater glaciers). When glaciers reach a ford or the sea, they form an ice clif, which is then detached and becomes an iceberg or drift ice. Glaciers transport freshwater to the Svalbard fords during the summer melt season, and they show high sensitivity to climate change (Noël et al. 2020). Infow of surface water (SW) from the North Atlantic (Atlantic infow) triggers the retreat of glaciers, which afects not only the hydrography but also the biological production (Hagen et al. 2003).

Biological and physicochemical variability in the Svalbard fords have been investigated owing to concerns about the impact of climate change on the Arctic hydrography (Hop et al. 2002, 2019; Svendsen et al. 2002). In general, the hydrography of Arctic fords is afected primarily by water mass balance among the Atlantic inflow, Arctic coastal waters, and freshwater input from glacial melting, and the dominant water masses are identifed according to the temperature-salinity (TS) diagram (Cottier et al. 2005). For example, SW in Arctic fords depends on the melting of glaciers in late spring and summer, whereas subsurface water (SSW) is afected primarily by the balance between the Atlantic infow and Spitsbergen currents. In addition, mixing between SW and Atlantic water forms intermediate water (IW). This hydrographic balance in Arctic fords is susceptible to seasonal changes in oceanic and atmospheric conditions (Cottier et al. 2005) and afects the ability of marine life to adapt to environmental change in the Arctic ecosystem (Rokkan Iversen and Seuthe 2011). In particular, the melting of glacial ice afects the habitat of bacteria, causing organic matter degradation in Arctic fords (Han et al. 2021).

Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) is one of the largest active carbon pools in the ocean. Because it is similar in amount to atmospheric $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ , DOC in the marine environment is an important factor in the global carbon cycle (Hedges 1992). Most oceanic DOC originates from biological production by marine organisms, and it is removed by either photochemical oxidation (Kieber et al. 1990) or microbial degradation (Benner et al. 1995). Most of the fxed carbon is released into the dissolved organic matter (DOM) pool and channeled into the deep ocean. It is readily remineralized by microorganisms in the microbial loop or persists for hundreds to thousands of years (Pomeroy 1974; Azam 1998; Jiao et al. 2010). In addition, microorganism function is strongly associated with the quality and concentration of the DOM present (Logue et al. 2016; Osterholz et al. 2016).

The DOC in Arctic fords has been found to originate from glacier melt water, in situ primary production, coastal erosion, and glacier-fed riverine runof (Kim et al. 2011; Holding et al. 2017; Müller et al. 2018). One study found that the DOC in an Arctic ford in spring and summer was predominantly of autochthonous origin, for example, from phytoplankton and microbial communities (Brogi et  al. 2019). The DOC fux from the Svalbard glaciers was estimated to be $0 . 0 2 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { C y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ , which is lower than the particulate organic carbon (OC) fux $( 0 . 0 6 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { y e a r } ^ { - 1 } )$ (Zhu et al. 2016). The efciency of OC burial in bottom sediments in the Svalbard fords is $2 8 { - } 9 8 \%$ , indicating that much of the OC reaching the sediments is efectively buried (Zaborska et al. 2018; Koziorowska et al. 2018). A study using a fuorescence excitation–emission matrix (EEM) revealed that tyrosine-like fuorescence dominated the fuorescent DOM (FDOM) composition of the Kongsforden in Svalbard in spring, but humic-like and tryptophan-like fuorescence prevailed in autumn (Brogi et al. 2019). Although Arctic ford zones are biogeochemical hotspots for organic matter cycling (Bianchi et al. 2020) and carbon burial (Zaborska et al. 2018; Koziorowska et al. 2018), the DOC biogeochemistry of Arctic fords is unclear.

Phytoplankton-derived organic matter can attract heterotrophic bacteria and thus afect bacterial community assembly (Mühlenbruch et al. 2018). Recent metagenomic surveys have increased our understanding of the bacterial degradation of organic matter in the ocean (Levine et al. 2012; Varaljay et al. 2012; Choi et al. 2015; Cui et al. 2015; Zeng et al. 2016; Kudo et al. 2018). Although heterotrophic bacteria are dominant in Arctic glacial fords during the summer melt season, the susceptibility of organic matter sources to the overall bacterial composition in this region is unknown (Rokkan Iversen and Seuthe 2011). This study aimed to determine which DOC source is dominant in four Arctic fords during the glacier melt season and to infer its potential efects on bacterial communities. We identifed the organic matter source using FDOM, an optically active component of DOM that absorbs and fuoresces ultraviolet and visible light, and surveyed the bacterial biogeography (diversity and community composition) using metabarcoding-based sequencing with a bacterial 16S rRNA gene in four Arctic glacial fords.

# Materials and methods

# Sampling and hydrological measurements

Seawater samples were collected at discrete depths $\left( n = 3 9 \right)$ ) from 10 vertical water columns using a conductivity-temperature-depth (CTD) rosette system on the RV Helmer Hanssen in August 2019 in Svalbard (Fig. 1). During sample collection, fuorescence, oxygen, salinity, and temperature were measured using sensors in the CTD system. All seawater samples were fltered in the feld using a GF/F flter (Whatman; pore size: $0 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ ). DOC samples were transferred to glass ampoules after fltration and stored at $4 ~ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until analysis. The samples used for FDOM analysis were transferred to dark glass bottles after fltration and stored in a refrigerator at $< 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ before analysis. All flters and glass bottles were precombusted at $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 5 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . For the metabarcoding analysis, $1 \mathrm { ~ L ~ }$ of seawater sampled at each depth was immediately passed through a $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore membrane flter (Merck, Darmstadt, Germany), which were then stored in a deep freezer $( - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ until environmental DNA (eDNA) extraction.

# Analysis of dissolved organic carbon and fuorescent dissolved organic matter

The DOC concentrations were measured using a total OC analyzer $( \mathrm { T O C - V _ { C P H } }$ , Shimadzu, Japan). Five-point standard curves for acetanilide were used to standardize the DOC measurements. In an injection system, acidifed seawater samples were bubbled with high-purity air gas (purity: $9 9 . 9 9 9 \%$ to completely remove inorganic carbon species. The carrier gas was passed at a controlled fow rate of $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L ~ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ through a combustion tube flled with a thermal decomposition catalyst heated to $7 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The DOC samples were oxidized to form $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ , which was detected by a non-dispersive infrared detector. The reliability of the measurements was verifed daily by comparing the measured values with those of a DOC-certifed seawater sample (DSR; $4 4 { - } 4 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ DOC; University of Miami) and procedural blanks, which were all in good agreement (deviation: $< 2 \%$ ).

FDOM fuorescence spectroscopy was performed using a spectrofuorometer (FS-2, SCINCO, Korea). The FDOM signals were analyzed in scan mode, and only the signal from the sample was monitored. The EEMs were recorded by repeatedly scanning emission (Em) wavelengths from 250 to $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $2 \mathrm { n m }$ intervals and excitation $( \operatorname { E x } )$ wavelengths from 250 to $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ intervals. On each day of analysis, deionized water was used as a blank, and its signals were subtracted from the sample spectra to remove

Raman signals. The fuorescence intensities were normalized using a quinine sulfate standard solution in $0 . 1 \mathrm { N } \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S } 0 _ { 4 }$ at $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ wavelengths of $3 5 0 / 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and are expressed in quinine sulfate units (QSU). Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) modeling of the 39 EEM data points was conducted using the MATLAB R2019b program with the DOMFluor toolbox (Stedmon and Bro 2008). Rayleigh and Raman scattering peaks were eliminated and replaced with missing values using three-dimensional Delaunay interpolation (Zepp et al. 2004). The non-negativity constraint was applied in all three modes. The model was validated by split-half analysis (Fig. 2) because the corresponding Tucker’s congruence coefcients exceeded 0.95.

# Deoxyribonucleic acid metabarcoding and statistical analysis

eDNA was extracted from the 39 membrane flters using a PowerWater DNA Isolation Kit (MoBio Laboratories, CA, USA), and its quantity was estimated using a Quant-iT PicoGreen dsDNA reagent (Molecular Probes, OR, USA). Bacterial community DNA was amplifed using the 16S rRNA V3–V4 gene region and sequenced using the Illumina MiSeq platform (Macrogen, Seoul, South Korea) via two-step (amplicon and index) polymerase chain reactions (PCRs) according to Illumina’s instructions regarding PCR primers, conditions, and program and metabarcoding library construction (Illumina 2013). Sequencing data were submitted to the NCBI Sequence Read Archive (https://www.ncbi. nlm.nih.gov/sra) under accession number PRJNA669167. Metabarcoding was preceded by sequence quality fltering as follows: (1) singleton removal, (2) correction of amplifcation and sequencing errors, and (3) normalization of the sequence number and clustering to operational taxonomic units (OTUs) at $9 7 \%$ similarity in mothur (v.1.40.5) (Schloss et al. 2009) according to the MiSeq SOP (Kozich et al. 2013). The OTUs were used to compare the community composition with the Silva.seed_v132 database in mothur according to the MiSeq SOP, and bacterial diversity was visualized by non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) in R (v.3.5.3) (https://www.R-project.org).

Statistical analysis was conducted using packages in R. Briefy, the base package (R Core Team 2018) was used to perform principal component analysis (PCA) using the prcomp function and Pearson correlation analysis using the cor function. The vegan package (Oksanen and Blanchet 2017) was used to calculate the Bray–Curtis distance for permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) using the adonis function and NMDS using the metaMDS function. In addition to PERMANOVA, post hoc pairwise comparison was performed using the Bonferroni method in the pairwise.adonis function (Martinez Arbizu 2017). Indicator species analysis (ISA) was conducted using the indval function of the labdsv package (Roberts and Roberts 2016). A linear regression model was created using the lm function of the stats package (R Core Team 2018) or the non-parametric Theil–Sen estimator of the mblm package (Komsta and Komsta 2013).

# Results

# Water mass classifcation in the surveyed Arctic fords

Thirty-nine seawater samples were obtained from distinct water columns in the Svalbard fords [Van Mijenforden $( n = 1 1 )$ ): M1–M2–M3; Van Keulenforden $( n = 8 )$ ): K1–K2; Hornsund $( n = 1 2$ ): H1–H2–H3; Storforden $( n = 8 )$ : S1–S2]. The sampling depth of each water column is shown in Fig. 1. The seawater samples were classifed as SW $( n = 2 3$ ), IW $( n = 6 )$ , and SSW $( n = 1 0 )$ ) according to the TS reference for Svalbard seawater (Cottier et al. 2005); these water types exhibited remarkable separation on the salinity gradient (Fig. 3a). PCA (Fig. 3b) distinguished SW, IW, and SSW on the basis of environmental factors (fuorescence, oxygen, salinity, temperature, and eDNA), primarily by salinity and temperature on the principal component 1 axis $( 4 4 . 0 9 \% )$ . The PCA clustering patterns from SW to SSW via IW appeared to follow the variation in the maximum fuorescence intensity of each component and eDNA (arrows in PCA plot). In addition, PERMANOVA revealed a signifcant diference between SW and SSW $( P { < } 0 . 0 1 )$ (Fig. 3b), whereas pairwise comparisons of SW and SSW with IW revealed no signifcant diferences (Fig. 3b).

# Distributions of dissolved organic carbon and fuorescent dissolved organic matter

The DOC concentration in the Arctic ford water ranged from 56 to $8 4 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (avg. $6 7 \pm 7 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ in SW, from 60 to $8 3 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (avg. $6 8 \pm 8 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ in IW, and from 60 to $8 3 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (avg. $6 8 \pm 7 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ in SSW (Fig. 4a). Scatter plots of DOC concentration and salinity in the Arctic ford water showed no signifcant relationship (Cor.: 0.28, $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 0 5$ , $P > 0 . 0 5$ ) (Fig. 4b), although there was a weak correlation within SW (Cor.: 0.43, $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 1 5$ , $P { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ ).

# Discussion

# Hydrography and its infuence on bacterial biogeography in Svalbard fords

The hydrography of Arctic fords in summer and the subsequent bacterial DOC degradation process depend on the fate of Arctic water and freshwater input from the discharge of glacial melt (Han et al. 2021). We surveyed four fords in Svalbard (Van Mijenforden, Van Keulenforden, Hornsund, and Storforden) and classifed their water as SW, IW, and SSW according to the TS reference for Arctic ford seawater (Cottier et al. 2005). PCA and PERMANOVA results (Fig. 2b) confrmed that the SW, IW, and SSW types represent the variation in environmental heterogeneity in Arctic ford seawater. In addition, the bacterial beta diversity identifed by NMDS revealed a more apparent separation of SW, IW, and SSW. Alphaproteobacteria was predominant among the bacterial sequences observed in this study. Previous surveys of Arctic fords revealed that heterotrophic Alphaproteobacteria are dominant in the glacier melt season (Zeng et al. 2009; Piquet et al. 2010, 2016; De Corte et al. 2013; Jain and Krishnan 2017). However, we determined that the relative abundances of the major bacterial taxa represent the depthwise variation from SW to SSW in the Arctic ford rather than spatial variation among Van Mijenforden, Van Keulenforden, Hornsund, and Storforden. These bacterial variations and the beta diversity indicate a gradual change in bacterial habitat that is mediated by the water mass balance, as suggested by the bacterial diversity of Kongsforden (Han et al. 2021).

# Factors afecting dissolved organic carbon distributions in Svalbard fords

The DOC concentrations in the Svalbard fords were comparable to those in the surface layer $( < 2 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ of major world oceans $( 6 0 - 8 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ), but lower than those in Arctic rivers $( 6 0 0 { - } 9 9 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ (Raymond et al. 2007; Stedmon et al. 2011) and in an Arctic ford in October, which was determined by diferent authors to be approximately $8 2 \mathrm { - } 1 1 2 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (Brogi et al. 2019). The major DOC sources in the Arctic Ocean are freshwater-associated DOC inputs (riverine inputs, sea ice melting, and glacial melting), and thus DOC concentration decreases with increasing salinity (Amon et al. 2012). According to previous studies, higher DOC concentrations $( 1 6 5 { - } 4 2 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ were observed in the meltwater rivers in

Svalbard (Tye and Heaton 2007; Stibal et al. 2008), whereas the DOC concentration $( 7 3 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ in the Bayelva River, Svalbard, during summer was similar to that in our study. If the main sources of DOC in this area during summer are river and ice-melt (sea ice and glacier) water, DOC should be negatively correlated with salinity. However, we found no signifcant or strong correlation between DOC and salinity in the Arctic ford water (Fig. 4b). In addition, the highest DOC concentrations were found in SW, where salinity ranges from 33 to 34 (Fig. 4b), indicating that the increase in DOC could be related to autochthonous biological production, for example, primary production or microbial activity. Therefore, these results indicate that river and ice-melt water are not the primary sources of DOC in the study area.

To evaluate the DOC sources in this study, we compared the DOC concentrations with the fluorescence properties of DOM (Fig. 7). These fuorescent components were signifcantly correlated with DOC in Arctic ford water. The relationship between DOsC and C1 in the Svalbard fords showed signifcant positive trends in SW and SSW $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 4 8$ , $P { < } 0 . 0 5$ and $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 4 5$ , $P { < } 0 . 0 5$ , respectively), whereas the relationships between DOC and C2 and C3, except for that with C2 in SW $\scriptstyle \cdot R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 3 7$ , $P { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ , were weak and widely scattered (Fig.  7). Marine humic-like FDOM, which may refect the recalcitrant nature of organic matter derived from various microbes, including hydrocarbon-oxidizing bacteria (Shimotori et al. 2009), can be an important component of DOC. To examine whether microbial activity is the origin of DOC in Arctic fords, we calculated the fuorescence index (FI), humifcation index (HIX), and biological index (BIX) from the EEM spectral data. The FI has been used to identify the source of humic DOM originating from terrestrial (approximately 1.4) or microbial activity (approximately 1.9) (McKnight et al. 2001). In this study, the FI ranged from 1.7 to 4.9 (avg. $3 . 5 \pm 1 . 0 \}$ in SW, from 2.2 to 3.0 (avg. $2 . 6 \pm 0 . 3 )$ in IW, and from 2.1 to 5.2 (avg. $3 . 7 \pm 1 . 0 $ ) in SSW (Fig. 8), indicating that the DOC in the water column was of microbial origin. The HIX, an indicator of the degree of DOM humifcation (Bai et al. 2015), ranged from 0.4 to 1.5 (avg. $0 . 8 \pm 0 . 3 )$ in SW, from 0.4 to 1.0 (avg. $0 . 7 \pm 0 . 3 )$ in IW, and from 0.6 to 1.9 (avg. $0 . 9 \pm 0 . 4 )$ in SSW (Fig. 8). These low values $( < 4 . 0 )$ indicate that the DOM in Arctic fords is related to autochthonous DOM rather than terrestrial DOM (Birdwell and Engel 2010). The BIX, an indicator of autochthonous biological production (Huguet et al. 2009), ranged from 0.9 to 2.0 (avg. $1 . 4 \pm 0 . 3 )$ in SW, from 1.3 to 2.6 (avg. $1 . 6 \pm 0 . 5 )$ in IW, and from 0.7 to 2.1 (avg. $1 . 4 \pm 0 . 4 )$ in SSW (Fig. 8). The average BIX value was higher than 1.0 and thus corresponded to freshly produced DOM (Huguet et al. 2009). According to PERMANOVA, the water masses did not difer signifcantly in HIX $\scriptstyle \cdot R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 0 3$ , $P { > } 0 . 0 5$ ), BIX $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 0 6$ , $P { > } 0 . 0 5$ ), or FI $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 1 2$ , $P { > } 0 . 0 5$ ). According to a previous study, the concentration of particulate OC (POC) in Svalbard fords was lower in October 2017 than in April 2018, and decoupling of the DOC concentration was observed (Brogi et al. 2019). In addition, the annual POC fux $( 0 . 0 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathrm { t y e a r } ^ { - 1 } )$ of Svalbard glaciers was higher than the annual DOC fux $( 0 . 0 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathrm { t \ y e a r ^ { - 1 } ) }$ (Zhu et al. 2016). Because most POC in Kongsfjorden, Svalbard, during the summer season originates from terrestrial organic matter (D’Angelo et al. 2018), the POC degraded by microbial activity is changed to DOC, including humic substances. Similarly, the main source of DOC in the East China Sea during summer was associated with microbial activity according to the optical properties of DOM (Kim et al. 2020). Thus, because subglacial microbial activity (Sharp et al. 1999) and cryoconite and supraglacial microbial contributions are well-known to be the main sources of OC in glacial meltwater (Sharp et al. 1999; Anesio et al. 2010; Irvine-Fynn et al. 2012), microbial activity can signifcantly afect the DOC distribution in Arctic fords during the glacier melt season.

# Bacterial population dynamics with dissolved organic matter degradation in the glacier melt season

Our metabarcoding results revealed that six heterotrophic bacterial groups/taxa (Alphaproteobacteria, Gammaproteobacteria, Bacteroidia, Verrucomicrobiae, Actinobacteria, and Acidimicrobiia) are predominant in Arctic ford water; this fnding is similar to the results of previous surveys of the Svalbard fords (Cardman et al. 2014; Han et al. 2021). ISA indicated that the bacterial population dynamics were partially mediated by the water mass balance in these Arctic fords. For example, Actinobacteria and Verrucomicrobiae showed water-type-specifc distributions in SW and SSW, respectively. By contrast, Alphaproteobacteria, Bacteroidia, and Acidimicrobiia had insignifcant indicator values, suggesting that they are ubiquitously distributed in Arctic ford water. Among these major heterotrophic bacteria, the relative abundances of Actinobacteria and Acidimicrobiia, both of which are assigned to the phylum Actinobacteria, were signifcantly representative of the beta diversity pattern identifed by NMDS.

Overall, our results suggest that specifc bacterial groups and DOM components covary by water mass, although there was no direct evidence supporting microbial utilization as a factor afecting DOM quality. Actinobacteria generally contributes to the decomposition of organic matter in the environment (Bao et al. 2021). Considering the possibility of bacterial production by marine humic-like FDOM, we assumed that marine bacteria such as hydrocarbon-oxidizing Actinobacteria may be associated with direct or indirect processes afecting the prevalence of microbiologically refractory DOM in SW and SSW. On the basis of the combined statistical results of PERMANOVA, ISA, and NMDS, we propose that Actinobacteria are ecologically signifcant in these Arctic fords. We neither argue that other heterotrophic bacteria can also contribute to the production of recalcitrant organic compounds nor explain how bacteria interact ecologically with DOM. We considered only which type of oceanic DOM is commonly found in these Arctic fords during the glacier melt season and how the ecological statistics can indicate its relationship with key bacterial populations.

In conclusion, the significant relationships between DOC and the microbial FDOM component, fuorescence index values (FI, HIX, and BIX), and bacterial biogeography (diversity and community composition) suggest that heterotrophic microbial activity is associated with the main source of DOC in Arctic fords during the glacier melt season. However, the fndings of this study will improve the understanding of the fate of DOM in these Arctic fords. Given that Arctic hydrography is mediated by the water mass balance during the glacier melt season, the bacterial contribution to oceanic DOM deserves consideration owing to concerns related to climate change and its impacts on Arctic fords.
