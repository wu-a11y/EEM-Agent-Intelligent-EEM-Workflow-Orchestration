ABSTRACT: Natural oil seepages contribute about one-half of the annual petroleum input to marine systems. Yet, environmental implications and the persistence of water-soluble hydrocarbons from these seeps are vastly unknown. We investigated the release of oil-derived dissolved organic matter (DOM） from natural deep sea asphalt seeps using laboratory incubation experiments. Fresh asphalt samples collected at the Chapopote asphalt volcano in the Southern Gulf of Mexico were incubated aerobically in artificial seawater over 4 weeks. The compositional changes in the watersoluble fraction of asphalt-derived DOM were determined with ultrahighresolution mass spectrometry (Fourier-transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry， FT-ICR-MS） and by excitation-emission matrix spectroscopy to characterize fluorescent DOM (FDOM) appying paralel factor (PARAFAC)analysis.Highly reduced aliphatic asphalt-derivedDOM wasreadilybiodegraded,whilearomaticand sulfur-enrichedDOMappeared tobelessbioavailableand accumulated in the aqueous phase.A quantitative molecular tracer approach revealed theabundance of highlycondensed aromatic molecules ofthermogenicorigin.Ourresults indicatethat naturalasphaltandpotentiallother petroleum seepages canbsourcesof recalcitrant dissolved organic sulfur and dissolved black carbon to the ocean.

# INTRODUCTION

About one-half of the annual input of petroleum to the ocean is released by natural oil seepage，which is estimated to contribute $6 0 0 0 0 0$ tons per year.1 A fraction of the oil enriched in polar molecules of low molecular mass can dissolve in seawater and enter the dissolved organic matter (DOM) pool. The environmental implications and the persistence of this water-soluble oil fraction from natural seeps are vastly unknown.

Several studies investigated the molecular composition of DOM associated with terrestrial2-5 and marine oil spils, and tracked oil sources and weathering products such as oxygenated hydrocarbons with ultrahigh-resolution mass spectrometry (Fourier-transform_ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry, FT-ICR-MS).7 FT-ICR-MS resolves thousands of molecules in complex organic matter mixtures such as oil8 and DOM in aquatic samples. Several laboratory experiments were conducted after the Deepwater Horizon oil spill investigating oil-derived DOM from Macondo oil surrogate on the molecular level. Liu and Kujawinskil° found an enrichment of heteroatom-containing DOM as compared to the parent oil and explained this with a higher polarity and consequently a higher solubility in seawater.Microbial incubation experiments were conducted by Kleindienst et al.1 and Seidel et al.1² In their oil-only incubations,oil-derived DOM exhibited a characteristic fingerprint of compounds with low molecular oxygen-to-carbon (O/C） ratios and low molecular masses as compared to the natural marine DOM background.A further laboratory incubation study by Liu et al.13foundanincreaseinthechemical diversityand oxygen content of oil-derived DOM following microbial transformations.Besides FT-ICR-MS,excitation-emission matrix fluorescence spectroscopy (EEM） and subsequent parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) are commonly used to characterize the fluorescent part of DOM (FDOM) and were previously applied to track oil-related DOM afteroil spils.4,14,15 In field studies2andlaboratoryexperiments,6,17 EEM-PARAFAC and FT-ICR-MS can be used as complementary techniques to trace changes in oil-derived DOM.However， our understanding of the fate of DOM released by natural oil seepages is still limited.

Oceanic DOM contains $6 6 2 ~ \mathrm { P g }$ dissolved organic carbon (DOC),8of whichafractionof $1 2 \ \mathrm { \ p g } ^ { 1 9 }$ is considered ultrarefractory with $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ ages older than 2Oooo years in the deep sea.18,26 This fraction contains dissolved black carbon (DBC),which has proposed core structures of fused rings with carboxylic functional groups，comparable to the compounds detected in petroleum asphaltenes.9,21 While DBC is assumed to be mainly produced by the incomplete combustion of terrestrial organic matter on land, recent studies have pointed out mismatches between $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ and $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ isotopic signatures of terrestrial DBC sources and oceanic DBC, suggesting additionalmariercesofIddu containing DOM,dissolved organic sulfur (DOS)，is a petagram inventory of oceanic DOM, and known DOS sources suchas pytoplakt4andsdiceviomentsdont balance out sinks of nonlabile DOS in a global inventory.24 In this study,we hypothesize that petroleum seeps are a source of DBC and water-soluble petroleum-derived DOS to the deep ocean.

Asphalt volcanism is a special type of deep-sea heavy oil seepage first described in the southern Gulf of Mexico (GOM).27,28S Several asphalt fields have been identified to date at the Campeche Knols, of which one,the Chapopote asphalt volcano,exhibits $1 0 0 0 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ of lava-like asphalt flows on the seafloor in $2 9 0 0 ~ \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ water depth.29By conducting a laboratory incubation experiment of Chapopote-derived asphalt in artificial seawater,we studied the composition of oil-derived DOM and assessed the lability to microbial degradation to provide an estimate on the potential impact of oil-seepage on the deep-oceanic carbon cycle. Unlike previouslaboratoryincubationstudies12,16 we chose artificial seawater instead of filter-sterilized seawater as the incubation medium, because seawater contains DOM that may conceal the oil-derived DOM imprint or metabolites of oil degradation. We targeted the following research questions: (1) What is the quantity of asphalt-derived dissolved organic carbon (DOC) that is released by fresh asphalts,(2) what are the optical properties and the molecular composition of the released DOM,(3） how do microbial transformations affect the DOM composition,and (4） are natural asphalt seeps a potential source of ultrarefractory DOM and DOS to the ocean? Our study shows that natural asphalt and potentially other petroleum seeps can be sources of recalcitrant DOS and DBC to the ocean.

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

Material and Incubation Setup.Visually nonbiodegraded asphalt samples were collected during RV Meteor leg 114 in March 2015 by a gravity corer(GeoB 19339-1） at the Chapopote asphalt volcano in a water depth of $2 9 0 5 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ from oxic bottom waters.30,31 The asphalt samples are similar to fresh asphalt described in Wegener et al.31 and contain a high proportion of asphaltenes ( $5 4 \bar { 5 } \%$ asphaltenes, ca. $4 0 \%$ aliphatic and aromatic hydrocarbons,and ca. $1 5 \%$ resins).The location was assigned to the most recent asphalt flow of the main asphalt field and was not covered by microbial mats or metazoans29 (Figure S1). Samples were kept at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark and aerated in filtered $( 0 . 2 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ bottom seawater from Chapopote for 3 years before this experiment.This filtration was performed to remove microbes and particles from the seawater.We did not monitor the integrity of the sterility of the added seawater, but instead expect microbial communities living on the surface of the asphalts1 to be primarily active during these several months to 10-year incubations.Artificial seawater was prepared using an adapted version described by Widdel and $\mathrm { B a k } ^ { 3 2 }$ with high nutrient concentrations to avoid nutrient limitation for microbial activity during the incubations (Table S1).Asphalt samples were cut into small cubes of approximately $3 - 5 \mathrm { ~ } \mathrm { \ m m }$ side lengths after the exterior and potentially weathered part of the asphalt was removed. Microorganisms are present in asphalt samples from the main asphalt feld that are affiliated to known hydrocarbon degrading organisms.31 To stimulate microbial growth and distribute microorganisms homogeneously on the asphalt, asphalt cubes were mixed with $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of artificial seawater and preincubated for 1O days in the dark at room temperature. Between 3.6 and $3 . 8 \ \mathrm { g }$ of asphalt and $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of the inoculum were then transferred into combusted and autoclaved $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ glass bottles (Schott） together with $4 9 0 ~ \mathrm { \ m L }$ of artificial seawater.The incubation was set up in triplicates on a roller table $\left( 4 5 \thinspace \mathrm { r p m } \right)$ at room temperature in the dark and performed over 28 days.A control was established by pasteurizing one incubation botle after the initial experimental setup and again after 20 and 24 days ( $^ { \mathrm { ~ \small ~ 2 ~ h ~ } }$ at $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a water bath,as previously described in Kleindienst et al.11). Pasteurization was chosen, because common techniques such as the addition of $\mathrm { Z n C l } _ { \mathrm { \ell } }$ ，autoclaving,and gamma-radiation could interfere with electrospray ionization (ESI) in mass spectrometry or alter the asphalts composition and DOC concentrations,3 respectively. The incubation bottles were loosely screwed with lids to minimize the loss of volatile compounds, left open for a few minutes, and gently shaken on sampling days to prevent anoxic conditions.In a preliminary study in closed bottles, $5 5 \%$ of the initial dissolved oxygen remained after 28 days. Sample aliquots for optical and dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) analysis were taken out of the incubation bottles with precleaned ( $\mathrm {  ~ \ p H ~ } 2$ rinsed）polystyrene pipettes (Sarstedt) after 0,2,6,10,20,and 28 days $( 2 \mathrm { m L } )$ and after0,10,and28 days for DOM analysis $\mathrm { ^ { \prime 1 0 0 - 1 5 0 ~ m L } }$ ).Asphalt samples for long-term incubation experiments were collected on RV Meteor expedition $\mathbf { M } 6 7 / 2 \mathrm { b }$ and M114.Approximately $^ { 2 0 - }$ $_ { 4 0 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ of asphalt was incubated in $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of filtered $( 0 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ Chapopote bottom seawater at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark over a period of 6 months to 10 years in glass bottles loosely screwed with lids (Table S2).

Dissolved Inorganic Carbon Analysis.For the determination of the isotopic carbon composition of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC),water samples were filtered through a $0 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ RC cellulose syringe filter (Sartorius）and stored frozen without headspace until analysis.Measurements were conducted on a Delta Ray isotope ratio infrared spectrometer (IRIS） with URI Connect and autosampler (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Germany). One mililiter of the water sample was acidified with $45 \%$ phosphoric acid,and after an equilibration time of $^ { 1 0 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ released $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ was analyzed in the Delta Ray IRIS with URI connect for a stable carbon isotope ratio of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ against $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ reference gas，using synthetic air as a carrier. Our microcosm experiments were performed under oxic conditions with regular ventilation intervals.This did not allow for tracing microbial activity by monitoring, for example, oxygen consumption.Instead，we determined the isotopic composition of DIC as a qualitative indicator for remineralization of asphalt,which we expect to only be minimally affected by the ventilation of the bottles. $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { \mathrm { D I C } }$ values became more negative with ongoing incubation time (Figure 1, Table S5), which showed that parts of the asphalt organic carbon were remineralized to carbon dioxide. The pasteurized control setup did not show negative isotopic excursions,indicating that pasteurization was suitable to suppress microbial organic carbon remineralization in the time frame of this experiment. Moreover, DOC concentrations were lower in the control as compared to the biotic incubations (Figure 2A and Table S5). We attribute the enhanced DOC release in the biotic experiment to asphalt dissolution largely initiated by the release of biosurfactants and enzymatically mediated partial breakdown or functionalization of unsoluble asphalt by oildegrading microorganisms.54

Optical Properties of Released DOM.A four-component model was validated with PARAFAC modeling of the 24 EEM spectra (Figure 3A and Table S3). Components $1 { - } 3$ were identified as protein-like components,7 whereas C4 was identified as microbial humic-like fluorescence.55 The PARAFAC components were compared to previous studies by using the OpenFluor database and by applying a threshold of $\mathrm { T C C } > 0 . 9 7$ (excitation） and $\mathrm { T C C } > 0 . 9 8$ (emission） for similarity6 (Table S3). Most of our PARAFAC components matched with similar components from a previous study from a groundwater-oil spill4 and a laboratory-based oil photodegradation experiment.16 C1 is similar to C2 in Podgorski et al.4and was described as low molecular weight aromatic FDOM. Further matches include C4 in Derrien et al.,7 which described this component as soil-related in an endmember incubation experiment.Although not present in the database, similarities were further found in C3 described by Zhou et al.14 and C1 in Dvorski et al.,² where this component was associated with polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs）and its degradation products. C2 is similar to C5 in Zito et al.16 and C1 in Podgorski et al.4 in which this protein-like compound was interpreted to be related to (alkyl-substituted）benzenederivates and considered semibiolabile.Hence,we interpret this PARAFAC component to be derived from the petroleum. This component was also found in porewaters from a mud volcano in the Beaufort Sea (C4).58 No matches above the threshold were found for our C3,which we attribute to its presence in a region with high noise due to physical scattering. Comparable components are C2 in Zito et al.16 (TCC ex/em: $0 . 9 2 / \bar { 0 . 9 3 } )$ and C5 in Podgorski et al.4 (TCC ex/em: 0.93/ 0.93),where these components were correlated to petroleum toxicity. C4 matches well with 16 studies describing it as humic-like；it was found in many different ecozones，for example, porewaters,58 the Arctic Ocean, and the Northern Pacific Ocean.49 Only one of the matches is associated with oil degradation and is probably related to molecular-level structural properties comparable to those of humic and fulvic acids (C1 in Zito et al.16).

At the beginning of the experiment，the PARAFAC components C1, C2,and C3 emitting in the UVA and UVB range made up most of the fluorescent signal (combined $9 6 \%$ of total fluorescence),whereas emission at longer wavelengths had only minor relative contributions (Figure 3B). From O to 28 days,C2 relative abundances stayed constant,while the relative contribution of C1 slightly increased and then stayed constant. Changes were observed for C3 and C4: the relative contribution of petroleum-"humic"-like C4 increased,whereas the potentially toxic petroleum-protein-like C3 decreased. This potential decrease in toxicity is in accordance with lower values of the toxicity index4 (Figure 2C)，suggesting that microbial processing removed potentially toxic FDOM.

Liu and Kujawinski discussed a solubilization effect of humic and fulvic acids on the enhanced solubilization of oil compounds in seawater,which can increase the solubility of semi-solubleandinsoublehdrocarbonsAssmingtat the petroleum-humic-like C4 has the same underlying molecular properties as environmental humic-like $\mathrm { F D O M } ^ { \mathrm { 1 6 } }$ such as conjugated aromatic structures,² the observed increase of DOC concentrations in the biological incubations could also be attributed to an increase of asphalt solubility due to the extended release of this compound class.This assumption is supported by a positive linear correlation between C4 and aromatic molecular formulae $\big ( R ^ { 2 } \mathrm { a d j . } = 0 . 9 4 , p < 1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 6 } ,$ $\operatorname { d f } =$ 10；Figure S6). Microbial Transformation of DOM on a Molecular Level.We observed drastic changes in the carbon-based extraction efficiencies (EE）of $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ by PPL:while EEs were low after the start of the incubation' $( 6 . 2 \pm 0 . 8 \% )$ ，an efficiency of $5 0 . 9 \pm 4 . 6 \%$ was determined after 28 days (Table S5),indicating noticeable changes in the DOM composition. The low EEs at the beginning of our incubations suggest either a DOM pool that was not well retained by the PPL resin63,64 and/or irreversible adsorption of a part of the DOM to the PPL resin,although a method comparison study generally recommended using SPE for extracting oil-related DOM.65 Following the mesocosm setup at $t _ { 0 } ,$ we observed a loss of DOC after the pasteurization of the control setup (Figure 2A). This can best be explained by a loss of volatile compounds,a compound class that is not well retained by SPE,63 thus resulting in low EEs at $t _ { 0 }$ for biological samples. With ongoing incubation time, the increase in EEs very likely reflected the decreasing relative abundances of volatile DOM and the increasing relative abundances of nonvolatile DOM as volatile compounds evaporated and/or were microbially processed while more water-soluble asphalt-derived DOM was released due to the microbial functionalization of previously waterinsoluble asphalt compounds.Moreover, isotopic fractionation of petroleum has been shown previously, for example, lower $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { \bar { C } }$ values of polycondensed aromatic $5$ and 6-ring asphaltenesas compared tothe parent oil.Even though EEs changed considerably, $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ of $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ was at around $- 2 8 \text{‰}$ at all time points (Table S5). Hence, we expect the fraction of DOM retained by PPL to be similar,allowing a comparison of $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ within the same analytical window. 63

We detected 6813 different molecular formulae with ESIFT-ICR-MS analysis of $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ in our incubation setups. The FT-ICR-MS signal intensity weighted-averages of DOM molecular masses were low as compared to natural marine DOM in the deep GOM (ca. $4 0 0 \mathrm { D a } ^ { 1 \{ 2 \cdot \} }$ ）at the beginning of the experiment and significantly $\left( p < 0 . 0 0 1 \right)$ decreased over time from $2 9 9 \pm 2 0$ Daat $t _ { 0 }$ to $2 5 1 \pm 9$ Da after 28 days (Table S6). This is consistent with previous studies investigating oil degradation in the water phase. 11,12 Furthermore，the weighted-average molar $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C }$ ratios decreased significantly $( p$ $< 0 . { \dot { 0 } } 0 1 { \dot { } }$ ）from $\stackrel { - } { 1 } . 4 9 \pm 0 . 0 3$ to $1 . 3 2 \pm 0 . 0 2$ ，which was further represented in higher $\mathrm { { { A I } _ { m o d } } }$ values,while the molar $\mathrm { O } / \mathrm { C }$ ratios stayed constant between O.28 and 0.29. Consequently, aromatic and polycondensed aromatic molecular formulae became relatively more enriched from O to 28 days,whereas the relative abundance of DOM aliphatic molecular formulae decreased (Figure S3). This is in accordance with a general preference of microbial degradation of aliphatic over aromatic hydrocarbons7 and was also observed at a groundwater-oil spill site and for DOM in general.68

To examine the potential of microbial degradation of asphalt-derived DOM，we compared the distribution of molecular formulae exclusively present in the biologically active incubations with the pasteurized control (Figure 4). After 28 days, 497 molecular formulae were found exclusively in the abiotic control but not in the biotic incubations, suggesting that they were biodegraded. These molecular formulae had relatively high molar $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C }$ and very low $\mathrm { O } / \mathrm { C }$ ratios，corresponding to reduced，highly unsaturated and aliphatic DOM (Table S6).On the other hand,1102 more aromatic and less reduced molecular formulae were exclusively present in the biologically active incubations,indicating either a release of DOM by microorganisms or the release from (microbially altered） asphalt due to enhanced DOM solubilization. The fact that DOM can increase the solubility of petroleum hydrocarbons in water has been shown previously. To assess the potential solubility， we calculated the molar ratios of NSO:C in molecular formulae,which were (along with molecular masses）related to modeled octanolwater partition coefficients of water-soluble petroleum compounds in a study by Liu and Kujawinski.1° Higher NSO:C ratios correspond to more hydrophilic DOM, whereas lower NSO:C ratios and higher molecular masses correspond to more hydrophobic DOM.Weighted-average NSO:C ratios of $0 . 3 9 \pm 0$ and their low molecular masses indicated that the majority of these exclusive molecular formulae were likely not hydrophobic and instead potentially more water-soluble than the bulk DOM after 28 days (Table S6). This implies that these exclusive molecular formulae were released as metabolites of microbial processing of asphalt organic matter and not leached out of the asphalt due to an enhanced DOM solubilization effect. Therefore，we expect most qualitative changes in the DOM pool observed by FT-ICR-MS to be related to microbial processing of particulate and dissolved asphalt.

Release of Dissolved Organic Sulfur Species.More than one-half of all molecular formulae detected in $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ contained sulfur,which increased from $4 5 . 2 \pm 5 . 6 \%$ to $6 2 . 1 \pm$ $0 . 1 \%$ after 28 days (Figure S3). To confirm the abundance of S-containing DOM as detected by ultrahigh-resolution mass spectrometry，we quantified DOS in the SPE extracts. The concentrations of $\mathrm { D O S } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ reached $4 0 . 1 \pm 4 . 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ after 28 days (Table S5 and Figure 2B),which was about S0O times higher than the deep sea $\mathrm { D O S } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ concentrations in the deep Atlantic Ocean.24 Moreover, $S / C _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ ratios between $0 . 0 5 2 \pm 0 . 0 0 3$ and $0 . 0 3 4 \pm 0 . 0 0 5$ exceeded the reported values in deep Atlantic water by a factor of 10-15 and even sulfidic porewater by a factor of $2 { - } 5 . ^ { 2 4 , 2 6 , 7 0 }$ Molar $\mathtt { S / C }$ ratios in the original asphalt were lower than these values (O.024),indicating a selective dissolution of sulfur-containing asphalt-derived DOM compounds.

Hundreds of highly unsaturated or aliphatic DOS molecular formulae were only present in the control and not in the biologically active incubations (Figure 4C)，illustrating that this fraction can be microbially degraded. However,more Scontaining molecular formulae were released,of which about one-half $( 4 5 \% )$ is expected to have an aromatic or polycondensed aromatic structure based on $\mathrm { A I } _ { \mathrm { m o d } } > 0 . 5$ and $\mathrm { A I } _ { \mathrm { m o d } } ~ > ~ 0 . 6 7 .$ ， respectively (Figure 4C). The S-containing marine DOM is likely composed of sulfonic acids and thiophenes.4o In petroleum，aromatic organic sulfur compounds such as thiophenes and condensed thiophenes can make up a substantial fraction of all sulfur compounds,7 and several methylated benzo- and dibenzothiophenes are present in solid Chapopote asphalt.7² Liu et al.73 analyzed sulfur-rich immature crude oils from China and showed that sulfurcontaining compounds polar enough to be ionized by ESI in mass spectrometry applications mainly contained thiophene rings in their molecular structures.It is therefore conceivable that the aromatic and polycondensed aromatic DOS formulae released by asphalt seepage contain thiophene or condensed thiophene(benzothiophene）rings，which are abundantly present in the Chapopote asphaltsand are relatively resistant to biodegradation.71,74 A potential recalcitrance is illustrated by the high abundance of aromatic DOS molecular formulae after 28 days of incubation (Figure 4C).An elevated abundance of DOS compounds from petroleum was reported in several studies using FT-ICR-MS,for example，in a laboratory experiment,10 groundwater-oil spills,2,3oil-entrapped water droplets,75and above an oil seep in the GOM.68

We hypothesize that DOS species released by petroleum seepage may be a consistent source of nonlabile DOS to the global marine DOS inventory. The global marine DOS inventory is currently unbalanced because known marine DOSsources from phytoplankton24 and sulfidic environments25,26 are not enough to balance out sinks of nonlabile DOS.24

Asphalt Leaching Releases Dissolved Polycondensed Aromatic Compounds. Optical analysis by fluorescence spectroscopy and UV-vis absorbance indicated an increased release of aromatic DOM (Figure 3，C4，HIX; Table S5, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 , }$ ).Furthermore,we detected an increase of aromatic and polycondensed aromatic formulae by FT-ICR-MS in our incubations，including dissolved_black sulfur (DBS)76 and dissolved black nitrogen $( \mathrm { D B N } ) ^ { 7 7 }$ (Table S4). Environmental implications of DBS and DBN are unknown, and their sources were previously associated with biomass burning.Roebuck et al.78 speculated that spilled diesel fuel may be a source for DBS.While DBS and DBN currently can only be identified qualitatively by FT-ICR-MS,concentrations of DBC without $_ \mathrm { N }$ or S heteroatoms can be determined by converting them to BPCAs.We quantified DBC as B5CA and B6CA，which indicated the presence of DBC with at least 5 fused rings,79 presumed to be of thermogenic origin. Here,we observed an increase of DBC concentrations of 0.83 to $1 3 . 3 \pm 2 . 9 \mu \mathrm { M }$ after 28 days (Figure 2C and Table S5). These compounds accumulated in the DOM pool: their relative contribution to DOC concentrations increased within 28 days to $0 . 5 5 ~ \pm$ $0 . 0 3 \%$ . For the deep ocean, DBC contribution to DOC is estimated to be $2 \%$ f9 but the DOC concentrations are much higher in our 28-day incubation,still including more labile fractions than deep-sea DOM.To get a better understanding of long-term leaching of DBC from these asphalt samples，we investigated additional asphalts from the same location that were incubated for 6 months to 1O years and observed this trend was continuing: between $0 . 5 { - } 0 . 9 \%$ of DBC in DOC were observed after 6 months and $2 . 9 \mathrm { - } 1 2 . 6 \%$ after 10 years, confirming its relative resistance to microbial degradation (Table S2).DBC in biotic incubations after 28 days showed high B6CA/B5CA ratios (0.6-0.7） as compared to natural deep sea DBC (ca. $0 . 3 ^ { 2 3 ^ { \cdot } }$ ),an indication for highly condensed DBC. 22,42

A recent study detected a mismatch between the isotopic carbon composition of known terrestrial DBC sources and DBC in the open ocean, indicating a so far unidentified DBC sourcewitha marineisotopicsignature.23In this study， Wagner et al.23 hypothesized that the dissolution of particulate petrogenic BC is a potential DBC source,which would match the isotopic composition of open ocean DBC.The first study describing DBC distribution in the open ocean by Dittmar and $\mathrm { K o c h } ^ { 8 1 }$ suggested a widespread source at the seafloor of deepsea basins.Here,we provide experimental evidence that this compound class can also be released by natural asphalt seepage into deep sea waters.A DBC release in the deep sea would not be affected by photooxidation，which isa major removal mechanism of terrestrial DBC transported by rivers.22,82 The $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ composition of $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ has been reported to be similar to that of isolated $\mathrm { D B C } , ^ { 2 3 }$ which was on average $- 2 8 \text{‰}$ in our experiment ( $( - 2 7 \text{‰}$ in particulate asphalt from Chapopote,53 Table S5). The stable carbon isotopic compositions of B5CA and B6CA in petroleum asphaltenes were reported to be similar (B5CA) or slightly $^ { 1 3 } \dot { \mathrm { C } }$ -depleted (B6CA) as compared to that of the parent crude oil in a recent study by Goranov et al.6 The stable carbon isotopic composition of crude oils varies over geologic times, but the mean $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values were reported around $- 2 6 \text{‰}$ 8. Natural asphalt and petroleum seepage with fossil $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ values could therefore be an overlooked source of $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -depleted DBC with a slightly more positive $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ signature as compared to riverine $\mathrm { D B C } ^ { 2 3 }$ in the deep ocean. Contribution of asphalt DBC may be an explanation for the observed very old $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ ages of deep-sea $\mathrm { D B C } ^ { 2 0 }$ and is consistent with reports of distinct young and very old marine DBC pools.84,85

Implications for Natural Environments.Although asphalt seepage is a localized phenomenon,it is not limited to the southern GOM and has been reported from offshore Algeria8Brazil,7the northern GOM,andtheSantaBarbara Basin. We have shown that fresh asphalt exposed to water can release large amounts of DOC,of which a considerable fraction of aliphatic DOM can readily be transformed by microbial degraders.Hydrocarbon seepage can stimulate microbial life at seeps and in the water column above and has been observed to increase the abundance of primary producers as indicated by chlorophyll concentrationsin euphotic zones above seep sites.90 Studies by Wang et al.91 and Pohlman et al.92 proposed hydrocarbon seps as potential major contributors of old DOC to the deep sea in the GOM. However, the fate of DOC from seepage is poorly understood: Chantoneal.eedrelaieg $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ ages of DOC in the water column of the abyssal GOM, concluding that seep DOC has a local effect only. Our results suggest that asphalt seepages release ultrarefractory DOM with a fossil $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ signature, contributing to the DBC pool that makes up $2 \%$ of the DOC pool in the deep sea.19 We hypothesize that while the general DOC release may only have a local effect, the released fraction of refractory fossil $^ { \mathrm { i } _ { 4 } } \mathrm { C }$ compounds could be a larger contributor to ultrarefractory DOC cycles that is not visible in the bulk $\Delta ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ analysis of DOC. Ditmar and Paeng19 proposed a possible molecular structure of deep-sea DBC containing fused ring structures similar to that of petroleum asphaltenes. Therefore，we hypothesize that an additional source of deep-sea DBC might be asphaltenes from natural petroleum seepage，which are rendered water-soluble by microbial degradation and functionalization.This could mean that particularly asphaltene-rich petroleum seeps may release DBC into the water column. However, this cannot be the only explanation, because DBC concentrations also increased in the pasteurized (abiotic) control setup (Figure 2 and Table S5). Podgorski et al.’ detected enhanced DBC concentrations via the BPCA method in groundwater of an oil spill site of light crude oil，suggesting that DBC can also be released by petroleum with a lower asphaltene content.

Oil seepage is estimated to release about 60o ooo tons of crude oil annually into the oceans,with an equal contribution of petroleum from anthropogenic sources.1 We applied the results from our 28-day incubation to get a rough, first-order estimate of global DBC and DOC release (Figure 2 and Table S7). We estimate between $6 . 3 \times 1 0 ^ { 8 }$ and $1 . 1 \dot { \times } 1 0 ^ { 9 } \mathrm { \ g }$ as DOC, $1 . 0 \times 1 0 ^ { 7 }$ and $4 . 3 \times 1 0 ^ { 7 } \mathrm { g }$ as likely nonlabile ${ \mathrm { D O S } } _ { \mathrm { S P E } } ,$ as well as $3 . 3 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 }$ and $5 . 0 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathrm { \bar { g } }$ as DBC could be released by natural seepage annually to the oceanic DOM pool without considering contribution from anthropogenic sources.This is $_ { 4 - 5 }$ orders of magnitude lower than the estimated annual removal of refractory DOS24,94 and $_ { 6 - 7 }$ orders of magnitude lower than the annual riverine DBC input95 to the ocean and may therefore be significant only on local scales.However, if natural seepage releases fossil $^ { \mathrm { i } _ { 4 } } \mathrm { C }$ DBC into the deep-sea DOM pool, the apparent very old bulk $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ ages of deep-sea DBC20 could be shaped by fossil DBC contribution.In a twocomponent $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ mixing model of recent terrestrial DBC and fossil petroleum DBC, reported deep-sea $\Delta ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ DBC values of $- 9 4 5 \text{‰}$ could in theory be explained bya contribution of up to $9 4 . 2 \%$ of fossil DBC (Figure SS).A relatively small proportion of recent and reactive DBC from land could therefore constrain apparent DBC ages dynamically. Nevertheless， the stable carbon isotopic composition of asphaltDOM in this study and DBC from the asphaltene fraction of petroleum are lighter than the observed $^ { \mathrm { { \scriptsize ~ \hat { 1 } 3 } } } \mathrm { C }$ values of deepsea DBC,23 suggesting a rather minor contribution of petroleum-derived DBC,which is in agreement with the estimated rates of DBC release in this study.Future work is needed to clarify if the contribution of natural seepage is significant to deep-sea cycles.
