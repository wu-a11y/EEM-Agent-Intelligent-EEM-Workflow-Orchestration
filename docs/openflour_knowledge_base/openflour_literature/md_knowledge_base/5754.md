The complexation mechanism between pharmaceuticals and natural colloids is still uncertain due to the complexity,heterogeneity,and polydispersityof coloids.Therefore,this study investigated the effectof fluorescence properties on the complexation of chloramphenicol (CAP) and carbamazepine (CBZ) to the colloids from Poyang Lake Basin based on the multiple spectroscopic techniques and methods.Three-dimensional excitation-emision matrix fluorescence spectroscopy-parallel factor analysis results illustrated that two humic-like componentsand two protein-like components of collids from the rivers and lakes were identified,with the much higher fluorescence intensity of the protein-like substance observed in lake samples.The protein-like substance decreased dramatically with the addition of CAP and CBZ,suggesting its higher binding capacity towards these drugs,especially for CBZ.In addition,the fluorescence quenching titration was proceeded to explore the binding mechanism between the coloids and the pharmaceuticals.Results of synchronous fluorescence spectra and two-dimensional correlation spectroscopy demonstrated that the fluorescencequenching effect occurred preferentially between the protein-like substancesand the pharmaceuticals，with the stronger complexation for CBZ.Ryan-Weber model fiting results showed thatthe stability constant ranged from 4.02 to 5.04 with the higher binding capacity observed for the tryptophan-like substance. Combined,the fluorescence components in aquatic collids could be significantly impacted the complexation of the pharmaceuticals.This study provides deep insights into the fate and pollution protection of pharmaceuticals.

# 1．Introduction

Pharmaceuticals,a representative of emerging organic contaminants,have attracted extensive attention in recent years due to their large production,consistent consumption,potential environmental risks,and adverse effects on human health (Desbiolles et al., 2018; Li et al., 2O19; Feng et al., 2O2O). Nowadays, pharmaceuticals have been frequently detected in multiple aquatic environments such as rivers (Sui et al.，2015;Ebele et al.， 2017),groundwater (Kivits et al., 2018), sediment (Xie et al., 2019),soil (Li, 2014),and drinking water (Jiang etal.,2Ol9a),etc.These pharmaceuticals may be released from wastewater treatment plant effluents,domestic wastewaters,and improper manufacturer disposal (Comber et al.,2O19). Chloramphenicol (CAP) and carbamazepine (CBZ) are two kinds of synthetic pharmaceuticals with relatively high detections in aquatic environments.For example, the concentration of CAP was detected at $2 0 6 \ n g \ L ^ { - 1 }$ in sewage treatment plants influent (Yang et al.,2O17).And the detection frequency of CBZ was $1 0 0 \%$ in drinking water sources of five major river watersheds in China at $0 . 0 1 { - } 3 . 5 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ level (Sun et al., 2015). CAP is a broad-spectrum antibiotic and widely used as a veterinary drug.It has been commonly used for the treatment of animal bacterial infections and typhoid fever.However, toxic effects such as genotoxic carcinogenicity and aplastic anemia,anemia that bone marrow ceases hematopoietic function,have been identified during the medicinal use of CAP (Hanekamp and Bast, 2O15). These adverse effects of CAP have resulted in the prohibition of the animal food production chain and human medication. CBZ,an important antiepileptic,is extensively used in seizure disorders in adults and children owing to its obvious clinical efficacy (Liang et al, 2020).The extended utilization of CBZ makes it available for unintended environmental exposure (Wang et al., 2O2O).Moreover, the derivatives of CBZ originated from the metabolism of humans，or degradation of CBZ in the environment could be more deleterious and recalcitrant (Paz et al., 2Ol6). Due to the low elimination efficiency of CBZ in wastewaters treatment plants $( 5 2 \% - 5 5 \% )$ (Sgroi etal., 2016), it is commonly detected in various aquatic environments.Hence, CAP and CBZ are continuously released and accumulate in the aquatic environment,resulting in a pseudo-persistent existence,which is harmful to aquatic organisms and may cause long-term adverse impacts on the aquatic environment and human health.

The migration, transformation,and ecotoxicity of pharmaceuticals in the aquatic environment are strongly associated with their environmental behavior including adsorption,degradation,and diffusion, etc. Hereinto,the complexation of pharmaceuticals to the colloids is the key process that affects its environmental fate,which can directly affect the distribution of pharmaceuticals in aquatic environments and their bioavailability (Maskaoui and Zhou, 2OlO).Colloids in the natural aquatic environment are heterogeneous mixtures of particles with one dimension between $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $1 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ (Lead and Wilkinson, 2006; Yan et al.,2Ol6a).Recent studies have indicated that the binding of pharmaceuticals to the natural colloids is strongly dependent on the colloidal physicochemical properties (Yan et al.,2O15b),including pH values (Hartland et al.,2O1O),organic carbon content, specific area,particle size (Yan et al., 2015a),and fluorescence component (Li et al., 2018). Colloidal fluorescence property is one of the important factors affecting the complexation of pharmaceuticals.For instance,the protein-like substances of colloids in urban road runoff had a stronger affinity with CBZ than humic-like substances (Yuan et al., 2O19). Similar results were verified in the study of the complexation between colloids in the three zones of Yangtze Estuary and CBZ,which revealed that colloids contain many functional groups that enable them to bond to CBZ,and the protein-like components played the dominant role (Wang et al., 2016). However,humic-like substances in a city-river-reservoir freshwater system had statistically significant correlations with five classes of antibiotics and could be recognized as an environmental risk indicator of norfloxacin (Zhang et al.,2O19).Because colloids from the natural aquatic environment are diverse and complex materials with various sources,the binding mechanisms between different pharmaceuticals and colloids from different sources are complicated and uncertain. Further research on the influence of fluorescence-colloid on the migration and transportation of pharmaceuticals is warranted.

In order to understand the complexation of pharmaceuticals and different sources of colloids impacted by the fluorescence components, several analytical techniques and methodologies have been applied in the relevant studies,including synchronous fluorescence spectra (SFS), three-dimensional excitation-emission matrix (3D-EEM) fluorescence spectroscopy,parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC),and two-dimensional correlation spectroscopy (2D-COS),etc.SFS is a simple and selective preseparation procedure before analysis of a sample mixture and provides narrow and sharp spectra allowing the resolution of overlapping spectra (Digambara Patra, 2002; Elbalkiny et al., 2019). 3D-EEM fluorescence spectroscopy is a sensitive analysis technique that has already been widespread in tracing the substance composition of colloids in recent years (Lii et al., 2O19).PARAFAC coupled with EEM (EEM-PARAFAC) has been applied to characterizing fluorescence components qualitativelyand quantitatively because of its reliability,high sensitivity,and easy operation (Yan etal.,2O16b).EEM-PARAFAC has also become one of the most important techniques to clarify the different organic compounds and sources of colloids in water bodies and the binding mechanism between colloidal fluorescence components and pollutants (Bai et al.,2019; Yang et al., 2019). 2D-COS can be used to resolve peak overlapping problems,sequential orders of the spectral changes caused by external perturbations,and consequently describe the complexation process of the fluorescence component and pollutants (Guo et al., 2019). Besides,Fluorescence quenching titration is widely used in the study of the complexation between colloids and pollutants due to its simplicity and efficiency (Wang et al.,2O15).However,due to the profound complexity,heterogeneity,and polydispersity of colloid(Yang et al., 2015b;Yan et al.，2Ol6a),there is necessary for a deep study on exploring the binding mechanism of colloids and pharmaceuticals from different perspectives.

Poyang Lake is the largest freshwater lake in China,which is fed by five tributaries (Gan,Fu, Xin,Rao,and Xiu River)and flows into the Yangtze River.Most studies generally focus on heavy metals pollution, biological diversity，and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons in the Poyang Lake region (Jiang et al., 2O19b; Liu et al., 2019; Wan et al., 2020),few studies pay attention to pharmaceuticals pollution (Ding et al.,2O17),especially for the complexation of pharmaceuticals to colloids from Poyang Lake.Therefore,natural aquatic colloids from the Poyang Lake Basin were collected including the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City (the city located downstream of the Gan River).Multiple spectroscopic techniques and analysis methods,including 3D-EEM，PARAFAC,2D-COS,and fluorescence quenching were optimally used.In the present study,we selected CAP and CBZ as target pharmaceuticals.In addition,natural aquatic colloids from Yao Lake,the largest lake in Nanchang City were selected to simulate and analyze the binding mechanism between the fluorescence components of the natural colloids and the two drugs,including the binding substance,binding sequence,and binding intensity.The objectives of this study are to:(1) investigate the spatial distribution of the fluorescence components of colloids from the Poyang Lake Basin; (2) characterize the binding behavior of the selected pharmaceuticals to the fluorescence components of natural colloids; and (3) explore the binding mechanism between the fluorescence components of natural aquatic colloids and pharmaceuticals.

# 2.2.Sample collection and treatment

Thirty sampling sites were distributed in the five tributaries (Gan,Fu, Xin, Rao,and Xiu River) of Poyang Lake and seven typical urban lakes in Nanchang City (Fig.1). The surface water samples were collected by hydrophore and their general hydrochemical parameters were measured bya multi-parameter water quality probe(YSI66oo,USA) during sampling.General hydrochemical parameters of water samples were published in the previous study (Yan et al., 2O2O). Once transported to the laboratory,all surface water samples were filtered immediately through $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filters (PVDF,Millipore,USA).The filtrates were stored in the refrigeratorat $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in dark for further experiment.

Colloids in the filtrates,within the size range from 1 kDa to $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ were isolated by cross-flow ultrafiltration (CFUF) system,referring to our previous study (Yan et al., 2O18). Ultrafiltration membrane molecular weight cut-off was $^ { 1 } \ k \mathbf { D } \mathbf { a }$ $1 \ \mathrm { k D a } = 1 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { D a } )$ .The colloid's concentration factor $( \mathrm { c } f )$ of20 was chosen for furtheranalysis.All the water samples were stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and diluted in different dilution ratios when necessary during the spectral measurements.The recovery of the CFUF process was $6 9 \% - 1 2 1 \ \%$ which was evaluated by the organic carbon recovery rate according to our previous method (Yan et al., 2020).

# 2.3.Binding behavior experiment

Firstly, CAP and CBZ standard solutions were prepared with ultrapure water at $5 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ . Colloids were dispersed using the ultrasonic treatment for $1 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ in advance,to avoid the influence of colloid flocculation. $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ colloidal concentrate and $5 \mathrm { m L }$ CAP or CBZ standard solution were both added into a $1 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ centrifuge tube.After mixed thoroughly,the reaction solutions were shaken in dark for $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ Then, the samples were transferred into the ultrafiltration centrifuge tube (Amicon Ultra-15,30oo MWCO) and centrifuged at $3 6 0 0 \mathrm { r } \operatorname* { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for 1 h.The recovery rate of CAP and CBZ in the ultrafiltration centrifuge tube was $9 6 \%$ and $9 0 \%$ ,respectively.At last, the concentration of CAP and CBZ samples was measured both for the colloidal concentrate before ultrafiltration centrifugation and the truly dissolved phase after ultrafiltration centrifugation.Fluorescence spectra of all colloidal concentrate and the truly dissolved phase samples (with and without pharmaceuticals） were recorded at the same time.Pharmaceutical concentrations were determined using high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) (126O Infinity II,Agilent,USA) equipped with a diode array detector.The analysis conditions were as follows:an Athena C18 column $( 1 5 0 \times 2 . 1 \mathrm { m m }$ $5 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ was employed; the flow rate was 0.2 $\operatorname { m L } \operatorname* { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ ; the injection volume was $2 \ \mu \mathrm { L } ,$ and the mobile phase was 30 $\%$ ultrapure water (A) and $7 0 \%$ methanol (B); the column temperature was $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ；ultraviolet detection wavelengths of CAP and CBZ were 276 nm and $2 3 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ,respectively.

# 2.4.Spectral measurement

SFS and 3D-EEMs were measured using a fluorescence spectrophotometer (F-710o,Hitachi, Japan).The 3D-EEMs were recorded with scanning excitation $\left( \mathbf { E x } \right)$ wavelength from $2 3 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ to $4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and scanning emission (Em) wavelength from $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at sampling intervals of $5 \mathrm { n m }$ . Other parameters were as follows: measurement type was 3D scan; PMT voltage was $7 0 0 \mathrm { V }$ ；Scan speed was $3 0 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ ： SFS was collected by varying Ex wavelength from 200 to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ intervals and a constant offset $\Delta \lambda = 5 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ (Assaad etal., 2017). The scan speed and PMT voltage of SFS were $2 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m \ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ and $7 0 0 \mathrm { V }$ To remove inner-filter effects on the analysis results,colloids were diluted with Milli-Q water to reach absorbance lower than 0.3 at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ (Ohno, 2002).Three-dimensional fluorescence spectra and correlative data were processed by Origin 16.0 software.

# 2.5.Fluorescence quenching titration

The natural water contained colloids for fluorescence quenching titration was collected from Yao Lake, the biggest lake in Nanchang city (yellow five-point star in Fig.1).Before the quenching process,the water sample was filtered through $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filters according to the previous studies (Bai et al., 2017a, 2017b). Then, a $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { m L }$ colloidal sample was placed in a $1 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ centrifuge tube.Different concentrations of CAP and CBZ solutions were prepared with microsyringe (0.1,0.2, 0.5,1,2,5,10, 25,and $5 0 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 } .$ ).Each concentration was processed in triplicate and shaken for two days in the dark to ensure complexation equilibrium. Simultaneously,CAP and CBZ solution at each concentration with no colloids and the colloidal sample with no pharmaceutical were also conducted as control.Finally,all samples were ultrasonically dispersed and determined by fluorescence spectroscopy.

# 3.2.Binding mechanism between pharmaceuticals and aquatic colloids

In order to reveal the binding mechanism between the aquatic colloids and the two pharmaceuticals,colloids from the Yao Lake, with high fluorescence intensity in the previous experiment,were selected for the fluorescence quenching titration. Because the protein-like substance was mainly existed in the colloidal concentrate and showed stronger complexation with the two drugs,the filtrates (i.e., $< 0 . 4 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ were chose to proceed with the fluorescence quenching titration experiment.

# 3.2.1．Colloids-pharmaceuticals binding characterized by SFS

SFS is not only used to clarify the impact of pollutants on the conformation of colloid,but also convey information about the molecular microenvironment in the vicinity of the fluorophores (Bi et al., 2016).As the concentrations of CAP and CBZ increased, the peaks at 230,275,and $3 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ in the SFS of colloids from Yao Lake decreased in varying degrees followed the order as $2 7 5 ~ \mathrm { n m } > 2 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m } > 3 5 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig.4).In detail, the reduction of the fluorescence peak at $2 3 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ derived from the complexation between the tyrosine-like substance and CAP,while the fluorescence quenching at $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ was attributed to the complexation of the tryptophan-like substance and CAP.While the humic-like substance occurred at $3 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ had a low complexation ability with CAP.A similar quenching effect occurred between the selected colloidsand CBZ around the peaksat 230,275,and $3 5 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ with the same order.Whereas a more significant quenching effect was observed at $2 7 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ，implying that CBZ was more likely to interact with the tryptophan-like substance.Additionally,with increasing concentration of CAP, the fluorescence peak of colloid at $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ red-shifted from 275 nm to $2 9 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ ,and the number of red-shift wavelengths was proportional to the quenching effect.However,a continuous blue-shift from $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ to $2 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ was observed in the fluorescence peak of colloids with increased CBZ concentration. It indicated that a different conformational change occurred in the tryptophan-like substance of colloids when CAP and CBZ were added (Bani-Yaseen, 2Ol1).These results illustrated that the complexation mechanism differed with the different pharmaceuticals with the fluorophores in colloids,which would further result in the different conformational changes of the fluorophores.

# 3.2.2.Colloids-pharmaceuticals binding characterized by 2D-COS

To reveal the binding sequence between different fluorescence components and two pharmaceuticals, the synchronous and asynchronous correlation maps were obtained from SFS (Fig.5). The synchronous map is symmetric with respect to the diagonal line,including auto-peaks along the diagonal lines and cross-peaks located at the off-diagonal line (Xu et al., 2Ol3).An auto-peak represents the overall susceptibility of the corresponding spectral region to the change in spectral intensity as an external perturbation is applied to the system (Noda and Ozaki, 2004; Yu et al., 20l2). In the synchronous map of CAP (Fig. 5a), two positive auto-peaks were observed at 228/228 and $2 8 0 / 2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ along the diagonal line,where it is corresponding to the tyrosine-like and tryptophan-like components.The higher fluorescence intensity at $2 8 0 / 2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ indicated that the tryptophan-like substance was more susceptible to the addition of CAP than that of the tyrosine-like substance.The synchronous map of CBZ had a positive auto-peak centered at $2 8 0 / 2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig.5c),yet characterized with a higher fluorescence intensity than that in the synchronous map of CAP.It demonstrated that the tryptophan-like component in the selected colloids was more susceptive due to the addition of CBZ compared to CAP.Namely, the complexation of CBZ and colloid was stronger and the fluorescence quenching effect by CBZ was more obvious,which was consistent with the above result that CBZ showed the higher binding capacity by the colloids (Table S2).Besides, there was a positive cross-peak at 280/228 nm along the off-diagonal lines both for the synchronous map of CAP and CBZ (Fig.5a and c),suggesting that the fluorescence intensity of the tyrosine-like and tryptophan-like substances reduced proceeded in the same direction with increasing the concentration of CAP and CBZ.

# 4. Conclusions

In this study,the complexation mechanism of CAP and CBZ by the aquatic colloids from Poyang Lake Basin was investigated through multiple methodologies and methods.Based on the binding behavior experiment, four fluorescence components were identified by the PARAFAC model,including two humic-like components (C1 and C3) and two protein-like components (C2 and C4).Compared with the humic-like substance,the protein-like substance existed primarily in colloids and decreased with the direction of the water flow.In addition,the proteinlike components had a higher binding capacity to the two pharmaceuticals,especially for CBZ.The binding mechanism between the colloids and pharmaceuticals was proceeded through combining with SFS, 2DCOS,PARAFAC,and Ryan-Weber model.Results showed that the tryptophan-like substance at $2 7 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ in the selected colloids from Yao Lake deeply complexed with the two pharmaceuticals.Moreover, the preferential binding sequence of CAP and CBZ with the colloids occurred from the protein-like to the humic-like components,and CBZ exhibited a stronger fluorescence quenching effect than that of CAP.The nonlinear complex modeling results demonstrated that tryptophan-like substances played an important role in the migration and transformation of CAP and CBZ.The results of this study suggested that the fluorescencecolloids are significant carriers for CAP and CBZ in the aquatic environment,and could provide basic data for pollution protection control of pharmaceuticals in the aquatic environments.
