Hudson Bayand James Bay are fed by numerous rivers,which also deliver large amounts of disolved organic matter (DOM). The objective of this study is to assss Hudson Bay wide DOM spatial distribution using ultraviolet-visible light absorption and excitation-emission matrix fluorescence spectroscopies.Overall,a fivecomponent PARAFAC model was validated using 106o discrete samples collcted over nine field campaigns between 20o9 and 2018 and including the first samples from James Bay $( n = 1 1 2 )$ .Regional and seasonal variations were observed.Particularly striking was a 9-fold diference in concentration between coastal and marine zones of these bays,which implies limited mixing between those regions.Seasonal variations in absorption coefficient at $3 5 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and terrestrial humic-like components were found only in east Hudson Bay, revealing a regional disparity in seasonal DOM behaviour.The summer intensities and relative abundances of DOM revealed that cyclonic circulation transports terrestrial DOM around James Bay following its eastern shores before being transported northward along the western coast of Belcher Islands.The vertical distribution of DOM indicatd that fluorescent DOMis delivered to the deeper layer by winter convective mixing in northern Hudson Bay.

# 1．Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is operationally defined as the material that passes through a $0 . 2 \substack { - 0 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filter.DOM comes from allochthonous sources such as riverine discharge,or from autochthonous origin including in situ biological activity,with a lifetime ranging from hours to millennia (Hansell and Carlson, 2Ol5). Colored DOM (CDOM) is the optically active fraction of DOM that strongly absorbs ultraviolet/visible light (Guéguen and Kowalczuk, 2013). For example, the absorption coefficient at $3 5 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { ( a _ { C D O M } ( 3 5 5 ) ) }$ has been used as a proxy for terrestrial CDOM quantification (Hoge et al.,1995) on Arctic shelves (Capelle et al., 202O; Guéguen et al., 2016) and in the central Canada Basin (Dainard and Guéguen， 2Ol3； DeFrancesco& Guéguen,2O2l).The fraction of CDOM that fluoresces is referred to as fluorescent DOM(FDOM) and is used to discriminate between DOM pools like allochthonous and autochthonous FDOM (Coble, 2Oo7).The analyses of excitation-emission matrix (EEM) spectra combined with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC; Murphy et al., 2O13) are commonly used to assess composition and sources of DOM(Coble,1996) in coastal (Osburn et al., 2012; Yamashita et al., 2008) and open ocean studies (Catala et al., 2O15) including in the Arctic Ocean (Gao and Guéguen, 2017; Goncalves-Araujo et al., 2016; Guéguen et al., 2011).

Hudson Bay is the largest seasonally ice-covered inland sea in the world (Macdonald and Kuzyk, 2O11) and is connected to the Arctic and Atlantic Oceans via the Canadian Arctic Archipelago and Hudson Strait, respectively. One of its key features is the presence of multiple rivers in its watershed (Déry et al.,2O1l),which discharge large amounts of terrigenous DOM (Mundy et al., 2O1O). The mean annual surface circulation in Hudson Bay is cyclonic (Prinsenberg, 1986; Wang et al., 1994).James Bay,located in southern Hudson Bay,is a shallow region (average depth $6 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ ）receiving considerable river runoff including La Grande River $( 8 4 . 2 2 ~ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 } ~ \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ ； Déry et al., 2016). The Hudson BayJames Bay complex has not been well studied in the past (Preston et al.,2Ol2),with models often excluding the contribution of James Bay to the DOM cycle of the Hudson Bay complex due to lack of data. Southern Hudson and James Bays contain large peatlands stabilized by permafrost (Gorham et al., 2012; Pironkova,2017) which are undergoing extensive thaw.Models suggest that climate warming could accelerate permafrost thawing and thus potentially release massive terrigenous carbon pools (Frey and McClelland, 2Oo9) to Hudson Bay (McLaughlin and Packalen， 2O21). Evidence of terrigenous organic carbon mobilization has been reported in Hudson Bay rivers (Godin et al.,2Ol7) but its fate in the Bay remains largely unknown.Although sea ice is only seasonally present within the Hudson Bay complex (from early October to early June; Kuzyk etal., 2O19),it plays an important role in the carbon cycle (Capelle et al., 2O2O; DeFrancesco and Guéguen, 2021).A recent study showed a prevalence of proteinaceous DOM in underlying ice water and sea ice in comparison to ice-free waters (Zablocka et al., 202O),where photochemical processing can be significant (DeFrancesco and Guéguen, 2021; Osburn et al., 20o9; Vione and Scozzaro，2019).In this study，we report CDOM and FDOM data collected between 20o9 and 2018 to provide a comprehensive understanding of the DOM circulation patterns in the Hudson Bay complex. Winter and summer data collected in two major Hudson Bay estuaries were used to assess the seasonal sea-ice impacts on the fate and composition of CDOM and FDOM.

# 2.Materials and methods

# 2.1． Water sampling

A total of 1063 samples (Fig.1） were collected from Niskin or Kemmerer bottle (water underlying the ice) and a rosette (open-water period) mounted on a conductivity-temperature-depth (CTD) SBE-911 probe (Seabird) in Hudson Bay across nine individual field campaigns (Supplemental Table S1). The open-water campaigns comprised part of the 2009-2017 ArcticNet cruises and the 2016 and 2018 BaySys cruises. The sampled rivers included Churchill (HB2010,BaySys2O18),Severn (HB2010),Winisk (HB2010),LaGrande (B2015,B2016),GreatWhale (HB09_winter,B2017,HB201O),Petite Baleine (HB2010),Povungnituk (HB201O),Jos (HB201O),Nastopoca (HB201O),Innuksuak (HB2010), Josephine (HB201O),Polmund (HB2O1O),and Kogaluk rivers (HB2010) (see Supplemental Table S1 for details).The winter campaigns comprised part of the sampling conducted for the southeast Hudson Bay community-driven research network and involved collaboration with Arctic Eider Society and Inuit research partners from the communities of Sanikiluaq and Kuujjuarapik.

Seawater was immediately filtered on precombusted ( $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 5 \mathrm { h } }$ glass fiber filters (Whatman GF/F in 2009-2015;Advantec GF75 in 2015-2o18) into precombusted amber glass vials that were previously acid washed and rinsed twice with Milli-Q water.No consistent differences in acDom(355) and C1-C5 values were found depending on the type of filter used $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ .Ultrapure water blanks were also prepared to monitor potential contamination through each step of filtration and storage.Samples and blanks were stored in the dark at $4 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until analyses.

# 2.2.Spectroscopic measurements

CDOM samples were analyzed using a Shimadzu UV 2550 spectrophotometer scanning from $2 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ to $7 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and equipped with a 10- cm quartz cuvette.A Milli-Q water blank was measured between each sample to ensure the cuvette cleanliness.The absorption coefficient was calculated at $3 5 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { ( a _ { C D O M } ( 3 5 5 ) ) }$ as a proxy to terrestrial CDOM (Blough et al., 1993) using Eq. (1):

$$
a _ { C D O M } ( 3 5 5 ) = \frac { 2 . 3 0 3 \bullet A _ { 3 5 5 } } { l }
$$

where $\mathsf { A } _ { 3 5 5 }$ is the absorbance at $3 5 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and lis the path length in meters.The relative standard deviation of true replicate samples was better than $0 . 8 9 \%$ for acDoM(355).We chose $\mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ to represent the CDOM concentration to be consistent with previous Hudson Bay studies (Granskog et al., 2007, 2009; Guéguen et al., 2016).

Excitation emission matrix (EEM) was performed using a Fluoromax4 fluorometer (Horiba Jobin-Yvon). Samples with A250 over O.5 were diluted to minimize inner filter effects (Kothawala etal.,2013).EEMs were generated in signal-to-noise ratio using a l-cm quartz cuvette by collecting fluorescence intensity measurements at 5-nm intervals for excitation wavelengths ranging from 250 to $5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and emission wavelengths ranging from 300 to $5 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ .EEMs of Milli-Q blank were acquired daily and subtracted from samples.EEMs were normalized to Raman units (r.u.) by integrating the areaunder the Raman scatter peak of Milli-Q water blanks (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2Oo9). Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was used to decompose the EEMs into independent fluorescent components.Analysis was carried out using the drEEM toolbox in Matlab 2021b (Mathworks) (Stedmon and Bro,2008) with a non-negative value constraint. The model was run for 3 to 8 components.A 5-component model was validated using split-half analysis, Tucker congruence coefficients,and random initialization (Supplemental Text S1 and Supplemental Fig. S1).

# 2.3.Seasonal variation

Two estuarine systems were used to assess seasonal variation in DOM behaviour and concentration.The Nelson River Estuary (NR),located in western Hudson Bay (Fig.1),receives input from the Nelson River, the largest river in Hudson Bay and recipient of water from the Churchill River diversion,with a regulated annual discharge of $1 0 2 . 7 0 \ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 } \ \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ (Déry et al.,2O16). The Great Whale River Estuary (GW; average annual discharge $1 9 . 6 1 \ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Déry et al., 2016) empties into southeastern Hudson Bay,east of the Belcher Islands (Fig.1).

# 3.1．DOM composition

Five fluorescent components were identified and validated including four humic-like C1-C4 and one protein-like component C5 (Supplemental Fig.S3).All PARAFAC components were reported in the online FDOM repository OpenFluor (Murphy et al., 20l4) or found in coastal and marine waters. Component C1 had two excitation/emission maxima at 255/440 and $3 2 0 / 4 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ,which resembles the traditionally defined terrestrial humic-like peaks A and C,respectively (Coble, 2Oo7).Such components are common in natural waters including the Canadian Arctic Archipelago (Walker et al., 20o9) and marine waters (Jorgensen et al.,2011).C2 showed two excitation/emission maxima at 270/500 and $3 7 0 / 5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ,corresponding to a terrestrial humic-like component. A similar component was found in sea ice (Stedmon et al., 2007a, 2007b) and Arctic rivers (Mann et al., 2016). C3 showed a peak with excitation maximum at $2 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and a broad emission peak with a maximum at $4 4 0 ~ \mathrm { \ n m }$ corresponding to a UV-visible,humic-like component peak A (Coble,1996, 2007).Peak A was found in Arctic pore waters (Chen et al., 2016) and seawater (Osburn and Stedmon, 2011; Walker et al., 2Oo9).A similar fluorescent component was associated with photodegradation of terrestrial humic-like material (Stedmon et al., 2007a, 2007b).C4 displayed a primary excitation/emission maximum at 305/385 nm and a secondary peak at $< 2 5 0 / 3 8 5 \mathrm { n m }$ .This corresponds to a marine,UV-visible,humic-like peak (M/A; Coble, 1996). Peak M is attributed to marine sources (Coble,1996) and more recently to microbially altered DOM (Stedmon and Markager, 2005). This component is similar to that in other polar studies (Guéguen et al., 2011; Mann et al., 2Ol6). C5 showed a peak at an emission maximum of $3 2 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and an excitation maximum of $2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ，corresponding to a protein-like component, similar to the tryptophan-like peak T (Dainard and Guéguen,2O13). Tryptophan-like component has been attributed to autochthonous sources like microbial growth (Coble et al.,1998) in marine studies (Dainard and Guéguen, 2O13). Recent studies suggest also an allochthonous origin of tryptophan-like component (Dainard et al., 2019; Maie et al., 2007; Yamashita et al., 2017). Components C2, C4 and C5 were also previously reported in Hudson Bay (Guéguen et al.,

2011, 2016).

Intensities of components C1-C5 at higher salinities $\mathrm { ~ ( > ~ 2 5 ) }$ ）were comparable to other studies in the Arctic (Supplemental Fig.S3).For example, the intensities of C1-C3 were similar to those found in the Canadian Arctic Archipelago study (Walker et al.,2Oo9),whereas the intensities of $\mathrm { C } 4 \cdot \mathrm { C } 5$ were higher than those found in Arctic waters (Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; DeFrancesco and Guéguen, 2021; Goncalves-Araujo et al., 2016).Across all components,Hudson Bay marine waters had higher FDOM content than Arctic Ocean waters,indicating that even though they are poorer than coastal waters,Hudson Bay and particularly James Bay (Supplemental Fig.S2),is still a high DOM region.Like CDOM absorption coefficients acDom(355),FDOM intensities also decreased with salinity (data not shown),but the trends were weaker as shown by lower $\mathbf { r } _ { s }$ values $( - 0 . 3 4 < \mathrm { r } _ { \mathrm { S } } < - 0 . 6 8 )$ .These results indicate conservative FDOM mixing albeit with less robust relationships compared to the colored component.

# 3.2.Coastal vs.marine DOM

The coastal region exhibited a wide range of $\mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ and C1-C5, with the lowest values in the northwestern coastal waters (Supplemental Fig.S4),a subregion with no major river outflows.

# 3.3. Seasonal differences

Two estuarine regions(GW and NR; Fig.1） were used to assess seasonal differences in DOM composition and mixing.Negative relationships were found between acDom(355) and salinity in both estuarine regions in summer $( r = - 0 . 8 9$ and - 0.96) and in winter (NR only; $\mathbf { r s } = - 0 . 9 7 )$ (Fig.4).NR showed a steeper slope than GW in summer $( 0 . 3 6 \pm 0 . 0 3$ VS $0 . 1 5 \pm 0 . 0 2$ $p < 0 . 0 5 $ ,implying that mixing occurs more rapidly in NR.This could be due to the funnel-like shape of the NR estuary,which combined with strong tides (Wang et al., 2Ol2), may cause more efficient mixing between high-DOM river water and lowDOM marine waters. The $\mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ values in GW were also significantly higher in winter than in summer fora given salinity.The highly stratified under ice plume in GW (Kazmiruk etal., 2021) prevents DOMrich river water from mixing with more saline deeper waters.No seasonal differences were found between C1-C5 intensities in NR (Supplemental Figures 4S5-S6),meaning comparable levels of DOM are present in NR throughout the year. Discharge from the NR exhibits low seasonality because of regulation for hydroelectric power generation and possibly the water (and DOM) sources are similar year round because previous work found significant seasonal variation in water isotope properties (Smith et al., 2015). Furthermore,an area of open water (flaw lead) is present in NR throughout winter,which means that photodegradation could occur in this estuary during both winter and summer seasons and thus does not cause difference in DOM levels.

The low seasonal variability of acDom(355) and C1-C5 intensities in NR contrasts with GW,where acDom(355)as well as the intensity and relative abundance of terrestrial humic-like Cl and C2 were consistently greater in winter (Fig.4; Supplemental Figs.S5).While discharge from the GW River is very low in winter and forms a small under-ice plume near the river mouth as mentioned above,the surrounding surface waters are affected by an accumulation of river waters throughout the icecovered winter,according to observations reported in Eastwood et al. (2020).This river water has been attributed to outflow from northeast James Bay,which carries winter discharge from the La Grande hydroelectric development (Eastwood et al., 2O2O).The presence of landfast ice in southeast Hudson Bay during winter (Eastwood et al.,2O2O) limits light penetration into the water column and thus DOM photodegradation (Bélanger et al., 2006). Déry et al. (2016) reported lower riverine discharge in spring and summer compared to winter in southeast Hudson Bay and eastern James Bay because of the regulated discharge from the La Grande complex,whereas the river discharge in western Hudson Bay was similar among the different seasons.Thus, the limited photodegradation under the ice during winter,together with greater river discharge from southeast Hudson Bay and eastern James Bay rivers, likely explain the finding of more terrestrial humic-like in surface water in winter in this work.This is especially evident with $\mathrm { C } 3 \%$ previously reported to be a result of photodegradation of terrestrial humic-like material (Stedmon et al., 2007a, 2007b), which showed an important increase in GW in summer, as opposed to NR.

One similarity between NR and GW was that the relative abundance of C4-C5 (Supplemental Fig.S6) showed significant seasonal differences in both regions. Seasonal difference for marine humic-like $( C 4 \% )$ were found in both regions,with a marked increase in summer in GW.This increase could be due to an increase in photosynthesis during the icefree summer months.Together these estuarine results show the DOM concentration and composition changes depending on the season but in ways that are specific to each estuary,the seasonality of discharge,and the winter coastal mixing dynamics.

# 3.4.2.Hudson Bay inflow and outflow

Stations west and east of Mansel Island (Fig.1) in northern Hudson Bay were selected as representing inflow and outflow waters from Hudson Bay,respectively,in order to compare the DOM properties of these two water masses.Although the circulation around the islands is not well known, the area east of Mansel Island and across to the mainland of northern Quebec is generally considered the main pathway of summer surface water outflow (Prinsenberg,1986).

The salinity of the top $_ { 5 0 - \mathrm { m } }$ outflow waters was 28.99-31.57 (Fig. 6), which is typical of the summer surface mixed layer in Hudson Bay (Granskog et al., 2Ol1). Surface outflow waters were characterized by lower salinities but higher acDom(355) values relative to surface inflow waters,highlighting the source of fresh and DOM-rich waters in Hudson Bay.The higher abundances of allochthonous Cl-C3 in the top $_ { 1 0 \textrm { m } }$ at the exit point extend the argument that riverine discharge accumulates during HB transport (Granskog et al.,2O07).At depth ${ > } 1 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ ，temperatures were closer to freezing (Supplemental Fig.S8)and salinities were comparable at both sites $( 3 2 . 5 8 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 9$ and $3 2 . 6 9 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 1$ ， respectively） consistent with subsurface exchange among Foxe Basin, west Hudson Strait, and northern Hudson Bay (Defossez et al., 2010). Similarly,comparable values in $\mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ $\left( 0 . 3 2 \pm 0 . 0 4 \right.$ and $0 . 2 8 \pm$ $0 . 0 7 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ and FDOM composition were found at both sites at depth over $8 0 \textrm { m }$ ，These findings indicate that intermediate waters in northern Hudson Bay (Jones and Anderson,1994) were not affected by the seasonal surface freshwater addition,which freshens the surface waters in this area by several units.The maximum in C1-C4 intensity was associated with salinity centered on 32.51-32.54 and 32.27in the inflow and outflow,respectively,and temperatures very close to freezing at both sites.In a comprehensive study in northern Hudson Bay,Jones and Anderson (1994) showed that salinity ${ > } 3 1 . 9 $ demarked the fresh and sea ice melt rich Hudson Bay Summer Surface Water from the saltier Hudson Bay Winter Summer Water,which is formed when river runoff mixes with Hudson Bay Strait Bottom water.Previous studies have shown that sea ice formation,and in particular brine rejection,is a key mechanism for carrying riverine DOM into deeper layers (Dainard et al.,2019; Jprgensen et al., 2015; Jung et al., 2021). The peak in humic-like DOM is consistent with the intrusion of terrestrial DOM during formation of the winter surface mixed layer in western Bay,where the mixing may reach $8 0 { - } 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ (Prinsenberg, 1986).

# 3.4.3.Regional DOM composition

Surface values of acDom(355) and FDOM composition in five Hudson Bay regions,namely Foxe Basin,northern and southern Hudson Strait, and western and eastern James Bay,were compared (Fig.3).An 8-fold difference in acDom(355) was found between the CDOM-poor Foxe Basin $( 0 . 2 5 \pm 0 . 0 5 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ）and the CDOM-rich coastal region of Hudson Bay $( 2 . 0 5 \pm 2 . 4 8 \mathrm { \ m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ).The mean acDoM(355) values in Foxe Basin and northern Hudson Strait were not significantly different $( 0 . 3 6 \pm 0 . 1 0$ $\mathbf { m } ^ { - 1 }$ and $0 . 2 5 \pm 0 . 0 5 \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 1 }$ respectively; $p > 0 . 0 5 )$ . However,western James Bay,with anaverage of $0 . 8 6 \pm 0 . 4 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ,was significantly higher than Foxe Basin and northern Hudson Strait,and also significantly higher than Southern Hudson Strait $( 0 . 5 4 \pm 0 . 1 2 \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ).Eastern James Bay $( 3 . 4 6 ~ \pm ~ 1 . 6 6 ~ \mathrm { { \ m } ^ { - 1 } ) }$ ）showed no significant difference from the CDOM-rich coastal region of Hudson Bay,indicating the role of terrestrial DOM from James Bay in contributing to the CDOM-rich outflow into southern Hudson Strait.

In terms of FDOM composition (Fig.7), the abundance of terrestrial humic-like ${ \mathrm { C } } 2 \%$ was similar in coastal Hudson Bay (excluding James Bay),southern Hudson Strait,and eastern James Bay surface samples $( 1 9 . 4 \pm 3 . 8 \%$ $1 7 . 2 \pm 3 . 1 \%$ and $2 1 . 4 \pm 1 . 7 \%$ ,respectively; $\mathtt { p } > 0 . 0 5 )$ ： However,these terrestrial humic-like abundances $( \mathrm { C } 2 \% )$ were up to $4 2 { - } 5 0 \%$ greater than those found in marine Hudson Bay $( 1 4 . 3 \pm 3 . 2 \% )$ and northern Hudson Strait $( 1 5 . 1 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 3 \% )$ .This contrast between coastal Hudson Bay,eastern James Bay,and southern Hudson Strait waters vs.marine waters supports the argument that terrestrial DOM is constrained to the coastal HB regions (Granskog et al., 2Oo7) and follows the southern shore of Hudson Strait after flowing out of Hudson Bay.No significant difference in C1- $C 5 \%$ was found between eastern James Bay and southern Hudson Strait $( \mathbf { p } > 0 . 0 5 )$ ，which shows terrestrial DOM originating in James Bay is largelyunchanged and rapidly transported out of Hudson Bay.Mooring observations (Sutherland et al.,2011; Straneo and Saucier,2Oo8) revealed a high DOM fluorescence signal associated with low salinity values in southern Hudson Strait waters. This freshwater signal exhibited a strong seasonal cycle with minimum salinity between September and December due in part to the timing of river input into Hudson Bay (Ridenour etal., 202l). Overall, we showed that terrestrial DOM largely flows along the coasts of Hudson Bay and remains along the eastern coast,albeit influencing the surface waters surrounding the Belcher Islands,as it circulates northward from James Bay to southern Hudson Strait.The DOM flow patterns were consistent with the recent numerical modelling studies in surface Hudson Bay (Ridenour et al., 2019, 2021).

# 4.Conclusion

This study describes the surface circulation patterns around HB as obtained using discrete CDOM and FDOM samples collected over 9 years.Absorption coefficient, C1-C4 and $C 1 \% - C 4 \%$ were found to differ between winter and summer in eastern Hudson Bay as a result of ice coverand riverine flow difference.These seasonal variations imply that a year-long model cannot be used to describe HB circulation; however, additional winter and spring observations are needed to test a seasonbased circulation model outside the summer-fall seasons.In summer conditions,DOM concentrations were up to 9 times higher in coastal than in marine regions,showing that coastal waters do not mix on short (weekly to monthly) time scales with marine waters.The DOM distribution showed that DOM-rich surface waters leave James Bay along the eastern shore and are transported northward without significant influence from saline and DOM-depleted southeastHudson Bay waters.The relatively fresh surface waters leave Hudson Bay along the Quebec coasts and southern Hudson Strait.Future work could utilize the optical characteristicsof DOM in concert with salinity to increase understanding of how human activities and climate change can affect Hudson Bay circulation.
