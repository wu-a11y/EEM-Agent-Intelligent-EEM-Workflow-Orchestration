# Abstract

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) released from soil (SDOM) and biochar (BDOM) plays an important role in the fate of pollutants for the application of biochar in soil remediation. However, little attention has been paid to the impact of DOM on the adsorption of soil and/or biochar-bisphenol A (BPA) system. Hence, batch sorption experiments combined with quenching titration experiments were processed to reveal these behaviors and complexation mechanisms based on red soil and corn straw biochar. Moreover, multiple spectral technologies and methods were also introduced including ultraviolet-visible, threedimensional excitation-emission matrix, synchronous fluorescence spectrum, two-dimensional correlation spectroscopy (2DCOS), and parallel factor analysis. Isothermal adsorption results showed that the non-linearity and the adsorption capacity both increased after the addition of biochar, indicating that biochar could improve the surface adsorption capacity of the sorption system. Besides, the sorption capacity of BPA decreased for soil while increased for biochar after removal of the waterextractable DOM. 2D-COS analysis showed that the protein-like components preferentially occurred for both SDOM and BDOM under BPA surrounding. In addition, C2 (humic-like), C4 (tyrosine-like), and C5 (tryptophan-like) in the two DOMs were all involved in the quenching reaction with BPA, with C4 and C5 having higher quenching degree than C2, suggesting that protein-like substances had stronger affinity quenching effect with BPA. This study highlighted the significance of fluorescence property for DOM, which can extend the knowledge of the fate of pollutants in the application of biochar.

Keywords BPA $\cdot$ Biochar $\cdot$ Soil $\cdot$ Adsorption $\cdot$ DOM $\cdot$ Fluorescence

# Introduction

Bisphenol A (BPA) is an important raw material for the synthesis of polycarbonate, epoxy resin, and other materials, which has been extensively used in our daily life and industrial production (Guo et al. 2018). Currently, BPA has been found in multiple environmental media, such as water, soil, and sediment, and its pollution is becoming more serious (Zhou et al. 2011). Moreover, BPA has been confirmed as an endocrine-disrupting compound with estrogen properties, i n c l u d i ng r e p r od u c t i ve t o x i c i t y , n e ur o t o x i c i t y , immunotoxicity, teratogenicity, and carcinogenicity (Chapin et al. 2008). Therefore, the management of BPA has attracted widespread attention. At present, advanced oxidation processes, electrochemical oxidation process, and adsorption, etc. are common methods used for BPA removal (Kusvuran and Yildirim, 2013; Nie et al. 2019). Due to the low preparation cost and less generation of secondary harmful substances, adsorption has received more attention.

Biochar, a carbon-rich solid substance, is prepared by pyrolysis or gasification of various types of biomass waste (such as crop straw, sewage sludge) at a certain temperature (Zhang et al. 2018). Biochar is generally considered an effective absorbent for pollutants because of its unique physicochemical properties, including large specific surface area, complex pore structure, and high chemical stability (Kambo and Dutta, 2015). Also, owing to its surface that contains a large number of oxygen-containing functional groups (including carboxyl, carbonyl), biochar has a high cation exchange capacity, and is highly sensitive to pollutants (Zhang et al. 2013). In light of these unique physicochemical properties, biochar has been used worldwide as soil improvement to improve soil fertility and reduce greenhouse gas emissions, and environmentally friendly adsorption materials to reduce the bioavailability of pollutants such as pharmaceuticals. However, the related study on the BPA adsorption in the biochar-soil system is still relatively inadequate.

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is ubiquitous in the soil and aquatic environment with complex structure and composition (Tipping et al. 1999). Soil dissolved organic matter (SDOM) can significantly affect the environmental chemical behavior of pollutants through covalent binding, redox, etc. (Ding et al. 2011). After biochar application in soil, biochar dissolved organic matter (BDOM) would be released into the soil. Besides, with the increasing application of biochar, a large amount of BDOM enters the soil pore water, which would increase the content of DOM in the soil environment, and further affect the migration ability and ecological toxicity of pollutants. This is of great significance to evaluate the environmental effects of biochar (Moon et al. 2003). Studies have shown that interaction between DOM and pollutants mainly depends on the physicochemical properties of DOM, including the variable, heterogeneous composition, and structure of DOM (Guo et al. 2012). Simultaneously, these properties of DOM are highly susceptible to change, which in turn affects the fate of pollutants. Moreover, current studies are mainly focused on the effects of BDOM on the adsorption behavior of heavy metals (e.g., lead, iron, and arsenic) (Zhao et al. 2013), while few studies paid attention to the organic pollutants, such as Zhang et al. (2019) and Yang et al. (2019), who investigated the effect of BDOM on the adsorption behavior of bisphenol S.

At present, spectroscopic techniques have been widely used to analyze the complexation between DOM and pollutants because of their high sensitivity, rapidity, and in situ measurement (Yang et al. 2018). These methods or techniques include parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC), twodimensional correlation spectroscopy (2D-COS), ultravioletvisible (UV-Vis) absorption spectrum, three-dimensional excitation-emission matrix (3D-EEM) fluorescence spectrum, synchronous fluorescence spectrum (SFS), etc. For instance, PARAFAC enables the separation of complex fluorescence spectroscopy chemometrics and gives quantitative changes (Rasmus 1997). 2D-COS can improve spectral resolution, reveal the sequence of changes in different fluorescence spectral features induced by external perturbations, and analyze intramolecular and intermolecular interactions (Fudge et al. 2013). UV-Vis absorption spectrum can be used to characterize the fluorescence quenching type (Xu et al. 2013).

In this study, biochar sample was produced by pyrolysis from corn straw, which was introduced as adsorbents as well as a red soil, to investigate their adsorption behavior of BPA.

In particular, combined with multiple spectroscopic methods, including UV-Vis, 3D-EEM, SFS, PARAFAC, and 2D-COS, DOM released from soil and biochar was isolated to explore and quantify the interaction between DOM and BPA. The aims of this study were to (1) compare the difference of BPA sorption behavior in soil, biochar, and biochar-soil system; (2) identify the type, content, and sequence of fluorophores in DOM-BPA reaction system; and (3) provide some data for the pollution prevention of BPA in biochar application.

# Materials and methods

# Sample preparation and reagents

# Reagents

Bisphenol A (purity $\geq 9 9 \%$ ) was purchased from SigmaAldrich (USA), and the basic physicochemical properties are shown in Table S1. $\mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ was provided by Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. (China). Methanol was an HPLC-grade solvent and was supplied from CNW Technologies GmbH (Germany). Other reagents were at least analytical grade and could be used directly without purification. All stock solutions were prepared by dissolving the chemical agents in Milli-Q water $( 1 8 . 2 5 \mathrm { M } \Omega \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ).

# Preparation of soil and biochar

The soil sample collected from southern China (Jiangxi Province) was a typical red soil (115.362275 E, 28.070082 N). Corn is one of the major crops in the region. Once transported to the laboratory, the soil sample was removed of debris (such as stones, and plant roots, etc.), air-dried naturally, and then pulverized and passed through a 100-mesh sieve.

Biochar was made by pyrolysis carbonization of corn straw. Before pyrolysis, the corn straw was washed with Milli-Q water, dried in an oven at $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and cut into $1 { \mathrm { - c m } }$ sections. Subsequently, the corn straw sample underwent pyrolysis in a muffle furnace under nitrogen flow at a heating rate of $8 . 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ per minute to the final temperature of $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , which was held for $^ { 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ (Liu et al. 2017). After pyrolysis, the biochar sample was cooled to room temperature, milled to pass through a 100-mesh sieve.

# Extraction of DOM from soil and biochar

To obtain the DOM from soil and biochar, $2 5 \ \mathrm { g }$ soil or $1 2 \ \mathrm { g }$ biochar was added into the $2 5 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ volumetric flask with Milli-Q water. Then, the mixture was oscillated for 3 days at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $1 8 0 \ \mathrm { r \ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ . After the process, the solution was transferred to a centrifuge tube and centrifuged at $3 5 0 0 \mathrm { r m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ . Lastly, the supernatant was filtered through the $0 . 4 5 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ membrane filter to obtain SDOM and BDOM. The left soil and biochar samples are defined as soil and biochar without water-extractable DOM (Wei et al. 2019). The concentration of SDOM and BDOM was 0.08 and $1 . 2 6 \mathrm { m g C g ^ { - 1 } }$ , respectively.

# Characterization of samples

In this study, the pH values of soil, biochar, and two DOMs were determined by PHS-3C pH meter (INESA Scientific Instrument Co., Ltd, China). Functional groups in solid samples were characterized by Nicolet 6700 Fourier transform infrared spectrometer (FTIR, Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). The sieved sample was initially ground with KBr in an agate mortar. Then the mixture was molded into a disc. KBr powder was used as the blank. The spectra were recorded in the range of wavelengths $4 0 0 { \ - } 4 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ . The spectral resolution was $4 ~ \mathrm { { c m } ^ { - 1 } }$ . The cation exchange capacity (CEC) of soil and biochar was determined by the Barium chloride method (ISO 11260-1994) (ISO 1994). The organic matter content and particle size of soil were measured by the $\mathrm { K } _ { 2 } \mathrm { C r } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 7 } \mathrm { - H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S O } _ { 4 }$ oxidation method (Sims and Haby 1971) and LA-950 particle size analyzer (Horiba, Japan). The elemental composition (C/H/O/N/S) of biochar was characterized by an EA3000 elemental analyzer (Euro Vector, USA). The concentrations of DOM were measured using a liquiTOC II (Elementar, Germany). UV-Vis absorption spectra were obtained using a UV-3300 UV-Vis spectrophotometer (Mapuda, Shanghai). SFS and 3D-EEMs of DOM were measured by Hitachi F-7100 fluorescence spectrophotometer (Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan) with a xenon lamp of 150 W. The PMT voltage was adjusted to $7 0 0 \mathrm { V }$ . The scanning excitation wavelength range of SFS was $2 0 0 { \ - } 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ; $\Delta \lambda$ (the difference between emission wavelength and excitation wavelength) was $6 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . The scanning speed was set as $2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $\operatorname* { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ with a 1-nm scan spacing. The 3D-EEMs was measured from 200 to $4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ for excitation $\left( \operatorname { E x } \right)$ and $2 5 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ for emission (Em) wavelengths, using a scanning speed of $1 2 0 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ with a 5-nm scan spacing. Different concentrations of water samples were diluted with Milli-Q water to avoid inner filter effect by detecting the absorbance lower than 0.3 at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ (Ohno 2002).

# Isothermal adsorption experiments

Sorption experiments were processed in triplicate based on the batch experiments and the specific steps were as follows. Firstly, Milli-Q water was used to prepare background solution containing $0 . 0 1 \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ (used to control the ionic strength during the adsorption equilibrium process) and $0 . 1 \ \mathrm { g }$ ${ \mathrm { L } } ^ { - 1 } { \mathrm { N a N } } _ { 3 }$ (used to inhibit microorganism degradation) (Sun et al. 2012). BPA stock solution of $1 5 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ was made with the prepared background solution. Biochar and soil adsorption systems with different solid-liquid ratios were designed based on the logKow value of BPA. The specific experimental steps were as follows: $0 . 8 8 9 \ \mathrm { g }$ soil, $0 . 0 1 \ \mathrm { g }$ biochar, and $0 . 8 9 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } 1 \%$ mass fraction of biochar in soil (Yue et al. 2017) $( 1 \% C \mathrm { { S } ) }$ were respectively weighed into a $1 5 \mathrm { - m L }$ centrifuge tube. Then 13 mL with different concentrations of BPA solution $\left( 0 . 0 2 - \right.$ $1 . 5 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ L ^ { - 1 } } ,$ ) was added. Next, the centrifuge tubes were sealed and placed in a constant temperature $( 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ oscillator at $1 8 0 \mathrm { ~ r ~ m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for 3 days. The preliminary experiments showed that all the samples could reach the apparent adsorption equilibrium within 3 days. Until reaching equilibrium, all centrifuge tubes were centrifuged by the low-speed centrifugation at $3 5 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , and then centrifuged by highspeed centrifugation at $1 8 0 0 0 \mathrm { r m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Finally, the concentration of BPA was measured by 1260 Infinity II highperformance liquid chromatography (HPLC, Agilent, USA) equipped with an Athena C18 column $( 1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m } \times 2 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , $5 \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ . The operating conditions were as follows: Ex and Em wavelength of the fluorescence detector were $2 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and 305 nm, respectively. The injection volume was $5 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ , and the column temperature was $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The mobile phase was methanol and water (volume ratio: 70/30) at a flow rate of $0 . 2 \mathrm { m L }$ $\operatorname* { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . The effect of DOM on the adsorption of BPA to soil and biochar was considered by comparing the adsorption system with and without DOM. The other conditions were the same as the sorption experiment above.

# Fluorescence quenching titration

A $1 3 \mathrm { - m L }$ DOM extraction solution was used in the fluorescence quenching experiments, added into $1 5 \mathrm { - m L }$ centrifuge tubes. Meanwhile, BPA solution with different concentrations (i.e., $0 . 1 , 0 . 2 , 0 . 5 , 1 , 2 , 5 , 1 0 , 2 5 , 5 0 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ was also placed into the centrifuge tube. All concentration was processed in triplicate. Then, the centrifuge tubes were shaken for about 3 days in the dark to ensure complexation equilibrium. Finally, the mixture of DOM-BPA was used to measure the 3D-EEM and SFS.

# Data analysis

# Adsorption model

The Freundlich and the linear models were employed to fit the adsorption isotherm data of BPA on soil and biochar (Zhao et al. 2017).

Freundlich model : $: \mathbf { C } _ { s } = K _ { \mathrm { f } } { \mathbf { C } _ { w } } ^ { n }$

Linear model : $: \mathbf { C } _ { \mathrm { s } } = K _ { d } C _ { w }$

where $C _ { w } ( \log \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ and $C _ { s } ( \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } )$ are the BPA concentration in the aquatic and solid phase, respectively. $K _ { f } [ ( \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } ) /$ $( \boldsymbol { \mathrm { m g } } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ) ^ { n } ]$ is the Freundlich sorption coefficient, which represents the sorption capacity. $n$ characterizes the linearity degree of the adsorption isotherm (Srinivasan et al. 2015). $K _ { d }$ is the partition coefficient of the target organic compounds in the linear model.

# Two-dimensional correlation spectroscopy

Two-dimensional correlation spectroscopy (2D-COS) analysis of SFS was conducted using the different concentrations of added BPA as the external disturbance. Then, the normalized SFS data was transformed into a new spectral matrix for 2DCOS. The detailed principles of 2D-COS have been described in the literature (Noda 1990). All the COS computations were performed using the “2D-Shige” software (KwanseiGakuin University, Japan) (Wei et al. 2016). The final 2D-COS maps were plotted using Origin Pro 2016 software.

# PARAFAC modeling

PARAFAC model was introduced based on a dataset of all 3D-EEMs in the fluorescence quenching experiment for identifying and quantifying the fluorescence composition of DOM. Before modeling, the Milli-Q water was used as blank, and the raw data were adjusted to trim the Raman and Rayleigh scatter effects according to the previous study (Thygesen et al. 2004). The fluorescence intensity of all samples was normalized to Raman units (r.u.) using the area of the Raman scattering peak of Milli-Q water at $\mathrm { E x } = 3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Lawaetz and Stedmon 2009).

PARAFAC analysis was completed by DOMFluor toolbox in Matlab R2012a software. The final component number of the model is determined by the minimization of the squaresum error, and the reliability of the result is guaranteed by split-half, load, and residual analysis. The concentration of parallel factor components is expressed by the fluorescence intensity at the highest fluorescence peak, which is $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ (R.U.) (Stedmon and Bro 2008). Finally, the fluorescence substance is determined by comparing the λEx (excitation wavelength) and λEm (emission wavelength) to those in the online Openfluor database (http://www.openfluor.org) (Murphy et al. 2014).

# Results and discussion

# Characteristics of sorbents

The physicochemical properties of soil and biochar are shown in Table S2. For soil, the pH value was 4.53, and the total organic matter was $6 . 1 8 ~ \mathrm { g ~ k g } ^ { - 1 }$ . Less than $0 . 0 0 2 \mathrm { m m }$ defined as silt was the major proportion of particles $( 6 3 \% )$ . The cation exchange capacity of the soil was 15.30 cmol $\mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ . These properties indicated the low fertility of the selected soil, which further impacted its sorption capacity to non- or weakly polar organic contaminants (Lapworth et al. 2012). For biochar, the $\mathrm { p H }$ value was 9.43 within the range of alkaline, which was consistent with the previous study (Xu et al. 2015). And the main constituent element of biochar in this study was C $( 5 8 . 8 1 \% )$ , while the proportions of other elements were O $( 2 3 . 7 \% )$ , H $( 3 . 1 2 \% )$ , N $( 3 . 0 5 \% )$ , and S $( 0 \% )$ , respectively. Earlier studies indicated that the $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C }$ ratio could be used to determine the carbonization degree of biochar; thus, it could be known that the biochar in this study had a high degree of carbonization and aromaticity due to the low value of H/C (0.05) (Tatarková et al. 2013). Therefore, from the advantages of high aromaticity and high carbon content, it suggested that biochar had a high affinity for BPA containing benzene rings, which was conducive to its adsorption.

# Sorption isotherm

# Sorption isotherms of BPA

Freundlich and linear models were both employed to describe the adsorption process of BPA by soil, biochar, and $1 \% \mathrm { C S }$ (Fig. 1). Compared with the linear model, the Freundlich model had a better fitting performance for the adsorption process of biochar and $1 \% \mathrm { C S }$ , indicating that the adsorption of BPA to biochar and $1 \% \mathrm { C S }$ was non-linear process. Based on the assumption of the Freundlich adsorption isothermal model, it could be known that the adsorption of BPA by biochar and $1 \% \mathrm { C S }$ was multi-layer heterogeneous adsorption with uneven energy distribution (Wu et al. 2013). In contrast to the Freundlich model, the linear model was a better fit for the adsorption process of BPA on soil, demonstrating that the adsorption process of BPA by soil was linear adsorption process.

The sorption parameters of BPA by soil, biochar, and $1 \% \mathrm { C S }$ are presented in Table 1. In the Freundlich model, the $K _ { f }$ value of BPA was 3.53, 450.16, and 7.19 for soil, biochar, and $1 \% \mathrm { C S }$ , respectively. This inferred that the order of the adsorption capacity of BPA by the three systems was as follows: biochar $> 1 \% \mathrm { C S } >$ soil. Namely, the soil adsorption capacity of BPA increased after adding with biochar. In this study, the adsorption capacity of the soil was higher than that of black cinnamon soil $( K _ { f } = 1 . 4 1 )$ ) ( $\mathrm { X u }$ et al. 2015). Yet BPA sorption capacity of biochar in this study was lower than that of blue eucalyptus $( K _ { f } = 9 8 2 \pm 5 3 . 2 )$ and silkworm fecal biochar $( K _ { f } =$ $1 4 1 5 \pm 3 6 . 5 )$ (Li et al. 2017) which made at the same pyrolysis temperature $( 5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ . Besides, $n$ value of BPA adsorption by soil in this study was close to 1, further verifying that the BPA adsorption process in soil system was linear, suggesting that the process was dominated by partition (Chen and Chen 2009). However, the $n$ value in this study differed from the literature (0.79) $\mathrm { { X u } }$ et al. 2015), that the sorption process resulted from the partition combined with the function of the soil organic matter and the surface adsorption by minerals. This might be mainly attributed to the high organic matter content $ { 1 6 . 4 \ \mathrm { g } }$ $\mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } )$ in the selected Beijing cinnamon soil.

For biochar, the $n$ value was 0.629, which revealed that BPA adsorption in biochar system was non-linear and influenced by both partition and surface adsorption (Zhang et al. 2013). Moreover, the nature of BPA adsorption process by soil and biochar was demonstrated by comparing the FTIR spectra gained before and after the sorption experiments (Fig. S1). It showed that the peak of biochar near $3 4 6 0 ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ became wider after adding BPA. Studies have shown that a broader $\boldsymbol { v } _ { \mathrm { O H } }$ band meant a variety of intermolecular hydrogen bond formation (Kubo and Kadla 2005). Furthermore, the peak intensity corresponding to each functional group in biochar showed different degrees of decrease, indicating that these functional groups in the biochar all participated in the binding with BPA in different degrees. This was consistent with the results of BPA adsorption by poultry waste and wheat straw biochar $( n = 0 . 3 7 \mathrm { - } 0 . 8 0 )$ (Sun et al. 2012). Besides, n value of BPA adsorption by $1 \% \mathrm { C S }$ system was 0.688, which was much lower than that in soil system. It meant that biochar could enhance the surface adsorption capacity of the sorption system. $\mathrm { X u }$ et al. (2015) found that $n$ value of BPA adsorption in the carbon-soil system with $4 \%$ biochar decreased from 0.79 to 0.74, which might be owing to the $\mathrm { p H }$ value increased from $8 . 7 4 \pm 0 . 1 4$ to $9 . 0 1 \pm 0 . 0 6$ after adding $4 \%$ biochar. With the increase of environmental pH, more BPA was dissociated, resulting in a decrease in the contribution of linear partition.

In the linear model, the $K _ { d }$ value represents the adsorption capacity was also in the order as biochar $> 1 \% \mathrm { C S } >$ soil, which was in agreement with that in the Freundlich model. The adsorption capacity of BPA was significantly increased after adding biochar, indicating that biochar greatly enhanced the adsorption capacity of BPA in soil. This was consistent with the previous studies that biochar had a strong adsorption effect on a variety of organic pollutants (such as atrazine and carbaryl, etc.) (Li et al. 2014; Zhang et al. 2013).

# Effect of DOM on BPA adsorption by soil and biochar

To explore the influence of DOM on BPA adsorption by soil and biochar, the soil and biochar with and without waterextractable DOM were chosen for adsorption experiments. The results showed that after water-extractable DOM removal, BPA adsorption capacity was significantly decreased and increased for soil and biochar, respectively (Fig. 2). Further analysis in the sorption parameters from the Freundlich model revealed that the adsorption capacity decreased from 3.53 to 1.70 for soil (Table 2), suggesting SDOM had a significant influence in the process of BPA adsorption in soil. This was in agreement with the early studies that the soil after removal of organic matter showed a certain decrease in the adsorption capacity of organic pollutants (such as pesticides) (Celis et al. 1998; Spark and Swift 2002). Studies had shown that SDOM contained active groups such as carboxyl and hydroxyl, which could be combined with pollutants through ion exchange, complexation, and a series of reactions, and then impacted the adsorption behavior of organic pollutants (Chi and Amy 2004). In contrast, the removal of water-extractable BDOM resulted in an increase in adsorption capacity of BPA for biochar (the $K _ { f }$ value changed from 450.16 to 737.37 after water-extractable DOM removed). This might be mainly because the dissolution of BDOM could release the surface pores of biochar which was previously blocked by BDOM. Then the adsorption sites on the surface of biochar particles further have an effect on promoting the fixation of biochar towards the pollutants (Mukherjee and Zimmerman 2013). Although the role of BDOM was not obvious in this study, some studies also showed that BDOM deeply impacted on the pollutant sorption (Yang et al. 2019). Besides, DOM in soil greatly influenced the sorption behavior of BPA in this study. Therefore, the following discussion focuses on the fluorescence quenching process between BPA and DOM to investigate the binding mechanism of DOM-BPA in soil and biochar.

# Binding mechanism of DOM and BPA

# DOM-BPA binding characterized by 3D-EEM and UV-Vis analysis

EEM fluorescence quenching was performed to identify the interactions between DOM and BPA. The EEM fluorescence spectra of SDOM and BDOM with no BPA dosage and the pure BPA solution are shown in Fig. S2. It could be seen that SDOM and the pure BPA have a similar fluorophore peaked at $\mathrm { E x } / \mathrm { E m } = 2 7 0 / 3 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , which was often identified as the protein-like fluorophore (Coble 1996). Additionally, it has been reported that the bisphenol compounds (e.g., bisphenol S and BPA) could be quenched by proteins of DOM (Yang et al. 2017; Yan et al. 2019). Thus, it suggested that the fluorescence intensity of this protein fluorophore would increase with the concentration of BPA increase after reaching a certain concentration, even though the proteins of DOM were quenched by BPA. This phenomenon was further confirmed by the results of SFS (Fig. S3). When BPA concentration increased from 0 to $0 . 2 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ , the fluorescence intensity of the peak at $2 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ decreased; in contrast, this peak red shifted and the fluorescence intensity increased when BPA concentration is higher than $0 . 2 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ . It indicated that BPA could react with the protein-like components of the selected DOMs (Lu and Jaffe 2001).

Since the quenching effect in the DOM-BPA complex system from SFS analysis was inconspicuous, UV-Vis spectroscopy was introduced to determine the quenching mechanism. The fluorescence quenching of DOM by BPA may be involved in two types of quenching processes including the dynamic and the static quenching processes (Chen et al. 2010). When the sum of the individual absorbance spectra of DOM and BPA was identical to that of the DOM-BPA complex system, it means the dynamic quenching occurred due to the collision between fluorophore and quencher (Xu et al. 2013). If the absorbance spectra of the DOM-BPA complex system change with the BPA dosage, it means that the static quenching was confirmed due to the formation of a nonfluorescence complex (Chen et al. 2010; Yan et al. 2019). As Fig. 3 displayed, the absorbance of the DOM-BPA complex system increased with the increasing BPA concentration, indicating the static quenching occurred.

# DOM-BPA binding characterized by SFS combined with 2D-COS

2D-COS was applied to the SFS to probe qualitative information relating to the binding order of two DOMs fluorophores to BPA (Fig. 4). Two auto-peaks were displayed in the synchronous correlation map at 225/225 and $2 3 5 / 2 3 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ along the diagonal line for SDOM (Fig. 4(a)). The appearance of auto-peaks represented the susceptibility degree with regard to the corresponding DOM structure due to the spectral intensity change caused by external perturbation (Guo et al. 2018). Thus, the high intensity of auto-peak at 225/225 and 235/235 nm indicated that SDOM at this spectral was relatively more susceptive because of the addition of BPA. The synchronous map of BDOM had three auto-peaks at the coordinates of 235/ 235, 270/270, and $3 3 5 / 3 3 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. 4(c)). However, the intensities of auto-peaks in BDOM were lower than those in SDOM, suggesting that the corresponding components (i.e., $2 3 5 \mathrm { n m } )$ in SDOM were more susceptive than those in BDOM due to the addition of BPA. Besides, one negative cross-peak was observed at $2 3 5 / 2 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ located at off-diagonal positions in SDOM, suggesting that the corresponding component changes proceeded in the opposite direction, while in BDOM, there was one positive cross-peak and two negative cross-peaks were observed at $2 7 0 / 2 3 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , $3 3 5 / 2 3 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , and $3 3 5 / 2 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , indicating that the corresponding components changes proceeded in the same direction at $2 7 0 / 2 3 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , whereas changes in the opposite direction at $3 3 5 / 2 3 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 3 5 / 2 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ .

The asynchronous map reveals the sequential or successive changes in the spectral intensity with increasing concentrations of BPA (Noda and Ozaki 2004). The asynchronous map is composed of the cross-peaks located at the offdiagonal line (Guo et al. 2017). In the asynchronous map of SDOM (Fig. 4(b)), there were two positive cross-peaks at 235/ $2 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and $2 7 0 / 2 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and a negative cross-peak at 285/235 nm, while there were two positive cross-peaks at $2 3 5 / 2 2 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $2 7 0 / 2 2 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and two negative cross-peaks at $3 3 5 / 2 3 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 3 5 / 2 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ under the diagonal line in the asynchronous map of BDOM (Fig. 4(d)). According to Noda’s rule, the preferential binding of BPA with SDOM could be ordered as follows: $2 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }  2 2 5 \ \mathrm { n m }  2 3 5 \ \mathrm { n m }  2 8 5 \ \mathrm { n m } .$ , which indicated that the quenching reaction occurred mainly at protein-like substances in SDOM. The result was the same as the study that characterized the binding of organic ligands with zinc in a eutrophic lake (Liu et al. 2015), while for BDOM, the order was as follows: $2 3 5 \ \mathrm { n m }  2 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m } $ $2 2 5 \ \mathrm { n m }  3 3 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . The results indicated that the proteinlike components occurred earlier than those of the humiclike components under BPA surroundings, which was in line with the previous studies about the complexation of tetracycline with DOM derived from algae and macrophyte and the binding of DOM-roxarsone (Bai et al. 2017; Fu et al. 2016). Therefore, these results suggested that BPA could be adsorbed by both SDOM and BDOM.

# PARAFAC component characteristics

The PARAFAC model can reduce the overlap of fluorescence peaks in EEM spectra and obtain more information on the fluorescence composition of DOM. In this study, five fluorescent components (C1–C5) were identified by PARAFAC model on the 3D-EEMs of DOM from the fluorescence quenching experiment, including three humic-like components (C1–C3) and two protein-like components (C4–C5) (Table 3). The detailed information of the PARAFAC components such as representative EEMs, spectral loadings, and substance types are presented in Table 3. All fluorescence components were well matched with those in the OpenFluor database, with the Tucher congruence coefficient (TCC) higher than 0.93. The maximum $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ of component 1 (C1) was 255 (305)/406 nm, which was classified as humiclike substances, which was defined as humic-like substances in the ultraviolet region (Wunsch et al. 2018) and mainly derived from marine environments or decomposed from microorganisms (Calleja et al. 2019; Coble 1996). Similar components had also been observed in leaves and DOM (Cuss and Guéguen 2013; Stedmon and Markager 2005). Besides, previous studies found that C1 was commonly associated with biological activity and was thought to be produced from biodegradation (Ishii and Boyer 2012). The maximum $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ o f component 2 (C2) was 225 (280)/406 nm, and was considered to be more derived from marine and terrestrial organic matter (Derrin et al. 2020). Component 3 (C3) had a maximum $\operatorname { E x } /$ Em of 260 (380)/478 nm, which was considered UVA humiclike substances (Murphy et al. 2006). The similar components of C1 and C3 were also observed in other BDOMs, which were characterized as the pyrolysis products consisted of (poly-) phenol substances, other aromatic structures similar to soil fulvic-like substances, and aromatic humic-like substances (Uchimiya et al. 2013). Component 4 (C4) and component 5 (C5) were protein-like components. Hereinto, C4 (the maximum $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ values were 225 (260)/302 nm) were considered tyrosine-like proteins (Derrien et al. 2019). Usually, tyrosine-like proteins were associated with the biological activity in natural waters, especially in biochar made from lignin-rich raw materials (Chen et al. 2018; Coble 1996). C5 had an $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maximum of 225 (275)/318 nm, which was classified as tryptophan-like and commonly derived from bacterial and seagrass communities (Wunsch et al. 2015).

Combined with the PARAFAC analysis results, the distribution of the five components in SDOM, BDOM, and BPA samples could be described (Fig. S4). The results showed that SDOM was mainly composed of C2, C4, and C5. Hereinto, the proportion of C4 was relatively high $(43 \% )$ , indicating that tyrosine-like substances were the main component of the selected SDOM. In contrast, the main components of BDOM were C1 $( 3 7 \% )$ , C2 $(42 \% )$ , and C3 $( 1 1 \% )$ . It suggested that corn straw DOM was mainly dominated by humic-like substances similar to the fluorophore derived from marine or terrestrial organic matter and UV region. Besides, the fluorescence components of BPA were mainly C4 and C5.

# The quenching effect of BPA on the selected DOM characterized by PARAFAC model

![](images/23197c3b739bd86643ae36fdf7aa8a1735c5eb5a6876b2ae911ad3e209bc4846.jpg)  
Fig. 5 The quenching effect of each PARAFAC component in SDOM and BDOM

In agreement with the results of SFS that the protein-like substance (i.e., $2 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ ) decreased at lower BPA concentration $<$ $0 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ ) and increased greatly at higher BPA concentration, the fluorescence intensity of C4 and C5 (representative of the protein-like fluorophores) increased dramatically (Fig. S5). In order to explore the quenching effect of BPA, the quenching degree of the PARAFAC components $( I _ { j } )$ at $j \ : \mathrm { m g }$ $\mathrm { ~ L ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ of BPA was calculated according to Eq. (3).

$$
I _ { j } = \big ( I _ { \mathrm { B P A } - j } + I _ { \mathrm { D O M } } \big ) { - } I _ { \mathrm { D O M - B P A } - j }
$$

where $I _ { \mathrm { B P A } - j }$ is the fluorescence intensity of BPA at a certain concentration $( j \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ) ; I _ { \mathrm { D O M } }$ is the fluorescence intensity of the PARAFAC component in SDOM or BDOM with no BPA dosage; $I _ { \mathrm { D O M - B P A - } j }$ is the final fluorescence intensity of the DOM-BPA complex system at the corresponding BPA concentration.

As Fig. 5 shows, the quenching degree $( I _ { j } )$ of C2, C4, and C5 increased with the increasing BPA concentration, whereas the counterparts of C1 and C3 remain constant around 0. Furthermore, the quenching degree was the highest for C4, followed by C5 and C2. For example, $I _ { 5 0 }$ of C1 to C5 in SDOM was 0.02, 0.78, 0.05, 3.11, and 1.54, respectively, while $I _ { 5 0 }$ of C1 to C5 in BDOM was 0.00, 1.24, 0.06, 4.12, and 1.96, respectively. These results indicated that the quenching effect of C2, C4, and C5 increased with BPA concentration increased, and there was no quenching effect of C1 and C3 by BPA at any concentration. Moreover, the quenching degree of the protein-like substance (C4 and C5) was higher than that of the humic-like substance (C2). Combined with the results in 2D-COS, it found that the quenching effect occurred earlier and more intense in the protein-like substance compared to the humic-like substance. Similar results were found in the study of the quenching effect of BPA on the extracellular polymeric substances that proteins had stronger affinity and higher priority to bind with BPA than humic acids (Yan et al. 2019).

# Environmental implications

Due to the many advantages, such as bargain price, abundant pore structure, and excellent carbon-sequestration ability (Lehmann and Ithaca York 2015), biochar was extensively used in the application of soil remediation to remove the soil pollutants. Inevitably, the feasibility of biochar applications would be greatly impacted by BDOM. The presence of BDOM would further influence the sorption behavior of the pollutants (e.g., BPA) onto soil. Information on the interaction between DOM and pollutants is thus of significance for better understanding the fates and behaviors of pollutants in soil remediation.

All results from the different methods in this study were consistent with each other which suggested that 2D-COS together with PARAFAC is useful for a quick assessment of the adsorption of BPA with SDOM and BDOM. It could help to provide more information on the mechanism of the biogeochemistry of BPA and DOM in soil, especially for the biochar application in soil. Cautions, however, the study found that biochar after releasing the DOM could increase its adsorption sites to improve the adsorption effect of BPA, and the released DOM (i.e., BDOM) could also participate in the binding with pollutants. That is, DOM should be removed before biochar is applied in soil remediation. In addition, DOM not only consists of the fluorescence fractions, but also the non-fluorescence fractions, and it had been found that non-fluorescent components also had a certain effect on the combination of pollutants (Yu et al. 2012), suggesting that the effect of non-fluorescence substances on the fate of contaminates could not be neglected in biochar application.

# Conclusions

This study investigated the different sorption properties between soil and biochar. The results of the present work demonstrated that the addition of biochar was found to significantly increase the adsorption capacity of soil for BPA. Besides, combination of multiple technologies (e.g., UV-Vis spectra, EEM spectral, and FTIR) and spectroscopic analytical methods (e.g., 2D-COS and PARAFAC) could better reveal the component species of DOM and their complexation mechanism with BPA. Qualitative analysis of the quenching sequence by 2D-COS indicated that complexation occurred preferentially in the protein-like components. Furthermore, three humic-like components (C1, C2, C3) and two proteinlike components (C4, C5) were identified by the EEMPARAFAC model. The quenching degree of protein-like components (C4, C5) was higher than that of humic-like components (C2), and it also increased with BPA concentration increased.
