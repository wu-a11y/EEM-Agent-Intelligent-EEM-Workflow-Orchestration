# a b s t r a c t

Lake Kinneret (Sea of Galilee) is one of the major water resources in Israel. The origin and characteristics of natural organic matter (NOM) in the lake and its tributary rivers were studied using fluorescence excitation emission matrices (EEM) and parallel factor analysis. Two humic-like and one proteinous components were sufficient to describe EEM variability among 167 water samples collected between 2/2005 and 9/2006. The two humic-like components showed different relations in lake and riverine samples. Their vertical distribution in the lake was affected by seasonal stratification and distance from water surface, presumably reflecting the release of humic-like matter from sediments, its production via NOM transformation in the bottom layers, and its photodegradation in the upper layers. Vertical distribution of the proteinous component, indicating biological activity at upper water layers, did not correlate with that of the humic-like components. Dissolved organic carbon concentrations did not show any vertical stratification, emphasizing the power of EEM to explore NOM dynamics.

# 1. Introduction

Lake Kinneret (‘‘The Sea of Galilee’’) is the only freshwater lake in the State of Israel and receives most of its surface water (about $70 \%$ ) from the northern Jordan River (Gvirtzman, 2002). Freshwater supplied from Lake Kinneret by the Israel’s National Water Carrier System comprises approximately $2 5 \%$ of Israel’s annual water consumption (Gvirtzman, 2002) and roughly one-third of the country’s replenishable water supply (Tal, 2006). Being a major freshwater resource, the lake water is strictly monitored by a set of physical, chemical and biological parameters (e.g. turbidity, chloride concentration, chlorophyll concentration and cyanobacteria content, biochemical oxygen demand). With respect to natural organic matter (NOM), concentrations of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and particulated organic carbon (POC) are routinely measured. However, these measurements are not informative in terms of NOM composition.

Aquatic NOM (such as riverine, lacustrian or groundwater NOM) may be produced in situ both photosynthetically by the activity of autotrophic organisms and from the transformation of present organic matter by heterotrophic organisms, or originated ex situ (cattle excreta, soil organic matter) and transported to water bodies. Characterizing the ‘‘dissolved’’ fraction of NOM (operationally defined as smaller than a certain filter pore size) is of great importance in aquatic ecology for better understanding of organic matter cycling (Cammack et al., 2004). Some NOM components may regulate light absorption, the depth of photic zones in surface water and can be involved in pH buffering of the aquatic environments (Aiken et al., 1985; Huovinen et al., 2003; Bracchini et al., 2004; Coble, 2007). The composition of NOM should reflect complex biogeochemical processes in various environmental compartments. Other possible environmental consequences associated with organic matter in water may include, in some cases, toxicity, reaction with other aquatic species, and creation of anoxic conditions in the water due to oxygen consumption (Van Loon and Duffy, 2005). NOM may be relatively recalcitrant, capable of forming water-soluble complexes with organic and inorganic contaminants thus assisting in their mobilization (Sabbah et al., 2004), affecting the bioavailability of chemicals in aqueous environment (Laor et al., 1999) and reacting with water disinfectants yielding unwanted by-products (Lu et al., 2009). Due to the complexity and heterogeneity of aquatic NOM, it is highly complicated to delineate the behavior of different components in the overall NOM dynamics.

Certain components of NOM demonstrate fluorescence activity, i.e. optical excitation by a certain wavelength triggers light emission at a longer wavelength. The fluorescence activity observed in NOM at emission wavelengths between 200 and $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ is believed to be related mainly to the presence of two important groups of NOM substances: (i) ‘‘proteinous’’ material containing tryptophan, phenylalanine and tyrosine (e.g. proteins, oligopeptides and their complexes with other components of NOM); and (ii) humic substances including both humic and fulvic acids (e.g. Senesi et al., 1991; Coble, 1996; Baker and Lamont-Black, 2001; Leenheer and Croue´, 2003; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003; Stedmon et al., 2003; Baker and Inverarity, 2004; Cory and McKnight, 2005; Coble, 2007). Bacteria and phytoplankton can also contribute to the fluorescence emission at this wavelength range (Determann et al., 1998) such that a portion of ‘‘proteinous’’ fluorescence may be associated with suspended NOM (Determann et al., 1994; Baker et al., 2007). This is in contrast to the fluorescent humic-like aquatic NOM present mostly in dissolved state (Baker et al., 2007).

Hence, fluorescence was considered in multiple studies as a sensitive tool to characterize aquatic NOM and to follow its dynamics in different aqueous environments, e.g. in rivers (Baker, 2001; Holbrook et al., 2005), estuaries (Stedmon et al., 2003), lakes (Liu et al., 2007; Mostofa et al., 2007), seawater (Coble, 1996, 2007; Jaffrennou et al., 2007) and sea ice (Stedmon et al., 2007), wastewater and effluents (Westerhoff et al., 2001; Esparza-Soto and Westerhoff, 2001; Her et al., 2003; Saadi et al., 2006; Carvalho et al., 2008), and groundwater (Kumke et al., 1999; Baker and Lamont-Black, 2001; Mostofa et al., 2007). However, it should be kept in mind that only a portion of aquatic organic matter is fluorescent. It is expected that dynamics of proteinous and humic-like fluorescent components represent important elements of aquatic organic matter dynamics (e.g. Rochelle-Newall and Fisher, 2002; Cammack et al., 2004; Yamashita et al., 2007; Coble, 2007), yet, generally, it is not known what fraction of aquatic organic matter is fluorescent in any given sample. Multiple examples of using the fluorescence spectroscopy for characterization of different natural, polluted and wastewater bodies are reviewed by Hudson et al. (2007).

In this work, the fluorescence measurements have been utilized in order to characterize aquatic NOM composition in Lake Kinneret and its catchment basin. For that, we have carried out a 20 months sampling program and analyzed the fluorescence of water samples from Lake Kinneret (at different depths and locations) and from some of the major rivers in its catchment basin. The fluorescence intensities obtained at the various excitation emission wavelength pairs were summarized in excitation emission matrices (EEMs) providing spectral fluorescent signatures (SFS) of NOM (Marhaba et al., 2000). To the best of our knowledge, this is the first SFS examination of aquatic NOM in Lake Kinneret and its catchment basin. Further, the contributions of dominating fluorescing components have been isolated from the EEMs using parallel factor analysis (Andersen and Bro, 2003; Stedmon et al., 2003; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Ohno and Bro, 2006).

By examining relationships between different fluorescing components and probing their spatial and seasonal patterns, we aimed at learning about differences between lacustrian and riverine-originated fluorescent NOM and differentiating between the various sources of organic matter in the lake. In particular, by studying vertical distribution of fluorescent organic matter in Lake Kinneret, we intended to investigate its relation to seasonal lake stratification and to look at possible processes contributing to the dynamics of fluorescent NOM such as biological production/photodegradation in the upper water layers and its transformation in the depth of the lake.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Water sampling

Lake Kinneret is a warm, monomictic lake, located at the northern end of the Afro–Syrian Rift Valley in Northern Israel, with a surface area of $1 6 8 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , a maximum depth of about $4 2 \mathrm { m }$ and a mean depth of ${ 2 4 } \mathrm { m }$ (Serruya, 1978). Lake Kinneret drains a catchment area of $2 7 3 8 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , from an altitude of ${ 2 8 0 0 } \mathrm { m }$ (Mount Hermon) down to the lake itself, located $^ { 2 0 9 \mathrm { m } }$ below sea level (Avnimelech, 1980).

Water sampling was carried out during the period between February, 2005 and September, 2006. Sampling locations are shown in Fig. 1. A total amount of 107 water samples were collected from Lake Kinneret at Station A which serves as the central monitoring station of the lake (Fig. 1; maximal depth is about $3 8 { \mathrm { - } } 4 2 \mathrm { m }$ ). Samples were collected from water depths (m) of 0 (surface water), -5, -10, -15, -20 -30 and -38. Ten water samples were collected at Station H (located near the pumping point of the National Water Carrier System) close to its maximal depth $( - 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ ; Fig. 1). At the end of May 2005, additional 28 water samples were collected from the metalimnion strata (zone of the largest temperature and oxygen gradients) along the inshore–offshore transect connecting Station A and the Kinneret Limnological Laboratory (KLL; close to the Station H; Fig. 1). This transect series of water samples was obtained at the following distances (km) from Station A (the number of different sampled water depths at each location is indicated in the parentheses): 0, at Station A (7), 4.35 (8), 5.26 (7), 5.45 (4) and 5.7 (2). Water sample collection was made using a 10 l Van Dorn water sampler.

Additional 13 water samples were obtained during the whole sampling period from the Jordan River which is the main source of surface water in the lake (sampled at Arik Bridge located about $3 \mathrm { k m }$ from the river mouth, Fig. 1). During spring 2006 (March, April, May), six water samples were collected from other lake’s tributaries, including the Dan river (three samples) and the Banias river (three samples) (Fig. 1). Additionally, three water samples were obtained during spring 2006 from the Yarmouk River which is a tributary of the Jordan River entering from south-east (Fig. 1). All water samples were stored and shipped to the lab in a cooled container. Fluorescence was measured usually within a day after sampling.

# 2.2. Water characterization

Temperature and oxygen concentration of the sampled waters were measured in situ with a YSI electrode. The concentration of suspended particles was measured gravimetrically after filtration through pre-combusted $( 5 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ GF/F filters (Tamar, Israel). Particulate organic carbon (POC) was estimated as a $50 \%$ loss on combustion at $5 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ of the suspended matter collected on the GF/F filters (Eaton et al., 1995). The protocol for POC determination was verified using a C/H/N analyzer (Perkin Elmer) for suspended solids (Eckert, 2006). Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) was measured in the GF/F filtered water with a total organic carbon analyzer (TOC1010 Analytical Instrument). Chlorophyll (Chl) concentrations were determined fluorometrically (Turner instrument; HolmHansen et al., 1965) following acetone extraction. For surface water $( 0 \mathrm { m } )$ and some water samples obtained at $- 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ , the ${ \tt B O D } _ { 5 }$ (5-days biochemical oxygen demand; Eaton et al., 1995) was also measured. UV-absorbance of water samples was measured in a $1 . 0 \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cell on a computer-controlled Cary 50 Bio UV-visible spectrophotometer.

# 2.3. Obtaining excitation emission spectra of fluorescence

Fluorescence spectra of water samples were measured in a standard $1 . 0 \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cell using a computer-controlled spectrofluorometer (Shimadzu, RF-5301PC) equipped with 150 W Xenon lamp (Ushio Inc., Japan). The average Raman peak intensity of water (at excitation/emission wavelengths of 270/ ${ 2 9 8 \mathrm { n m } }$ ) was 29.6 with standard deviation of 1.7 thus indicating the machine stability over the analysis period. Excitation emission matrices (EEM) were generated at $2 3 \pm 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , at excitation and emission slit widths of $5 . 0 \mathrm { n m }$ band pass. Fluorescence emission spectra were examined between 236 and $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $2 \mathrm { n m }$ intervals, while the excitation wavelengths were comprised between 235 and $5 9 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at 5 nm intervals (with scan time of 9 min). Spectra were not corrected for instrumental bias and may differ slightly in position and intensity from spectra collected on other instruments, however, any deviations from true spectra due to our instrumental configuration are consistent across all samples within this dataset.

To cover a wide range of fluorescent aquatic NOM including a suspended proteinous matter (Determann et al., 1994; Baker et al., 2007), aqueous samples were not filtered before analysis. Water samples were usually characterized by low UV-absorbance (less than 0.1 at any wavelength considered) due to the low organic matter concentration. Hence, following Ohno and Bro (2006), the UV-absorbances smaller than 0.1 were considered sufficient to neglect the correction factors in the EEM due to ‘‘inner filter effect’’ (i.e. light absorption by the solution itself; Lakowicz, 1999). For the fluorescence intensities included in further PARAFAC analysis, this correction not taken into account would be maximal at excitation/emission wavelengths of $2 3 5 / 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ $1 7 \%$ shorter emission wavelengths were unimportant due to the PARAFAC-assigned missing values for intensities]. At longer wavelengths, this non-accounted inner filter effect correction would be even lower (e.g. $8 \%$ of the fluorescence intensity at excitation/emission wavelengths of $2 5 0 / 2 6 5 \mathrm { n m }$ ). Typically, the pH of water samples was around 8.5–9.3 and no special pH adjustments were made before fluorescence analyses.

# 2.4. Fluorescence data analysis

For fluorescence data examination, parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was applied as described in detail previously (Bro, 1997; Andersen and Bro, 2003; Stedmon et al., 2003; Ohno and Bro, 2006). The use of PARAFAC is based on the assumption that in diluted solutions the fluorescence of one component measured at a certain pair of excitation/emission wavelengths is a tri-linear function of the concentration of this component and its specific absorption/emission properties. For a mixture of components, the overall measured fluorescence would be the sum of contributions from individual components (Bro, 1997; Ohno and Bro, 2006). By applying PARAFAC, it may be possible to obtain the number of components that control the overall fluorescence of water samples and to isolate the excitation and emission spectra of those components and their concentration scores in different samples. Components isolated using PARAFAC represent groups of substances with similar fluorescence properties rather than individual compounds. The ‘‘concentration scores’’ are not real concentrations but they are assumed to be proportional to the true concentrations of different components. In this study, the concentration scores were obtained directly from the PARAFAC analysis of measured fluorescence intensities (not normalized by Raman peak intensity of water). Actual concentrations cannot be determined because the real nature of the fluorescent components is unknown and therefore calibration curves are not obtainable. Notably, the concentration scores of different components cannot be compared. However, the examination of changes in concentration scores allows an evaluation of the dynamics in component concentrations. In this study, errors for concentration scores associated with reproducibility of spectral measurements and replicated PARAFAC fitting were about $3 \mathrm { - } 5 \%$ .

PARAFAC analyses were conducted using the Matlab toolbox for N-way PARAFAC model (Andersson and Bro, 2000; toolbox is freely available). Data preprocessing included the following steps: 1. zero emission intensities were assigned for $\lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } < \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } }$ . 2. To minimize the effect of Raleigh light scattering, emission intensities in the emission corridors $\lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } \le \lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } \le \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } + 1 6$ and $2 \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } - 1 6 \leq \lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } \leq 2 \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } + 1 6$ were considered as missing values (Bro, 1997; Andersen and Bro, 2003). 3. To account for Raman scattering effect of water, the EEM obtained for pure de-ionized water was subtracted from EEMs of studied water samples. In the PARAFAC analysis, nonnegative constraints were applied both for excitation, emission and concentration scores, considering that negative values of these parameters have no physical meaning. No constraints were applied for the shape of spectral peaks.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. PARAFAC-based evaluation of excitation emission spectra

The EEM data of all 167 water samples were included into one PARAFAC analysis aiming to determine the number of dominant fluorescing components, their spectral properties and factors related to their concentrations that could explain the whole data set. In order to estimate the number of fluorescent components, determination of residual sum of squares (RSS) and core consistency (CC) test (Bro, 1997; Andersen and Bro, 2003) were carried out. The CC values obtained were 100, 97.9, 80.7, 56.6, 38.1, when the number of components increased from 1 to 5, respectively. The decrease of RSS slowed down and the CC values began to drop when considering three and more components. Core consistency should be close to 100 and, when between 80 and 100, it is considered appropriate (Andersen and Bro, 2003). Hence, based on the RSS reduction and the CC test, three components were selected for further PARAFAC examination. Using three components, $9 8 . 1 \%$ of the fluorescence variability within 167 samples was explained. In the fitted model the standard deviation versus leverage plot was also examined (Andersson and Bro, 2000; Andersen and Bro, 2003) to detect strongly deviating and/or influencing samples and their association with the riverine set. Only one riverine sample demonstrated a significant leverage yet being within the averaged model fitting.

The selection of three components was supported also by split-half verification (Bro, 1997). For that, two randomly selected halves of the whole EEM data set were fitted with PARAFAC to yield three hypothesized components which could explain 98.1 and $9 8 . 2 \%$ , of the whole variability in the first and the second half, respectively. Excitation and emission fluorescence spectra obtained for each of the three components in both halves and in the whole set of samples are compared in Fig. 2A. Separation of the whole EEM set into two randomly selected sets of the same size did not result in a meaningful change of the spectra of each of the components thus providing additional indication that the selection of three components was reasonable. This test is also helpful to minimize the probability of arriving at a local minimum during a PARAFAC fitting (Stedmon et al., 2003).

A further validation of the three component PARAFAC model was obtained analyzing separately two subsets of data including lacustrian or riverine samples. For both subsets, three components have been selected, based on the above RSS, CC and split-half tests. In Fig. 2B, excitation and emission spectra of all three components obtained from separate analysis of lacustrian and riverine samples are compared with excitation and emission spectra of these components determined from the whole data set. There is very high resemblance between spectra obtained from the whole data set and from subsets, with some minor deviations seen for riverine subset-based spectra. This correspondence provides justification for choosing three components for describing fluorescence of aquatic NOM in Lake Kinneret and catchment basin.

Introducing a fourth component into the PARAFAC analysis resulted in an ‘‘irregular’’ emission peak at a lowwavelength excitation. Emission and excitation spectra of this component were characterized with strong dependence on whether the whole dataset or a certain random half is used in PARAFAC fitting (Fig. 3). This dependence suggests that the current data do not allow us confidently isolating more than three fluorescent components.

# 3.2. Isolated fluorescent components: possible nature and relation between concentration scores

As seen in Fig. 2A, component I demonstrates two excitation peaks (240 and $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ) and one emission peak $( 3 9 6 \mathrm { n m } )$ ). For component II, two excitation peaks are observed at 260 and $3 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , and the maximal emission is detected at $4 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . Component III is characterized by maximal excitation at $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (another excitation peak may be expected at wavelengths less than $2 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ), and the emission peak is found at $3 3 8 \mathrm { n m }$ . Following the literature compilation made by Stedmon et al. (2003), their own data on excitation/emission spectra of different groups of fluorescing substances and an operational definition given by Chen et al. (2003), component I is considered to be similar to ‘‘marine humic-like material’’. Component II is comparable to ‘‘humic material from terrestrial sources’’ (Coble et al., 1998; Chen et al., 2003; Stedmon et al., 2003). Based on these studies, components I and II are termed herein as humic-like matter or ‘‘humic’’. Component

III is likely related to the tryptophan-containing material that may be associated with a biological production and activity of microorganisms (Stedmon et al., 2003; Cammack et al., 2004; Hudson et al., 2008). The similarity found between component I and marine-type humic matter is presumably formal and only based on the correspondence in emission and excitation peak positions. It was not intended to use this similarity for suggestions about the origin of this type of fluorescent organic matter in Lake Kinneret and its catchment basin. However, it may be worth mentioning that ‘‘marine-type’’ humic matter may also be terrestrially derived or produced autochthonously in aqueous environments from terrestrial (and marine) organic substrates (Murphy et al., 2008).

In order to examine the relation between concentration scores of different fluorescent components in lacustrian and riverine environments, the whole set of samples was divided into two groups, i.e. 145 samples of Lake Kinneret and 22 riverine samples. A strong linear relationship $( r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 9 7$ s $\mathsf { l o p e } = 1 . 2 0 \pm 0 . 0 4$ ; $p \leq 0 . 0 1$ ) was found between the concentration scores of components I and II in riverine waters (obtained from the Jordan, Dan, Banias and Yarmouk rivers), regardless the sampling date (Fig. 4). Conversely, the linear correlation between the concentration scores of these two components in the Lake Kinneret samples was substantially weaker and a different slope was obtained $( r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 6 6 ;$ $\mathsf { s l o p e } = 0 . 6 3 \pm 0 . 0 4$ ; $p \leq 0 . 0 1$ ; Fig. 4). The different relations observed in Fig. 4 indicate differences in the composition of humic-like matter between Lake Kinneret and rivers. The larger slope in riverine samples as compared with samples from Lake Kinneret (Fig. 4) points to the larger fraction of ‘‘humic-like matter from terrestrial sources’’ (component II) relative to fraction of component I. No meaningful correlation between concentration scores of different humic (I, II) and proteinous (III) components was found in water samples of this study.

# 3.3. Vertical distribution of fluorescent components in Lake Kinneret

Vertical variability of fluorescent components in Lake Kinneret was examined by inspecting the concentration scores at different depths and seasons in Station A (the deep part of the lake; Fig. 5). As seen in Fig. 5, the concentration scores of all three components (I–III) varied with depth by factors of 3 at the most, at a given sampling date.

Concentration scores of both humic components (I and II) from January to April (2005–2006) did not show distinct changes with depth. However, in samples obtained from May to December (2005–2006) there was a clear vertical stratification of these fluorescent components. In this period, the concentration scores of both components I and II were relatively constant in the upper layers of the lake and then, at a depth of approximately $^ { - 2 0 \mathrm { m } }$ , they started to increase as water samples approached the bottom sediments. Proteinous matter (component III) shows less distinct and rather opposite trends in Fig. 5 as compared to humic components I and II. In the January–April period, the concentration score of component III is diminishing until approximately $- 2 0 \mathrm { m }$ and then remains constant. As opposed to components I and II, no clear stratification was observed for this component in the May– December period.

It is known that Lake Kinneret water is usually completely mixed and homothermal from January to March and becomes thermally stratified during the rest of the year (Serruya, 1978). Lake stratification is evident from the vertical distribution of water temperature and oxygen concentration measured in Station A (Fig. 6). Additionally, chlorophyll concentrations (not shown) demonstrated a vertical distribution similar to that observed for temperature and oxygen.

Lake stratifications as derived from the fluorescence of humic components I and II (Fig. 5) were similar but not identical to those derived from temperature and oxygen profiles (Fig. 6). For example, humic fluorescence (i.e. concentration scores of components I and II) does not show any vertical gradient in the February–April period. On the other hand, the thermocline is present in April, and the oxygen gradient seems to appear in February (for instance, at 27.02.05, Fig. 6).

Multiple processes could contribute to the vertical distribution of fluorescent substances in the lake. During stratification, humic-like substances produced by NOM transformation in the hypolimnion and/or released from the lake bottom sediments could contribute to the greater humiclike concentration in deep water layers (Fig. 5). The oxidizable organic carbon content in surface sediments was reported to vary between $4 \mathrm { - } 5 \%$ in the center of the lake and $2 \mathrm { - } 3 \%$ in the northwestern coast (Serruya, 1971). Photodegradation is another important mechanism affecting the vertical distribution of humic components in the stratified water body and reducing their relative concentration in upper water levels (Momzikoff et al., 1994; Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Determann et al., 1996; Chen et al., 2002; Nieto-Cid et al., 2006). During complete lake mixing, the vertical stratification of humic substances disappears.

Biological production in the upper water layers of the completely mixed homothermal lake could be responsible for some increased concentrations of a proteinous matter. A concurrent release and consumption of proteinous matter in the summer months might result in the apparent lack of concentration variability of component III with depth (Fig. 5).

Vertical distribution of fluorescent components in Lake Kinneret depicts changes in its organic matter that cannot be envisioned from DOC concentrations. As seen from Fig. 6, there were no gradients in the DOC vertical distribution in any of the sampling events at Station A. It had been already shown that vertical and seasonal variability of DOC concentrations in Lake Kinneret is relatively small (Parparov et al., 1998; Berman et al., 2004).

Similarity might be expected between the vertical distributions of humic components I and II observed in Station A and in other locations across the lake. In Fig. 7 the concentration scores obtained in Station A are plotted together with the scores obtained at different locations and depths along the inshore–offshore transect connecting Station A and the KLL (May, 2005). The concentration scores along the transect follow the overall vertical trend established for Station A, despite the fact that maximal depths (and, therefore, distances of examined water layers from sediments) along the transect are different (and change from $\mathtt { - 4 0 }$ to $- 1 2 . 3 \mathrm { m } )$ ). This similarity suggests that the trends in Fig. 7 are affected by the distance from water surface rather than by the distance from sediments. As proteinous component III did not show a clear stratification in Station A in the May–December period (Fig. 5), no comparison with the transect samples was made.

This hypothesis on the role of the distance from water surface in vertical distribution of humic components is also supported by Fig. 8 where the concentration scores of all three components at Station H ( $- 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ ; close to its maximal depth) were found to be very similar to their concentration scores at the same depth at Station A. In contrast, no similarity was found when the two stations were compared based on the water layers close to the sediments (from $^ { - 3 8 }$ and $- 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ , in Stations A and H, respectively). This critical role of distance from the water surface may indicate the significance of organic matter photodegradation in upper water layers and/or could be related to oxidation–reduction conditions affected by oxygen concentration.

# 3.4. Testing relations between selected water properties and concentration scores of fluorescent components in Lake Kinneret

The presence of different fluorescent components in Lake Kinneret water samples was examined in relation to oxygen, chlorophyll, POC, DOC and $\mathtt { B O D } _ { 5 }$ . Considering all sampling dates, locations and depths in the lake data set, there were no strong linear relationships between a concentration score of a component and any single variable. Of all trends, the highest (positive) linear correlation was obtained between the concentration scores of humic component II and ${ \tt B O D } _ { 5 }$ (with $r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 5 2$ , statistically significant at the $p \leq 0 . 0 1$ level; not shown).

Due to distinct differences between vertical distributions of components in the January–April and the May–December seasons (Fig. 5), the whole data set was divided into two respective seasonal subsets. By examining the May–December season (when the humic components are stratified in the lake), only slightly better (but negative) linear correlations were obtained between the concentration scores of the humic components I and II and the oxygen concentration $( r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 5 4$ and 0.64, respectively; significant at $p \leq 0 . 0 1$ ; not shown). In the January–April season (when the proteinous component III shows a certain vertical distribution), the concentration scores of component III demonstrated statistically significant (at $p \leq 0 . 0 1 $ ) positive linear correlations with POC and ${ \tt B O D } _ { 5 }$ $( r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 5 8$ and 0.65, respectively; not shown).

![](images/51ba237dffedde358f7aaae72e005a03769e46799a48b03c275ebccb0ffb4e33.jpg)  
Fig. 8 – Concentration scores of components I–III at Station H $\bf \Pi ( - 1 0 m )$ plotted against their concentration scores at Station A $( - 1 0 \mathbf { m } )$ . All comparable sampling events are included. The line represents a 1:1 relationship.

In these examinations, a correlation between concentration scores and water properties was somewhat linked to a variable water layer depth. Hence, the concentration scores of the three fluorescent components were tested also against water properties (oxygen concentration, concentration of chlorophyll, POC, DOC and ${ \tt B O D } _ { 5 }$ –when applicable) in a given location (A and H) at a constant depth, when varying the sampling date. In the water layers between 0 and $- 2 0 \mathrm { m }$ , the strongest statistically significant (at the $p \leq 0 . 0 1$ level) negative linear relation characterized with $r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 5 5$ was obtained between the concentration scores of component I and oxygen concentration (at the depth of $- 2 0 \mathrm { m }$ ; Fig. 9). Slightly stronger correlations between concentration scores and oxygen concentration were found in deeper water layers (-30 and $^ { - 3 8 \mathrm { m } }$ ; Fig. 9). As seen in Fig. 9, the strength of the best obtained linear associations between variables is low. It is obviously seen also in Fig. 9 that a substantial variability of concentration scores persists, for example, at zero oxygen concentration.

Although oxygen may play an important role in NOM transformation and humic-like matter production (Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Yamashita et al., 2007), it was hardly possible to find a meaningful relation between the concentration scores of the fluorescent components with a single water parameter, including the oxygen concentration.

# 3.5. Seasonal variability in concentration scores of fluorescent components

Seasonal variability in concentration scores of fluorescent components is presented in Fig. 10 for water sampled in the Jordan River (Arik Bridge) and at different depths at Station A in Lake Kinneret. Similar trends are seen for seasonal fluctuations of humic (I, II) and proteinous (III) components in water samples from Jordan River and the bottom water layers at Station A $\cdot - 3 0$ and $^ { - 3 8 \mathrm { m } }$ ; Fig. 10). In water layers of Lake Kinneret closer to the surface, a seasonal biological activity associated with a tryptophan-containing matter (III) could result in dissimilarity of temporal changes observed for concentration scores of humic (I, II) and proteinous (III) components.

Upper water layers of Lake Kinneret are the photic zone where autochthonous biological material is produced continuously. Hence, the development of biological activity in the photic zone of Lake Kinneret is a possible reason for an enrichment of upper water layers with proteinous matter (III) relatively to humic components (I, II) (as compared with bottom water layers and the Jordan River). This relative enrichment can be demonstrated by examining the changes in relation between concentration scores of proteinous and humic-like components.

Indeed, the concentration scores of a proteinous matter (III) in the Jordan River are lesser than the concentration scores of humic components (I, II) (Fig. 10). This specific relationship between concentration scores of different components cannot be interpreted alone as a ratio of concentrations. However, further change in this relationship reveals relative dynamics of concentrations of these components.

![](images/188316bb7e562e108c5fe81f111b3eb96f9bd3facf5ecba8379e2f8952b1efe7.jpg)  
Fig. 9 – Station A: linear regressions of concentration scores versus oxygen concentration.

In the bottom water layers of the lake, typically the proteinous matter scores are less or comparable with humic concentration scores. However, in the upper water layers (Fig. 10), proteinous matter (III) appears to show greater concentration scores than humic-like matter (I, II). Thus, when compared with Jordan River and bottom water layers in the lake, the increased proteinous/humic concentration score ratio in upper water layers in Lake Kinneret indicates the relative enrichment with proteinous matter (III).

![](images/345869a2427a3a1c294fab60ba92277a760da0a40c9b796b8b91fcf6dff9553d.jpg)  
Fig. 10 – Seasonal changes in concentration scores of three fluorescent components (I–III) in the Jordan River (Arik Bridge) and at Station A of Lake Kinneret at different depths (indicated in meters). Breaks in plots appear for missing sampling events.

Markedly, concentration scores of all three components in the water layer near sediments $( - 3 8 \mathrm { m } )$ tend to increase from February to December, i.e. in months when Lake Kinneret is most of the time thermally stratified. This increase mostly occurred under anoxic conditions (the oxygen concentration between May and December at $^ { - 3 8 \mathrm { m } }$ depth is close to zero; Fig. 6) and is suggestive for the release of fluorescent matter from sediments to the lake and/or its production via NOM transformation near sediments. Between December (2005) and February (2006), there was a partial disappearance of fluorescent components from the water layer close to sediments $( - 3 8 \mathrm { m } )$ that appears to correlate with lake mixing in this period.

# 4. Conclusions

1. Based on parallel factor analysis of fluorescence EEM, the variability, and therefore spatial and seasonal patterns of fluorescent organic matter in water sampled from Lake

Kinneret and rivers including its catchment basin (Jordan River, Dan, Banias) and Yarmouk are sufficiently characterized by using two humic-like and one proteinous component.   
2. A difference in the composition of humic-like matter between Lake Kinneret and rivers is evidenced from the different relations between the concentration scores of two dominant humic components in lacustrian and riverine waters.   
3. Vertical distributions of humic-like components in Lake Kinneret are indicative of seasonal lake stratification. When the humic-like matter stratification is established, the concentration of humic-like substances is greater in the bottom water layers. At the layer closest to the sediments, it increases also with time (at anoxic conditions) thus linking humic-like matter production to NOM transformation in the bottom water layer and/or to its release from sediments.   
4. Depth-related distribution of humic-like components appears to be similar in different lake locations thus: (i) indicating the important role of a distance from the water surface in the vertical distribution of humic-like matter; and (ii) supporting a possible influence of photodegradation on concentrations of humic-like components in the upper water layers.   
5. Vertical distribution of humic-like components does not correlate with the distribution of a proteinous component in Lake Kinneret. The latter is indicative of a biological activity at upper water layers in the January–April period.   
6. Fluorescence-derived concentration scores of different components provide a quantitative measure for changes in NOM composition and its dynamics in the lake, in contrast to the slightly variable DOC concentrations.
