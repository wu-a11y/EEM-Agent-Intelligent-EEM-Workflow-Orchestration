In this study, ideal mixing behaviors of the three commonly-used source tracking tools, which include fluorescence spectroscopy, stable isotope ratios, and lipid biomarkers, were evaluated in controlled particular organic matter (POM) mixtures of two contrasting end members e.g., soil and algae. In parallel, three different extraction methods based on water (WEOM) and two alkaline solutions were compared to identify the preferential pretreatment option that leads to a good performance of the spectroscopic source tracking indices on the dissolved organic matter (DOM) extracted from the POM. Based on three evaluation criteria concerning the linearity of the relationships, the discrimination sensitivity, and the conservative mixing behavior of fourteen tested indices, the fluorescence proxies such as the relative distribution of the fluorescence components and the modified fluorescence index YFI of the WEOM were found to be reliable and robust indices for POM source tracking. The carbon stable isotope ratios of the POM samples exhibited an ideal mixing behavior even after the DOM extraction, while the biomarkers of sterols/stanols did not present a good linear and conservative behavior at similar conditions. In addition, the WEOM was identified as the preferred extraction method for the application of spectroscopic indices for POM source discrimination. This study provides a guideline to select the optimum indices for the POM source discrimination via the end member mixing analysis.

# 1. Introduction

In aquatic systems, natural organic matter (NOM) can be categorized into two major sources: (i) autochthonous and (ii) allochthonous origins. Autochthonous organic matter (OM) is produced by heterotrophs, phytoplankton and other photosynthetic organisms, which comprise of detritus and the organic carbon excreted by photosynthetic organisms or by heterotrophic organisms, such as microzooplankton and mesozooplankton (Tulonen, 2004). Allochthonous OM, which is derived from vascular plants and/or soil materials, is exported from the upstream catchment into rivers and lakes. Among the allochthonous OM sources, soils represent the largest active cycling pool of terrestrial carbon (C). Over two-thirds of C in the terrestrial biosphere is stored as soil organic matter, which originates from plants, animals, and microbial sources (Tfaily et al., 2017). It is presumed that the NOM found in aquatic systems can be represented by the mixtures of different end members, such as the autochthonous and the allochthonous OM, at varying ratios and/or the by-products from the biogeochemical processes.

On the other hand, the NOM is commonly divided into two fractions that are based on the filtering, which are the dissolved organic matter (DOM, a fraction $<$ the filter pore sizes ranging from 0.2 to $0 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m } \ i$ and the particulate organic matter (POM: unfiltered fraction). The exchanges between both forms occur readily and often by various mechanisms, such as the adsorption/desorption, the aggregation/dissolution, and the dissolution/precipitation (He et al., 2016), which impacts a range of biogeochemical processes and/or systems, such as the carbon storage, the ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ release, and the coupling of the terrestrial and the marine systems (Cole et al., 2007; Tranvik et al., 2009). The POM in freshwater and coastal systems is mainly derived from primary production, which includes: the phytoplankton, the bacteria, the invertebrates, the detrital particles, and the zooplankton fecal pellets, (Briand et al., 2015; Volkman and Tanoue, 2002) but it also receives terrestrial inputs from land. The POM is a complex organic carbon pool with various origins, which interacts to a large degree with the DOM fraction. Therefore, the source discrimination for the POM samples can provide valuable information to understand the dynamics of the NOM in aquatic environments.

Several source tracking tools have been utilized to identify the OM sources, which include the stable carbon isotopes, the molecular biomarkers, and the fluorescence proxies (Aiken, 2014; Bianchi and Canuel, 2011; Briand et al., 2015). The carbon stable isotope ratio $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C } )$ has been considered to be one of the most effective and reliable methods for tracking the sources and the transformation processes of NOM (Gao et al., 2012; Lambert et al., 2011; Ogrinc et al., 2005; Toming et al., 2013). The molecular biomarker approach also provides a powerful tool for sources discrimination (Bianchi, 2011; Bianchi and Canuel, 2011; Derrien et al., 2017b; Zhang et al., 2017). Among the number of the molecular biomarkers, the sterols and the stanols are taken into account as reliable biomarkers for assigning the sources of the OM in several environments (Derrien et al., 2015; Martins et al., 2007; Rontani et al., 2014; Volkman, 1986). Meanwhile, the fluorescence spectroscopy is recognized to be a relevant source tracking tool, which has been an excellent alternative to the conventional tools mentioned above due to the minimal labor and time for sample pre-treatments, the quick analysis, and the low cost for measurements (Chen et al., 2003; Lambert et al., 2016; Meng et al., 2013; Yao et al., 2011).

All of these tools are widely utilized to track the OM sources in many aquatic environments, such as wastewater, rivers, groundwater, lakes, rainwater, and oceans (Affouri and Sahraoui, 2017; Derrien et al., 2018; Lambert et al., 2016; Li et al., 2015; Retelletti Brogi et al., 2018). Many of the related studies are based on a conservative mixing behavior of these tools (e.g., their accuracy for the linearity of the relationship between measured indices and mixing ratios of different sources), without considering the potential limited applicability. The conservative mixing behavior is critical to validate the accurate assignment of the OM sources for the given samples as well as to understand the carbon flux in the given environments from the perspective of a global or a local carbon cycling. Unfortunately, the conservative nature of the source tracking tools has not been rigorously examined yet for the POM despite the importance. Only a few studies mentioned the source assignment issue regarding the non-conservative behavior of the fluorescence indices or the biomarkers (Lee et al., 2018; Shah et al., 2007; Stedmon and Markager, 2003; Yang and Hur, 2014). Moreover, for the proper utilization of the fluorescence-based source discrimination indices for POM samples, which require the measurements for DOM, the DOM fraction extracted from the particulate samples needs to be validated for its representativeness, and the procedure should be well established for universal usage.

In this context, this study mainly aimed to evaluate the conservative nature of the three most frequently used source tracking tools, which are the fluorescence spectroscopy, the stable isotope ratios, and the lipid biomarkers, in controlled POM mixtures of two contrasting end members at different mixing ratios. In parallel to this main goal, three different OM extraction methods were compared for the POM mixtures to find out the preferred pretreatment options resulting in the best source discrimination based on the fluorescence spectroscopy. Although the OM extraction is commonly performed with ultra-pure water, the alkaline extraction has been recognized as a suitable choice due to its higher extraction percentage of organic carbon from the POM (Molina, 2013; Osburn et al., 2012). As a result, the specific objectives of this study were (i) to compare the pre-treatment performance of the different extraction solvents on the isolation of the DOM fraction from the POM mixtures that are suitable for source tracking using the fluorescence spectroscopy, (ii) to examine the conservative/nonconservative behaviors of the three source discrimination tools in the controlled POM mixtures, and (iii) to evaluate the applicability of these tools for the POM source tracking in natural environments.

# 2. Material and methods

# 2.1. Sampling and preparation of samples

The soil sample, which is defined as an Entisol based on USDA soil taxonomy (Kwon et al., 2016), was collected at Bukhansan National Park $\langle 3 7 ^ { \circ } 4 3 ^ { \prime } 3 7 . 0 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N }$ $1 2 7 ^ { \circ } 0 0 ^ { \prime } 5 0 . 9 ^ { \prime \prime } .$ ) in South Korea. The top soil $( 0 { - } 1 0 \ \mathrm { c m } )$ ) sample was collected using a shovel from an area $3 2 5 ~ \mathrm { { m } }$ above the sea level in a fully forested area. The forest is mainly composed of broadleaved trees such as Quercus mongolica, Q. serrata, the coniferous tree, Pinus densiflora, and the Robinia pseudoacacia plantation (Choi and Yang, 2012). The commercial unicellular green algae (Chlorella vulgaris), which is commonly found in lakes and ponds in South Korea, was purchased from Aquanet Co., Ltd. in Gyeongsangnam-do, South Korea. The collected soil was homogenized, dried in an oven at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ and sieved using a $2 . 0 \mathrm { m m }$ mesh in order to prepare an allochthonous POM end member. The algae sample was freeze-dried and sufficiently ground for further analysis.

The two end members, which were the soil and the algae, were mixed at the soil to algae ratios of 1:0, 9:1, 3:1, 1:1, 1:3, 1:9, and 0:1 respectively, on the basis of their organic carbon (OC) concentrations (Table S1). It can also be expressed in the percentages of end members as follow: 100:0, 88.9:11.1, 66.6:33.3, 50:50, 33.3:66.6, 11.1:88.9, and 0:100. The mixing experiment was conducted in triplicate.

# 2.2. OM extraction experiments

Three different methods were used for the OM extraction from the POM mixtures, which included the water extraction, the alkaline extraction, and the alkaline extraction with sodium pyrophosphate $( \mathrm { N a } _ { 4 } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 } 0 _ { 7 } ) ,$ ). The water extraction was chosen because it is the most commonly used method to obtain a representative DOM fraction from the POM samples. The alkaline extraction is also widely adopted due to its high extraction efficiency, but it has a tendency to preferentially extract relatively high molecular weight molecules, such as humic substances (HS) (Hur et al., 2014; Molina, 2013). The third choice was a modified version of the conventional alkaline extraction, in which a neutral salt of a complexing agent was added to minimize the undesirable features such as organic matter alterations and chemical changes (Lehmann and Kleber, 2015; Stevenson, 1982). An example of this is ${ \mathrm { N a } } _ { 4 } { \mathrm { P } } _ { 2 } { 0 } _ { 7 } .$ A benefit of using salts for the DOM extraction lies in their abilities to precipitate or complex the polyvalent cations, such as Ca(II), Fe(III) and Al(III). This action can maintain the OM bound to the minerals or in a flocculated and insoluble condition, which facilitates the solubilization of the OM (Molina, 2013). The water extractable organic matter (WEOM) was based on ultrapure water (Barnstead™ Easypure™ RoDi, Thermo Scientific). The alkaline extractable organic matter (AEOM) was obtained with a $0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { N }$ NaOH solution, while the modified AEOM was prepared using a mixing solution that consisted of NaOH and ${ \tt N a } _ { 4 } { \tt P } _ { 2 } { \tt O } _ { 7 }$ (sodium pyrophosphate) with a 1:1 ratio at $0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ M ~ }$ . The latter alkaline extract is denoted here as ASPEOM. The following procedures were the same for the three extraction methods.

The POM mixtures were briefly soaked in the extraction solvents at a solid-to-liquid ratio of 1:20, and were then shaken for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ before centrifugation (5000 rpm for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ ). Both alkaline extracts (i.e., the AEOM and the ASPEOM) were passed through cation exchange resin (Dowex 50WX8-100, Sigma) to remove any salt that possibly existed in the extracts. Finally, all the extracted solutions were filtered through a precombusted and pre-washed GF/F filter (Whatman). The filtered extracts were used to test the applicability of the optical source discrimination for the POM.

# 2.3. Measurements of the spectroscopic indices of the extractable OM

The dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations were measured using a Shimadzu V-CPH TOC analyzer. The absorption spectra were scanned from 200 to $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $0 . 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ -interval using an ultravioletvisible (UV–vis) spectrometer (Shimadzu UV-1300). The fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) were obtained with a luminescence spectrometer (Hitachi F7000, Japan) following the procedure previously described by Derrien et al. (2018). A total of 63 EEMs were collected for the PARAFAC modeling, and it was processed using MATLAB R2013b (Mathworks, USA) with a DOMFluor toolbox (www. models.life.ku.dk). The maximum fluorescence intensities $\left( \operatorname { F } _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } \right)$ of the identified components were adopted to estimate their relative abundance $( \% )$ and ratios.

The modified fluorescence index (or YFI) (Heo et al., 2016), the humification index (HIX) (Zsolnay et al., 1999), and the biological index (BIX) (Huguet et al., 2009) were calculated as fluorescence-based source discrimination indicators. The measured indices are the most frequently used for DOM source tracking. Detailed information on the methods is described in Text S1.

# 2.4. The carbon stable isotope analysis of the POM fraction

The vapor acidification method was applied for the POM samples on the GF/F filters to remove the inorganic carbon before the carbon stable isotope analysis (Carabel et al., 2006). The total organic carbon (TOC) and the carbon stable isotope ratio $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C } )$ were measured using an elemental analyzer that was coupled with an isotope ratio mass spectrometer (EA-IRMS; EuroEA-Isoprime IRMS, GV Instruments, UK). The stable isotope ratios were calculated using the standard notation:

$$
\delta ^ { 1 3 } C ( \% ) = \left( \left( R _ { s a m p l e } / R _ { R e f e r e n c e } \right) - 1 \right) \times 1 0 0 0
$$

where, R is the corresponding ratio of $^ { 1 3 } { \mathsf { C } } / { } ^ { 1 2 } { \mathsf { C } }$ . The standard reference material was Vienna Pee Dee Belemnite (VPDB). The analytical precision was $0 . 0 5 \text{‰}$ .

# 2.5. The sterols/stanols analysis of POM fraction

The lipid fraction of the POM mixtures was extracted with a solvent mixture of dichloromethane/methanol (DCM/MeOH; $9 / 1 , \nu / \nu ,$ ) using an accelerated solvent extractor (ASE 200, Dionex) at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 1000 psi (Kim et al., 2017). The total lipid extract was fractionated using solid/ liquid chromatography (aluminum oxide column) to isolate the polar fraction (Gal et al., 2018). After derivatization, the samples were analyzed by gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS) using an Agilent 7820A GC equipped with a HP-5 ms capillary column (from Agilent, length $= 3 0 \mathrm { m }$ , diameter $= 0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , film thickness $= 0 . 2 5$ $\mu \mathrm { m } .$ ) and an Agilent 5977E MSD mass spectrometer with an electron impact ionization energy of $7 0 \mathrm { e V } .$ . More details on the injection and quantification are provided in Text S2.

# 2.6. Conservative mixing relationship and statistical analyses

In order to assess the applicability of the different source discrimination indices for the POM, several evaluation criteria were established. First, the relationships of the individual indices with an increasing abundance of the algae-end member in the POM mixtures were examined for the measured values. The relationships were considered “linear” if the p-value of the coefficient of determination $\mathtt { R } ^ { 2 }$ values were $< 0 . 0 1$ The regression slope of the measured values $( \mathsf { S } _ { \mathrm { m } } )$ was used to evaluate the sensitivity of the source discrimination. In addition, the regression slope of the measured values $( \mathsf { S } _ { \mathrm { m } } )$ was normalized by the standard deviation of the measurement $( \mathsf { S } _ { \mathrm { m } } / \mathsf { S D } )$ in order to consider any effects of the inherent uncertainties of the indices on the sensitivity and to sharpen the evaluation criteria. It is noteworthy that a higher regression slope associated with a lower standard deviation would generate a stronger capability of the index to discriminate sources (Yang and Hur, 2014). A value of the $\mathsf { S } _ { \mathrm { m } } / \mathsf { S D }$ superior equal to 1.0 was considered as an acceptable sensitivity with the source discrimination relative to the measurement uncertainty. Meanwhile, the predicted values were calculated using the end member's values and the mixing ratios under a linear conservative mixing assumption. The differences $( \% )$ between the predicted and the measured values of the selected indices were then calculated in percentage values (Table S4). Finally, the slopes for the measured $\left( { { \cal S } _ { \mathrm { m } } } \right)$ and the predicted $( \mathsf { S } _ { \mathsf { p } } )$ values were compared, and the percent difference (%difference) between both were calculated to evaluate the deviation. A %difference of ${ < } 1 0 \%$ was considered to be an acceptable deviation for the conservative mixing relationship.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Spectroscopic indices of extractable OM for POM mixtures

3.1.1. The identification of the fluorescent components from the EEMPARAFAC

Four different components were identified, which included one humic-like component (C1) and three protein-like components (C2– C4) (Fig. S1 and Table S3). All the identified components were consistent with those previously reported and/or well matched with the Open Fluor database with similarity scores of ${ \tt > } 0 . 9 2 0 $ . Component 1 (C1), which had a maximum at 220/446 nm (Ex/Em), can be assigned to a typical humic-like component found in water samples (Guéguen et al., 2014; Jørgensen et al., 2011). Component 2 and 3 (C2 and C3), which exhibited the maxima at 220 (275)/350 nm $\left( { \mathrm { E x / E m } } \right)$ and 220/ $3 0 2 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , respectively, can be associated with protein-like components (Nimptsch et al., 2015; Osburn et al., 2012; Osburn et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2018) and/or amino acid-like components, which are free or bounds in proteins. (Murphy et al., 2006; Murphy et al., 2008). Component 4 (C2), peaked at 230,260/368 nm (Ex/Em), was reported as a protein-like or tryptophan-like fluorophore that likely had microbial origins (Graeber et al., 2012; Williams et al., 2010).

3.1.2. Spectroscopic characteristics of extractable OM for end members

There were clear discrepancies in the measured spectroscopic indices between the two end members (i.e., soil and algae) (Table 1). In general, the soil end member was characterized by a high proportion of the humic-like component (C1: 54.3 to $6 8 . 6 \%$ , while the algae end member contained an enriched protein-like component $( \mathsf C 2 + \mathsf C 3 + \mathsf C 4 )$ with the combined relative abundances ranging from ${ \sim } 8 4$ to $9 8 \%$ . The two end members also exhibited distinct differences in other spectroscopic indices (Table 1). The measured index values fell within the ranges previously reported (Ly et al., 2017; Zsolnay et al., 1999). The largest differences between the two end members were found with the HIX. Soil samples are usually characterized by high HIX values $( > 6 ,$ , indicating a significant humic content (Zsolnay et al., 1999)), which is probably due to the composition of lignin-derived materials and other plant residues that undergo long humification processes (Kramer et al., 2004). In contrast, algae usually presents a low HIX $( < 4 )$ value, which has been used as a signature of aquatic and/or biological origins (Zsolnay et al., 1999). The low YFI (\~0.6) values in the soil end member can be explained by a high abundance of humic substances, which were much higher in the algae end member (Table 1). Similarly, the soil end member showed a lower BIX and higher SUVA values than the algae counterparts did (Derrien et al., 2017a; Huguet et al., 2009).

By comparing three different extraction solvents, the larger differences with the relative abundances of the fluorescent components were observed in the WEOM samples between the two end members compared with the AEOM and the ASPEOM samples (Table 1). For example, the WEOM of the algae end member was characterized by a very low distribution of the humic-like component (C1: $0 . 6 \%$ ), while the relative abundance of the C1 was substantial $( > \sim 1 3 \% )$ for both the alkaline extracts (i.e., the AEOM and the ASPEOM). In addition, the relative distributions of the individual protein-like components highly depended on the extraction types for both the end members. The most abundant protein-like component in the WEOM (C2: $6 6 . 6 \pm 0 . 3 \%$ was found to have low relative abundances of $8 . 5 \pm 1 . 9$ and $9 . 0 \pm 4 . 8 \%$ in the AEOM and the ASPEOM samples, respectively, among the four components. Such differences between the different extraction methods were also found for the component's ratios (Table 1). The largest differences were observed for the ratios of the $C 2 / C 4$ and the $C 3 / C 4$ in the algae end member, which were above 58 in the WEOM, but below 5 in both the alkaline extracts. Notable differences were also observed in the ratios of the C1/C3 and the C2/C3 between the WEOM and both the alkaline extracts for the soil end member (Table 1).

The non-PARAFAC indices, which were the SUVA, the YFI, the HIX, and the BIX, were also largely affected by the extraction types. The four indices showed substantial differences in the values for the algae end member between the WEOM and the two alkaline extracts. Meanwhile, in the soil end member, only the SUVA and the HIX presented a distinction between the two types of solvents. For instance, the HIX values for the soil end member were $1 3 . 9 \pm 0 . 1$ in the WEOM, $7 . 3 \pm$ 0.1 in the AEOM, and $8 . 9 \pm 1 . 1$ in the ASPEOM, while the YFI exhibited similar values among the three extraction methods, which were $0 . 5 \pm$ 0.0 for the WEOM and $0 . 6 \pm 0 . 1$ for the alkaline extractions.

# 3.1.3. The changes in the spectroscopic indices with varying end member mixing ratios

Variations in the relative distributions of the fluorescent components with algae increasing in the end member were compared for three different extraction methods (Fig. 1). Two types of relationships were found with a similar pattern for both the alkaline extracts. The WEOM samples exhibited linearly increasing trends in the $\% C 2$ and the $\% C 3$ with a higher portion of algal end member and linearly decreasing tendencies in the $\% C 1$ and the $\% C 4$ (Fig. 1a). The coefficients of determination $( \mathbb { R } ^ { 2 } )$ values of the relative abundances were all higher than 0.95 (p-value ${ \bf \ } { \bf < } 0 . 0 1$ ) (Table 2). In contrast, no such linear relationships were exhibited for both the alkaline extracts (Fig. 1b and c), in which the extractable OM samples presented similar values at the end member mixing ratios of 9:1, 3:1, 1:1, 1:3, and 1:9 (soil: algae). Alkaline solvents have been recognized to intensify the preferential extraction for specific types of compounds, such as high molecular weight molecules (e.g., HS), which may result in lowing the representativeness of the original POM samples (Molina, 2013). It may also alter the inherent nature of the OM by dissolving the protoplasmic and the structural components of the fresh organic tissues and/or by creating chemical changes of the condensation between the amino acids and the aldehydes or quinones (Molina, 2013; Stevenson, 1982). Most of all, excessive leaching of the HS from the POM samples, through a strong extraction, may overshadow the unique source proxies for the autochthonous POM, which is enriched with the non-HS constituents.

# 3.1.4. The selection of the optimum spectroscopic indices for the POM source discrimination

In order to assess the applicability of the different source discrimination indices for the POM, several evaluation criteria were established. All these evaluation criteria are presented in Table 2. Among the three extraction methods, the indices that satisfied two or the three evaluation criteria were only found for the WEOM (Table 2), which indicated that the water-based extraction may be the most appropriate method for utilizing the spectroscopic indices to track the different POM sources in the present case, such as the two contrasting end-members. Among the 14 spectroscopic indices of the WEOM, only $\% C 1$ , $\% C 3$ , and the YFI exhibited a high performance by satisfying the threshold values of the three evaluation criteria (i.e., $\mathtt { R } ^ { 2 }$ with p-value $< 0 . 0 1$ , $\mathrm { S } _ { \mathrm { m } } / \mathrm { S D } > 1 . 0$ , and $\%$ difference $< 1 0 \%$ ; Table 2). The components ( $\% C 2$ , and $\% C 4$ ), the ratios of the components (C1/C2, C2/C4, and C3/C4) and the non-PARAFAC index, HIX, can be also suggested as good indices in terms of the relationship linearity as they satisfied 2 on the three criteria. Among these successful indices, the sensitivity of source discrimination was greater in the following order of the YF $( 2 . 2 1 ) \% \mathrm { C 1 }$ $( 1 . 6 7 ) > \% \mathrm { C } 3$ (1.39), followed by $\% C 2$ $( 0 . 9 4 ) > \mathrm { C } 1 / \mathrm { C } 2 ( 0 . 5 6 ) > \% \mathrm { C } 4 ( 0 . 3 4 ) > \mathrm { H } \mathrm { X } ( 0 . 2 4 ) > \mathrm { C } 2 / \mathrm { C } 4$ and $\mathsf { C 3 } /$ C4 (0.22) which did not satisfy this specific criteria based on the regression slopes normalized by the standard deviation of the measurements (i.e., ${ \cal S } _ { \mathrm { m } } / { \cal S } \mathrm { D }$ ). The lowest degree of the deviation from the conservative mixing behavior was found for $\% C 4$ with a %difference of 0.4, followed by HIX (%difference $= 2 . 2$ ), $\% C 3$ (3.3), YFI (5.9), $\% C 1$ (6.0), C/C2 (6.4), $\% C 2$ (6.5) and finally ${ \cal C } 2 / { \cal C } 4$ and $C 3 / C 4$ (7.4 and 7.3, respectively). From the combined results, it can be concluded that the relative abundance of the C1 and the C3, each of which was the most enriched component in the soil and the algae end members, respectively, and the YFI values in the WEOM samples represented the best optical proxies for the POM source discrimination with varying end member mixing ratios.

# 3.3. The Sterol/Stanols distribution in the POM mixtures

# 3.3.1. The characteristics of the two end members

The soil end member was characterized by abundant amounts $( >$ $5 0 \%$ ) of terrestrial sterols, such as sitosterol, stigmasterol, and campesterol (Table 1), which agreed with the previous literature (Martins et al., 2007; Volkman, 1986). The associated products, which included sitostanol, 24-ethylepicoprostanol, and stigmastanol, were also detected in substantial amounts $( 9 . 9 \% , 5 . 7 \%$ , and $6 . 8 \% ,$ respectively). Cholesterol, the most ubiquitous sterol (Leeming and Nichols, 1998), and fecosterol, a fungi or lichens sterol biomarker (Debieu et al., 1992), were present at ${ < } 1 0 \%$ (e.g., $8 . 2 \%$ and $8 . 9 \%$ , respectively). In contrast, the algae end member was characterized by a high abundance of green algae sterol biomarker (e.g., ergosterol) and the occurrence of dinosterol (i.e., a biomarker for dinoflagellates) (Volkman, 1986, 2006), which corresponded to ${ \sim } 6 5 \%$ of the sterol compounds in the distribution. Stigmasterol, fecosterol, and campesterol were also detected in the algae end member with the percent abundances of $1 5 . 2 \% ,$ $1 1 . 2 \%$ and $9 . 5 \%$ , respectively. Although these sterols are recognized as terrestrial and/or vascular plant biomarkers, their presence in algae was also previously reported (Fabris et al., 2014; Volkman, 1986). There were not any stanols detected in the algae end member.

# 3.3.2. The changes of the sterol/stanol distribution in the varying end member mixing ratios

The relative distributions of the individual sterol/stanol compounds in the corresponding bulk lipid groups are shown with the increasing algal carbon composition in the POM mixtures in Fig. 4. The increasing trends were generally observed for the ficosterol, dinosterol and the ergosterol with a substantial fluctuation. In fact, ergosterol was the only compound presenting a linear relationship according to established criterion $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 8 3 0$ with the p-value of ${ \bf \ } { \bf < } 0 . 0 1$ ). The other sterols did not show a consistent variation with the increase of the algal carbon abundance, which may be attributed to their occurrence in the OM samples from both origins (Volkman, 1986) or potential inherent variability during the multiple-step pretreatment process (e.g., incomplete or selective extraction) that might be related to the nature of the samples. There were not any consistent trends of the stanol compounds by increasing the algal carbon in the POM mixtures. Among the sterols/ stanols examined, campesterol, 24-ethylepicoprostanol, and sitostanol exhibited an acceptable deviation range from the conservative mixing behavior as indicated by the %difference (slope) of ${ < } 1 0$ (Table 2). Unfortunately, none of these identified compounds satisfied the criterion regarding the relationship linearity (i.e., p-value $< 0 . 0 1$ ), which suggests that the sterols/stanols may not be a good quantitative biomarker for tracking the sources of the POM samples in a context of mixing. This result was consistent with a prior study by Shah (2007), who tested the capacity of the fecal sterol biomarker to differentiate the OM sources in the mixed fecal samples with different origins and reported the difficulties to accurately identify the relative contributions of the sources. The disability of the abundances of the biomarker in linearly tracking end-member contribution could be related to various factors including production biases, differential rates and times of degradation, and specificity or non-specificity of certain compounds (Bianchi and Canuel, 2011, Poynter and Eglinton, 1991, Rontani et al., 2014, Volkman, 1986).

# 3.4. Implications to the applicability of the source tracking tools for the POM samples

In this study, the discrimination capacities of the commonly used source tracking indices and tools were compared using the controlled POM samples with known mixing ratios of the two contrasting OM sources. To the best of the authors' knowledge, it is the first study that explored the conservative mixing behavior of the indices/tools for POM samples. The extraction methods for using spectroscopic indices were also compared for their feasibility as the pretreatment options for the POM source discrimination. Yang and Hur (2014) studied variations of a number of spectroscopic proxies during controlled mixing processes of the two end members in DOM (Suwannee River fulvic acid and algal DOM) to compare their efficiencies of the source discrimination for the DOM with mixed sources. The reported coefficients of determination, $\mathtt { R } ^ { 2 }$ , of the linear regression relationships $\mathrm { ( R ^ { 2 } > } 0 . 9 7 8 \mathrm { ) }$ were much higher than those examined here for the POM. Using spectroscopic indices to characterize the DOM fraction extractable from POM samples may raise concern about the representativeness of the fraction compared to the total OM in the original samples. This can be attributed to (1) the restricted analytical window of the chromophoric/fluorescent DOM for the total OM pool, and (2) the potential modification of the extraction on the OM properties, such as the preferential extraction (Derrien et al., 2017b; Provenzano et al., 2010). However, the results of this study demonstrated that several spectroscopic indices, such as the relative distribution of the fluorescent components the most enriched in each end member, and the YFI, could still operate as robust indicators that were applicable for source apportionment to the POM samples in the WEOM. The carbon stable isotope ratios showed its strong capacity to identify and estimate the source contribution even in the POM left after the extraction of the DOM fraction, while the lipid biomarkers failed in its proper operation in the controlled POM mixtures. It should be noted that the presented results, which were based on a limited number of POM sources (i.e., soil and algae), do not necessarily signify the exclusive uses of the optimum indices/tools suggested in this study. The reason is that the real POM samples likely contain a variety of different sources, and they may undergo many biotic and abiotic processes (Yang and Hur, 2014), which warrants further studies for validation. At least in the present case of a controlled two end-members mixing experiment, $\delta { } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C } _ { \mathsf { I } }$ the relative abundance of the different EEM-PARAFAC components, and the YFI optical index could be suggested as reasonable source tracking indices with the most conservative behavior and the highest sensitive source discrimination capability.

# 4. Conclusions

The applicability of three commonly used source tracking tools (i.e., fluorescence spectroscopy, stable isotope ratio, and lipid biomarker) was tested for controlled POM mixtures of two contrasting end members at different mixing ratios. Based on three evaluation criteria concerning the linearity of the relationships, the discrimination sensitivity, and the conservative mixing behavior, the fluorescence proxies such as the relative distribution of the fluorescent components and YFI index of the WEOM were found to be reliable indices for POM source tracking. Carbon stable isotope ratios exhibited a linear conservative relationship with varying mixing ratios of end members for the POM samples even after DOM extraction, demonstrating its strong capacity for identifying the source's contribution. The biomarkers of sterols/stanols did not present a good linear and conservative behavior at similar conditions. In addition, this study identified the preferred extraction method for the application of the spectroscopic tool to POM source discrimination and the appropriate optical indices for the extracted DOM (i.e., WEOM). This study was performed in controlled and limited conditions as opposed to natural environments. Hence, further studies need to be performed to fully validate these results.
