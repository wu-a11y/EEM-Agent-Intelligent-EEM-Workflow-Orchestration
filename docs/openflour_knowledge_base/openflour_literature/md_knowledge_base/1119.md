Particulate organic matter (POM) in the coastal zone plays an important role in global carbon cycle, yet its spectral properties under human disturbance are not well understood. This study investigated the source and dynamics of POM in a typical coastal area (Minjiang Estuary, China) under the influences of estuarine mixing, urbanization and dam construction, based on spectral and isotopic analysis. The yield of particulate organic carbon from the watershed $( 0 . 3 8 { \bf { g } } \mathrm { { m } } ^ { - 2 } { \bf { y } } { \bf { r } } ^ { - 1 } )$ was in part limited by the historical decrease in sediment discharge downstream hydropower station. The absorption and fluorescence indices of POM suggested an important contribution from biological production with a low humic content. The importance of river and estuarine production was supported by elemental and isotopic analysis, in particular for N-bound constituents of POM. Three humic-like and two protein-like fluorescent components were identified from POM using fluorescence excitation-emission matrices-parallel factor analysis (EEMs-PARAFAC). They showed weak correlations with salinity, suggesting non-conservative behaviors of fluorescent POM during the estuarine mixing. Principal component analysis (PCA) identified two principal factors, which were related to the level of chromophoric POM (PC1) and biological production (PC2), respectively. The PCA results indicated stronger influence of biological production in the estuarine zone and potential effect of sediment re-suspension and/or wastewater discharge in the urban zone. Overall, our results demonstrated significant influences of human disturbance on the flux, source and dynamics of POM in the coastal zone, which could be assessed by spectral and stable isotopic analysis.

# 1. Introduction

Particulate organic matter (POM) is an important component in global carbon cycle. The deposition and burial of particulate organic carbon (POC) in estuaries and coastal margins account for ${ \sim } 9 0 \%$ of global oceanic carbon sink (Hedges and Keil, 1995; Hedges et al., 1997; Bauer et al., 2013). Annually, ${ \sim } 0 . 2 \ \mathrm { P g }$ of POC is discharged by global rivers to the ocean, which is partly buried depending on a series of factors such as the respiration of terrestrial POC during its transport and deposition, and the retention of fluvial POC in reservoir after the impoundment of hydropower station in many rivers worldwide (Ludwig et al., 1996; Dagg et al., 2004; Syvitski et al., 2005; Blair and Aller, 2012). Autochthonous sources such as freshwater and marine phytoplankton production also contribute to POC in the river-estuary-ocean continuum, although the autochthonous POC is generally more susceptible to biodegradation before the final burial than allochthonous

POC (Schlunz and Schneider, 2000; Goñi et al., 2005). Both terrestrial and autochthonous POC in coastal environments are affected notably by human activities such as land use, wastewater discharge and dam construction, since a large proportion of global population live in the coast (Bauer et al., 2013). Therefore, it is important to study the biogeochemistry of POC in coastal environments under the impacts of freshwater-seawater mixing and anthropogenic disturbance, for better understanding the carbon cycle.

Elemental and stable isotopic composition of carbon and nitrogen (i.e., N/C, $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ and $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N } )$ are popular tools for determining the source of POM in aquatic environments (Hedges et al., 1997; Middelburg and Herman, 2007; Goñi et al., 2005). For example, POM from terrestrial C3 plants typically has lower N/C, $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { : }$ , and $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ than that from phytoplankton production (Meyers, 1997; Middelburg and Nieuwenhuize, 1998; Lamb et al., 2006). Absorption and fluorescence spectroscopy are widely used for studying the source, chemical composition and environmental behaviors of dissolved organic matter (DOM) in different waterbodies globally (Coble, 2007; Fellman et al., 2010; Guéguen et al., 2011; Chen and Jaffé, 2014; Derrien et al., 2017). Recently, these spectroscopic techniques have been introduced to characterize POM in coastal environments, which have the potential of discriminating POM from different sources with different chemical composition (Osburn et al., 2012, 2019; Yang et al., 2013a; Brym et al., 2014; He et al., 2016). In particular, fluorescence excitation-emission matrices coupled with parallel factor analysis (EEMs-PARAFAC) can provide information for investigating the dynamics of different fluorescent components (Osburn et al., 2012, 2015; Brym et al., 2014; Yang et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2019). However, the information on absorption and fluorescence spectroscopic characteristics of POM in human-disturbed estuaries is still lacking, although spectral analysis could provide a series of indices for tracking the source and behaviors of POM in the dynamic coastal zone.

Minjiang River is a large subtropical river in SE China with a length of $5 7 7 \mathrm { k m }$ , which covers a watershed area of $6 0 , 9 9 2 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ . The annual freshwater discharge of Minjiang River is $5 . 3 4 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ in 2017, which ranks third in Chinese rivers (http://www.mwr.gov.cn/). The local climate is characterized by warm and humid summer but dry and wet winter. The land cover of the watershed is dominated by forestland $( \sim 7 4 \%$ , Bai et al., 2015). However, the lower Minjiang River flows through Fuzhou City with a population of 7.66 million, and the biogeochemistry in the lower river-estuarine zone may be affected by the urban (Fig. 1A). The construction of Shuikou hydropower station at the middle reach of Minjiang River $8 4 \mathrm { k m }$ upstream from the Fuzhou City) strongly modifies the hydrology and biogeochemical processes of the river since 1993, which results in a notable decrease in the sediment discharge (Fig. 1B). Therefore, the lower Minjiang River-Estuarine zone is a typical area for studying the impacts of human activities on the spectral properties of POM. This study aimed to: (1) examine the absorption and fluorescence properties of POM in the lower Minjiang River-Estuarine zone; (2) identify the source of POM using spectroscopic indices, elemental and stable isotopic composition of carbon and nitrogen; and (3) test the influences of estuarine mixing, urban and dam on the spectral properties of POM.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Sampling, elemental and isotopic analysis of carbon and nitrogen

Surface water samples were collected from 18 stations in April, July, October and December 2017, which covered a broad range of salinity from 0 to 31.2 (Fig. 1). April and July were representative of the wet season (monthly rainfall: 194 and $2 2 4 \mathrm { m m }$ , respectively), while October and December were representative of the dry season (monthly rainfall: 25 and $1 1 \mathrm { m m }$ , respectively, Fig. S1). Salinity was measured in-situ with a handled Thermo Scientific Orion Star A Meter. Water samples were filtered through pre-combusted $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ GF/F filters, and the particles on the filters were stored at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ before further analysis.

The collected particles on filters were dried at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , and the concentration of total suspended matter (TSM) was calculated by dividing the dry particle weight with the filtered water volume. The dried samples were then used for elemental and isotopic analysis following the procedures described previously (Wu et al., 2007; Li et al., 2015). Briefly, samples were placed in desiccators with HCl vapor for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ to remove inorganic carbon, and were dried again for measuring POC and $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ . Particulate nitrogen (PN) and $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ were measured for dry samples without acidification. POC, $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C } .$ , PN and $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ were measured using a coupled elemental analysis-isotopic ratio mass spectrometer (Thermo Delta V advantage). The concentrations of POC and PN (in mg $\mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ ) were corrected for the filtered water volumes, while the percentages of POC and PN in TSM (in $\%$ ) were calculated by dividing their concentrations with TSM. $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ and $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ were calculated as:

# 2.2. Absorption and fluorescence measurements

Absorption and fluorescence spectra of base-extracted POM (BEPOM) were measured for characterizing chromophoric and fluorescent POM (i.e. CPOM and FPOM), following the method described in Osburn et al. (2012). Previous studies showed that ${ \sim } 3 8 \%$ of POC could be extracted with $\mathrm { N a O H }$ , and the extraction efficiency was independent of sampling location and seasonality, although there was a moderate but not significant correlation between the extraction efficiency and salinity $( r = - 0 . 5 2$ , $p = 0 . 0 9 7$ , Brym et al., 2014). Briefly, the collected particles were dried at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , and POM was extracted with $0 . 1 \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ NaOH solution at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in dark for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ . Then, the BEPOM solution was neutralized with $1 \mathrm { m o l L } ^ { - 1 }$ HCl solution, and was filtered through pre-cleaned $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate filters for optical measurements.

Absorption spectra of BEPOM over $2 4 0 { - } 8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ were scanned using a Techcomp 2600 UV–Vis spectrometer, with Milli-Q water as the blank (Yang et al., 2017). The measured spectra were baseline-corrected by subtracting the average absorbance over $7 0 0 { - } 8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and then the absorption coefficient of CPOM at $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ $\left( a _ { \mathrm { C P O M } } ( 3 0 0 ) \right)$ was calculated as an index for the level of CPOM:

$$
a _ { \mathrm { C P O M } } ( 3 0 0 ) = 2 . 3 0 3 A ( 3 0 0 ) / l ,
$$

where A(300) and l were the absorbance at $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and the path length, respectively (Morel and Smith, 1982). $a _ { \mathrm { C P O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ was then corrected by multiplying the volume sum of NaOH and HCl solutions added in the extraction and neutralization processes and dividing the filtered water volume. The spectral slope over 275–295 nm $( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ was calculated following the procedure described previously as an index for the average molecular weight of organic matter (Helms et al., 2008).

Fluorescence EEMs of BEPOM were scanned using a Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer over excitation/emission $\left( \mathrm { E x / E m } \right)$ wavelengths of 240–450/280–600 nm, following the procedure described previously (Yang et al., 2013a, 2013b). EEMs were corrected for inner-filter effects based on absorption spectra (McKnight et al., 2001) and subtracted the blank signals of Milli-Q water. Then, each EEM was corrected by multiplying the volume sum of NaOH and HCl solutions in the extraction and neutralization processes and dividing the filtered water volume (Osburn et al., 2012).

A total of 63 EEMs were modeled with PARAFAC in MATLAB 7.0 using the DOMFluor toolbox 1.7 (Stedmon and Bro, 2008; Yang et al., 2017). The number of PARAFAC components was determined based on split-half validation, and the fluorescence intensity $\left( F _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } \right)$ of each component was used to represent its level in Raman Unit (RU; Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Humification index (HIX) and biological index (BIX) were also calculated from EEMs, which were indices for the humic content and source of organic matter respectively (Zsolnay et al., 1999; Huguet et al., 2009). HIX was the ratio of fluorescence over Em $4 3 4 { \mathrm { - } } 4 8 0 { \mathrm { n m } }$ to that over $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 6 \mathrm { n m }$ (at $\operatorname { E x } 2 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ ), and BIX was the ratio of fluorescence at $\mathrm { E m } 3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that at $4 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (at $\operatorname { E x } 3 1 0 \mathrm { n m } )$ .

Correlation analysis, principal component analysis and t-test for comparing different groups of samples were carried out in SPSS Statistics 19.

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Export flux of POC from the Minjiang River: potential impacts of dam construction

The concentration and percentage of POC $( 0 . 1 1 { - } 2 . 5 7 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ and $0 . 4 5 \% - 3 . 1 8 \% $ ) in this study were comparable with those for rivers worldwide $\left( 0 . 2 6 { - } 1 9 1 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \right.$ and $0 . 3 \% { - } 1 0 \%$ , Ludwig et al., 1996; Dagg et al., 2004; Wu et al., 2007; Huang et al., 2012). The mean percentage of POC in TSM was $2 . 3 3 \% \pm \ 0 . 6 9 \%$ for the freshwater samples (salinity $< 1$ , $n = 1 3 ,$ ) in this study. Thus, the POC flux of Minjiang River was roughly estimated to be $2 . 3 0 \pm 0 . 6 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } { \mathrm { g } } { \mathrm { y r } } ^ { - 1 }$ , by multiplying the freshwater POC percentage in TSM and the sediment flux of $9 . 8 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 1 } { { \bf g } } { \bf y } { \bf r } ^ { - 1 }$ in 2017 (http://www.mwr.gov.cn/). The export flux of POC was $4 2 \%$ of the dissolved organic carbon flux from the Minjiang watershed $( 5 . 4 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } { \bf g } { \bf y } { \bf r } ^ { - 1 }$ in 2017, Yang et al., 2019). Correspondingly, the POC yield of Minjiang watershed was $0 . 3 8 { \bf { g } } { \bf { m } } ^ { - 2 } { \bf { y } } { \bf { r } } ^ { - 1 }$ in 2017, which was comparable with those for rivers worldwide $( 0 . 1 - 1 4 . 7 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { y } \mathrm { r } ^ { - 1 }$ Ludwig et al., 1996; Huang et al., 2012).

The POC flux in this study was 1–3 orders lower than the global large rivers $( 0 . 1 7 { - } 1 3 . 0 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 2 } { \mathrm { g y r } } ^ { - 1 }$ , Dagg et al., 2004). It should be noted that the long-term average sediment flux of Minjiang River $( 5 . 9 9 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 2 } { \mathrm { g y r } } ^ { - 1 }$ during 1953–2015) is 6.08 folds of that in 2017, which would result in a much higher long-term average POC flux comparable with some of world large rivers (e.g., $1 . 7 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 1 } { { \bf g } } { { \bf y } } { { \bf r } } ^ { - 1 }$ from the Yenisey River, Dagg et al., 2004). Therefore, the current export of POC from the Minjiang River is largely affected by the dam construction and the hydrological condition, which would further affect the carbon cycle in the dynamic downstream coastal zone.

# 4.2. Source of POM in the lower Minjiang River-Estuarine zone: assessed with isotopic and spectral analysis

The input from different sources is an important factor determining the chemical composition, biogeochemical reactivity and environmental effects of POM in freshwater and ocean (Hedges et al., 1997; Schlunz and Schneider, 2000; Bauer et al., 2013). The PN concentration correlated strongly with that of POC with a positive intercept of POC at zero PN $( r = 0 . 9 6$ , $p \ : < \ : 0 . 0 0 1$ , Fig. 2A), indicating most PN was bound within POM with neglectable particulate inorganic nitrogen (Goñi et al., 2005). Thus, N/C was used together with $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ and $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ for identifying the source of POM. The values of $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } ,$ N/C and $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ for POM in the river (M01–M02), urban (M03–M11) and estuarine (M12–M18) zones of this study were compared with the values for typical POM sources reported previously (Hedges et al., 1997; Meyers, 1997; Middelburg and Nieuwenhuize, 1998; Kendall et al., 2001; Goñi et al., 2005; Lamb et al., 2006; Wu et al., 2007; Fig. 7). The biplot of $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ and N/C was used for assessing the source of POC (Fig. 7A), while $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ provided further information for the source of POM constituents containing nitrogen (Fig. 7B). The results showed that POM in this study originated mainly from terrestrial C3 plants, soil, river and estuarine plankton, and marine sources, while terrestrial C4 plants probably had limited contribution. The estuarine samples were characterized by higher $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ than the freshwater samples, which was consistent with a significant correlation between $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ and salinity $( r = 0 . 8 5 , p < 0 . 0 1$ , Table S1) and indicated a transition of POC source from terrestrial end-members or freshwater plankton to estuarine/ marine plankton. Another notable feature was the higher $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ values of most samples than terrestrial sources, suggesting an important contribution of river and estuarine sources to PN. Phytoplankton and bacteria can utilize the residual ammonium which is isotopically enriched after the preferential uptake of $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { N }$ by nitrifiers (Middelburg and Nieuwenhuize, 1998; Middelburg and Herman, 2007). In addition, nitrogen from wastewater has a high $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ of $1 0 \text{‰}$ (McKinney et al., 2001) and the uptake of this nitrogen by aquatic plankton can contribute to the elevated $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ in POM (Cole et al., 2004). The influence of wastewater is largely dependent on the distance to the pollution source (Savage et al., 2004), and the N/C and $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ of wastewater are close to those of terrestrial C3 plants and soil (Andrews et al., 1998).

The contribution of autochthonous sources to POM in this study was further indicated by the absorption and fluorescence characteristics. On one hand, the absorption spectra of BEPOM were characterized by a shoulder peak around $2 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Figs. 3A, S2). A similar shoulder peak has been identified previously for CDOM from algae and zooplankton (Hulatt et al., 2009; Xie et al., 2012) and for BEPOM in the Neuse River Estuary (Brym et al., 2014). This peak was probably related to aromatic compounds such as tryptophan, tyrosine and C5 in this study, which had absorption maximum at around $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and could be derived from biological production (Brym et al., 2014). $ { S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } }$ were high for freshwater samples $( 0 . 0 2 3 3 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 0 4 1 \mathrm { { n m } ^ { - 1 } } )$ ) and increased to even higher values in the estuary (up to $0 . 0 4 1 7 \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ ), in particular in April and July (Fig. 3C; Table S2). This was mainly caused by the presence of shoulder peak around $2 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and probably indicated increasing autochthonous contribution in the estuary (Helms et al., 2008; Spencer

On the other hand, HIX and BIX are popular fluorescence indices for the humic content and source of DOM, respectively. HIX is high $( > 1 6 )$ for strong humic character and is low $( < 4 )$ for lowest humic content (Zsolnay et al., 1999; Huguet et al., 2009), while BIX is $< 0 . 7$ for important terrestrial contribution and is $> 1$ for biological/aquatic bacterial origins (Huguet et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2017). Low values of $\mathrm { H X } < 1$ and high values of BIX ${ \sim } 0 . 9$ have been reported recently for BEPOM derived from a natural phytoplankton assemblage (Osburn et al., 2019). The values of HIX $( 2 . 6 8 ~ \pm ~ 1 . 5 1 )$ and BIX $( 0 . 8 1 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 2 0 )$ in this study suggested an overall low humic content of BEPOM with a significant contribution from biological/aquatic bacterial sources. HIX was generally lower while BIX was higher in the estuarine zone than in the freshwater (HIX: $3 . 2 4 ~ \pm ~ 1 . 5 4$ vs. $1 . 9 6 ~ \pm ~ 1 . 1 1$ ; BIX: $0 . 7 0 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 1 2$ vs. $0 . 9 5 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 2 1$ ; independent-samples t-test, $p \ < \ 0 . 0 1$ ; Fig. 4). HIX generally decreased while BIX increased with salinity, in particular in April and December (Table S2). This indicated an increase in the autochthonous contribution to BEPOM with a low humic content in the estuarine zone, which was consistent with the elevated $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ and $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ . It is noteworthy that the isotopic parameters are indices for the source of bulk POM while spectral properties provide information on the chromophoric and fluorescent constituents. However, both isotopic and spectral techniques suggested an important contribution of autochthonous source which increased in the estuarine zone in this study. Thus, the results indicated that the two different techniques could be used in combination for providing comprehensive information on the source of POM.

# 4.3. Dynamics of POM in the river-estuarine continuum: influences of estuarine mixing and urban

The parameters of POM showed notable variations in the Minjiang River-Estuarine continuum. The values of POC, $a _ { \mathrm { C P O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ , and $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1–C5 were lowest at the river site M01 during all sampling periods (Figs. 6, S2, S4–5). This was partly related to the retention of particles and associated organic matter in reservoir after impoundment, as discussed above in Section 4.1. These parameters all increased to higher values in the urban freshwater zone than at M01. This was probably related to the sediment re-suspension as indicated by the increasing TSM (Fig. 1C) and the significant correlations between TSM and POM parameters in freshwater (Table 1). Wastewater discharge from the urban was an additional source of POM. The residential, agricultural and industrial activities in the Fuzhou City used $3 . 1 3 \times 1 0 ^ { 9 } \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ of water in 2017, which was approximately $6 \%$ of the freshwater discharge from Minjiang River (http://slt.fujian.gov.cn). The importance of wastewater might be limited by the dilution effect in the large Minjiang River. The Dazhang Stream was another source of POM in the urban zone (Fig. 1), accounting for $6 \%$ and $9 \%$ of the freshwater discharge and sediment flux from the Minjiang River, respectively (http://www.mwr.gov.cn/). The TSM at station M05 of this tributary $( 3 4 - 6 7 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 } )$ was close to that at M04 of the mainstream $\left( 1 3 \mathrm { - } 5 3 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 } \right) .$ ), but was lower than the elevated values in the estuarine zone (up to $> 2 0 0 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ , Table S5).

The behaviors of POM were assessed with the relationships between POM parameters and salinity. POC and PN showed no significant correlation with salinity, while they were dependent moderately on TSM $( r ; ~ 0 . 5 7  – 0 . 6 9$ , $p \ : < \ : 0 . 0 1$ , Table S1). The $a _ { \mathrm { C P O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ and C1–C5 also correlated weakly to moderately with salinity in the estuarine zone (Table 1; Fig. S6), which might be partly related to possibly lower extraction efficiency for saline samples (Brym et al., 2014). These results suggested they were non-conservative and were affected by biogeochemical processes in the estuarine zone. For example, the behaviors of POM might be affected by sediment re-suspension and coastal erosion in the dynamic estuarine zone (Fig. 1C), which could be forced by tidal energy and/or energy transferred from surface by strong wind (Andutta et al., 2019). The Minjiang Estuary is dominated by strong semi-diurnal tides with a typical tidal range of $> 4 \mathrm { m }$ (Fig. S7), which could lead to notable re-suspension due to the interaction between tidal current and bottom. The coastal zone is also affected by winds between land and ocean, although no storm event occurred in our sampling periods. In addition, a turbidity maximum zone was present in the Minjiang Estuary, which might be related to the tidal current and the particle flocculation/aggregation when freshwater and seawater mixed and the ion strength changed (Fig. 1C, Zhao et al., 2012). The highest TSM in the turbidity maximum zone could contribute to the elevated levels of POM and CPOM (Yang et al., 2013a). Finally, POM in the estuarine zone could be affected by phytoplankton production, which was supported by the elevated $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } , \delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ , BIX and low HIX as discussed in Section 4.2.

The dynamics of BEPOM in the river-estuarine continuum were further revealed with PCA based on optical parameters. Two principal factors were identified (PC1 and PC2), which explained $4 8 . 5 \%$ and $3 2 . 2 \%$ of total variance, respectively (Fig. 8A). PC1 correlated strongly with $a _ { \mathrm { C P O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ , C1–C3 and C5, indicating this factor represented the levels of CPOM, humic-like and tyrosine-like components. PC2 correlated positively with C4, BIX and salinity, suggesting this factor was associated with tryptophan-like component and biological production of autochthonous POM. In addition, HIX for the humic content correlated positively with PC1 but negatively with PC2. BEPOM in April was characterized by low PC2 scores $< 0$ for all samples, indicating a low autochthonous fraction (Fig. 8B). This was consistent with the lowest C4 and BIX but highest HIX in April among all months (Table S4). In contrast, the PC2 score was elevated in the estuarine zone during other three months, indicating a significant addition of tryptophan-like component (Fig. 8C–E). This is consistent with the elevated C4 in the estuarine zone in July, October and December (Fig. 6B; Table S4). BEPOM in many urban sites showed higher PC1 scores than the upstream river sites (M01–M02) in December, suggesting elevated $a _ { \mathrm { C P O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ , C1–C3 and C5 in the urban zone. In addition, the PC2 score was higher in urban sites than upstream sites in October, indicating higher C4 and BIX. These results could be explained by the downstream increase of TSM due to sediment re-suspension (Fig. 1C; Table 1) and/or the input of organic matter from wastewater discharge in the urban. However, the difference between urban and upstream sites was less evident in July, likely due to weak re-suspension in this investigation and/or a dominant impact of dilution effect under higher freshwater discharge.

# 5. Conclusions

The export flux of POM was probably lowered by the hydropower station in the middle reach of Minjiang River, which might partly explain the low POC, $a _ { \mathrm { C P O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ and C1–C5 at the river site M01. Spectral indices (e.g., $ { S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } }$ , HIX, and BIX) and isotopic composition of POM further indicated an important contribution of aquatic production. The quantitative indices of POM (POC, PN, $a _ { \mathrm { C P O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ and $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1–C5) correlated weakly with salinity in the Minjiang estuarine zone, indicating non-conservative behaviors of POM in the estuarine zone. The correlations of optical properties and POC, PN, TSM in the freshwater indicated the influence of sediment re-suspension. PCA analysis provided further information on the difference of POM in different zones, with higher autochthonous contribution in the estuarine zone and elevated scores for several urban sites likely due to sediment re-suspension and/or wastewater discharge. Overall, the combined application of spectroscopic, elemental and isotopic analytical techniques can provide comprehensive information on the changes of POM under human disturbances such as urbanization and dam construction.
