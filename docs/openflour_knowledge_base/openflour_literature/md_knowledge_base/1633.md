# a b s t r a c t

Biodegradation is one of the main processes causing the changes in amount, composition and properties of organic matter in sediment and water-sediment interface. The degradation processes of sediment organic matter lead to a release of dissolved organic matter (DOM) into the pore water via hydrolysis and depolymerization of particulate organic matter (POM). Whereas the pore waters represent a reactive zone in sediment closely linked to biogeochemistry of the substrate, they are still poorly characterized under diagenetic processes. In this study, we examined the DOM alterations in sediment pore water originating from the mixtures of two contrasting POM sources at known ratios through controlled incubation experiments with two different oxygen conditions (i.e., oxic versus anoxic). The changes in pore water DOM were tracked using fluorescence and absorbance spectroscopy and size exclusion chromatography. The results based on the sediments artificially mixed of two end-member sources (e.g., soil and algae) showed that the most affected parameter during the biodegradation process was the amount of the pore water DOM. It was also demonstrated that the changes in the spectroscopic and molecular properties were more dependent on the POM sources and the mixing ratios rather than on the oxygen conditions. This study provides insights into the responding features of DOM in a reactive sediment zone as pore water to the main biogeochemical process.

# 1. Introduction

# 2. Material and methods

In aquatic environments, natural organic matter (NOM) is described as a heterogeneous mixture of particles and molecules having variable physical (e.g. size and porosity) and chemical (e.g. functional group content and solubility) properties (Baldock et al., 2004). Sources of NOM are multiple and diverse, which greatly increase the complexity of the chemical properties. They are commonly categorized into two major origins: (i) autochthonous and (ii) allochthonous sources. Autochthonous organic matter (OM) is composed of aquatic detritus and organic carbon derived from photosynthetic organisms or by heterotrophic organisms, such as microzooplankton and mesozooplankton (Tulonen, 2004). On the other hand, allochthonous OM is produced from vascular plants and/or soil materials and exported from upstream catchment into rivers and lakes. Among the external OM sources, soils represent the largest active cycling pool of terrestrial carbon (C). It is presumed that NOM commonly encountered in aquatic systems can be simplified as the mixtures of different end members, encompassing the autochthonous and the allochthonous OM, at varying ratios and/or their by-products from biogeochemical processes.

In the water column, sediments operate as both sources and sinks of NOM. The large reservoir for reduced carbon in sediments is a key factor in numerous biogeochemical cycles (Cole et al., 2007; Schmidt et al., 2017). Pore waters represent a reactive zone with a continuous flux of dissolved organic matter (DOM) from sediments (Fox et al., 2018; Herzsprung et al., 2006; Reader et al., 2019). It often contains a higher amount of DOM by orders of magnitude than that of the overlying water, and its composition is closely linked to the biogeochemical processes occurring in sediments (Herzsprung et al., 2006; Marchand et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2013). Previous studies that examined the chemical composition and reactivity of pore water DOM have highlighted the heterogeneity of this pool and the complexity of its dynamics within the sediment column (Alperin et al., 1994; Burdige, 2002; Burdige and Komada, 2015; Henrichs and Farrington, 1979; Sansone and Martens, 1982). The amounts and the characteristics of pore water DOM are greatly influenced by biogeochemical processes such as biodegradation as well as by the status of oxic-anoxic interfaces in sediments and the mixed OM sources (Brune et al., 2000). To the best knowledge of the authors, effects of biogeochemical processes on sediment pore water DOM have been rarely examined under controlled experimental settings, which can ensure a trustworthy approach to identify the individual effect of several involving factors (Luek et al., 2017; Valle et al., 2018). In addition, comparing degradation-induced changes in the molecular/chemical properties of pore water DOM for oxic versus anoxic conditions may provide a better understanding of the effects of redox conditions on the pore water DOM characteristics truly found in natural systems.

In this framework, a controlled degradation experiment was designed at laboratory scale in this study using organic-rich sediments artificially composed of two contrasting POM sources (e.g., two end members) at known mixing ratios. To track the alterations in the pore water DOM during the biodegradation process, two DOM analytical tools (i.e., fluorescence spectroscopy and size exclusion chromatography coupled with organic carbon detector (SECOCD)) were employed. The main goal was to examine the chemical and molecular variations of the pore water DOM in sediments with different OM sources at aerobic and anaerobic degradation conditions. This work specifically aimed to answer the following five questions: (i) What pore water DOM characteristics are affected by biodegradation processes? (ii) Are the changes dependent upon the OM sources of sediments? (iii) Does the availability of oxygen affect the degradation-induced changes? (iv) Which is the prevalent factor shaping the spectroscopic and molecular characteristics during biodegradation process: the mixing ratios of sources or the oxygen condition?

# 2.1. Experimental settings, incubation experiments, and pore water DOM sampling

Two end members (i.e., soil and algae), as the major organic sources of sediments, were mixed in the soil to algae ratios of 100:0, 75:25, 50:50, 25:75, and 0:100, respectively, on the basis of organic carbon (OC) concentrations, not their masses, after the OC contents of the two end members were taken into account. The exact OC concentrations are provided elsewhere (Derrien et al., 2019). Briefly, top soil $( 0 - 1 0 \mathrm { c m } )$ sample was collected at Bukhansan National Park $( 3 7 ^ { \circ } 4 3 ^ { \prime }$ $3 7 . 0 ^ { \prime \prime }$ N $1 2 7 ^ { \circ } 0 0 ^ { \prime } 5 0 . 9 " $ ) in South Korea. A commercial unicellular green alga (Chlorella vulgaris), which is commonly found in lakes and ponds in South Korea, was purchased from Aquanet Co., Ltd. in Gyeongsangnam-do, South Korea. The collected soil was homogenized, dried in an oven at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ and sieved using a $2 . 0 \mathrm { m m }$ mesh, while the algae sample was freeze-dried and ground before incubation (Derrien et al., 2019). Incubation experiments were performed in pre-washed and pre-combusted ( $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 \mathrm { h }$ ) $1 2 5 \mathrm { m L }$ Wheaton $\textsuperscript { \textregistered }$ amber glass bottles with Teflon screwcaps. Samples for anoxic incubation were prepared and sampled using sterile Aldrich $\textsuperscript { \textregistered }$ AtmosBag two-hand glove bag under nitrogen atmosphere. A mass $( 1 2 { \mathrm { g } } )$ of the end-member mixture samples were mixed with ultrapure water (Barnstead™ Easypure™ RoDi, Thermo Scientific) to obtain a solid to solution ratio of 1:4 (mass) and equilibrated for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ in the dark at room temperature after shaking at $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ for $1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . River water from Jungnang river in Seoul $( 3 7 ^ { \circ } 4 0 ^ { \prime } 1 6 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { \Delta N }$ , $1 2 7 ^ { \circ } 0 4 ^ { \prime } 4 7 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { ~ E ~ }$ ) was used as inoculum for microbial incubation. The collected water sample was first passed through a GF/ C (pre-combusted, $1 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore-sized). Then, $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ of inorganic nutrients $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 }$ and $\mathrm { K _ { 2 } H P O _ { 4 } }$ ) was added to the filtered inoculum for sufficient microbial growth, and the solution was kept at room temperature at slow shaking ${ \mathrm { } } ^ { \prime } 8 0 { \mathrm { ~ r p m } } )$ for 2 days. The solution was covered by aluminum foil to protect the solution from light to avoid the growth of algae. All samples were spiked with $3 \% ( \mathrm { v / v } )$ of the prepared inoculum, and a sufficient amount $( 1 \% , \mathrm { v / v ) }$ of nutrients $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 }$ and $\mathrm { K _ { 2 } H P O _ { 4 } } ,$ ) at $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ was added to each sample to avoid nutrient limitations during the incubation. The low inoculum-to-sample ratio had a negligible effect on the final concentrations of the end member mixtures $( < 1 \% )$ . Finally, the samples in oxic and anoxic conditions were incubated in the dark at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 60 days. No control was prepared because it required the poisoning of the samples with either mercuric chloride or sodium azide, which are extremely toxic and environmentally harmful and more importantly result in fluorescence quenching (Park and Snyder, 2018; Retelletti Brogi et al., 2019). Samples under oxic condition were aerated every 3 days under a fume hood in order to minimize the contamination by microorganisms in the air. Samples were sacrificed for sampling on day 0 and 60, with “day $0 "$ corresponding to the day when the samples were inoculated. The degradation experiments were carried out in duplicate.

The sampling was performed in the following steps. First, samples were taken from the incubator and the overlying water was carefully removed not to disturb the sediment. The sample was then centrifuged at 5000 rpm for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The extracted pore waters were filtered through a filter syringe of acetate cellulose ( $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , Advantec) and were stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ before further analyses in the day.

# 2.2. Dissolved organic carbon and spectroscopic measurements

Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations were measured using a Shimadzu V-CPH TOC analyzer. Absorption spectra were scanned from 200 to $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $0 . 5 \mathrm { n m }$ -interval using an ultravioletvisible (UV–vis) spectrometer (Shimadzu UV-1300). Specific UV absorbance (SUVA) at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ , as an indicator of DOM aromaticity, was calculated based on the decadal UV absorption coefficient at a wavelength between $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ normalized by the DOC concentration (Weishaar et al., 2003) Fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) were measured with a Hitachi F-7000 fluorescence spectrophotometer. Excitation $\left( \operatorname { E x } \right)$ scan ranged $2 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with a 5-nm interval, while emission (Em) was scanned between $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with a $1 { - } \mathrm { { n m } }$ interval. The slit width was set at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ for both wavelengths, scan speed was $2 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m / m i n }$ , and the voltage was set at 650 V. A total of 81 EEMs (including controls) were collected for the parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) modeling, and it was processed using MATLAB R2013b (Mathworks, USA) with the drEEM toolbox (Murphy et al., 2013). The validation of the PARAFAC model was made by split half analysis and percentage of explained variance $( 9 9 . 3 \% )$ . Based on corrected EEMs, several fluorescence parameters were calculated, which have been widely used to differentiate between terrestrial and microbial DOM sources (Derrien et al., 2017). They included (i) the classical fluorescence index (FI), which serves as a proxy of aquatic humic substances sources (i.e., microbial versus terrestrial sources) (McKnight et al., 2001), (ii) a modified fluorescence index (YFI) (Heo et al., 2016) and (iii) humification index (HIX), which are the indicator for the degree of DOM humification (Zsolnay et al., 1999), and (iv) biological index (BIX) (Huguet et al., 2009) as an surrogate for recent autochthonous or biological contribution. FI was calculated using the ratio of the emission intensity at $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that at $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at Ex of $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , while the calculation YFI was based on the ratio of the fluorescence intensities in the Em range of 350–400 nm over 400–450 nm at Ex of $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . HIX was estimated using an area ratio of the emission spectra at $4 3 5 { - } 4 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ to 300–345 nm at Ex of $2 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . BIX was calculated by the ratio of the fluorescence intensity at the Em of $3 8 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ to $4 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ at $3 1 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ $\left( \operatorname { E x } \right)$ . Meanwhile, the maximum fluorescence intensities $\left( \mathrm { F _ { m a x } } \right)$ of the identified components from EEM-PARAFAC were adopted to estimate their relative abundance $( \% )$ and ratios.

# 2.3. Molecular size distribution analyses using size exclusion chromatography

The molecular size distributions of the pore water DOM collected at the incubation times of 0 and 60 days were measured using a size exclusion chromatography system (pump: S-100, Knauer, Berlin, Germany and chromatographic column: $2 5 0 \times 2 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , Toyopearl HW-50S, 30 $\mu \mathrm { m }$ , Dr Maisch theoretical plates, Ammerbuch, Germany) equipped with organic carbon detector (OCD). Pore water samples were diluted below $3 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { L }$ to avoid column contamination. Injected volume and flow rate were set at $1 \mathrm { m L }$ and $1 . 1 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ , respectively, with a retention time of $1 3 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ . The identification and the quantification of different size fractions, including biopolymers (BP, ${ \tt > } 1 0 \mathrm { ~ k D a } \operatorname { ) } { \tt a } .$ ), humic substances (HS, approximately 1 kDa), building blocks (BB, 300–500 Da), low molecular weight acid and neutrals (LMW, $< 3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { D a }$ ), were based on its built-in software (Huber et al., 2011). Due to the limited amount of samples, the molecular size distribution analyses were only performed on one representative sample for each case.

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Identification of different fluorescent components via EEM-PARAFAC

Four different fluorescent components were identified from the collected sediment pore water, which included two humic-like components (C1 and C4) and two protein-like components (C2 and C3) (Fig. 1). The four components were consistent with those previously reported and/or well matched with the Open Fluor database with similarity scores of ${ \tt > } 0 . 9 7$ . Component 1 (C1), which had a maximum at $2 2 0 /$ $4 7 8 \mathrm { n m } \left( \mathrm { E x } / \mathrm { E m } \right)$ , can be assigned to a typical terrestrial humic-like component (Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2016; Murphy et al., 2011; Tanaka et al., 2014). Component 2 and 3 (C2 and C3), which exhibited their maxima at $2 2 0 / 3 5 7 \mathrm { n m ( E x / E m ) }$ and $2 2 0 / 3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively, were associated with protein-like substances. Each component can be assigned as tryptophan-like and tyrosine-like components, respectively (D'Andrilli et al., 2017; Nimptsch et al., 2015; Wünsch et al., 2016; Yamashita et al., 2013). Component 4 (C4), peaked at 220/391 nm (Ex/Em), has been reported as microbially derived humic-like component (Lapierre and del Giorgio, 2014; Williams et al., 2013).

# 3.2. Original spectroscopic and molecular size profiles of two pore water DOM end-members

Clear discrepancies were found in the measured spectroscopic parameters and molecular size distributions for the pore water DOM of the two end members (Table 1). The soil end member (i.e., the pore water DOM from the sediment consisting of soil only) was characterized by a high abundance of both humic-like components, C1 and C4, with the percentages of $5 5 4 \%$ and $> 3 2 \%$ respectively. By contrast, the algae end member only consisted of the two protein-like components, C2 and C3, at equal proportions. Meanwhile, the spectroscopic parameters estimated for each end member were in lines with those reported in previous studies (Derrien et al., 2019; Hansen et al., 2016). High HIX values of the soil end member $_ { ( > 1 0 ) }$ indicated a significant content of humic substances, which contrast with a much lower value $( 0 . 1 2 \pm$ 0.00) in the counterpart (i.e., algae end-member). Compared to HIX, BIX values exhibited the opposite trend between the two end members (Table 1). SUVA values were slightly higher for the soil $( 1 . 0 0 \pm 0 . 0 9 ) $ versus the algae $\left( 0 . 6 3 \pm 0 . 0 1 \right)$ ) end member. All the values fell within the typical ranges previously reported for these contrasting DOM sources (Derrien et al., 2017; Huguet et al., 2009; Zsolnay et al., 1999). However, the difference in FI values between the two end members was not evident (Table 1), implying the low resolution of this index in discriminating between these two contrasting OM sources in sediment pore water. This observation can be partially ascribed to the limitation of the index to reflect humic-like fluorophores only. In this context, it is worthwhile to note a previous study of Heo et al. (2016), which had proposed a modified fluorescence index (i.e., YFI) by taking into account both protein-like and humic-like fluorophores in the calculation. For the present study, the algae end member exhibited a higher YFI value $( > 4 . 0 )$ than the soil counterpart $( 0 . 5 0 \pm 0 . 0 0 ) $ ), which agreed with the trend expected from their study.

The molecular size distributions of the two end members were generally in line with the previous spectroscopic parameters. The soil end member was characterized by $> 3 0 \%$ of BP and HS fraction each and $1 0 \% { - } 2 0 \%$ of BB and LMW molecules. The more dominant presence of the two largest size fractions agreed with the fact that soil consists of lignin-derived materials and other plant residues which undergo long humification processes (Kramer et al., 2004). In contrast, the algae end member was featured by its molecular size distribution with $> 3 6 \%$ of LMW fraction and ${ > } 2 1 \%$ of BP fraction followed by $1 3 \mathrm { - } 2 1 \%$ of HS and BB fractions.

On the other hand, no significant difference was found for the whole set of the investigated parameters, either in soil or algae pore water DOM end member, between the oxic and the anoxic conditions (student t-test ${ \tt p } = 0 . 9 9$ for each end-member).

# 3.3. Changes in spectroscopic and molecular size profiles of end-members after incubation

Biodegradation-induced changes in the DOC concentrations of pore water were in the opposite trend between the two end members (i.e., consumption or production) (Table 1). After incubation in the oxic condition, the DOC of pore water was depleted by $9 . 1 \pm 1 9 . 1 \%$ in the soil end member, while it was increased by $3 3 . 5 \pm 1 . 5 \%$ in the algae end member. Under the anoxic condition, similar trends were observed for both end members but with more pronounced changes (i.e., $\Delta \% _ { \mathrm { D O C } } = - 3 8 . 4 \pm 8 . 2 \%$ for soil and $5 1 . 6 \pm 3 . 4 \%$ for algae). For example, the DOC removal and the production were more pronounced for the anoxic versus the oxic condition by 4-fold and 1.5-fold, respectively. The decrease of DOC in the soil end member can be explained by the consequence of microbial degradation of the organic substances present in the original soil pore space or the adsorption of DOM to the soils particles. In contrast, the DOC trend for the algae end member can be attributed to the preferential microbial degradation towards algae POM followed by the release of DOM. The biodegradation-induced changes were also manifested in the chemical composition represented by the relative abundances of different fluorescent components (Table 1). For the soil end member, a slight to moderate decrease was observed for the two humic-like components (C1 and C4) for both oxygen conditions, while the tyrosine-like C3 became more enriched after incubation with a nearly doubled increase in the abundance from $6 . 9 \pm 0 . 3 \%$ to 15.5 $\pm 0 . 2 \%$ and $1 1 . 9 \pm 1 . 5 \%$ to $1 7 . 8 \pm 3 . 0 \%$ for the oxic and the anoxic conditions, respectively. A slight increase $( 3 - 9 \% )$ in the relative distribution of the C2 component over incubation was only observed under the oxic condition. Different from the soil end member, there were no notable changes in the chemical composition of the algae end member. In addition, the pore water DOM with the algal origin seems relatively less affected by oxygen conditions compared to the soil counterpart as only a slight difference was observed for the C3 (e.g., $5 . 4 \%$ increase in the percentage) under the oxic condition.

The trends of the changes in measured spectroscopic indices were more dependent upon the OM origins rather than oxygen conditions (Table 1). For the soil end member, SUVA showed an increasing trend over incubation, changing from $1 . 0 0 \pm 0 . 0 9 \mathrm { L } { \cdot } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ to $3 . 4 2 \pm$ $0 . 4 7 ~ \mathrm { L } { \cdot } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ and from $0 . 9 9 \pm 0 . 0 1 \ \mathrm { L } { \cdot } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ to $3 . 3 7 \pm$ $0 . 5 9 \mathrm { L } { \cdot } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ for oxic and anoxic conditions, respectively. The HIX values slightly decreased from $1 2 . 4 5 \pm 0 . 0 3$ to $1 0 . 0 6 \pm 0 . 0 4$ for the oxic condition and from $1 1 . 1 5 \pm 0 . 4 8$ to $9 . 4 7 \pm 1 . 1 9$ for the anoxic condition, following the trends of the distribution of the terrestrial humiclike component, C1. The three other indices (i.e., FI, YFI, and BIX) remained nearly unchanged over incubation with the relative changes of ${ < } 1 0 \%$ irrespective of the oxygen conditions. For the algae end member, no notable changes were observed for BIX, HIX, and SUVA. However, the FI values notably increased from $0 . 8 9 \pm 0 . 0 3$ to $1 . 1 2 \pm 0 . 0 1$ . In contrast, YFI displayed a slight decrease over time, particularly for the oxic condition, from $4 . 3 5 \pm 0 . 0 5$ to $4 . 0 4 \pm 0 . 0 1$ .

Substantial changes in the molecular size distributions were observed during incubation (Table 1). For both end members, the BP fraction was depleted over incubation with more pronounced changes shown for algae (by $\sim 7 6 \%$ ) versus soil (by ${ \sim } 2 1 \%$ ) end members.

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Biodegradation-induced signatures on the characteristics of two end members

Biodegradation is an important biogeochemical process shaping the quantity and the quality of organic matter (Moran et al., 2000; Sankar et al., 2018). The process usually results in a rapid loss of labile and/or low molecular weight organic compounds (e.g., molecular weight $< 1$ kDa) (Curtis-Jackson et al., 2009) such as some carbohydrate compounds (glucose, fructose, etc.), amino acids, and sugars, and LMW proteins (Bolan et al., 2011), while it can also lead to the production of high molecular weight compounds such as fulvic and humic acids through degradation of existing large organic biomolecules or humification processes (Hansen et al., 2016; Schmidt et al., 2017). The present study revealed that the variations of the DOC concentration and the spectroscopic and molecular size characteristics of pore water DOM were affected by the OM sources (or end member types) as well as by the oxygen conditions.

DOM consumption by biodegradation is typically accompanied by a decrease of DOC concentration as observed in this study for the soil end member. The DOC decrease in pore water can be explained by various mechanisms including mineralization, precipitation, adsorption, and aggregation (Bastviken et al., 2004; D'Andrilli et al., 2019; He et al., 2016; Moran et al., 2000; Sankar et al., 2018). In mineralization process, labile DOM fractions are preferably consumed by microorganisms compared to the organic substances that are considered to be recalcitrant to microbial decomposition (Suseela et al., 2013). During aggregation and adsorption processes, exchange occurs between two OM types from DOM to POM. These processes are commonly encountered in soil DOM as this pool consists of relatively large sized molecules, such as polysaccharides, which tend to aggregate more easily compared to the smaller sized molecules. In addition, the mineral surfaces in soil have a high adsorption affinity for DOM (Gao et al., 2012; He et al., 2016; Verdugo and Santschi, 2010). The biodegradation-induced variations observed in the soil end member, featured by decreased DOC concentration, increased SUVA, and the depletion of small-sized fractions (e.g., BB and LMW), suggest the initial consumption of labile substances followed by the further microbial degradation towards refractory terrestrial substances. This explanation is supported by the decrease of both humic-like components (C1 and C4) for the benefit of the protein-like C3 and HIX values. Similar observations and conclusions have been reported by Zhao et al. (2018) based on a sediment organic carbon remineralization experiment. Meanwhile, the depletion of a large part of BP fraction along with the enrichment of HS in this study was also consistent with a previous study reporting the degradation of biopolymer fractions into oligomers and monomers and/or HS upon a microbial activity (Valle et al., 2018). However, the decrease in HIX values in the present study implies that the HS size fraction might be newly produced by heterotrophs (e.g., direct exudation during bacterial production, viral lysis of microorganisms) rather than by the alteration of existing compounds into humic-like materials (Jiao et al., 2010; Repeta et al., 2002).

For the algae end member, the production of DOM, as evidenced by the increased DOC in the pore water during degradation, can be explained by a preferential microbial attack towards POM and subsequent release of DOM into pore water. Similar phenomena for algae have been observed in other studies (Baines and Pace, 1991; Otsuki and Hanya, 1972). In contrast to soil, algae as POM consists of rather labile organic materials which can be easily broken and depolymerized into DOM by hydrolysis (Schmidt et al., 2017; Tremblay et al., 2015; Yoshimura et al., 2010). Slight changes in the spectroscopic characteristics suggested that aromatic moieties in the pore water DOM were rather invariant. Nevertheless, several notable changes after incubation, such as the appearance of C4 and the decreased YFI, suggest the possibility of the microbial transformation from labile organic substances into humic-like substances. The molecular composition of Green algae like Chlorella vulgaris can be described as the mixture of proteins, carbohydrates, and lipids (Dineshkumar et al., 2017). These molecules are present in different sizes and are less recalcitrant compared to those of soil, which can be more vulnerable to microbial degradation (Catalán et al., 2017; Tremblay et al., 2015).

# 4.2. Aerobic versus anaerobic biodegradation: do different OM sources behave the same?

Oxygen is one of the critical parameters which can affect OM degradation as it serves as the universal electron acceptor for biogeochemical reactions. When oxygen is not available in the environment, bacteria and fungi utilize a variety of inorganic and organic compounds as the alternative electron acceptors to break down OM (Wetzel, 2001). Although comparing aerobic versus anaerobic degradation has been a widely studied topic in natural environments (Bastviken et al., 2004; Kristensen et al., 1995; McKew et al., 2013; Valle et al., 2018), questions regarding how the presence of oxygen affects the OM degradation and the resultant OM characteristics are still open to debate, particularly for sediment pore water.

Our results showed no considerable differences in the measured spectroscopic and molecular size distributions in the pore water DOM between oxic and anoxic conditions regardless of the OM sources. The DOC level in pore water was found to be the parameter the most affected by the oxygen condition. As previously described, the incubation led to the opposite trends in the relative changes in the pore water DOC between the two end members with the depletion and the production found for soil and algae, respectively. Interestingly, such changes were more pronounced for the anoxic versus the oxic condition. For example, in the anoxic condition, the extents of the DOC removal in the soil end member and the DOC production in the algae end member over incubation were 4-fold and 1.5-fold greater than those under the oxic condition. Limitation of available oxygen seems to stimulate and enhance the biogeochemical processes occurring in the sediment pore water such as the release of DOM from POM and/or the microbial consumption of DOC. On the other hand, the oxygen availability does not seem to make significant changes on the spectroscopic and molecular size characteristics as demonstrated by the relative changes of $< 2 0 \%$ and the p-values of ${ \tt > } 0 . 9$ for both end members (Table 1). This signifies that oxygen may play direct roles in biodegradation or microbial activity, but the contribution may be very limited to the intra-molecular changes in DOM and/or POM induced by biodegradation.

# 4.3. Implications from pore water DOM characteristics at different endmember ratios

Terrigenous OM is transported into aquatic environment via runoff, overland flow, and/or infiltration, which plays a major role in the carbon cycle (Bianchi, 2011; Hedges, 1992). From the previous observations that two contrasting POM sources behaved differently in the pore water DOM with respect to their responses to microbial degradation, it can be inferred that the mixtures of the two end members would also reflect the same signatures in the spectroscopic and molecular size characteristics according to the mixing ratios.

From the results of the current work, the DOC, the spectroscopic and molecular size characteristics of the sediment pore water at various mixing ratios of two POM sources did not follow the linear trends expected from the ideal mixing behaviors of the two end members after incubation and/or showed different trends from the relationships anticipated before incubation (Figs. 2, 3, and 4). The parameter the most affected by the mixing ratio of sources during biodegradation was the DOC concentration because both the removal and the production were observed simultaneously in the mixtures at different ratios, which led to no consistent trends with increasing algal OM proportion in the mixtures (Fig. 2a). The spectroscopic indices of the mixtures like SUVA or FI or HIX behaved independent of the mixing ratios, and they did not show linear trends with changing the relative proportion of each end member (Fig. 2). For example, similar values for SUVA or HIX were observed for the soil to algae mixing ratios of 100:0, 75:25, and 50:50 (Figs. 2b, e). By contrast, YFI and BIX displayed linear trends, suggesting an ideal mixing behavior (Figs. 2d, f). These parameters were rather invariant with respect to either the biodegradation process or the oxygen conditions. Regarding the relative distributions of the different fluorescent components, linear and/or linear-like trends were observed after incubation except for the C3 component (Fig. 3), in which the relative proportions of C3 were analogous for all the pore water from the sediments with 25 to $1 0 0 \%$ of soil POM. In general, such linear-like changes with respect to the mixing ratios were also observed for the molecular size distributions (Fig. 4). However, the interpretation of the results should be made with special care as they were not based on replicate samples.

The deviation from the linear relationships between the pore water DOM characteristics and the mixing ratios of two end members after incubation may suggest the occurrence of priming effects. Several decades ago, it was believed that the input of labile and fresh OM in soil could intensify the mineralization process (Bingeman et al., 1953), which was conceptually called the “priming effect”. In fact, several past studies have shown that the addition of labile OM tended to increase the degradation rates of the soil OM (Fontaine et al., 2003; Kuzyakov, 2002; Kuzyakov et al., 2000). More recently, priming effects have been further delineated as negative, neutral, or positive effects, depending on the consequences in the ways that they may stimulate/reduce or exert no effect on the mineralization of soil OM at given conditions (Guenet et al., 2010a, 2014). The concept of the “priming effect” was revisited by Guenet et al. (2010a), in which it was assumed that this phenomenon may occur in an aquatic environment where two pools of OM (e.g., terrigenous and aquatic sources) are mixed together, creating various routes and rates for the degradation of OM. Despite the wide consensus on this concept, the question still remains to be resolved, and further studies are warranted to fully understand the associated phenomena (Bengtsson et al., 2018). The results of this current study suggest a significant effect of the ratio of the mixing sources on the biodegradation-induced changes in the spectroscopic and molecular examined parameters. Fonte et al. (2013) have demonstrated that the combination of differences in carbon sources affects the bacterial community and its growth efficiency. As the priming effects are governed by microbial activity (Blagodatskaya and Kuzyakov, 2008), it seems plausible that our results also highlighted the occurrence of priming effects, both positive and negative effects, as no linear relationship between priming effects and the increasing amount of labile organic matter was observed. Guenet et al. (2010b) previously observed that the priming effect intensity did not increase linearly with increasing fresh organic matter additions during a controlled experiment. One of their hypotheses was the existence of antagonistic mechanisms: an increase of the total active microbial biomass accelerating OM mineralization (i.e. a positive priming effect) and preferential substrate utilization of fresh OM over soil OM decreasing OM mineralization (i.e. a negative priming effect). This hypothesis in part supports our results and interpretation.

# 5. Conclusions

In this study, the biodegradation-induced changes in the pore water DOM were examined with the ideal sediments composed of two contrasting POM sources (i.e., soil and algae) at different mixing ratios via controlled degradation experiments under oxic and anoxic conditions. Based on the results, the following conclusions can be drawn:

• Biodegradation of the sediments led to the removal and the production of the pore water DOM for the soil and the algae end members, respectively. The decrease of DOC in the soil end member can be attributed to the consequence of the microbial degradation of the

DOM originally present in the soil pore space, whereas the microbial attack towards algae POM and subsequent release of the DOM can explain the DOC trend for the algae end member.

• The presence of oxygen plays significant roles in changing the DOC level of the pore water during biodegradation, but not in the measured spectroscopic and molecular size distributions. This suggests that oxygen may make a limited contribution to altering the intramolecular mechanisms induced by biodegradation, although it affects the extent of the biodegradation.

• Our results also imply the importance of the mixing ratios of different POM sources in the biodegradation-induced changes in the pore water DOM. However, the changes did not reflect ideal mixture behaviors with no linear trend with an increasing proportion of one end member in the mixtures. These biodegradation-induced changes can be linked to the mixing effects on the bacterial community composition and the degradation efficiency due to the presence of multiple DOM sources with varying chemical composition. The observations also highlighted the occurrence of priming effects, both positive and negative effects, in shaping the characteristics of the pore water DOM.
