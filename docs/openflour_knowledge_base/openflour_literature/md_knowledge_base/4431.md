# Long-term Trends in Dissolved Organic Matter Composition and Its Relation to Sea Ice in the Canada Basin, Arctic Ocean (2007-2017)

C. DeFrancesco1 ① and C. Guéguen² $\textcircled{1}$

1EnvironmentalandLifeciences Graduate Program,Trent University,Peterborough,ON,Canada,²Departementde chimie, Université de Sherbrooke,Sherbrooke,QC,Canada

Abstract Absorbance and fluorescence properties of disslved organic matter (DOM) were measured in the upper and lower polar mixed layer (UPML and LPML,respectively) over an 1l-year period (2007-20l7) to assess for yearly changes in the quality and quantity of colored and fluorescent DOM (CDOM and FDOM,respectively) in relation with sea ice concentration in central Canada Basin waters. The LPML waters were enriched in CDOM,total dissolved lignin phenols,terrestrial humic-like Cl,and in microbial humic-like C2 and C7 relative to the UPML waters ( $\mathit { p } > 0 . 0 5 $ ).The low ice years (i.e.,2012, 2016-2017) were characterized by lower humic-like fluorescence intensity(C4 only)and abundances relative to high ice years (i.e.,2007-20l,20l3-20l5),likelythe resultof the preferentialphotoalteration of humic material when sea ice concentration was reduced.Significant time increases were found in tryptophan-like C3 in UPML and terrestrially derived humic-like C4 in LPML,suggesting an increase in the proteinaceous and terigenous character of FDOM in UPML and LPML during the 11-year period, respectively.No interannual variation in dissolved organic carbon concentration was found in LPML and UPML.Weak but positive Spearman correlations were found between the humic-like intensities and abundances,and sea ice concentration in UPML waters,a consequence of reduced photodegradation in ice covered waters.This 11-year survey provides the first insight into the influence of summer sea ice concentration and river runoff on the quality and quantity of CDOM and FDOM.

Plain Language Summary The Arctic Ocean is a hostile environment undergoing rapid changes.However,it is not clear how the fast decline in sea cover and the increase in freshwater content in the Beaufort Gyre region observed over the last decade affect the carbon cycle and particularly the quality and quantity of dissolved organic matter (DOM).In this work, we use absorbance and fluorescence properties of DOM collcted over1l cruises in the central Canada Basin between 2007and 2017 to assess the yearly changes in DOM amount and composition. A time increase in proteinaceous and terrestrial dissolved organic material was found in the upper and lower polar mixed layer of the central Canada Basin,respectively.Together these results highlight that DOM composition has been already afected by the change in sea ice concentration and freshwater content in the Beaufort Gyre region.

# 1. Introduction

The Arctic region is undeniably changing, with significant losses in sea ice cover and changing riverine inputs to the Arctic Ocean (Peterson et al.,2006).A record low sea ice extent was reported in September 2012 (D. Perovich et al.,2012)and the summer sea ice has not exceeded or returned to pre-2007 levels yet (Perovich et al.,2Ol9).The rapid reduction of Arctic summer sea ice causes an increase in the amount of light penetrating into the ocean(Nicolaus et al.,2012),enhancing sea-ice melt (Markus et al.,2009)and affecting the aquatic ecosystems (Arrigo& van Dijken,2015; Arrigo et al.,2012).Arrigo and van Dijken (2015) showed that the $3 0 \%$ increase in the annual net primary production in the Arctic Ocean between l998 and 2012 was associated with reduced sea ice.Planktonic dissolved organic matter (DOM) is considered as a major source of marine DOM (Osburn et al.,20l9).The increase in the primary production of the Arctic Ocean(Arrigo et al., 2008) could contribute to increased production of autochthonous DOM in polar waters (Shen et al., 2018).

Optical properties including absorbing and fluorescing properties have been used to assess DOM composition and fate (Coble,2007; Nelson & Gauglitz,20l6),and trace water masses in Arctic waters (Dainard and Gueguen,20l3; Goncalves-Araujo et al.,2016; Guéguen et al.,2014; Logvinova et al.,2015; Stedmon et al.,2011; Walker et al.,2009; Yamashitaet al.,2019).Colored DOM(CDOM) is definedas the fractionof DOM (up to $2 5 \%$ ; Benner, 20o2) that has light absorbing properties in the ultraviolet (UV)and visible regions of the electromagnetic spectrum (Hansell& Carlson,2002)whereas thefluorescent DOM(FDOM) refers to the DOM pool that fluoresces. CDOM is highly photoreactive (Del Vecchio & Blough,2004),causing loss of color(i.e.,photobleaching)(J.R.Helms etal.,20l4),increasing light penetration depths (Del Vecchio & Blough,2002)and exposure of microorganisms to harmful UVradiation (Blough & Green,1995), which affect both autotrophic and heterotrophic metabolism (Walsh et al.,2O03).Solar irradiation also caused important changes in FDOM intensities in sunlit waters (Dainard et al.,2015; Logvinova et al.,2016).Protein-like (Logvinova et al.,2016)and humic-like FDOM (Dainard etal.,2015; Logvinova et al.,2016) were shown to be photoreactive in Chukchi and Beaufort Seas. CDOM and FDOM have been shown to increase (Dainard et al.,2019; Jorgensen et al.,2015; Shen etal.,2016; Walker et al.,2009)or decrease (Gueguen et al.,2007; Logvinova etal.,2016; Mathis et al.,2005; Shen et al.,20l6)with increasing sea ice melt in polar waters.The presence of sea ice can therefore play a key role in CDOM and FDOM composition and distribution in surface ocean waters.

Circulation within the Canada Basin is mostly controlled by the anticyclonic Beaufort Gyre that is generally located over the central Canada Basin (Proshutinsky et al., 2009; Timmermans et al.,2014).An increase of $6 { , } 4 0 0 \ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 }$ of freshwater was observed in the Beaufort Gyre from 2003 to 20l8,the result of persistent wind forcing associated with sea ice melt and redirection of Mackenzie River discharge (Proshutinksky et al.,2019).Brine from ice formation and river runoff also have strong implications for the distribution and the composition of DOM in the interior mixed layers (Dainard et al.,2019; Mendoza etal.,2017; Shen et al.,20l6),which can extend to about $1 5 0 { - } 2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ in the Canada Basin (Guéguen et al., 2OO7; Shen et al., 2016).

Studies that assess whether the quality and quantity of DOM have changed over temporal scales face a daunting task of logistics and funding. Ocean time series are critical for identifying the marine ecosystem shift ina time of unpredictable and rapid environmental changes.In a previous study,Dainard et al.(2019) showed that the average FDOM intensities and composition remained relatively unchanged in surface Canada Basin waters in 2010-20l2. Here, we aim to investigate long time changes in DOM composition using a l-year monitoring program,part of the Joint Ocean Ice Study/Beaufort Gyre Observing System.Specifically,we aim to 1) assess the interannual variability of CDOMand FDOMand 2)determine the influence of sea ice concentration on CDOM and FDOM in the upper and lower polar mixed layer(UPML and LPML, respectively)waters of the central Canada Basin from 2007 to 20l7.DOM was characterized based on the absorption coefficient at a wavelength of $3 5 5 \mathrm { n m } \left( \mathrm { a } _ { 3 5 5 } \right)$ as a proxy for terrestrial CDOM quantification (Hoge et al.,1993); the spectral slope from 275 to $2 9 5 \ \mathrm { n m } \ \left( \mathrm { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } \right)$ as an approximation of molecular weight of CDOM(Helms et al.,2008); the concentration of nine total disolved lignin phenols (TDLP9)as biomarker of terrigenous DOM(Opsahl et al.,199); and dissolved organic carbon (DOC)concentrations.Excitation emission matrices (Coble,1996)and parallel factor analysis (EEM-PARAFAC) were used to determine the main FDOM components (Murphy et al.,2013; Stedmon and Bro,2008)and their sources and composition (e.g.,Dainard et al.,2Ol9)in central Canada Basin.This time series observation helps provide aframework to explore the biogeochemical consequences of a warming Arctic Ocean on the Arctic DOM cycle.

# 2.4.Sea Ice Concentration

Sea ice concentration data was derived from visual sea ice observations during the annual JOIS/BGOS cruises following Antarctic Sea ice Processes and Climate observation protocols and archived using the Arctic Shipborne Sea Ice Standardization Tool (ASSIST) (Hutchings & Faber, 2Ol8). The sea ice concentrations archived through the website (https://icewatch.met.no/assist) were uploaded for each sampling sites in the central Canada Basin visited between 2oo7 and 20l7.The annual averages of sea concentration (in percentage) were used here.Because averages were calculated based on sampling locations, they may not be a true representation of the sea ice concentration within the entire central Canada Basin but only where DOM samples were collected each year (Figure S1).

# 2.5. Statistical Analysis

Student's $t { \cdot }$ -tests were used to compare averages in Chla, CDOM,and FDOM between sampling years and between UPML and LPML. The temporal trends in yearly averages of CDOM and FDOM were evaluated using the nonparametric Mann-Kendall test and Sen's slope estimates at $9 5 \%$ confidence level using the MAKESENS template (Salmi et al.,2002)in Excel.For time series with more than 10 datapoints,the Q test is used;otherwise the S test is used. Spearman's rank correlation coefficients between CDOM,FDOM and sea ice concentration were calculated to examine the change in DOM composition in relation with sea ice.

In the UPML,the FDOM pool was dominated by protein-like ( $\mathrm { C } 3 + \mathrm { C } 6$ $0 . 0 3 5 \pm 0 . 0 5 1$ R.U.) followed by terrestrial $\mathrm { C 1 + C 4 }$ and microbial humic-like $( \mathsf { C } 2 + \mathsf { C } 5 + \mathsf { C } 7 )$ $( 0 . 0 2 7 \pm 0 . 0 0 8$ R.U.and $0 . 0 2 3 \pm 0 . 0 2 6$ R.U., respectively)over the1l-year survey ( $p < 0 . 0 5$ ; Figure 5).Among the seven validated components,protein-like C3 and C6 were the most predominant with intensities averaging $0 . 0 2 0 \pm 0 . 0 2 0$ and $0 . 0 1 5 \pm 0 . 0 3 2$ R.U. in the UPML,respectively $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 ) $ ).The highest values of the labile (Stedmon and Markager, 2Oo5) protein-like components can be attributable to recent productivity (Determann et al,1994) and/or sea ice melt sources (Mendoza et al.,2017).In the UPML,the terrestrially derived humic-like ( $\mathrm { ( C 1 + C 4 ) }$ was the second most dominant component of the FDOM pool, with C4 having a significantly higher fluorescence intensity than C1 $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ . C1 is more red-shifted than C4(Figure 4)and thus can more readily absorb light and be more photoreactive than C4 (Dainard et al., 2015).

In the LPML,the fluorescence intensity of terrestrially derived humic-like $\mathrm { C 1 + C 4 }$ was $1 4 \%$ lower than microbial FDOM intensity $( \mathsf { C } 2 + \mathsf { C } 5 + \mathsf { C } 7 )$ but not different from microbial humic-like ( $( \mathsf { C } 2 + \mathsf { C } 5 + \mathsf { C } 7 )$ $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ Significant differences were found in terrestrial humic like components ( $\mathrm { C 1 + C 4 }$ and C1),and microbially humic-like components (C2 and C7) between UPML and LPML (Figure 5). Despite a greater influence of river runoff in UPML,the UPML terrestrial humic-like intensity was $1 4 \%$ lower than in the LPML water mas,likely aresult of the preferential photodegradation of humic-like DOM(Dainard etal.,20l5).In terms of relative abundance,significantly lower fluorescence intensities of humic-like components (terrestrial humic-like C1,and microbial/marine derived C2, C5,and C7) were found in UPML compared to LPML $\cdot ( p < 0 . 0 5$ ； Figure 5),suggesting an alteration or removal of humic-like due to a greater light exposure in UPML (Dainard et al., 2015).

Like CDOM, no significant latitudinal changes in C1-C7 intensities were found in the UPML over the 1l-year survey ( $3 ^ { \circ }$ intervals; Figure S4),consistent with previous studies in the Canada Basin (Dainard et al., 2019; Shen et al., 2016).

# 3.4. Temporal Variability of CDOM and FDOM in UPML and LPML

The Mann Kendall trend tests (Table 1) were used to identify trends in CDOM and FDOM in UMPL and LPML over the 11-year survey (Figures S5-S6). No significant temporal monotonic trends were found for Chla,a355, $\mathrm { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ , TDLP9,and DOC in UPML and LPML over the 11-year survey (Table 1). Despite some variations between years,the absorbance based proxies remained essentially unchanged in both UPML and LPML over the 1l-year survey. In terms of FDOM intensity,only protein-like C3 significantly increased in UPML over the 1l-year study (slope $Q = 0 . 0 0 1 4$ R.U. $\mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ $p < 0 . 0 5$ ; Table 1). However, Chl a showed no significant trends ( $\langle p > 0 . 0 5 \rangle$ suggesting the increase in C3 was not due to an increase in primary production, similar to previous work in the Canada Basin (Dainard et al.,2019)and western Arctic Ocean (Mendoza et al.,2017).Although protein-like components are biologically related material,the lack of correlation with $\operatorname { C h l } a$ suggests that other microbial sources (Rochelle-Newall & Fisher, 2002)can be asource of C3. No significant trends were found in C1-C2 and C4-C7in UPML $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ . No significant monotonic trend was found with the other protein-like component C6 found in central Canada Basin suggesting that C3 and C6 have different stability and/or sources.A significant decrease in the relative abundances of humic-like C1 and C2 was found with annual rates of $0 . 6 8 \%$ and $0 . 4 3 \%$ ,respectively.The fact that C1 and C2 were the least abundant components in the tFDOM and mFDOM pools explains the lack of significant temporal trends in tFDOM and mFDOM in UPML.

In LPML,a significant monotonic trend was found with terrestrial humic-like C4 ( $\cdot ( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ whereas the other terrestrial humic-like C1 showed no significant temporal trend $\langle p > 0 . 0 5$ ; Table 1). The difference in temporaltrends between the two terrestrial derived humic-like components C1 and C4 revealed diferences in their stability over time and/or their sources in LPML.The annual increase in humic-like C4(0.0007 R.U. $\mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 } .$ ）was congruent with the increase in freshwater content in the Beaufort Gyre observed between 2003 and 2018 (Proshutinsky et al.,2O19).The relative abundance of terrestrial humic-like C1 significantlydecreased over time in LPMLwith an average slope of $0 . 4 4 \% \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ which is 1.5 times lower than the annual rate observed in UPML $( 0 . 6 8 \% \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Table 1).The greater annual rate in UPML may be the result of more intense photoalteration in the UPML but further studies are required. Overall,only fewFDOMcomponents showed significant temporal changes over the 11-year survey in the central Canada Basin UPML and LPML waters.

The impacts of cruise timing on the temporal variability of CDOM and FDOM in UPML and LPML were significant only in fall cruises with the increase in protein-like C3 over time ( $\cdot ( p < 0 . 0 5$ ; Figure S7). The annual rate was 0.0021 and 0.0046 R.U. $\mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ in UPML and LPML, respectively. The percent abundance in terrestrial humic-like C1 showed negative temporal trend during the fall cruises with annual changes of $0 . 9 4 \%$ and $0 . 7 0 \%$ in UPML and LPML,respectively (Figure 6).The temporal decreases in humic-like abundances 0 $\% { \bf C 1 }$ $\% \mathbf { C } 2$ $\% { } C 5$ ,and $\% \mathrm { C } 7$ ; Figure S8) coincided with the increase in the relative abundance of protein-like C3 in UPML,suggesting a decrease in the humic character of the DOM pool in UPML in fall.These results can have important consequences for the cycle of essential elements associated with humic material such as iron (Whitby et al., 2020)and thus oceanic primary productivity.

Unlike CDOM,FDOM intensity and relative abundances showed significant differences between low and high ice years in fall surveys in UPML (Figure 6b-6c). The intensity(C4 only) and abundance of humic-like components ( $\% { \bf C } 1 { - } \% { \bf C } 2$ ， $\% C 4 - \% C 5$ ， $\% { \bf C } 7 \mathrm { \cdot }$ ）were lower during the lowice years $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ .Humic-like are known to be easily photodegraded (Nieto-Cid etal., 2005; Stedmon and Markager, 2005) and therefore their intensity and relative abundance are likely to be lower under higher irradiation when sea ice cover is reduced. The relationships between CDOM and FDOM parameters alongside sea ice concentrations were assessed in UPML and LPML (Table 2). No significant relationships ( $\mathit { p } > 0 . 0 5 $ )were found between salinity, $\mathrm { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } ,$ TDLP9,and DOC with sea ice concentration in UPML or LPML.A weak but significant negative relationship was found between $\mathbf { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ and sea ice concentration ( $r _ { s } = - 0 . 1 9$ $p < 0 . 0 5$ in UPML,suggesting a small influence of sea ice concentration on CDOM level in the UPML. Weak and positive relationships $( 0 . 1 5 \leq r _ { s } \leq 0 . 2 5$ $p < 0 . 0 5$ ; Table 2) were found between sea ice concentration and, the intensities and abundances of humic-like components C1-C2 and C7 whereas no relationships were found for humic-like C4-C5 intensities and abundances (except $\% { } C 5$ ） $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ . An increase of the red shifted humic-like components (C1and C2; Figure 2) with increasing sea ice concentration may be the result of reduced photodegradation as sea ice limits UV light penetration in underlying waters (Logvinova et al., 2015; Stroeve et al., 2012). Sea ice can be a source of FDOM,including humic-like components, formed during ice formation and autochthonous material produced within the ice(Brogi et al.,20l8; Stedmon et al.,2007). Marine humic substances are known to be photodegraded in upper water layers while being microbiologically generated in lower layers (Chenand Bada,1992).Itcanbe noted that despite the correlations of a355 with humic-like C1, C2,and C7 in UPML ( $\zeta { r _ { s } } = 0 . 3 4 – 0 . 4 8$ ; data not shown), CDOM and humic-like FDOM showed opposite trends with sea ice concentration (Table 2) but further work is required before any conclusions can be made in this regard. Negative correlations were found between sea ice concentration and tyrosine-like C6 (intensity and percent abundances) in both UMPL and LPML (Table 2). Photoproduction of tyrosine-like fluorescence has been previously observed in sea ice melt waters (Logvinova et al.,2015). Increasing sea ice cover associated with the deterioration of light conditions may not constitute favorable conditions for phytoplankton growth and thus the production and the release oflabile protein-like material. Together these results showed that the change in sea ice concentration observed between 2007and 2017 has already influenced the quantity and quality FDOM in central Canada Basin surface waters.

# 4. Conclusions

The Arctic regions are experiencing unprecedented environmental changes but the consequences on the biogeochemistry of marine DOM remain unclear. Based on a 11-year time series in central Canada Basin, we showed that the UPML waters were characterized by lower a355,TDLP9 and humic-like FDOM(C1-C2, C7)relative to the LPML waters,consistent with the preferential alteration of aromatic DOMin illuminated surface waters.No significant diffrences were found in CDOMand FDOM observed in both UPML and LPML during the summer cruises.This contrasts with the fall surveys where FDOM showed pronounced changes over time.Based on Mann Kendall trend tests,only protein-like C3 displayed a significant positive trend,with annual rates ranging from 0.0021 R.U.in UPML to 0.0046 R.U.in LPML.The temporal decrease in humic-like abundance suggests adecrease in the humic character of the FDOM pool in UPML between 2007and 2017.Based on weak but positive corelations,humic-like intensities of C1-C2and C7 were related with sea ice concentration in UPML waters $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ ,likely the result of reduced photodegradation when sea ice concentration increases.Together these results showed that FDOM amount and composition has changed over the last 1 years with a decrease in the humic character of DOM in central Canada Basin polar mixed layers,and particularly in the UPML waters.
