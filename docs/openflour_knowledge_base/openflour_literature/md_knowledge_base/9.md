# Absorption and fluorescence of dissolved organic matter in the waters of the Canadian Arctic Archipelago, Baffin Bay, and the Labrador Sea

Celine Gu - eguen - 1, Chad W. Cuss1, Chase J. Cassels1, and Eddy C. Carmack2

1 Department of Chemistry, Trent University, Peterborough, Ontario, Canada, 2 Fisheries and Oceans Canada, Institute of Ocean Sciences, Sidney, British Columbia, Canada

Abstract The optical properties of dissolved organic matter (DOM) were investigated for the first time in the Canadian Arctic Archipelago (CAA), Baffin Bay (BB), and Labrador Sea (LS) as part of the International Polar Year Canada’s Three Oceans project (C3O). The dynamics and composition of absorbing DOM (i.e., colored DOM, or CDOM) and fluorescent DOM (FDOM) were evaluated in several distinct water masses occupying the three regions: surface waters, Arctic outflow waters, West Greenland Intermediate waters (WGIW), upper Labrador Sea waters (uLSW), and Bottom Baffin Deep Water (BBDW). Four fluorescent components were identified by applying parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) to 522 excitation emission matrix (EEM) spectra: three humic-like and one protein-like. The FDOM in surface waters of the CAA and BB differed considerably in character from those of the LS, with higher fluorescence intensity in the former. The fluorescence intensities of the two terrestrial humic-like components (C1 and C3) were linearly correlated with apparent oxygen utilization (AOU) in the CAA Arctic outflow and the WGIW whereas only humic-like C3 was significantly correlated with AOU in uLSW. These findings suggest that the humic-like components were produced in situ as organic matter was bio-oxidized. The slopes of the linear relationship between humic-like intensity and AOU were significantly greater in the WGIW relative to the Arctic outflow waters, which implies that FDOM in the Arctic-derived deeper layer was less prone to mineralization.

# 1. Introduction

The colored or chromophoric fraction of dissolved organic matter (CDOM) is ubiquitous in all natural waters including the open ocean [Gu-eguen and Kowalczuk, 2013, and references therein]. The overall distribution of CDOM in the world’s oceans is determined by autochthonous and to some degree allochthonous sources (e.g., rivers) [Gu-eguen et al., 2005, 2012], by photodegradation near the surface [Del Vecchio and Blough, 2004; Determann et al., 1996; Swan et al., 2009], and by mineralization in deeper waters [J-rgensen et al., 2011; Nelson et al., 2007, 2010; Swan et al., 2009; Yamashita et al., 2007, 2010]. CDOM regulates light penetration, mediates photochemical reactions, and plays an important role in the global biogeochemical cycles of C, N, P, and trace metals. Despite its importance, very little is known about the dynamics and major constituents of CDOM.

The optical properties of DOM can be used to describe the dynamics of its supply by Arctic rivers [e.g., Gu-eguen et al., 2005, 2011; Mann et al., 2012; Matsuoka et al., 2012; O’Donnell et al., 2012; Stedmon et al., 2011; Tank et al., 2011; Walker et al., 2013]. Changes in the shape of CDOM absorption spectrum and spectral slope (S) have been previously used to provide insight into the nature of DOM through its sensitivity to photo-oxidation, microbial activity, source differences, and changes in molecular weight [Gu-eguen and Cuss, 2011; Helms et al., 2008]. A fraction of absorbing CDOM fluoresces (i.e., fluorescent DOM, or FDOM). The nature and/or source of FDOM (e.g., protein-like, terrestrial humic-like, and marine humic-like) can be determined based on the excitation and emission wavelength maxima [Coble, 2007]. The combination of excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence with the multivariate data analysis method parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) can be used to separate the complex fluorescence signal into its major underlying components [Stedmon and Bro, 2008; Stedmon et al., 2003]. EEM-PARAFAC has been broadly applied to characterize FDOM in coastal and marine environments [e.g., Dainard and Gu-eguen, 2013; Gu-eguen et al., 2007, 2011, 2012; J-rgensen et al., 2011; Murphy et al., 2008; Yamashita et al., 2008; 2010]. Based on a limited data set (62 EEMs), Walker et al. [2009] also showed the usefulness of PARAFAC for differentiating near-surface water masses (i.e., $1 2 \mathsf { m }$ depth) between the coastal Beaufort Sea, Canadian Arctic Archipelago, and Labrador Sea.

The Arctic Ocean Basin contains only about $1 \%$ of global ocean volume, but receives ${ \sim } 1 0 \%$ of global river discharge [Opsahl et al., 1999]. This discharge is mainly from large and DOM-rich rivers such as the Mackenzie River in Canada, and the Ob, Yenisey, and Lena Rivers in Siberia [Gu-eguen et al., 2005; Lobbes et al., ${ 2 0 0 0 } ;$ Stedmon et al., 2011; Walker et al., 2009]. The Canadian Arctic Archipelago (CAA), Baffin Bay (BB), and Labrador Sea (LS) are major pathways of water flow from the Arctic Ocean to the North Atlantic [Aksenov et al., 2010; Carmack et al., ${ 2 0 1 0 } ;$ Carmack and McLaughlin, 2011; Melling et al., 2008; Tremblay et al., 2012]. Rapidly changing conditions in the Arctic Ocean can propagate downstream through the CAA, BB, and LS southward to the North Atlantic. Determining the fate of river-borne DOM in the Arctic Ocean is of global importance because a large fraction of carbon stored in frozen Arctic soils is susceptible to mobilization under warming conditions. This mobilized carbon may in turn, alter biogeochemical cycling at the points of outflow into the Arctic Ocean, with unknown consequences downstream. The dynamics and composition of CDOM and FDOM through the Arctic and into North Atlantic, however, remain poorly understood.

Here we describe the dynamics of CDOM and FDOM running from the CAA eastward to LS via BB in surface waters and four vertically distributed water masses (i.e., Arctic outflow, West Greenland Intermediate Waters, Bottom Baffin Deep waters, and upper Labrador Sea waters). The distribution and composition of CDOM and FDOM are also assessed in the context of hydrographic properties. This is the first comprehensive survey of absorbing and fluorescent DOM (i.e., 522 EEMs) conducted in both surface and deeper layers to provide insights about DOM cycling in the top $1 3 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ of waters from the western Arctic Ocean through CAA.

# 2. Materials and Methods

# 2.1. Sample Collection

Water samples were collected between 4 July and 17 July 2008, and between 20 July and 5 August 2009 on board the CCGS Louis S. Saint Laurent (Figure 1) as part of the Canada’s Three Oceans project (C3O): a Canadian contribution to International Polar Year research. Samples for optical properties of DOM were collected in the Canadian Arctic Archipelago (CAA; between Coronation Gulf and Barrow Strait), Lancaster Sound (LSd), Baffin Bay (BB), and Labrador Sea (LS) (Figure 1). A total of 522 samples were collected from the water intake line ${ \sim } 1 0 ~ \mathsf { m }$ below the surface) and from CTD/rosette casts ranging from 4 to $1 3 1 8 \mathrm { m }$ in depth. The

Seabird ${ \mathsf { S B E 9 + } }$ CTD was setup with two temperature sensors, two conductivity sensors, two oxygen sensors, and an in situ Seapoint chlorophyll-a fluorometer. The CTD dissolved oxygen (DO) data were continuously checked against the Winkler titration of water samples. Apparent oxygen utilization (AOU) was calculated by subtracting the concentration of DO from the oxygen level at saturation using algorithms in Ocean Data View (v 4.5.3) [Schlitzer, 2012]. Samples collected for absorbance and fluorescence analysis were immediately filtered through precombusted $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ GF/F filters (Whatman) [Gu-eguen et al., 2012; Walker et al., 2009], and stored in precombusted amber-glass vials at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ until analysis. Absorbance analysis was immediately conducted aboard ship, whereas fluorescence analysis was performed onshore within 6 months of collection.

# 2.2. Water Mass Definitions

Water mass distributions spanning the study region are complex and reflect waters of Pacific and Arctic origin. In addition to Arctic waters exiting southward via the multiple channels of the CAA and along eastern Baffin Island and Labrador, waters of Atlantic origin flow north along western Greenland, while water mass transformations also occur within the system. Arctic waters also enter the southern Labrador Sea as a continuation to the East Greenland Current around the south tip of Greenland and flow northward with the West Greenland Current. The description of the main water masses used here is based on earlier work in CAA [McLaughlin et al., 2005; Melling et al., 2008], BB [Bacle et al., 2002; Yang, 2005], and LL [Yashayaev, 2007; Yashayaev et al., 2008].

For the purposes of this paper, we will discuss the composition and dynamics of CDOM and FDOM in terms of four distinct water masses encountered along the C3O transect: (1) Arctic outflow waters, (2) West Greenland Intermediate Water (WGIW), (3) Bottom Baffin Deep Water (BBDW), and (4) Upper Labrador Sea Water (uLSW) (Figure 2). In doing so, we recognize that the relative mixtures of Arctic/Pacific and Atlantic origin water vary in passing from one domain to the next. We also recognize that sloping isopycnals associated with the inflowing and outflowing waters means that no single depth range can be used to characterize the vertical layering of water masses; thus, in the following we use depth ranges that approximate vertical partitioning along the center of each basin. Finally, we note that because data were collected in summer, distinct seasonal mixed layers are found in each domain, progressing form the colder and fresher surface waters of the CAA to the warmer and more saline surface layers of the Labrador Sea. The above noted water masses are described as follows:

1. Surface waters $( < 1 5 ~ \mathsf { m }$ depth) encountered in the CAA are ultimately of Pacific origin and influenced by river runoff, sea ice and brine [Jones et al., 2008; Yamamoto-Kawai et al., 2010], whereas Atlantic-origin waters are also a component of surface waters in the BB-LS region [Yamamoto-Kawai et al., 2010] (Figure 2). The influence of the FDOM-rich Mackenzie River waters [Gu-eguen et al., 2005; Walker et al., 2009] was reported in Amundsen Gulf in the western CAA (Figure 1) [Walker et al., 2009] until Dolphin Union Strait $( 1 1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { W } )$ Figure 1) [Yamamoto-Kawai et al., 2010]. Inputs from relatively large CAA Rivers including Coppermine River $( \sim 2 6 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ and Back River $( \sim 6 0 0 \ : \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \ : \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) can be observed in Coronation Gulf and Queen Maud Gulf in western CAA. The surface waters exiting CAA and LSd eastward into BB are mixed with the cold surface waters from the Arctic Ocean that flow southward through Jones and Smith Sounds (Figure 1) into BB and LS. On the other hand, a northward flow of warm and saline Atlantic-origin surface waters enters the Arctic Seas from the south via eastern Davis Strait (DS; Figure 1) and the West Greenland Current in LS [Aksenov et al., 2010]. Thus, surface waters flow both southward (west side) and northward (east side) through DS. As a result of these flow patterns, surface waters properties vary for each of the domains (CAA, $T < 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $S p < 3 1$ ; BB, $0 < T < 6 ^ { \circ } C$ and $3 0 < { \mathsf { S p } } < 3 3 ;$ LS, $T > 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $S _ { \mathsf { P } } > 3 3 .$ ) and are noted by the ellipses in Figure 2.

2. Arctic outflow waters $( \mathsf { S p } < 3 3 . 7$ and $\bar { T } { < } 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ [Azetsu-Scott et al., 2010, 2012; Tang et al., 2004] occupy the upper $2 0 0 { - } 3 0 0 \ \mathrm { m }$ in CAA (Figure 2). These waters include the polar mixed layer and the upper halocline from the Arctic Ocean and are strongly influenced by sea ice melt and river inflow in CAA.

3. West Greenland Intermediate Waters (WGIW, $\sigma \sim 2 7 . 6 ;$ Figure 2) are characterized by saline $( \mathsf { S p } > 3 4 . 5 )$ ) and warm $( T > 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ waters at intermediate depths $( 3 0 0 - 1 2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ in BB and DS [Azetsu-Scott et al., 2010, 2012; Tang et al., 2004]. These waters flow northward in BB along the Greenland slope. Some of these waters can also be seen on the west side of DS.

# 2.3. Absorbance Analysis

Samples were warmed to room temperature prior to analysis aboard the CCGS Louis S. Saint Laurent in 2008. No data are available in 2009 due to instrument failure. CDOM absorbance measurements were made on a Shimadzu UV 2550 spectrophotometer over a wavelength region of $2 6 0 { - } 7 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ with a $1 0 \mathsf { c m }$ quartz cuvette using Milli-Q water as a reference. In order to correct for instrumental drift and light scattering, an offset correction was applied by subtracting the average absorbance between 650 and $7 0 0 \mathsf { n m }$ from the Milli-Q absorbance spectrum [Gu-eguen et al., 2006, 2012]. The measured absorbance at wavelength k was converted to the absorption coefficient a $( \mathsf { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ according to the relationship:

$$
a _ { \lambda } = 2 . 3 0 3 A _ { \lambda } / \mathrm { L } ,
$$

where $A _ { \lambda }$ is the absorbance and L is the path-length of the optical cell in meters (here $_ { 0 . 1 \textrm { m } }$ ). The absorption coefficients at 350, 355, and $3 7 5 { \mathsf { n m } }$ (a350, a355, and $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 7 5 }$ , respectively) in the surface samples ranged between 0.19 and $0 . 4 5 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , 0.18 and $0 . 4 5 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , and 0.10 and $0 . 3 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively. Similar ranges were reported in earlier studies of the Arctic Ocean [Granskog, 2012; Granskog et al., 2012; Gu-eguen et al., 2005, 2011, 2012; Stedmon et al., 2011]. The values of a350, a355, and $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 7 5 }$ were strongly correlated $( r > 0 . 8 2$ ; $p < 0 . 0 5$ ). Therefore, only $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ data were used for further analysis in this study.

The wavelength dependence of the absorption coefficient was parameterized with a nonlinear exponential model:

$$
a _ { \lambda } { = } a _ { \lambda 0 } \cdot \mathrm e ^ { - S . \lambda } ,
$$

where $a _ { \lambda 0 }$ is the absorbance at reference wavelength and S is the spectral slope. The slope parameter S was calculated over a range of 275–295 nm (S275–295) [Helms et al., 2008] using nonlinear least squares fitting procedures in Matlab.

# 2.4. Fluorescence Analysis

Samples were warmed to room temperature prior to analysis with a Horiba Jobin-Yvon fluorometer (FluoroMax4) using a $1 \subset { \mathsf { m } }$ quartz cuvette with excitation and emission slit widths each set to $5 \mathsf { n m }$ . The quartz cuvette was checked for cleanliness between each sample at excitation wavelengths of 270 and $3 5 0 \mathsf { n m }$ . Fluorescence analysis was performed onshore within 6 months of collection. The excitation-emission matrix (EEM) of each sample was recorded in signal-to-noise mode by collecting a series of emission wavelengths ranging from 250 to $6 0 0 \mathsf { n m }$ at excitation wavelengths ranging from 250 to $5 0 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , both in $5 \mathsf { n m }$ increments. The EEMs were corrected for instrument bias using the correction files provided by the manufacturer, and Raman calibrated by normalizing the intensities to the area under the Raman scatter peak at an excitation wavelength of $3 5 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ for a daily Milli-Q water sample [Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009]. No significant variation in the integral of the Raman peak was observed during the investigation $( 1 . 2 \mathrm { - } 1 . 3 \% )$ . The fluorescence intensity was reported in equivalent water Raman units (R.U.). Reabsorption and inner filter effects were minimized as absorbance values were lower than 0.01 at $2 6 0 \mathsf { n m }$ for all samples collected in 2008. This allowed us to work in the domain where fluorescence intensity varies linearly with concentration [Ewald et al., 1984]. Although no absorbance data were available in 2009 due to instrument failure, fluorescence intensity was not significantly different between both cruises $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ , suggesting negligible reabsorption and inner filter effects in the 2009 data set. Hence, the EEMs were not corrected for inner filter effects.

PARAFAC decomposes the complex mixtures of DOM fluorophores into its main components [Stedmon et al., 2003]. PARAFAC analysis was carried out in Matlab using the DOMFluor toolbox [Stedmon and Bro, 2008] and DrEEM [Murphy et al., 2013], and outlier identification was performed using the OutlierTest function included therein. Scatter peaks were removed using the smootheem function [Murphy et al., 2013].

Twenty-two samples with extreme leverage were found and were excluded from the data set. Determination of the most suitable number of components was achieved by the split-half analysis (Figures S1 and S2) and random initialization [Stedmon et al., 2003] whereby both halves were successfully validated. The model was constrained to non-negative values [Stedmon et al., 2003], and no systematic residuals were found in the modeled EEMs (e.g., Figure S3).

# 3. Results and Discussion

# 3.1. Fluorescence Characterization by PARAFAC

The PARAFAC model revealed four independent fluorescent components (C1–C4) in the DOM data set. Their spectral characteristics were similar to components identified in previous studies (Figure S4). Components 1–3 were characterized as humic-like, as they displayed an emission maximum around and above $4 0 0 \mathsf { n m }$ . Components 1 and 3 were categorized as a mixture of the traditional humic-like peaks A and C [Coble, 1996] which were previously considered to be representative of fluorophores in terrestrial environments. The spectral features of C2 were similar to a component reported as marine humic-like (Peak M) [Coble, 1996]. In recent studies, C2 has also been shown to be evident in DOM that has been altered by microbial reprocessing [Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Yamashita et al., 2008]. Component 4 was characterized as protein-like since it displayed an emission maximum well below $4 0 0 \mathsf { n m }$ .

The number of PARAFAC components and their nature depend on the EEMs used in the model. A larger data set may result in models with a larger number of components that cannot be separated with data containing fewer samples. For example, a study of estuarine DOM with a limited number of samples $( n = 9 0 )$ ) derived five components [Stedmon et al., 2003] while the follow-up study on the same catchment with an extensive data set $( n = 1 2 7 6 )$ revealed eight components [Stedmon and Markager, 2005]. However, given the heterogeneity of CDOM, adding more samples does not always generate more components. This may be the case for data sets containing both surface, intermediate, and deep waters (i.e., this study) as sources contributing to the fluorescence signal differ with depth [Gu-eguen et al., 2007; J-rgensen et al., 2011] augmenting the heterogeneity of the fluorescence signal. The increase in heterogeneity can make it difficult to cross-validate for a large number of components. Despite the difference in number of samples used in PARAFAC analysis, the comparison to previously identified components is meaningful, as it helps assessment of the spatial distribution of a given component and the processes affecting its distribution.

# 3.2. Surface Variation in Salinity, Temperature, CDOM, and FDOM

Figure 3 illustrates the potential temperature, salinity, CDOM $\left( \mathsf { a } _ { 3 5 0 } \right.$ and $\mathsf { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ , and FDOM variations (C1– C4) along the cruise transect at a depth of $5 \mathsf { m }$ in 2008 and 2009. The physical and chemical characteristics of surface water varied along the route between CAA and LS through LSd, BB, and DS; four regions with distinct characteristics were evident:

1. The warm, fresh, and FDOM-rich waters found in western CAA (Coronation Gulf and Queen Maud Gulf; Figure 1) $( 0 \mathrm { - } \sim 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k m } )$ in 2009 were influenced by large CAA river inflows (i.e., Coppermine and Back Rivers, respectively). Indeed, Arctic rivers are typically CDOM-rich and FDOM-rich systems [Gu-eguen et al., 2005, 2011; Mann et al., 2012; Matsuoka et al., 2012; Stedmon et al., 2011; Tank et al., 2011; Walker et al., 2013]. Unfortunately, this region was not sampled in 2008.

2. The cold and fresh surface waters in central and eastern CAA $( 6 0 0 - 1 6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k m } )$ ), with surface temperature $< 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and surface salinity gradually increasing eastward. Although lower salinity waters in CAA have been previously attributed to the influence of ice melt [Fransson et al., 2009; Walker et al., 2009], the relatively high $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ values reported in low-salinity CAA waters suggest the influence of terrigenous sources.

3. LSd, BB, and DS $( \sim 1 6 0 0 - 3 6 0 0 ~ \mathsf { k m } )$ ) with midsalinities (Sp 31.3–33.5) indicative of Arctic outflow [AzetsuScott et al., 2010; Tang et ${ \boldsymbol { a } } { \boldsymbol { I } }$ ., 2004]. Sea ice coverage was also less prevalent in LSd, BB, and DS than in CAA $( < 1 5 - 3 5 \%$ versus $3 5 \mathrm { - } 6 5 \%$ respectively; Canadian Ice Service Archive http://ice-glaces.ec.gc.ca/). This is consistent with a previous study in surface waters around northern North America, where Yamamoto-Kawai et al. [2010] found that both meteoric water and sea ice meltwater fractions were smaller in LSd than in the CAA. In terms of FDOM intensity, this domain was characterized by important spatial variation in FDOM. For example, the LSd and northern BB waters ( $1 6 0 0 { - } 2 1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { k m }$ ) were characterized by very high FDOM signal, while the southern BB waters showed moderate FDOM intensity. The strong spatial variation in fluorescence intensity is the result of mixing processes. Indeed, the LSd surface waters are formed by mixing between both low-DOM CAA waters (Figure 4) [Walker et al., 2009] and Arctic Ocean waters that have entered from the North past Smith Sound and through Nares Strait (Figure 1). These southward flowing waters originate in the western Arctic Ocean and are characterized by relatively high concentrations of terrestrially derived CDOM [Gu-eguen et al., 2012; Walker et al., 2009] and barium [Thomas et al., 2011]. The BB surface waters are influenced by the warm and saline DOM-poor Atlantic waters entering from the south eastern part of LS (Figures 2 and 3) [Muench, 1971], and the outflow of CAA through LSd and the Nares Strait. While the varying level of mixing in LSd and BB explains the large variability in the surface water optical properties, the low variation in optical properties in LS and CAA, identifies these waters as mixing end-members.

# 3.3. Vertical Distributions of CDOM and FDOM

Figure 4 illustrates the vertical distribution of $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ as a function of distance from Coronation Gulf in 2008. The vertical profiles show that the eastward decrease in $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ from CAA and LSd through LS coincided with the eastward increase in $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ . This decrease in molecular weight (i.e., higher S275–295) coupled with an overall decrease in the terrestrial DOM indicator $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ suggests a reduction in terrestrial influence from CAA to LS. This is reasonable because the waters along this transect become further away from land masses and river influence, so that influences of terrestrial runoff and river-borne DOM decrease.

The distribution of humic-like C1 was almost identical to that of C2 (Figure 5), suggesting similar processes affect their distribution throughout the study area. The highest C1–C2 signal was found in the top $5 0 \mathrm { m }$ in LSd and northern BB $( \sim 1 6 0 0 { - } 2 5 0 0 ~ \mathsf { k m } )$ , a result of mixing between the Arctic outflow, the highly productive [Tremblay et al., 2012] northern Baffin Bay near Nares Strait and the west Greenland current [Carmack et al., 2010, and references therein]. The significant drop in C1–C2 intensity observed in LSd and BB between 175 and $^ { 2 0 0 \mathsf { m } }$ coincided with the interface between the cold Arctic outflow and the warmer deep water $( \mathsf { S p } > 3 3 . 6$ and ${ > } 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ arriving in BB via the West Greenland Current from the North Atlantic [Hamilton et al., 2008]. The vertical distribution of the terrestrial-like humic-like C3 did not exhibit a consistent trend with increasing depth northeast of DS. Indeed, no major differences in C3 level were observed between the cold arctic outflow waters and the warmer deeper waters. This suggests that the distribution of the most redshifted terrestrially derived humic-like component (C3; Figure S4) was not directly related to that of terrestrial derived C1. # 3.4. PARAFAC Components in Water Masses

In order to describe the general features of the CDOM dynamics along the $5 7 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ cruise track from the CAA to the LS, FDOM composition is reported for the Arctic outflow, the West Greenland Intermediate water (WGIW), the Bottom Baffin Deep water (BBDW), and the upper Labrador Sea water (uLSW).

# 3.4.3. Bottom Baffin Deep Water

The mean FDOM fluorescence intensity in BBDW $( { \bar { \mathsf { T } } } { \mathsf { p } } < 2 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } } ,$ $S _ { \mathsf { P } } { > } 3 4 . 5$ , $\sigma > 2 7 . 6 5$ ) [Azetsu-Scott et al., 2012] ranged from $0 . 0 1 0 \pm 0 . 0 0 7$ R.U. for C4 to $0 . 0 3 3 \pm 0 . 0 0 2$ R.U. for C1, which is 1.04–2.0 times lower than in BB Arctic outflow (Figures 2 and 5). The BBDW waters were characterized by relatively stable AOU ( $1 6 0 \pm 7$ $\mu \mathrm { m } \mathsf { o l } \mathsf { k } \mathsf { g } ^ { - 1 } )$ . Based on a limited data set $( n = 5$ ), no strong linear relationship between FDOM distribution and AOU was observed in BBDW $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ . These results suggest that in situ regeneration may not a dominant process affecting FDOM distribution in BBDW.

# 3.4.4. Upper Labrador Sea Water

The mean FDOM fluorescence intensity in uLSW $( 2 0 0 - 1 0 0 0 \mathsf { m }$ , $\gamma _ { \mathsf { N } } \sim 2 7 . 8 \substack { - 2 7 . 9 } )$ [Hall et al., 2004] ranged from $0 . 0 0 4 \pm 0 . 0 0 5$ R.U. for C4 to $0 . 0 1 7 \pm 0 . 0 0 3$ R.U. for C1, which is 2–10 times less than in the CAA Arctic outflow (Figure 5). The depletion in uLSW can be explained on the basis of its origin in the marine domain of the North Atlantic. Low $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ $( 0 . 0 7 \pm 0 . 0 3 \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) and high $\mathsf { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } ( 0 . 0 3 1 \pm 0 . 0 0 1 \ \mathsf { n m } ^ { - 1 } )$ (i.e., low molecular weight) [Helms et ${ \boldsymbol { a } } { \boldsymbol { I } }$ ., 2008; Gu-eguen and Cuss, 2011] characterized AW, confirming its oceanic source. Similar $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ values were found in the Northern Atlantic [Fichot et al., 2013].

As in the North Atlantic [J-rgensen et al., 2011; Nelson et al., 2010], uLSW samples displayed very low AOU $( 5 { - } 3 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ k g ^ { - 1 } )$ ), suggesting that these waters were well oxygenated and recently have been in contact with the atmosphere. No strong linear relationship between humic-like C1–C2 distribution and AOU was observed in uLSW $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ . A weak but significant linear increase in C3 intensity was found with increasing AOU concentration $( r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 3$ , $p < 0 . 0 0 5 )$ ), indicating that in situ regeneration can affect C3 distribution in uLSW. This contrasts with previous studies in the North Atlantic [J-rgensen et al., 2011; Nelson et al., 2007, 2010] and on western Greenland shelves [J-rgensen et al., 2011] where no significant relationships were found. It is clear that further work is required before substantial conclusions on humic-like remineralization can be made in LS.

# 4. Summary

This study explored for the first time the dynamics of CDOM and FDOM in the Canadian Arctic Archipelago, Baffin Bay, and the Labrador Sea during the summers of 2008 and 2009. The application of EEM-PARAFAC to DOM collected in the upper ${ \sim } 1 3 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ of the water column showed the presence of four components previously found in fresh and marine waters: three humic-like (C1–C3) and one protein-like (C4). A decrease in molecular weight (i.e., higher $\mathsf { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ ) coupled with an overall decrease in the terrestrial DOM indicator $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ and FDOM humic-like signal suggests a reduction in terrestrial influence as waters move from the CAA to the LS. Vertical profiles of humic-like DOM show the highest levels in the upper $2 0 0 { - } 4 0 0 ~ \mathrm m$ , and the lowest levels in deeper layers in CAA, LSd, BB, and DS. This contrasts with vertical profiles in the LS where more homogenous distributions of humic-like DOM were observed. Terrestrial humic-like FDOM and AOU were significantly correlated, implying that in situ processing and production of terrestrial and microbial humiclike DOM was substantial in the CAA Arctic outflow and WGIW. The correlation slope, however, was dissimilar in the two layers. The degree of the organic matter remineralization was clearly reduced in the Arctic outflow relative to the Atlantic-derived WGIW, confirming the refractory character of FDOM in deeper Arctic-derived waters.
