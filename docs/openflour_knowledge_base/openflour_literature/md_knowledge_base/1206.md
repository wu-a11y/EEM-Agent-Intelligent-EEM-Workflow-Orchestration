# Tracing Organic Footprints from Industrial Effluent Discharge in Recalcitrant Riverine Chromophoric Dissolved Organic Matter

Mikhail Borisover $\cdot$ Yael Laor $\cdot$ Ibrahim Saadi & Marcos Lado $\cdot$ Nadezhda Bukhanovsky

Received: 27 November 2010 /Accepted: 19 April 2011 /Published online: 10 May 2011   
$©$ Springer Science+Business Media B.V. 2011

Abstract Excitation–emission matrix fluorescence spectroscopy, combined with parallel factor analysis and measurements of UV absorption and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations, was used to trace the footprints of industrial effluents discharged into the lower Kishon River (Israel). The lower Kishon River typifies streams that are affected by seawater tidal intrusion and represents an extreme case of severe long-term pollution caused mainly by a variety of industrial effluents. The industrial effluents may contribute about $90 \%$ , in terms of biochemical oxygen demand, of the total organic carbon discharged into the lower Kishon River. Water samples were collected along the river, including the points of effluent discharge from industrial plants, between November 2005 and September 2006. Two types of fluorescent components characterized the fluorescence of the lower Kishon River water: component I corresponded to humic-like matter and component II spectrally resembled material known to be associated with biological productivity, but different from typical tryptophan-like fluorophore. These fluorescent components and other substances that absorbed light at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m }$ contributed to the DOC pool that resisted riverine microbial degradation under laboratory conditions, and that constitutes up to $70 \%$ of the overall riverine DOC. The variations in DOC concentration, absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , and concentration of humiclike matter (characterized by component I) correlated with the distance from the sea and the water electrical conductivity, and were linked to seawater tidal intrusion. The increased concentration of component II, as well as its enlarged fraction in the overall riverine DOC pool, was found to be associated with the location of major inputs of the industrial effluents. These findings support the use of this fluorescent component as an indicator of industrial pollution in such severely contaminated riverine systems.

# 1 Introduction

Aquatic dissolved organic matter (DOM) includes components that absorb light over a broad range of visible and UV wavelengths and are termed colored (chromophoric) DOM (CDOM; Coble 2007). The CDOM may include humic-like substances, proteinaceous matter containing tryptophan, tyrosine, and phenylalanine, and certain xenobiotics such as polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. Components of CDOM can be differentiated according to their ability to absorb and emit light in the visible and UV regions, and many studies have used excitation–emission fluorescence spectra to characterize the nature and sources of DOM in natural and engineered aquatic systems (Coble 1996; Her et al. 2003; Kumke et al. 1999; Stedmon et al. 2003, and many others).

An important application of excitation–emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectroscopy is in detecting and characterizing aqueous pollution, such as contamination of rivers or estuaries. For example, EEM fluorescence spectroscopy was employed to fingerprint the impact of sewage treatment plants on several rivers and provided better fingerprinting than light absorption at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { \ n m }$ (Baker 2001). Holbrook et al. (2005) suggested that the organic matter downstream of a sewage treatment plant had unique fluorescence and perylene sorption characteristics compared to that of upstream organic matter. Distinctly different fluorescent signatures were obtained for aquatic organic matter from domestic wastewater as compared with DOM from soil, algae, and leaf mold (Galapate et al. 1998). In particular, protein-like (tryptophan-like) fluorescence was hypothesized to correlate with water quality when the river contamination was associated with treated sewage, sewer overflow, or agricultural organic pollutants (Baker and Inverarity 2004; Galapate et al. 1998; Holbrook et al. 2005). Based on EEM fluorescence spectroscopy, untreated sewage effluents were considered as the major pollution source in Nanming River, with dominating detergent- and protein-like fluorescent substances (Mostofa et al. 2010). Landfill leachates were correlated with fluorescence of fulviclike and xenobiotic compounds (Baker and Curry 2004; Baker 2005). The ratio of humic-like to protein-like fluorescence was proposed as a means of identifying trace sewage contamination in river waters and estuaries (Henderson et al. 2009). EEM spectroscopy together with multivariate classification techniques was suggested as a promising tool to discriminate among water sampling sites (Hall et al. 2005). In a large series of water samples, including those affected by industrial effluents, the protein-like fluorescence was examined and correlated with biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) (Hudson et al. 2008). The impact of paper industry effluents on riverine EEMs was related to the increased tryptophan fluorescence and fluorescence linked to whitening agents (Baker 2002). Further studies of the fluorescence of protein-like materials and anthropogenic compounds in wastewater and wastewater-contaminated rivers were reviewed by Hudson et al. (2007).

Therefore, significant efforts have been made on using EEM fluorescence spectroscopy for detecting riverine pollution caused by sewage effluents, land leachates, and xenobiotics traces. The literature data demonstrated that water pollution may be tracked based on fluorescence of certain (but not necessarily unique) aquatic CDOM components. However, less attention has been given to the excitation–emission matrices of fluorescence in rivers whose pollution is associated with massive discharges of mostly industrial effluents as distinct from contamination by sewage effluents and land leachates. Such an application of EEM fluorescence spectroscopy for examining riverine pollution by severe and long-term discharge of industrial effluents was the focus of the present study.

The Kishon (or Qishon) River in Israel constitutes an extreme case of severe long-term pollution caused mainly by the discharge of varied industrial effluents; at $7 0 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ in length the Kishon, which drains an area of $1 , 1 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , is one of the largest rivers in Israel. Several heavy industrial plants, including oil refineries, petrochemical, chemical and biochemical plants, and the Haifa municipal wastewater treatment plant discharged their effluents into the lower section of the river for tens of years, and many inorganic and organic pollutants were found in the lower Kishon River and its sediments (Cohen et al. 1993; Herut and Kress 1997; Krumgalz 1993; Oren et al. 2006; Richter et al. 2003).

In the year of the present study (2006), the Kishon River Authority (KRA) (2006) reported a volume of at least 7.3 million $\mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ of industrial effluents (not including those associated with the municipal wastewater treatment plant) discharged into the river, which was almost half of the annual fresh water flow of the Kishon River, including that of its tributary, the Zippory River (Israel Water Commission 2007, 2008). In 2006, industrial effluents contributed about $90 \%$ of the total organic carbon, as expressed as BOD, discharged into the lower Kishon River in the locations monitored by the KRA (2006). Therefore, these effluents were the dominant sources of organic matter pollution in this river section. However, distinct seawater tidal effects (Herut and Kress 1997) can affect, and possibly even mask the distribution of contaminants in the lower Kishon River.

Therefore, the present hypothesis was that the “footprints” of industrial effluent discharged into the lower Kishon River could be traced by looking at the spatial and seasonal dynamics of the riverine CDOM. Specifically, we intended: (a) to examine the impact of the massive discharge of industrial effluents on riverine DOM by means of EEM fluorescence spectroscopy combined with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC; Bro 1997; Stedmon et al. 2003) and measurements of UV absorption and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations; and (b) to evaluate the stability of major fluorescent components detected in the contaminated river water against biodegradation. The expected novel outcome of this study will lie in our improved ability to monitor non-specific industrial pollution using EEM fluorescence spectroscopy, and an improved understanding of the resistance of CDOM components to microbial degradation, in such polluted riverine systems.

# 2 Materials and Methods

# 2.1 Water Sampling and Characterization

The Kishon River originates in the Gilboa mountains, flows through the Jezre’el Valley and reaches the Mediterranean Sea in Haifa Bay (Fig. 1). Surface water was sampled at locations Nos. 1–8 along the river on a monthly basis from November 2005 until April 2006, and in September 2006; these locations are routinely monitored by the KRA (Fig. 1). Samples were collected with a bucket lowered from the riverbank or from a bridge (in the case of location No. 4), and then transferred into 1-L brown glass bottles pre-washed with distilled water. Additional water samples were collected from location No. 6A, which is the discharge point of effluents from Gadot Biochemical Industries.

This sampling program is referred to here as “Monthly Sampling”. For biodegradation assays, surface water samples were collected on May 28, 2006 from additional locations (B1-B5; Fig. 1), at the points of effluent discharge from several of the industrial plants along the lower section of the river (Gadot Biochemical Industries, Haifa Oil Refineries, Haifa Chemicals, and Carmel Olefins). This sampling program is referred to as “Biodegradation”.

On the day of sampling, the collected water samples were filtered through a pre-washed $1 . 6 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ glass-fiber filter (GF/1; Macherey-Nagel, Germany) followed by a $0 . 7 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ glass-fiber filter (GF/F; Whatman), and most of them were analyzed within 2 days after sampling. DOC concentrations were determined by catalyzed combustion, with a TOC-5000 total organic carbon analyzer (Shimadzu) as described by Saadi et al. (2006). UV absorbance was measured at wavelengths from 210 to $6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ in a $1 . 0 \mathrm { - c m }$ quartz cuvette on a CARY 50 Bio UV–Visible Spectrophotometer. Solution absorbance measured at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $\left( \mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 } \right)$ was considered to represent the content of aromatic structures in the overall DOM. The riverine water temperature, pH, electrical conductivity (EC) and dissolved oxygen (DO) concentration are regularly monitored in situ by the KRA (http://www.kishon.org.il/).

Fluorescence spectra of the filtered water samples were measured with an RF-5301PC spectrofluorometer (Shimadzu) equipped with 150-W Xenon lamp (Ushio Inc., Japan) according to Borisover et al. (2009). In brief, excitation–emission matrices (EEM) were generated at $2 3 { \pm } 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , at excitation and emission band-pass slit widths of $5 . 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . Fluorescence emission spectra between 220 and $6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ were collected at $2 { \cdot } \mathrm { n m }$ increments, with excitation wavelengths ranging from 220 to $5 9 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 { \mathrm { - n m } }$ increments. Instrument stability was evaluated by measuring the intensity of the Raman peak of water (29.6 in arbitrary fluorescence units) at excitation/ emission wavelengths of $2 7 0 / 2 9 8 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , with standard deviation of 1.2 units over the analysis period. No instrument-related excitation and emission corrections were introduced; however, the impact of the specific instrumental configuration was consistent across all the samples studied. The $\mathrm { p H }$ of the water samples varied between 7.5 and 8.4, with $\mathrm { p H }$ variability not exceeding 0.4 units on any sampling date. No special $\mathrm { p H }$ adjustments were made before fluorescence analyses.

# 2.2 Biodegradation Assays

Biodegradability of DOM in water samples from locations B1–B5 (Fig. 1) was examined according to Saadi et al. (2006), with some modifications. In brief, the 1-L aliquot of each sample, after filtering as described in Section 2.1, was transferred into an airtight 2-L glass jar. Each jar was inoculated with a mixed culture, obtained by filtering $1 \textrm { L }$ of river water (from B1; location No. 3) through a $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter (Schleicher & Schuell). Then, the jars were incubated in the dark for 10 days at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . In this setup, the 1-L headspace in the 2-L jar provides a sufficient amount of oxygen for aerobic oxidation of the DOM, i.e., over $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m g } }$ of oxygen was available for about $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ of DOC. To obtain sterile control samples, aliquots, after filtering as described in Section 2.1, were additionally filtered through $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ pre-washed filter (Schleicher & Schuell). To suppress microbial activity, these control samples were amended with sodium azide to a final concentration of $3 \mathrm { m M }$ . During the incubation, $1 0 { \mathrm { - m L } }$ subsamples were withdrawn from the jars after 0, 1, 3, and 10 days of incubation. Aliquots were taken for bacterial counts $\mathrm { R } _ { 2 } \mathrm { A }$ agar, incubation for $2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ). Then, the samples were filtered through a pre-washed $0 . 4 5 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ syringe filter (Schleicher & Schuell) for further analyses that comprised determination of DOC, UV absorbance, and fluorescence emission. Biodegradation in any sample, including the sterilized ones, was terminated by filtration through a $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter and storage at $4 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ in the dark, pending analysis.

# 2.3 Fluorescence Data Examination with PARAFAC

For examination of EEMs, PARAFAC was applied as described previously (Andersen and Bro 2003;

Borisover et al. 2009; Bro 1997; Ohno and Bro 2006; Stedmon et al. 2003). A PARAFAC analysis involves a number of fluorescent components which control the overall fluorescence of the water samples, excitation and emission spectra of these components, and their concentration scores in various samples. Fluorescent components isolated by means of PARAFAC represent detectable groups of substances with similar fluorescence properties, rather than individual compounds. Concentration scores are proportional to the true concentrations of the respective fluorescent components.

PARAFAC analyses were conducted with the Matlab toolbox (Matlab 6.5) for the N-way PARAFAC model (Andersson and Bro 2000); the toolbox is freely available via http://www.models.kvl.dk/ source/nwaytoolbox/. For each combination of excitation and emission wavelengths the measured EEMs were corrected for the “inner filter effect” (IFE), which is related to light absorption by the solution itself (Lakowicz 1999). Corrections were applied by using UV absorbances of the respective water samples, according to Gauthier et al. (1986). To reduce the impacts of instrumental noise and IFE, only emissions obtained at excitation wavelengths exceeding $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ were considered. For two water samples associated with location 6A, which exhibited significant light absorption above $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , the cutoff was shifted to $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The IFE corrections did not exceed a factor of 2 in any of the water samples analyzed. For EEM preprocessing, zero emission intensities were assigned for $\lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } < \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } }$ . The Rayleigh and Raman scattering effects were taken into account and the constraints in the PARAFAC analysis were selected as described by Borisover et al. (2009).

# 3 Results and Discussion

# 3.1 PARAFAC-Based Assessment of EEMs

A total of 119 EEMs, comprising 61 from the “Monthly Sampling” and 58 from the “Biodegradation” series, were included in one PARAFAC analysis. After PARAFAC fittings with various numbers of fluorescent components, it was found that only one sample (collected from site 6A in September 2006) exhibited significant and systematic deviations from the various models at distinctly significant leverages, and therefore it was removed from the dataset. The core consistency (CC) values (Andersen and Bro 2003) obtained for the remaining data set, which comprised 118 samples, were 100, 99.9, and 74.7, as the number of fluorescent components increased from 1 to 3; a reasonable CC was considered to lie between 80 and 100 (Andersen and Bro 2003). A further increase in the number of fitted components resulted in smaller and unstable CC values, i.e., they varied substantially in replicated runs. When two or three fluorescent components were used, $9 7 . 0 \%$ or $9 8 . 4 \%$ of the fluorescence variability among the 118 samples could be explained, respectively.

Figure 2 presents the excitation and emission fluorescence spectra of two hypothesized components that were obtained by using the whole data set. In addition, excitation and emission fluorescence spectra of two components were obtained independently by applying the PARAFAC fitting to two separate subsets: “Monthly Sampling” and “Biodegradation”. As seen in Fig. 2, excitation and emission fluorescence spectra of both components, obtained from separately analyzed subsets and from the whole data set, resemble each other. Separation of the whole dataset into two random halves, i.e., a split-half check, resulted in even closer correspondence between excitation and emission spectra of the components obtained from the full data set and those obtained from the two halves independently (not shown).

Fitting three components by means of PARAFAC analysis did not yield comparable results when the whole data set was used, and when separate “Monthly Sampling” and “Biodegradation” subsets were used: differing excitation and emission spectra of at least one component were obtained in repeated PARAFAC fitting runs for the “Biodegradation” subset. This instability represents the dependence of a minimum of a fitting function on the initial parameters of a model, and, in this case, limits use of the model to not more than two components.

After 10 days of incubation the DOC concentration was reduced by $22 \%$ , $32 \%$ , $1 8 \%$ , $34 \%$ , and $2 6 \%$ in water samples from B1 to B5, respectively. This reduction consistently exceeded the losses observed in control vessels containing sterile samples: $9 \%$ , $24 \%$ , $- 8 \%$ , $1 5 \%$ , and $1 5 \%$ in B1 to B5, respectively. The DOC reduction in sterile samples could be related to the precipitation of some soluble matter (which was visually observed). The net biodegradable fraction was thus estimated to be only between $7 \%$ and $2 6 \%$ of the total DOC.

The change in UV absorbance $( \mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 } )$ during the 10 days of incubation barely exceeded the measurement errors. The constant UV absorbance, together with the above-mentioned decrease in DOC, suggests that UV-absorbing (aromatic) molecules were relatively resistant to degradation, so that their relative fraction increased. These small differences indicate that both fluorescent components were resistant to biodegradation.

The resistance of component I to biodegradation was expected, because of its assumed humic-like nature. No further indications to maximum values were revealed by examination of spatial distribution of such DOM compositional characteristics as: Specific UV absorbance, i.e., $( \mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 } / \mathrm { D O C } ) \times 1 0 0$ ; F1 normalized by DOC concentration; F1 and F2 scores normalized by $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (not shown) and the F1/F2 ratio (Fig. 4b). So, from location No. 8 to location No. 6, F2 increases but F1 decreases, and then, downstream of location No. 6, both F1 and F2 demonstrated similar dynamics (Fig. 3) such that the F1/F2 ratio was approximately constant (Fig. 4b). Components I (humic-like) and II, considered to represent labile organic matter (Coble et al. 1998; Stedmon et al. 2003), are expected to exhibit differing chemistry and reactivity, e.g., humic-like substances are more photodegradable than protein-like fluorescing compounds (Moran et al. 2000; Mostofa et al. 2007). Therefore, the approximate constancy of the F1/F2 ratio, while both F1 and F2 declined strongly downstream of location No. 4 (Fig. 3), suggests the presence of a relatively conservative mixing of both fluorescent components with saline seawater.

The input of saline seawater (and discharged effluents) is reflected in the variations in salinity represented by EC values, along the river (Fig. 3). Between locations Nos. 8 and 6, there was an increase in EC caused by discharged effluents and, possibly, by seawater intrusion. Then, EC remained relatively constant between locations Nos. 6 and 3, except for the February data at location No. 3, and strongly increased again as the estuary was approached (location No. 1), because of seawater penetration and mixing with river water. The variability in the effect of mixing riverine water with seawater and discharged effluents, in different sampling dates (at the same location), may contribute to the variability of the DOM composition. That is why the use of EC instead of the distance from the sea as a regressor improved the strength of correlations for F1 and $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Table 2). However, the use of EC instead of the distance from the sea did not improve the strength of a correlation for DOC (not shown), possibly because DOC includes a biodegradable fraction. In mixing riverine water with seawater, DOC should be less conservative as compared with recalcitrant CDOM.

Statistically significant linear correlations between F1, $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , and EC were also found when the data was separated in series, i.e., (a) monthly samplings at a given location, and (b) samplings at different locations along the river on a given sampling date. For example, correlations between F1 and EC were found in November 2005, January 2006, February 2006, March 2006, April 2006, and in locations Nos. 1 and 2 (with statistical significance at $p { \le } 0 . 0 1$ , 0.01, 0.05, 0.01, 0.01, 0.01, 0.05, respectively). Statistically significant linear correlations between $\mathsf { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and EC were observed in November 2005, December 2005, January 2006, April 2006, and at location No. 1 (with $p { \le } 0 . 0 5$ , 0.01, 0.01, 0.01, 0.05, respectively).

In principle, increases in salinity and ionic strength of a solution could also result in fluorescence quenching of certain CDOM components, and, consequently, in apparent decreases in their concentrations as expressed in F1 and F2. However, Sheng and $\mathrm { Y u } \left( 2 0 0 6 \right)$ noted that, in solutions with $0 . 1 \mathrm { M N a C l }$ , ionic strength did not have a substantial effect on protein- and humiclike fluorescence. Mobed et al. (1996) concluded that the ionic strength of KCl solutions at $0 { - } 1 . 0 \mathrm { M }$ concentrations had no significant influence on the EEM of aquatic- and soil-derived humic substances, and Henderson et al. (2009) reported that $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ and $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ did not quench tryptophan-like fluorescence. Thus, in the present study, CDOM fluorescence quenching caused by increasing salinity is expected to be negligible.

# 3.4 Fluorescent Component II as a Tracer of Non-Specific Industrial Pollution: Considerations of Possible Nature

The maximum in the spatial distribution of component II (Figs. 3 and 4a) might indicate a supply of nutrients provided by the discharge of effluents, and consequently enhanced biological activity. It is doubtful that an increased concentration of this component at location No. 6 represents certain non-degraded industrial chemicals released with effluents since significant concentrations of this component were also observed at locations No. 7 and, specifically, No. 8, i.e., prior to the massive discharge of industrial effluents (Fig. 1). In particular, the supply of nutrients capable of stimulating biological activity could be related to discharged wastewater from Haifa Chemicals which produces potassium nitrate, phosphates, and fertilizers for agriculture and from the Oil Refineries which processes crude oil (Fig. 1). Haifa Chemicals and Oil Refineries are responsible for the major part of effluents discharged between locations Nos. 6 and 7, i.e., contributing by $22 \%$ and $54 \%$ respectively, to the total discharge reported in 2006 (KRA 2006). Effluents from both industries are also major contributors of organic matter as expressed as BOD ( $3 6 \%$ each, KRA 2006).

Fluorescent component II is characterized by the same excitation wavelength of the maximal fluorescence, i.e., $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , as the protein-like (tryptophan-like) fluorophore that has been linked to contamination of rivers by treated sewage and agricultural organic pollutants (Baker and Inverarity 2004; Hudson et al. 2007). Nevertheless, component II differs from the typical tryptophan-like fluorophore in the emission wavelength of the fluorescence maximum, i.e., $3 6 4 ~ \mathrm { { \ n m } }$ and $3 4 0 { - } 3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { ~ n m }$ , respectively. This difference could be an indication that component II is a mixture of proteinaceous and nonproteinaceous substances (Stedmon et al. 2003), where the latter could protect the proteinaceous component against biodegradation (see Section 3.2). For example, based on literature sources, Yamashita and Tanoue (2003) indicated that free amino acids and proteins added to seawater become incorporated into a refractory DOM and protected against microbial degradation. Another explanation for the difference between the emission maximum of component II and that of typical tryptophan-like fluorophore could be related to the bathochromic (red) shift as induced by polarity and composition of a local environment. Specifically, this red shift could reflect an increasing exposure of tryptophan from proteinaceous matter to the solvent (Vivian and Callis 2001). However, if this would be the case, a change of solvent polarity or composition in a microenvironment that affects an energetic gap between ground and excited states should also involve a shift in the excitation maximum. Yet, no such shift was observed, and the excitation maximum of component II (i.e. $2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ) is typical for tryptophan fluorescence. Hence, more investigations are needed to elucidate the variability of excitation and emission maxima of dissolved proteinaceous matter in various aquatic environments.

# 3.5 Spatial Variability of DOM in the Lower Kishon River: Relations Between Different DOM Characteristics

Since DOC concentration, F1, and $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ exhibited a certain concomitant dependence on the distance from the sea, mutual relationships among them could be expected and, in fact, statistically significant linear dependencies were observed for F1 vs. DOC (with low $r ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 1 6$ , F1 vs. $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ $scriptstyle \gamma ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 2$ ; Fig. 5a), and $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ vs. DOC $\scriptstyle \overbrace { r ^ { 2 } } = 0 . 4 3$ ; Fig. 5b). Parameters of these linear regressions are provided in Table 2.

Linear relations similar to those shown in Fig. 5a, b were frequently reported in the literature. For example, relationships between CDOM absorbance and DOC concentration or fluorescence intensity were found by Yacobi et al. (2003) and Zhang et al. (2007). A strong link between DOC concentration and humic-like fluorescence in a stream was found by $\mathrm { W u }$ et al. (2007). Statistically significant correlations between total organic carbon, absorbance (at $4 1 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ) and fluorescence intensity of tryptophan- and fulvic-like substances were demonstrated by Baker (2005) in a landfill leachate contaminated river. Furthermore, several examples of correlations between fluorescence intensity, absorbance, and DOC concentration were also discussed by Coble (2007). In the present study, the links between F1 and $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Fig. 5a) and between DOC and $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Fig. 5b) indicate a parallelism in changes of these variables in the studied water samples (e.g., due to the mixing of riverine water and seawater) and may also reflect a dominant contribution of humic-like substances to absorbance measured at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { { \ n m } }$ and a significant fraction of CDOM in the total pool of DOM.

A multiple regression analysis was additionally used in order to examine the changes in DOC, F1, F2, and $\mathsf { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ . For that, each of these four variables was analyzed in a multiple linear model as a function of all the other measured parameters, i.e., DOC, F1, F2, $\mathsf { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , distance from the sea, EC, pH, DO, and temperature. In this analysis, the regressors contributing significantly at $p { \le } 0 . 0 5$ were found first, and then fitting was carried out again to include only the regressors contributing significantly at $p { \le } 0 . 0 1$ . In the result of this two-step fitting procedure, the only significant regressions were found for a variable from the group of DOC concentration, F1 and $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ linked with the two remaining variables (Table 2). No statistically significant effects of any regressors were found for F2 using this specific procedure. The significant regression equations relating DOC concentration, F1, and $\mathsf { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Table 2) suggest a link between processes controlling the variability of total DOC, fluorescent humic-like matter, and substances absorbing light at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . Negative coefficients in multiple regression equations for DOC concentration and F1 (Table 2) may be indicative of formation of humic-like substances via transformation of DOC and consequent reduction of DOC concentration.

Multiple regression analysis was also used to characterize water quality in terms of DO. Baker (2005) suggested that DO could be related to fluorescence of certain xenobiotic substances originating from landfill leachate. The DO of water samples from the lower Kishon River positively correlated with aqueous $\mathrm { p H }$ and $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Table 2). It may indicate that the greater the content of aromatic components, the greater the concentration of biodegradation-resistant substances and, therefore, the lower the oxygen consumption needed for DOM biodegradation.

# 3.6 Seasonal DOM Variability

The seasonal variability of DOM along the lower Kishon River was examined via DOC concentrations, $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , and concentration scores of fluorescent components F1 and F2. These parameters are plotted in Fig. 6 for different sampling locations against sampling date (day/month). In many streams and rivers, increased DOC concentrations were reported in periods of increased riverine discharges (e.g., Wu et al. 2007), but such a positive relation between DOC concentrations and the extent of riverine discharge was not observed in the present study.

Figure 6 shows that for a given sampling location (except No. 6A) changes in $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and F1 between sampling dates follow similar dynamics. An increase in both $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and F1 was observed from November (2005) to January (2006), followed by a reduction in the $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and F1 in all the locations, with the second rise from March (2006) to April (2006). This similarity in seasonal changes of the parameters $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and F1 could be expected in light of the observed correlations between these variables (Table 2 and Fig. 5).

Water samples from location 6A were typically characterized by DOC concentrations, $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values, and F1 and F2 concentration scores higher than those in other locations (Fig. 6). When comparing the values of these parameters at location 6A in different sampling dates, the UV absorbance and concentration scores of fluorescent components appear inversely related to DOC concentrations. For example, the DOC concentration increased from November (2005) to December (2005), while the values of $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , F1, and F2 decreased. DOC decreased from December (2005) to January (2006), and $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , F1, and F2 increased in this period. This opposite seasonal trend (except for the period between January and February 2006; Fig. 6) could reflect a link between primary DOM degradation (and concomitant DOC reduction) and accumulation of fluorescent aromatic byproducts of biodegradation. The formation of fluorescent matter during biodegradation of DOM originating from Haifa wastewater treatment plant (which is one of the effluent sources in the Kishon River, Fig. 1) was assumed also by Saadi et al. (2006). No such inverse seasonal relationship between the dynamics of DOC concentration and the parameters $\mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , F1, and F2 could be seen at other locations along the river (Nos. 1–8), which suggests the prevalence of site-specific seasonal DOM dynamics at the effluent discharge site at location 6A.

# 4 Conclusions

The lower Kishon River typifies streams that combine continuous heavy loading of industrial effluents with the effects of seawater tidal intrusion.

The riverine CDOM characterized by absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { \ n m }$ and two EEM-derived fluorescent components is resistant to microbial degradation under laboratory conditions and contributed to the recalcitrant DOC pool that forms up to $70 \%$ of the overall riverine DOC. Dilution of the river water with intruding seawater is an important factor controlling the distribution of DOC and CDOM components along the river.

& Fluorescent component II linked to biological activity can be a useful indicator of non-specific river pollution caused by massive discharge of industrial effluents. The use of this fluorescent tracer may expand the potential of EEM fluorescence spectroscopy for monitoring various types of water pollution.
