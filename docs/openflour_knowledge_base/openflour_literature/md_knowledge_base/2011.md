Green manure (GM) cultivation and incorporation (i.e., GM management) may change soil organic matter (SOM) composition and the agroecosystem functioning. However, the understanding of GM effects on SOM composition, specifically in deeper soil layers, is limited. The objectives of this study were to examine the effects of GM management (as part of an organic agriculture practice), following two years of various doses of compost application, on the changes in SOM and water-extractable organic matter (WEOM) compositional characteristics up to a soil depth of $6 0 \mathrm { c m }$ . Soil samples from a two years compost amended field that was subsequently subjected to GM management were taken in four intervals to a depth of $6 0 \mathrm { c m }$ (0–5, 5–15, 15–30 and $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 ~ { \mathrm { c m } } ,$ ) and characterized for organic C content and SOM composition, by FT-IR transmission. Characterization of WEOM included excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) of fluorescence, UV absorbance and dissolved organic C (DOC) measurements. The response of SOM and WEOM to GM management resulted in some opposite trends. The SOM became rather aromatic, with aliphatic CH-containing structures contributing to a greater extent to SOM composition at deeper layers following GM management. By contrast, GM management resulted in (i) a substantial increase in dissolved organic C concentration, (ii) WEOM becoming enriched by hydrophilic aliphatic organic compounds, and (iii) aromatic and fluorescent components increasingly being found at deeper soil layers and hydrophilic aliphatic components at the surface soil. Fluorescent portion of WEOM became enriched by relatively less biodegradable and weaker-soil adsorbing humic-like components; this enrichment increased in deeper soil layers. The effects of past compost application rates on the changes in SOM and WEOM characteristics were either non-significant or negative. In the latter case, an increase in application rate decreased the changes in SOM content of hydrophilic groups and in the fractions of fluorescent components in WEOM. Growing and incorporating plant biomass may mask, at least on a short time scale, the effects of earlier compost applications on changes in SOM and WEOM compositional characteristics.

# 1. Introduction

Green manure (GM) practice, involving growing cover crops, their uprooting and incorporation into the soil, has long been used in agriculture and, specifically, in organic farming, aiming at increasing soil organic C and N contents (Hill, 1926; Pieters, 1927; Cherr et al., 2006). Use of GM is known to increase soil productivity and crop quality, contribute to C sequestration and N accumulation with reduced losses compared to those of mineral N, to allow a slow N release, enhance microbial biomass and rates of enzyme activity in soil and to increase aggregate stability (Aulakh et al., 2001; Becker et al., 1995; Danga et al., 2009; Drinkwater et al., 1998; Elfstrand et al., 2007; Kimetu et al., 2008; Krull et al., 2004; Montemurro et al., 2013; Cherr et al., 2006). Contrary to freshly applied or composted animal manure, GM application allows avoiding excessive P loading and salinization (Cherr et al., 2006; Zhang et al., 2011).

Organic amendments have the potential to change soil organic matter (SOM) composition, and, therefore, the agroecosystem functioning and adoption of best possible management practices (Peltre et al., 2017). This potential could be linked to the nature of organic input sources which, in turn, influences the biodegradation of the organic matter added, and, thus, further turnover of SOM (KögelKnabner, 2002, 2016; Leifeld et al., 2002; Beraud et al., 2005; Antil et al., 2011; Crème et al., 2016). Therefore, it is of interest to study the compositional characteristics of SOM under GM incorporation. Examination of the top soil from a long-term field experiment involving different organic amendments revealed that isotopic composition of SOM changed with adding GM but no changes in extractable humic substances, as compared with non-amended soils, were detected by means of FT-IR measurements (Gerzabek et al., 1997). On the other hand, soil content of labile humic substances was related to the chemical composition of GM, as expressed in terms of C to N ratio, cellulose and lignin contents (Tripolskaja et al., 2014). The combination of GM with straw and mineral N fertilization led to an enrichment of carboxyl groups in SOM in top soils (Ellerbrock et al., 1999). Effect of GM (among other organic amendments) was examined on SOM pools and C13 natural abundances in various particle-size fractions from top soil (Gerzabek et al., 2001). The composition of water-extractable organic matter (WEOM) obtained from top soil samples and characterized by means of excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) of fluorescence was hardly affected by GM (and other) amendments; alternatively, changes in WEOM, if any, occurred among non-fluorescent WEOM components (Ohno and Bro, 2006). Carbohydrate content of SOM was enhanced by applying GM treatment within lab incubation of soil samples (Zubair et al., 2012). Accumulation of soil organic C affected by GM and its association with different size-aggregates in the $_ { 0 - 1 0 0 \mathrm { c m } }$ depth profile was a part of a work by Blanco-Canqui et al. (2017), but this study did not examine changes in SOM composition. When pyrolysis gas chromatography was applied to soil samples, a difference was seen between soils treated with mineral fertilizer (ammonium nitrate) and GM, which transpired in higher levels of formed benzene, phenol and pyrrole in the latter case (Macci et al., 2012). The formation of these products was linked to the GM-stimulated organic matter turnover and humification. Transformation of SOM associated with its labile fractions in surface soil from 13 tomato fields managed with a series of organic amendments including vetch could be related to changes in aliphatic CH abundance determined by infrared absorbance (Margenot et al., 2015). However, despite some results were reported regarding the effects of GM application on SOM composition, systematic studies are yet desirable, especially because the investigations on GM application paid attention mostly to the top soil layer. Examination of changes in SOM composition in deeper layers of soil under GM application is also of interest and is, therefore, novel. This is particularly interesting in cases of in situ cultivation and incorporation of green manure, as leguminous roots like vetch can reach depth of tens of centimeters (Ramirez-Garcia et al., 2015) and degrade slower than shoot biomass (Puget and Drinkwater, 2001).

Recently, we have characterized organic matter composition within $_ { 0 - 6 0 \mathrm { c m } }$ of the soil profile in an organically managed field subjected to multiple compost applications, using spectroscopic techniques requiring no or minimal soil sample pre-treatment (i.e., transmission FTIR spectroscopy of the whole soil samples, EEMs of fluorescence and absorbance measurements of WEOM; Sharma et al., 2017). Enrichment of compost-treated soil by hydrophobic aliphatic CH-containing organic matter and water-extractable aromatic and humic-like components, decrease of the SOM content of hydrophilic groups due to compost addition (with their elevation in deeper soil layers), were among the findings of this work. Continuation of that field experiment involved in situ cultivation and incorporation of leguminous green manure (vetch), whose ability to enrich the soil with N and to enhance soil fertility is well recognized (Drinkwater et al., 1998; Danga et al., 2009).

Further dynamics of SOM and WEOM in the aforementioned experimental platform may naturally be affected both by the history of earlier compost application and by recently added GM. Therefore, the objectives of this study were to examine whether (i) the changes (if any) in SOM and WEOM compositional characteristics following GM rotation are related to earlier compost applications, and (ii) these changes may also appear in subsoil layers (below $_ { 0 - 2 0 \mathrm { c m } }$ soil layer). These objectives were addressed by using IR, fluorescence and UV absorbance spectroscopies to characterize organic matter in soil sampled to a depth $6 0 \mathrm { c m }$ .

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study site, soil characterization and experimental design

We used the same experimental field platform that was studied by Sharma et al. (2017) and is described therein in detail. Briefly, the platform, located at Newe Ya'ar Research Center, the Jezre'el Valley, northern Israel, was originally established in late 2009 to examine factors which affect soil fertility under organic management. Local climate conditions and selected properties of the soil (a Vertisol-type, classified as Chromic Haploxeret, fine clayey, montmorillonitic, thermic) are reported by Sharma et al. (2017). The experimental field comprises a total area of one hectare where a randomized block design was used to accommodate four compost treatments (0 [control], 20, 40, and $6 0 \mathrm { { m ^ { 3 } h a } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { { y r } ^ { - 1 } ) } }$ , in five replicate plots. The control treatment was fertilized with synthetic fertilizer following soil tests and the recommended doses in conventional agriculture.

The field was converted to organic management, first, by growing vetch in 2009 and applying it as GM in 2010. Then, in 2010, a $6 0 \mathrm { { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { { h a } ^ { - 1 } } }$ of compost was uniformly applied to all the plots, except the control treatments, and incorporated to a depth of $1 0 { - } 1 5 \mathrm { c m }$ . Between 2011 and 2012, wheat, corn and sunflower were grown, and the soil was fertilized either with compost (in 2011, 2012; at the application levels specified above) or with urea (control). Compost characterization, details on fertilization and C and N quantities added with GM (vetch) in 2010 are provided in detail by Sharma et al. (2017). Vetch (Vicia faba cv. Sadot) serving as a leguminous GM and as an additional source of N besides compost was sown at $7 0 ~ \mathrm { K g }$ per hectare on November 22nd, 2012. The vetch was harvested on April 14th, 2013 and left on soil surface for two weeks until April 28th, 2013 on which it was incorporated into a depth of $_ { 1 5 \mathrm { c m } }$ by a disk harrow and thereafter the soil was flattened by a roller. The above ground dry biomasses of the vetch in the control, 20, 40, and $6 0 \mathrm { \ m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ treatments were 7.49, 7.76, 7.87 and $7 . 7 6 ~ \mathrm { t } \mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively and were not significantly different among treatments $( \mathtt { p } \le 0 . 0 5 )$ ).

The present study examined soil samples taken on May 21st, 2013, 23 days after vetch incorporation and 8 days following additional soil disk harrowing. The time selected for soil sampling (23 days after GM incorporation; i.e., 37 days after vetch harvesting) was a compromise between, on one hand, the time required to let decomposition process to stabilize and, on the other hand, the fact that sampling of the Newe Ya'ar clayey soil becomes difficult after a long drying period. Based on literature data from warm climate regions (Njunie et al., 2004; Danga et al., 2009; Danga et al., 2013), the optimal time for sampling may vary between 4 and 6 weeks after biomass incorporation, however most of the decomposition takes place in the first 3–4 weeks. Hence, the 23 days period was considered to be representative for residues decomposition.

The soil was sampled from four depths: 0–5, 5–15, 15–30 and $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 \ { \mathrm { c m } }$ . Altogether a total of 80 soil samples (4 treatments $\times 4$ depths $\times 5$ replicate plots) were collected, air-dried, passed through a $2 \mathrm { m m }$ -sieve and stored in the refrigerator at $4 { - } 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ .

# 2.2. Organic matter characterization

Total organic C (TOC) content of soil samples was determined by dry combustion with a Flashea™ 1112 NC elemental analyzer (Thermo Fi Elementar, Hanau, Germany). Examination of the whole pre-dried soil samples by FT-IR spectroscopy was carried out using KBr (Fluka Analytical) pellets $( 1 3 \mathrm { m m } )$ that were placed in a Bruker Tensor 27 Spectrometer and scanned in the range $4 0 0 0 { \mathrm { - } } 4 0 0 { \mathrm { c m } } ^ { - 1 }$ with $4 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ resolution, and 200 scans on each acquisition. The FT-IR measurements and the data pre-processing were performed following Bernier et al. (2013) and the full procedure is described in detail by Sharma et al. (2017).

Soil extracts containing WEOM were obtained by shaking $1 0 \ : g$ of the soil samples with $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of de-ionized water for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ on a horizontal shaker at $1 4 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ (modified after Maxin and Kogel-Knabner, 1995), with further centrifuging for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at a relative centrifugal force of 3430. The solutions filtered through a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ polyethersulfone membrane filter (Bonna-Agela Technologies) were characterized for dissolved organic C (DOC) concentration with Formacs HT Combustion TOC analyzer and for absorbencies in a $^ { 1 \mathrm { { c m } } }$ quartz cuvette with a Genesis 10UV (Thermo Scientific) spectrophotometer. The specific UV absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ (SUVA, $\mathrm { L } \ : \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { \Sigma }$ ) was determined according to $\mathrm { S U V A } = 1 0 0 \times \mathrm { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 } / \mathrm { D O C }$ where the optical path length is $^ { 1 \mathrm { c m } }$ , and DOC is in $ { \mathbf { m } }  { \mathbf { g } }  { \mathrm { L } } ^ { - 1 }$ .

The EEMs of fluorescence were determined for the WEOM solutions in a $^ { 1 \mathrm { c m } }$ fluorescence-free quartz cuvette using spectrofluorometer (Shimadzu RF-5301PC) equipped with 150-W Xenon lamp (Ushio Inc., Japan). When necessary, the solutions were pre-diluted to obtain the absorbencies $< 0 . 0 5$ at $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The $\mathtt { p H }$ of the aqueous soil extracts prepared for the fluorescence measurements varied between 7.0 and 7.4. The protocol of measurements was identical to that used by Sharma et al. (2017). The excitation and emission band-pass slit widths were $5 . 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . Emission spectra were determined between 220 and $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $2 \mathrm { - n m }$ increments, whereas excitation wavelengths varied from 220 to $5 9 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at ${ 5 } { \mathrm { - } } { \mathrm { } } { \mathrm { n m } }$ increments.

The EEMs data were decomposed into the contributions of separate fluorescent components, based on parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis (Andersen and Bro, 2003; Stedmon et al., 2003) by applying the DOMFluor Toolbox-v1-7-2009 (Stedmon and Bro, 2008) for Matlab $\mathrm { R 2 0 0 7 b }$ . The EEM data pre-processing and the PARAFAC analysis were performed as described by Cohen et al. (2014). Among other procedures, the pre-processing included normalizing the emission intensities by the Raman scattering intensity of pure water at $2 7 0 / 2 9 8 \mathrm { n m }$ , determined at the same day of WEOM analysis (Borisover et al., 2009, 2011; Cohen et al., 2014). In the PARAFAC analysis, only emission intensities at excitation wavelengths above $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ were used, in order to exclude absorbance effects at lower excitation wavelengths. One outlying sample (out of 80) was removed from the PARAFAC analysis. In reporting emission intensities of separated components, the factor of dilution used for preparation of WEOM solution was accounted for.

# 2.3. Statistical analysis

Examination of changes in the various soil and SOM characteristics was carried out by using a General Linear Model module (GLM; Statistica 7.0, Statsoft Inc.) which allows investigating differences between groups/samples and relations between variables. The GLM was applied to differences between a given soil property (SOM, WEOM) measured under GM management and that measured prior to growing vetch. Within the GLM, these differences are considered as a linear function of predictors, either continuous or categorical, and the contributions associated with interactions between predictors, if any. In the GLM analysis performed in this work, the depth of soil sampling and the rate of past compost application carried out prior to the GM cropping and incorporation in 2013 were used as continuous predictors. The spatial variability between plots was represented by considering blocks as categorical predictors. For the categorical predictors a sigma-restricted parameterization was used in which the code −1 was assigned to block 5 and $^ { + 1 }$ to any other block, such that the sum of codes of the groups under comparison was zero (Electronic Statistics Textbook, StatSoft, accessed at 20.05.2016). This way, block differences were evaluated in reference to block 5, with the model coefficients describing the impacts of these differences. Only GLM results characterized by probability level $\mathbf { p }$ not exceeding 0.05 were considered significant in the work. Testing interactions between the predictors was performed by using the factorial design, however, no statistically significant contributions of these interactions to the target function were found. Hence, the terms associated with interactions between predictors were removed from the final model.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Soil organic matter

# 3.1.1. Total organic carbon (TOC) content

In the soil layers within the $_ { 0 - 3 0 \mathrm { c m } }$ depth TOC tended to increase, due to compost application in the years prior to the GM rotation of 2013 (Fig. 1). In order to demonstrate the changes following GM management, the TOC values determined in a soil sample (from a given plot, depth and history of compost treatment) after GM management (the 2013 samples) were plotted against those values obtained from the same plots prior to GM management, i.e., in samples obtained in October 2012, a month after harvesting the sunflower that was grown during 2012 (the latter data were taken from Sharma et al., 2017) (Fig. 2). The TOC values of the 2013 samples tended to exceed the TOC values of the 2012 samples, thus reflecting an expected effect of GM management. The median value of the relative increase in TOC was $2 0 \%$ . In total, a relative increase in TOC under GM management was observed in $7 9 \%$ of the compared samples.

In the long term, increase in soil TOC due to organic farming, and specifically due to GM management, was reported to be limited to the upper ${ 1 5 \mathrm { c m } }$ soil layer (Blanco-Canqui et al., 2017). It is clearly seen from the data in Fig. 2 that in our short-term field experiment, 37 days after vetch harvesting and 23 days after its incorporating into soil, increase in TOC was detected also in the $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 \ { \mathrm { c m } }$ layer.

We also examined changes in TOC content, by subtracting a value determined for a given treatment in 2012 (data taken from Sharma et al., 2017) from the value obtained for the same treatment in 2013. The median value of the differences in TOC contents was $0 . 2 6 \% \mathrm { ~ w ~ w ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ . A GLM test was also used to examine the observed changes in TOC following the GM management. The GLM parameters, including the model coefficients, their $9 5 \%$ confidence limits, and the multiple correlation coefficients with their significance levels, are presented in Table 1. When changes in TOC values were examined (Table 1), the multiple correlation coefficient (r) was found significant at $\mathrm { ~ p ~ < ~ } 0 . 0 5$ . However, the $9 5 \%$ confidence limits of all the GLM coefficients included zero, thus indicating that none of the factors demonstrated significant linear links with the observed changes in TOC under GM management (as compared with the data obtained in 2012). This observation suggests that the observed difference in TOC level between 2013 and 2012 might have resulted from factors other than compost dose, soil depth and blocks.

# 3.1.2. SOM characterization by means of IR absorbance

SOM composition of the soil samples was characterized by the quartz absorbance-normalized band areas (R) at three wavenumbers, i.e., at 2929, 2875 (combined also with the band at $2 8 5 5 \thinspace { \mathrm { c m } } ^ { - 1 } ;$ ) or $1 6 4 0 ~ \mathrm { { c m } ^ { - 1 } }$ , similar to that made by Bernier et al. (2013) and Sharma et al. (2017). This approach is based on using absorbance at $3 0 2 0 { \mathrm { - } } 2 8 0 0 { \mathrm { c m } } ^ { - 1 }$ associated with symmetric and asymmetric stretching vibrations of aliphatic CH bonds in methyl and methylene groups as an indication for abundance of aliphatic CH-containing organic compounds in soil (Capriel et al., 1995; Ellerbrock and Gerke, 2004; Ellerbrock et al., 2005; Nadav et al., 2013; Margenot et al., 2015). The absorbance at $1 6 4 0 ~ \mathrm { { c m } ^ { - 1 } }$ corresponds to Band B $( 1 7 4 0 - 1 6 0 0 { \mathrm { c m } } ^ { - 1 } )$ ), used by Ellerbrock and Gerke (2004) to characterize the abundance of relatively hydrophilic components in soil. This absorbance may be contributed by multiple chemical groups containing oxygen and/or nitrogen atoms that are relatively hydrophilic (or sensitive to the presence of neighboring hydrophilic groups as in the case of the double $\scriptstyle { \mathrm { C } } = { \mathrm { C } }$ bond; Bernier et al., 2013). Quartz present in soil is characterized by a diagnostic peak at $8 0 0 { - } 7 9 5 \ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Bosch Reig et al., 2002; Hanna, 1965; Ravisankar et al., 2011). Since quartz content in soil is assumed to be weakly affected by land management, its absorbance was used as an internal reference in examining absorbance of aliphatic CH and hydrophilic groups (Bernier et al., 2013). Examples of fragments of IR spectra used for obtaining band areas are provided in Supplementary data (Fig. S1).

Thus, quartz absorbance-normalized band areas (R) at the three aforementioned wavenumbers, obtained from soil samples taken in 2013 after GM management, were plotted against soil depth for the different amounts of compost applied in the past (Fig. 3a, c, e). Changes in R values characterize changes in the contents of relevant functional groups present in the soil. In addition, R values normalized by TOC content were plotted in Fig. 3b, d, f. They represent changes in the content of these functional groups in SOM (or more specifically, in the functional groups content related to soil organic C).

Major trends in the depth distribution of the R and R/TOC values in the current study (Fig. 3) are similar to those observed earlier for the 2012 soil samples, prior to GM management (Sharma et al., 2017). For example, the $\mathrm { R _ { 1 6 4 0 / 7 9 7 } }$ values show no change with soil depth and lack of effect of past compost application. The $\mathrm { R _ { 1 6 4 0 / 7 9 7 } / T O C }$ values tended to (i) increase with depth in the $_ { 0 - 3 0 \mathrm { c m } }$ layers and (ii) decrease with past compost addition, without a distinct effect of compost application rate, compared with the control (no compost addition). Soil content of aliphatic groups revealed by R2875/797 and R2929/797 tended generally to decrease with depth, whereas the R2929/797 and R2929/797/TOC values seemed to increase with the increase in the rate of compost addition in former years (Fig. 3). These trends suggest that after GM management the soil keeps multiple features of SOM composition detected in the 2012 samples (i.e., a year prior to GM management).

The IR-based indices determined in the 2013 samples (after GM management) were also plotted against those reported by Sharma et al. (2017) for the 2012 samples taken prior to GM management (Fig. 4). Each point in the set of graphs given in Fig. 4 represents a sample from a certain plot and depth, having a history of compost application (from 0 (control) to 20, 40, and $6 0 \mathrm { { m } } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { { h a } } ^ { - 1 }$ ). In addition, for every index, the ratio between values after GM management to those before was calculated. The median values of the calculated ratios and the percentage of the points with the ratio values less than one (i.e. the cases in which values after 2013 were lower than in 2012; in parentheses) are shown too in each subplot of Fig. 4.

No distinct systematic tendencies in the distribution of points relative to the 1:1 line in Fig. 4a, c, e could have been identified. The median values of the ratios based on R1640/797, R2929/797, and R2875/797 in Fig. 4a, c, e are close to one, and, therefore, the percentages of the points with ratio values smaller or greater than one are similar. This suggests that there were hardly any shifts in the soil contents of aliphatic CH-containing groups (absorbing at 2875 and $2 9 2 9 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ ) and hydrophilic groups (absorbing at $1 6 4 0 ~ \mathrm { { c m } ^ { - 1 } }$ ) between the samples taken before (2012) and after the GM management (2013).

However, some tendencies in the distribution of the points relative to the 1:1 line may be noted for the $\mathrm { R _ { 1 6 4 0 / 7 9 7 } / T O C }$ , R2929/797/TOC and $\mathrm { R _ { 2 8 7 5 / 7 9 7 } / T O C }$ values in Fig. 4b, d, f. These tendencies are characterized by the medians of the ratio values being $< 1$ (e.g., 0.84 and 0.83 in Fig. 4b and f, respectively). Hence, the number of points characterized by ratios smaller than one exceeds $5 0 \%$ . This suggests that within SOM, the contents of both hydrophilic and aliphatic groups (i.e., absorbing at 1640 and $2 9 2 9 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively) tended to decrease under GM management (Fig. 4b, d, f).

The depletion of aliphatic CH-groups fraction in SOM under GM management differed from the observation made by Margenot et al. (2015) who reported relative enrichment in aliphatic CH functional groups with increasing soil organic C as the most striking change in functional group composition of SOM across 13 organic tomato fields, including one with GM management. The GM management used in our work was indeed capable of increasing the soil TOC content (Fig. 2a), but, based on Fig. 4a, c, e, no concurrent accumulation of aliphatic CHgroups (as well as hydrophilic groups absorbing at $1 6 4 0 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ ) in the soil was noted, and therefore, the SOM content of these groups tended to decrease. However, a variable behavior of aliphatic CH functional groups might be expected, since they could belong to organic substances differing in the extents of recalcitrance, e.g., amino sugars and lignin (Margenot et al., 2015). Thus, it appears that following GM management, the SOM becomes depleted both of aliphatic CH-containing substances and of those containing hydrophilic groups, which suggests accumulation of aromatic moieties. The latter may be associated with increased fraction of N directly linked to aromatic C. Such an aromatic ring-linked N is less bioavailable, and its formation was proposed to explain a decrease in crop uptake of soil N and subsequent yield decline (Schmidt-Rohr et al., 2004). However, it has to be kept in mind that the results of a single GM application examined in this work 23 days after vetch incorporation into soil should not be straightforwardly extrapolated to long-term effects and/or effects of repeated GM incorporation. Instead, short-term effects of a single GM application have immediate relevance to the following cropping cycle as well as representing a “time window” along the dynamic fate of green manure biomass and its effect on soil properties.

# 3.2. Water-extractable organic matter (WEOM)

# 3.2.1. Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations

Concentrations of DOC in WEOM from the 2013 soil samples experienced GM management tended to decrease with soil depth (Fig. 5a). Samples having a history of compost application of $6 0 \mathrm { { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { { h a } ^ { - 1 } } }$ exhibited a trend whereby DOC concentrations were similar to or higher than that of the control, whereas samples with a history of low and medium levels of compost application (20 and $4 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively) resulted in DOC concentrations similar to or lower than that of the control (Fig. 5a). These findings are in contrast to the noted increasing effect of compost application rate on DOC concentration in soil samples taken in 2012 prior to the GM management (Sharma et al., 2017). This difference may indicate that DOC originating from GM management had masked the reported impact of past compost applications (at rates up to $4 0 \mathrm { { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { { h a } ^ { - 1 } ) } }$ on DOC level in WEOM.

Indeed, the data presented in Fig. 5b clearly demonstrate the dramatic increase in DOC concentration in WEOM after GM management (2013), in comparison with the same data collected in 2012 prior to the GM management. The range of DOC concentrations, considering all the replicate plots, was ${ 1 0 9 - 4 0 1 } ~ \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ after the GM management vs. $1 2 { - } 2 2 5 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ prior to it (Sharma et al., 2017). It follows that after GM management, DOC concentrations increased by a factor in the range of 1.5 to 13.3. Since GM management adds to soil readily degradable biomass, this DOC increase is well-expected. Also, due to the dynamic nature of fresh WEOM, the concentration of DOC may be timedependent.

The GLM test applied to the DOC changes was characterized by statistically significant correlation $( \mathbf { r } = 0 . 5 2$ , at $\mathrm { ~ p ~ < ~ } 0 . 0 5$ ; Table 1). The effect of soil depth was statistically significant (with $9 5 \%$ confidence interval not including zero) and negative thus indicating that the DOC addition caused by GM management becomes smaller with depth, probably due to the fact that vetch was incorporated in the top soil layer, and then, organic matter may become adsorbed and/or degraded at the upper soil.

# 3.2.2. UV absorbance and SUVA

Depth profiles portraying the absorbance of WEOM solutions at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ $( \mathsf { A b } s _ { 2 5 4 } )$ and its DOC-normalized derivative (SUVA) obtained from soil samples taken after GM management are presented in Fig. 6a, c. $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ is proportional to the concentration of aromatic WEOM components in an aqueous sample, and SUVA is widely used to represent fraction of aromatic components in WEOM (Zsolnay and Steindl, 1991; Weishaar et al., 2003). In general, $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were weakly affected by depth in the $_ { 0 - 3 0 \mathrm { c m } }$ soil layer and showed a decline in the $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 \ { \mathrm { c m } }$ layer. A weak dependence, if at all, was observed between $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and past compost addition rates (Fig. 6a). SUVA values at the $_ { 0 - 3 0 \mathrm { c m } }$ soil layers were consistently higher for the samples that received compost in previous years as compared with the control samples (Fig. 6c), but showed no effect of compost rate. Thus, the SUVA values indicate that the fraction of aromatic compounds in WEOM in samples taken following GM management increased due to preceding compost application.

The $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values from the 2013 samples were, by and large, greater than those determined in the 2012 samples (Fig. 6b). This suggests that after the GM management the aqueous phase containing WEOM was enriched with aromatic compounds. However, the SUVA values following GM management were generally similar to or lower than those determined prior to applying the GM management (Fig. 6d), indicating that the fraction of aromatic compounds per unit DOC actually decreased. This was expected when considering that GM represented fresh plant material incorporated into soil only 23 days prior to sampling.

The GLM description of the $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ shifts due to GM management was characterized by ${ \bf r } = 0 . 4 8$ and $\mathbf { p } = 0 . 0 2$ (Table 1), however, the coefficients describing the effects of depth and compost rate were not significant (at $\mathrm { ~ p ~ } < \ 0 . 0 5 )$ . In the GLM analysis of SUVA differences (with ${ \bf r } = 0 . 5 7$ and $\mathtt { p } = 0 . 0 0 0 6 $ ), the positive effect of depth was statistically significant (Table 1), showing that the shift in the content of aromatic components in WEOM was larger in deeper layers as compared with that in the top layers (i.e., the decrease of aromatic fraction in WEOM was greater in the surface layer as compared with deeper soil). This positive effect of soil depth on the SUVA shift is consistent with the expectation that aliphatic components added to WEOM with GM are concentrated mostly in the top soil layer and their downward movement, due to adsorption or degradation, is limited.

# 3.2.3. Fluorescent components of WEOM

The EEM $^ +$ PARAFAC analysis of the WEOM obtained from soil collected in 2013 included the same number of samples (80, with one excluded outlier) as that in the similar analysis of WEOM from soil collected in 2012 (Sharma et al., 2017). However, in the analysis of the 2013 samples, only two fluorescent components were identified in contrast to the four components found in the examination of the 2012 samples (Sharma et al., 2017). The fluorescence contours of the components determined in the WEOM samples after GM management (2013) are shown in Fig. S2 (Supplementary data).

One component showed emission maximum at $4 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and two excitation maxima, at 260 and $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. It was similar to component 1 reported by Sharma et al. (2017), which was characterized by emission maximum at $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and two excitation maxima, at 265 and $3 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . Another component found in the current study (2013 samples) was characterized by emission maximum at $4 0 2 \mathrm { n m }$ , and two excitation maxima, below $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and at $3 0 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. It resembled component 2 observed in the 2012 soil samples prior to GM management (Sharma et al., 2017; the emission maximum at $4 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and the excitation maxima are below $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and at $3 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ). Both components represent humic-like substances (Coble et al., 1998; Stedmon et al., 2003; Fellman et al., 2008; Borisover et al., 2009; Ishii and Boyer, 2012). Therefore, in the current work the two detected fluorescent components are labeled by the same numbers used to mark the similar components detected by Sharma et al. (2017), i.e., components 1 and 2.

Based on Ishii and Boyer (2012), component 2 as opposed to component 1 may be characterized by a smaller size, less intensive biological production, weaker biodegradation and lower ability to be adsorbed by soils and sediments. Two additional components found in the 2012 soil samples (Sharma et al., 2017) included (i) humic substances assigned to biodegradable hydrophobic large-size humic substances and (ii) small-size humic components “mixed” with the compounds exhibiting tryptophan-like fluorescence. The latter, often associated with proteinaceous matter, is expected to be relatively easily degradable (Marschner and Kalbitz, 2003; Fellman et al., 2008; Mostofa et al., 2010). The disappearance of these two components from WEOM of the soil under GM management suggests that enhanced biological processes in the soil after GM and, possibly, the concurrent leaching associated with rain may lead to enrichment of WEOM by relatively less degradable intermediate size humic substances. Although, based on EEM $^ +$ PARAFAC analysis, it was thought that the chemical composition of fluorescent WEOM in soil may not be influenced by addition of animal or green manure (Ohno and Bro, 2006), yet our results suggest that fluorescent organic matter became relatively more homogeneous following GM management.

Fluorescence intensities of components 1 and 2 identified in the WEOM from soil samples taken in 2013, following GM management, are presented and briefly discussed in Fig. S3 (Supplementary data). In general, the responses of fluorescence intensities (i.e., the concentrations of fluorescent components) and their DOC-normalized values (i.e., fractions of components in WEOM) to earlier compost additions were similar to those observed for four components identified in WEOM from soil prior GM management (Sharma et al., 2017).

Comparison of the fluorescence intensities and their DOC-normalized values for the two components identified both in 2012 prior to GM management (Sharma et al., 2017) and in 2013 after GM management is given in Fig. 7. It is evident that the aqueous extracts from soil subjected to GM management became generally enriched by these two fluorescent components (Fig. 7a, c), yet their DOC-normalized fractions decreased (Fig. 7b, d). Recently, results of analyses of soil WEOM extracts affected by long-term organic farming reported by Sun et al. (2017) showed decreases in the contents of fluorescent PARAFACanalyzed components and of DOC but an increase in SUVA; these observations are opposite to those obtained under GM management in the current study (Figs. 5b, 6d, 7a, c).

We have also compared the ratio of fluorescence intensities of component 2 to component 1 in 2013 after GM management, vs. 2012, prior to GM management (Fig. 8). The results show that following GM management there was a large increase in the relative portion of component 2 which is considered to be less biodegradable, relative to component 1.

The GLM analysis showed significant correlations (at $\mathrm { ~ p ~ < ~ } 0 . 0 5$ , Table 1) for the shifts in fluorescence intensities (proxy for concentrations) of component 1 $( \mathbf { r } = 0 . 4 8 )$ ), TOC-normalized fluorescence (i.e., \~ fraction of the whole WEOM) of both components 1 and 2 $( \mathbf { r } = 0 . 6 8$ and 0.71, respectively), and for the changes in the fluorescence intensity ratio (\~ relative abundances) of components 2 and 1 $( \mathbf { r } = 0 . 7 6 )$ ) where the corresponding shifts, as above, represent the difference between a property determined under GM management and that prior to it. Based on the $9 5 \%$ confidence intervals of the GLM coefficients, it follows that all the above-mentioned shifts become more positive with depth, and, except for fluorescence of component 1, more negative in the plots having a history of larger rates of compost addition (Table 1).

# 3.3. General discussion and conclusions

This study examined changes in compositional characteristics of SOM and WEOM following GM management and the possible relations of these changes to earlier compost applications and soil depth. Generally, GM decomposition time may significantly vary depending on residue management, temperature and water availability, such that major decomposition and N release of soil-incorporated legumes may require 15 to 20 weeks in temperate environments but only 2 to 6 weeks in tropical environments (Bowen et al., 1993; Thonnissen et al., 2000; Cherr et al., 2006). Indeed, when incubating 5 different legumes with 5 different soils at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , the fast mineralization step ranged from 2 to 8 weeks (Nakhone and Tabatabai, 2008). As noted above, although a relatively short time period elapsed since incorporating plant biomass into the soil (i.e., 23 days), in a warm climate, yet, a significant or even a major portion of the residues is expected to degrade in the first 3–4 weeks (Njunie et al., 2004; Danga et al., 2009; Danga et al., 2013). Hence, the accumulated data are expected to represent a GM decomposition step which may lead to significant changes in amounts and composition of SOM and WEOM and be relevant for the following cropping cycle.

Our results suggest that GM management led to an increase in organic C content compared with prior seasons where compost was applied, although SOM content of aliphatic CH-containing compounds (absorbing at $2 9 2 9 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 } \dot { } \dot { }$ and hydrophilic substances (absorbing at $1 6 4 0 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 } \dot { }$ ) tended to decrease. Roughly, organic matter could be thought as having three types of structures (with the understanding that all three types of groups may be found also in one organic component), as follows: (1) aliphatic CH-groups, (2) hydrophilic groups involving O and N, and (3) aromatic moieties. Based on this separation, the observed increase in organic C following GM management is associated with an increase in SOM content of aromatic substances which probably lack hydrophilic groups. The difference in SOM content of aliphatic CHcontaining compounds absorbing at $2 8 7 5 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ also increased with depth, indicating that these groups, relative to the soil prior to GM management, enrich SOM with depth.

Aqueous extracts from soil samples subjected to GM management became enriched, relative to the 2012 samples, by DOC, water-soluble aromatic $( \mathsf { A b } s _ { 2 5 4 } )$ and in particular, fluorescent components, although the fractions of aromatic (SUVA) and fluorescent (expressed by fluorescence/DOC) components in the whole WEOM decreased. These findings imply that WEOM is enriched by water-soluble aliphatic organic compounds (e.g., polysaccharides originating from plant residuals), as distinct from aromatic components contributing to SUVA and fluorescence/DOC. The change in DOC concentrations, resulting from GM management, decreased with the increase in depth whereas the changes in the fractions of aromatic (expressed by SUVA) and fluorescent components increased. These findings might imply that the change in the contents of water-soluble aliphatic organic compounds apparently decreases with depth, probably due to their adsorption by soil components and microbial consumption. When examining the composition of fluorescent WEOM in terms of fluorescence ratio (i.e., relative portions) of component 2 to 1, it was noted that after GM management in 2013, the soil WEOM became enriched by component 2, as compared to the 2012 samples. The difference in this ratio, obtained between the samples collected in 2013 and 2012, increased in deeper soil layers. This indicates that the possibly less biodegradable and more weakly soil-adsorbing fluorescent component 2 (as compared with component 1) is accumulated in WEOM from deeper soil layers.

Thus, SOM and WEOM demonstrated some opposite trends in response to GM management. The first became rather aromatic, with aliphatic CH-containing structures contributing to a greater extent in the SOM at deeper layers, as compared to soil prior to GM management. By contrast, WEOM become enriched by hydrophilic aliphatic organic compounds, with aromatic and fluorescent components increasingly found at deeper soil layers and hydrophilic aliphatic components at the surface soil.

Responses to the rates of compost application carried out in the past are either non- significant or negative, i.e., in the latter case, with the increase in application rate, the changes in soil and SOM contents of hydrophilic groups as well as the changes in the fractions of fluorescent components in WEOM decreased. The negative response of changes in the contents of hydrophilic groups might be perceived as an indication of enhanced degradation of such components, with the increase in earlier compost rate addition. The decrease in the changes of the fractions of WEOM fluorescent components, at increased rates of past compost application, might be related to greater adsorption of fluorescent components to the soil (or their enhanced degradation). Thus, in a soil environment that was amended in the past by higher amounts of compost, SOM becomes under GM management less hydrophilic and WEOM depleted of aromatic fluorescent constituents, as compared with soil receiving smaller compost application rates. It may be concluded that soil incorporation of plant biomass may mask effects of earlier compost application on changes of SOM/WEOM compositional characteristics.
