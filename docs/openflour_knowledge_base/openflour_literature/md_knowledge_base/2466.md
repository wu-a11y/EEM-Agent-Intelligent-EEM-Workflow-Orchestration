# ABSTRACT

Despite the growing attention to the effects of hydrological precipitation on organic carbon export along the continuum land-river-ocean, limited effort has been made to understand the export and the reactivity of particulate organic carbon (POC) compared to those of dissolved organic carbon (DOC). Yet, further understanding of the controlling mechanisms on the export of particulate organic matter (POM) from terrestrial systems is fundamental. In this study, we assessed the temporal changes of the source contributions to riverine POM in two adjacent rivers of the same watershed during two summer storm events, which included the early and the late events, using end-member mixing analysis (EMMA) based on spectroscopic proxies. The EMMA showed relatively high contributions of terrestrial materials to the riverine POM for both rivers during the early summer storm event. However, this trend did not persist until the late summer storm event, which presented the decreased contributions of the terrestrial sources and less dynamic changes in the source distributions compared to those observed in the early summer rain event. These results demonstrate the combined impacts of the land use/surrounding area, the hydrology, and the intra-seasonal variations on the dominant riverine POM sources. This study provides an interesting insight into the importance of the intense hydrological events on the export of the terrestrial OM and further on the potential modification of the existing carbon mass balance along the continuum land-river-ocean.

# 1. Introduction

Organic matter (OM) refers to the largest reactive reservoir of reduced carbon on the earth, which corresponds to a total amount of carbon of ${ \sim } 3 2 0 0 \mathrm { P g } \mathrm { C } .$ . This carbon is in principal distributed over three different categorized environments, which include soils $( 1 6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { P g } ~ \mathrm { C } )$ , sediments $( 1 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { P g } ~ \mathsf { C } )$ , and the ocean $\langle 6 8 5 \mathrm { \ P g \ C } ,$ comparable to the global atmospheric ${ \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ reservoir) (Bianchi, 2011; Hedges, 1992). Aside from its role as a source of carbon, OM serves as the most important component of riverine materials involving in a wide range of biogeochemical and ecological processes and functions (Jiao et al., 2010; Stubbins and Dittmar, 2014; Thurman, 1985). It also plays a critical role in the solubility, toxicity, bioavailability, mobility, and the distribution of pollutants (Deb and Shukla, 2011; Farrington and Takada, 2014; Romera-Castillo et al., 2018).

Terrestrial OM, which includes in the forms of both dissolved and particulate fractions, is naturally discharged into continental waters and the ocean with an estimate of ${ \sim } 0 . 2 \mathrm { - } 0 . 5 $ of $\mathsf { P g C }$ (Battin et al., 2009; Ludwig et al., 1996). The extents of the exports are strongly influenced by rainfall events especially by heavy storms (Dhillon and Inamdar, 2014). Consequently, rainfall events associated with runoff processes are considered as a key player to determine the quantity and the quality of OM exported from the land to the continental waters (Wagner et al., 2019). Indeed, several previous studies have highlighted the pivotal role of hydrological events in terms of the changes in OM concentrations either in the dissolved and the particulate fraction, chemical composition, reactivity, and the dynamics (Fellman et al., 2009; Inamdar et al., 2011; Jeanneau et al., 2018; Johnson et al., 2018; Lu and Liu, 2019; Raymond et al., 2016; Singh et al., 2014; Yang et al., 2013). Yet, understanding the mechanisms leading to the observed dynamics at the field-scale is crucial, and the related topics have been scarcely studied. Furthermore, one major challenging point for such studies lies in the fact that there is a wide spectrum of the terrestrial sources for OM on a land basis. In this context, identifying the source of the OM and quantitatively tracking the different inputs of diverse OM sources in a watershed during hydrological events can contribute to broadening the related knowledge and help to elucidate the dynamics of the OM at a local and a global scale. Although many studies previously investigated these processes, most of them focused on the dissolved fraction of the OM (DOM) (Inamdar et al., 2011; Jeanneau et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2019, Nguyen et al., 2010; Saraceno et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2015a, 2013). Only a few studies have examined the particulate OM fraction (POM) (Dhillon and Inamdar, 2014; Rowland et al., 2017) even though this fraction is truly involved as much as the DOM in various aspects of the biogeochemical processes (Derrien et al., 2019a).

In Korea, heavy storms mainly occur in summer from late June through August, and precipitation during this period accounts for more than $4 0 \%$ of the annual record (Jung et al., 2002). The topography of the country favors rapid discharges to the ocean due to the steep slopes and the short river lengths $< 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k m } )$ (Kim et al., 2007), which facilitates the remobilization of the terrestrial OM to the aquatic systems through the rainfall-runoff processes (Tarboton, 2003; Wagner et al., 2019). The present study was based on the end-member mixing analysis (EMMA) combined with the analyses of spectroscopic properties with a special attention paid to the changes in the runoff patterns, flow paths, and the sources of POM during the storm events in typical watersheds in South Korea. Fluorescence spectroscopy and the derived optical indices have been commonly used to trace OM sources in many aquatic environments since the last decade (Cawley et al., 2012; Derrien et al., 2019b, 2017; Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2016; Retelletti Brogi et al., 2018; Yamashita et al., 2013; Yang and Hur, 2014). EMMA is a simple and effective method for quantifying the contributions of different sources on the basis of a mass balance equation. It has been commonly practiced primarily with isotopes data. However, it has recently started to be used with fluorescence proxies (Derrien et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2015a). This dual approach allowed us to set the specific objectives that include (i) to compare the fluorescence characteristics of various potential OM end-member sources distributed in the watershed studied, (ii) to track the dynamic changes of the riverine POM quality during two summer storm events in summer, and (iii) to estimate the relative contributions of the potential OM sources to the riverine POM at different phases during a summer storm season. Finally, we hoped that this study would provide some clues to understand the dynamics of the POM in similar other environments during intense hydrological events. Another significance of this study resides in the fact that optical indices-based EMMA were applied for the first time to track the POM sources in a catchment during storm events.

# 2. Material and methods

# 2.1. Study area and sampling

The Topyeong River and the Geumho River basins are situated in the Nakdong River watershed located in the southeastern part of South Korea (Fig. 1). Both rivers are tributaries of the Nakdong River, which is the longest river in South Korea. The annual amount of precipitation is $1 2 6 5 \ \mathrm { m m }$ (Korean Meteorological Administration, www.kma.go.kr) and two-thirds of it falls during the summer monsoon season, which is from June to September (Nkomozepi and Chung, 2014). The Topyeong River basin is mostly covered by forests and agriculture fields, which comprises of forests $6 2 . 2 \%$ , agricultural fields $2 6 . 7 \%$ , urban areas $3 . 5 \%$ , and other areas $7 . 6 \%$ . The Geuhmo River basin consists of the same land use but with a higher percentage of urban areas, which includes forests $6 0 . 8 \%$ , agricultural fields $2 2 . 5 \%$ , urban areas $1 0 . 1 \%$ , and other areas $6 . 6 \%$ This basin is considered to be a typical urban watershed as the main channel of the river passes through Daegu Metropolitan City, which has a population of 2.5 million people (Lee et al., 2019).

The river water samples were collected at two different times during the rainy summer season in 2018. The first storm occurred in early July, and the later storm occurred at the end of August 2018. The sampling was performed using a customized auto-sampler (RoboChemTM Autosampler S3-1224R, Centennial Technology Co., Korea) installed at the lowest reach of each river (GPS coordinates: $3 5 ^ { \circ } 3 1 ^ { \prime } 1 0 . 5 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N } 1 2 8 ^ { \circ } 2 2 ^ { \prime } 3 8 . 1 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E }$ for the Topyeong River and $3 5 ^ { \circ } 5 2 ^ { \prime } 1 3 . 0 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N }$ $1 2 8 ^ { \circ } 2 7 ^ { \prime } 4 5 . 0 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E }$ for the Geuhmo River). The early summer storm event consisted of the samples for the period starting from the 1st until the 3rd or the 4th of July for the Topyeong River and the Geuhmo River, while the late summer storm event persisted from the 26th to the 29th or the 30th of August. The total number of collected samples was 6 from both rivers in the early summer storm event, but it was 12 for Topyeong River and 19 for Geuhmo River in the late summer storm event. The water samples were acidified on-site and transported to the laboratory. They were then filtered through a pre-combusted and pre-washed $\mathrm { G F } / \mathrm { F }$ filter (Whatman) after a pH adjustment to ${ \sim } 7 . 0 .$ . All the GF/F filters containing the POM samples were stored at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , and they were freeze-dried before further analyses. Sampling for the OM end-members was conducted in early June 2018 prior to the first storm events. Several common end-members were collected with a special attention paid to the land uses of the upper catchments of the basins, which included attached algae $( \mathtt { n } = 4 )$ ) (dominant species: green algae), soils $\langle \boldsymbol { \mathrm { n } } = \boldsymbol { 7 } \cdot \boldsymbol { }$ ), fallen leaves $( \mathtt { n } = 2$ ), and riparian plants $( \mathtt { n } = 8 )$ as natural OM sources from the forest and the agricultural areas (allochthonous and autochthonous) and a road runoff ${ \bf \Pi } ( { \bf n } = 1 ) $ ) as an urban OM source from the urban areas. In total, 22 end-member samples, which represented 5 different sources, were collected in the field. Details on these end-member samples are available in Lee et al. (2019). At the laboratory, the end-member samples were stored at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ . They were freeze-dried and ground for further analyses. The branches and the leaves were removed from the collected soils before grinding.

# 2.2. Measurements of optical properties

The water extractable organic matter (WEOM) was obtained from the POM samples contained on the GF/F filters or in the form of freeze-dried and ground samples according to Derrien et al. (2019b). Briefly, the GF/F filters and the ground samples were soaked in ultra-pure water at a solid-to-liquid mixing ratio of 1:10 and 1:20, respectively (i.e., $_ { 4 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ in $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ and $_ { 2 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ in $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ , respectively). They were then shaken for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at $1 3 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ at room temperature. The samples were subject to the centrifugation at $5 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ for $2 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ and subsequent filtration through a precombusted and pre-washed GF/F filter (Whatman). The filtrates were analyzed within the day.

The dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations of the WEOM samples were measured using a Shimadzu V-CPH TOC analyzer. The absorption spectra were scanned from 200 to $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $0 . 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ -interval using an ultravioletevisible (UVevis) spectrometer (Shimadzu UV-1300). The specific ultraviolet absorption coefficient $\left( { \mathsf { S U V A } } _ { 2 5 4 } \right)$ ) was calculated using the DOC and the UV absorption coefficient at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ (Weishaar et al., 2003). The fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) were obtained with a fluorescence spectrometer (Hitachi F7000, Japan). An excitation (Ex) scan was performed, which ranged from 220 to $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with a 5-nm step, while the emission scan range spanned between 280 and 550 with a $1 { - } \mathrm { { n m } }$ interval. The slits were set at $5 \textrm { n m }$ , the scan speed was $2 4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m / m i n } }$ , and the voltage was set at $6 5 0 ~ \mathrm { V }$ (Retelletti Brogi et al., 2019). A total of 84 EEMs were collected for the PARAFAC modeling (Murphy et al., 2013). The drEEM toolbox was used to perform the EEMs correction, which included the blank subtraction, the inner filter correction, and the Raman normalization, and the parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) modeling. The validation of the PARAFAC model was made by a split-half analysis and the percentage of the explained variance $( > 9 9 \% )$ . By using the corrected EEMs, the classical fluorescence indices, which included the modified fluorescence index: YFI, the humification index: HIX, and the index of recent autochthonous contribution BIX, were calculated (Heo et al., 2016; Huguet et al., 2009; Zsolnay et al., 1999). Details on the calculations can be found in Derrien et al. (2019b). The maximum fluorescence intensities $\left( \operatorname { F } _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } \right)$ of the identified components were used to estimate their relative abundance $( \% )$ .

# 2.3. End-member mixing analysis (EMMA) using spectroscopic parameters

The potential contributions of the selected OM end-members to the riverine POM samples on the GF/F filters or the representative WEOM samples were estimated using the freeware package named IsoSource version 1.3.1. This software calculates the proportions of the different end members in the mixture via an isotopic mass balance equation (http://www.epa.gov/eco-research/stableisotope-mixing-models-estimating-source-proportions) (Phillips et al., 2005; Phillips and Gregg, 2003). In the present study, this software was applied to the derived spectroscopic parameters. The optical parameters were selected according to the two considerations outlined below, which included (i) the exclusion of the optical parameters with the values for the POM samples exceeding the ranges of the end-members values, and (ii) preferentially choosing the parameters, such as the optical tracers with a high capacity to discriminate between the 5 considered end-members in this study (Derrien et al., 2018a; Yang et al., 2015a, 2015b). As a result, even though many spectroscopic proxies, which included the relative abundances and the 4 absorbance and fluorescent indices, displayed a good performance for the discrimination $( \% C 1 , \% C 2 , \% C 4 , \%$ C5, HIX, and YFI), only two parameters satisfied the outlined condition, and the ranged values of the POM samples should be included in the range of the end-members (Table S1). Based on the outcome, the distribution in Component 4 $( \% C 4 )$ and the modified fluorescence index (YFI) were found to be the most appropriate proxies for the EMMA application. Both parameters have demonstrated a linear mixing behavior, which validates their use for the mass balance-based equation of EMMA, as shown in the results from Derrien et al. (2019b). The EMMA was finally constructed using the 5 end-members and these two optical parameters. The source increment and the mass balance tolerance parameter values were set at $1 \%$ and $0 . 5 \text{‰}$ for the POM samples of event 1. A higher mass balance tolerance value $( 1 . 1 \\\\% _ { 0 } )$ was applied to the second storm event, because no statistics were generated at the previous setting with the lowest value. The modification in the mass balance tolerance does not likely affect the EMMA results (Phillips and Gregg, 2003).

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Identification of the fluorescent components via EEM-PARAFAC

Five different components were identified from the WEOM samples, which included three protein-like components (C1, C3, and C5) and two humic-like components (C2 and C4) (Figure S1). All the identified components were consistent with those previously reported and/or well-matched with the Open Fluor database with similarity scores of ${ \bf > } 0 . 9 7 .$ . Component 1 (C1), which had the maximum at 220/301 nm $\left( \operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m } \right)$ , can be assigned to a typical protein-like component or a tyrosine-like component (D’Andrilli et al., 2017). Component 3 (C3) exhibited the maxima at $2 2 0 / 3 4 4 \mathrm { n m ( E x / E m ) }$ , which is usually associated with a mixture of protein-like substances, such as tyrosine, amino acid (tryptophan), and phenolic moieties (Garcia et al., 2018; Kowalczuk et al., 2013). The protein-like C5, which presented the maxima at $2 2 0 / 3 3 6 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , has been commonly linked with recent biological production (Dainard et al., 2015). Both humic-like components C2 and C4, which displayed the maxima at $2 2 0 / 4 7 6 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $\left( \operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m } \right)$ and $2 2 0 /$ $4 0 6 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , can be associated with the classical terrestrial humic-like component and the microbial humic-like component, respectively (Catala et al., 2015; Joshi et al., 2017; Murphy et al., 2011; Yamashita et al., 2013).

# 3.2. Optical properties of POM end-members

Three profiles of the fluorescent components were identified among the 5 end-members. Both humic-like components (i.e., C2 and C4), were the most dominant component for the end-members of soil $( 4 4 . 0 \% \pm 4 . 6 \%$ and $2 7 . 8 \% \pm 8 . 2 \% )$ and leaf $( 4 5 . 8 \% \pm 2 . 0 \%$ and $2 9 . 3 \% \pm 0 . 2 \% )$ (Table 1). The remaining $\sim 2 5 \%$ were occupied by the protein-like components with the highest proportion of component C3, which represents a mixture of protein, amino acid, and phenolic moieties, with the values of $1 0 . 7 \% \pm 1 2 . 0 \%$ and $1 2 . 4 \% \ \pm \ 2 . 1 \%$ for soil and leaf end-members, respectively. In contrast, the C3 was the most abundant component in the algae and the riparian plants with the values of $5 3 . 1 \% \pm \ 1 5 . 3 \%$ and $5 8 . 6 \% \pm 2 2 . 2 \%$ , respectively. The protein-like component (C5) linked with recent biological production was the second most abundant component in these end-members with the values of $2 5 . 2 \% \pm 1 6 . 7 \%$ for algae and $1 6 . 8 \% \pm 1 3 . 1 \%$ for riparian plant end-members. The road runoff end-member exhibited a distribution of the fluorophores dominated by C5 with a value of $4 3 . 9 \%$ , which was followed by $2 2 . 3 \%$ of C2, $1 7 . 0 \%$ of C4, and $1 5 . 4 \%$ of C1. Regarding the spectroscopic indices, the highest values of $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and HIX were found for the soil and leaf end-members, which indicated the typical characteristics of terrestrial materials (Derrien et al., 2017, 2018a). Meanwhile, the values of both indices were ${ \sim } 1$ for the rest of the end-members except for the road runoff end-member with a HIX value of 3.51, which suggests the dominant presence of nonaromatic UV sensitive compounds and slightly humified terrestrial materials. The YFI and BIX indices presented limited ranges of the values compared to the two latter indices (i.e., ${ \tt S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and HIX). However, they both clearly demonstrate the specificity for discriminating the 5 end-members. The highest values of YFI were found for the algae and the riparian end-members $( 2 . 1 2 \pm 0 . 2 8$ and $1 . 8 5 \pm 0 . 6 1 $ ). The highest values of the BIX were found for the algae $( 0 . 7 0 \pm 0 . 0 9 )$ and for the road runoff end-member $( 0 . 5 8 \pm 0 . 0 0 ) $ . In general, the spectroscopic indices observed for the end-members were in line with those previously reported (Derrien et al., 2018b; Yang et al., 2015a, 2015b).

# 3.3. Changes of spectroscopic parameters during the rainy summer season

# 3.3.1. Early summer storm event

In the Topyeong area, the storm event started on the 1st of July at $1 0 \ : { \sf a m }$ and finished on the 3rd of July at $1 0 \mathrm { a . m }$ . The total rainfall recorded by the nearest on-site monitoring station for the period studied was $1 1 5 \mathrm { ~ m m }$ (www.kma.go.kr). This storm event was characterized by a moderate rainfall $< 1 0 ~ \mathrm { m m } )$ at the beginning of the period, which was between 0.5 and $9 \mathrm { m m }$ per hour, followed by a break of ${ \sim } 1 7 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ before starting again with a maximum rainfall intensity on the last day of the event, which was on the 3rd of July (Figure S2). The turbidity peaked after the first period of the rain during the second day, but the discharge peaked after the last period where the maximum rainfall intensity was observed. No changes in the total fluorescence intensity were observed during the event. However, there were variations in the individual fluorescent component. All three protein-like components, which included C1, C3, and C5, displayed a similar pattern with a decrease after a moderate rainfall namely during the break time (Fig. 2a). In contrast, the two humic-like components, which included C2 and C4, displayed the opposite trend with the highest distributions during the break time. The latter observation was consistent with the trends observed for the indices of the HIX and the SUVA (Fig. 2b), which showed increasing trends at this period. The increases of the humic-like components and the HIX and SUVA values suggested the input of POM consisting of humified and aromatic molecules after the first rain. This can be interpreted as the consequence of the pulse-shunt of the terrestrial organic matter further downstream driven by the rainfall (Raymond et al., 2016; Wagner et al., 2019). During the second period of the event, which is characterized by more intensive rainfall, all the values of these parameters decreased until the recovery to their original values (Fig. 2a and b), which suggests that dilution effects overshadowed over the pulse-shunt due to the high rainfall. There were no significant changes in the BIX and the YFI values $\mathbf { \bar { p } } > 0 . 0 5 $ during the event. This is likely related to the low ranges of values of both indices, which may results in their low sensitivity to discern the variations created by different sources.

In the Geumho area, the storm event ended one day after the peak, which was on the 4th of July at 1 a.m. The total rainfall recorded was slightly lower, which was $9 1 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , than that of the Topyeong area. Nevertheless, the same hydrologic characteristics were observed between the two areas (Figure S2) as the turbidity and the discharge simultaneously peaked during the break. The immediate influence of the rainfall on the flow rate suggested a short water residence time controlling in the studied area. As in the Topyeong area, no changes in the total fluorescence intensity were observed during the event, but there were variations were noticed for the individual fluorescent components and the spectroscopic indices (Fig. 3a and Fig. 3b). The first variation in the component distribution was observed during the break before the second rainfall of the event, which was on the 2nd of July. The microbial humic-like component, C4, and the protein-like component from biological production, C5, increased by $\sim 2 0 \%$ and $12 \%$ respectively, while the component C1, which was the protein-like, and C2, which was the terrestrial humic-like, reached their minimum $2 4 . 3 \%$ and $1 4 . 8 \%$ . At the end of the second period of rainfall, both humic-like components (C2 and C4) decreased to their minimum, which were $1 2 . 1 \%$ and $1 3 . 4 \%$ , respectively, while the protein-like C5 reached its maximum corresponding to an increase of $3 2 \%$ . After a decrease in the intensity of the component C1 (protein-like), the value returned to its initial value. The component C3 was not detected for the event in this area. These observations were in line with the trends observed for the HIX and the SUVA indices (Fig. 3b). The trends illustrate the flush and the subsequent input of the terrestrial POM into the river following the first rainfall of the event. These types of observed OM dynamics have been commonly reported in the literature (Inamdar et al., 2011; Johnson et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2010; Rowland et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2015b). No significant changes were found for the BIX and the YFI $\begin{array} { r }  \mathbf { \tilde { p } } < 0 . 0 5 \ \end{array}$ ) during the event.

# 3.3.2. Late summer storm event

In the Topyeong area, the storm event started on the 26th of August at $2 \mathsf { p m }$ and ended on the 28th of August at $1 0 { \mathrm { p . m } }$ . The total rainfall was $2 3 5 \ \mathrm { \ m m } .$ . (www.kma.go.kr). This storm event was characterized by an intense rainfall with a peak $4 0 \ \mathrm { m m }$ ) on the $2 7 \mathrm { t h }$ at $8 \ \mathrm { a m }$ , and it ended with a moderate rainfall on the 28th (Figure S2). The distributions of the individual fluorescent component are presented in Fig. 2c. The relative distribution was dominated by the tyrosine like component, C1, with a slight fluctuation shown throughout the event (minimum: $7 9 . 0 \%$ and maximum: $8 6 . 0 \%$ . Similarly, the terrestrial humic-like C2 did not vary much, and it only changed from 4.2 to $7 . 0 \%$ . In contrast, the component C3, which is a mixture of protein, amino acid, and phenolic moieties, and the microbial humic-like C4 displayed a maximum of the intensities after the most intensive rainfall on the 27th of August. The protein-like C5 exhibited a large variation in the distribution before the first rainfall. After the rainfall, the relative abundance reached ${ \sim } 4 . 5 \%$ before it increased again to $6 \%$ one day after the last rainfall, which was on the 30th of August. All the spectroscopic indices, which included in particular the HIX and the SUVA values, showed a range of variability before the first rainfall (Fig. 2d), which could reflect the background noises of the river, because there were no substantial variations in the hydrology during the period. After the intensive rainfall, it was notable to observe a slight increase with both the SUVA and the HIX values, which reached 0.82 and 2.2, respectively, followed by a decrease to their minimum, which reached 0.57 and 1.52 respectively. On the contrary, the BIX and the YFI slightly decreased during the event to finally return to their initial values at the end of the monitoring period. All the results of the late storm event do not reflect a strong impact of a potential terrestrial input as much as it was observed for the early storm event.

# 3.4. Evolution of the POM sources along the rainy summer season

As stated in the method section, the EMMA model based on the selected spectroscopic parameters, which include $\% C 4$ and FI, were applied for the WEOM from all the POM samples in order to estimate the relative contributions of the five major end-members, which include algae, soil, riparian plant, leaf, and road runoff, to the riverine POM, which allowed to track its evolution along the rainy summer season (Table S2). Each storm event was divided into 3 different phases that included before, during, and after the storm event as shown in Fig. 4.

Thus, the relative contributions of the different POM sources in this early summer rain event seem to be largely affected by the surrounding land uses and the hydrologic variations with time.

# 3.4.2. Late summer storm event

A clear difference in the distributions of the riverine POM sources was observed for both areas between the two storm events. Regardless of the different phases (i.e., before, during, and after the rainfall), the main source of the POM was found to be algae with the percentage ranging from $4 4 . 1 \% \pm 1 9 . 4 \% - 7 1 . 1 \% \pm 1 1 . 9 \%$ (Figs. 4b and d). For example, in the Topyeong area, the algal contributions corresponded to $5 7 . 9 \% \pm 1 3 . 7 \%$ and $6 1 . 0 \% \pm 1 4 . 0 \%$ before and after the rainfall, respectively (Fig. 4b). Similar dominant contributions of algae were observed for the Geuhmo area for the two phases, which included before and after the rainfall (Fig. 4d). The most obvious change of the POM source (i.e., algae) was observed during the rainfall. For both areas, the algal contribution was substantially diminished during the storm event, which was probably due to the increased contributions of allochthonous sources, even though the allochthonous sources differed in the contributions between the two areas. In detail, in Topyeong area, the decrease of algal POM was compensated by increased contribution of the riparian plant $( 3 3 . 2 \% \pm 2 4 . 0 \% )$ (Fig. 4b), while it was led by the enhanced contribution of both the riparian plant and the road runoff $( 2 2 . 5 \% \pm 1 7 . 9 \%$ and $1 0 . 8 \% \pm 4 . 4 \%$ , respectively) in the Geumho area (Fig. 4d). These discrepancies may be the result of the intrinsic differences in the land uses and the hydraulic residence times between the two areas.

# 3.5. Implications on the POM dynamics during rainy summer seasons

Our results clearly demonstrated that terrestrial/allochthonous POM from the surrounding areas was mobilized into the rivers during an early storm event. The input is likely followed by a rapid downstream transport into the rivers as revealed by the results, which implies a flush of the allochthonous materials at the beginning of the storm event and a decrease at the ending period. This trend has been commonly observed for storm DOM samples (Inamdar et al., 2011; Jeanneau et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2010; Saraceno et al., 2009; Wagner et al., 2019), which has been explained in general by Raymond et al. (2016) as the pulse-shunt concept. This concept explains that during periods of high discharge, a large amount of terrestrial DOM is mobilized to rivers according to the hydrological event (pulse), and then it shunts further in the river networks to the ocean, which highlights the hydrological controls on the quality and the quantity of the DOM within the rivers (Raymond et al., 2016; Wagner et al., 2019). This study demonstrated that the POM might have the similar behavior during the early summer storm event, which was previously reported by Jung et al. (2014) in Korea.

In contrast, the observations based on the late summer storm event have not been previously reported in the literature to the best of our knowledge. During this late summer storm event, the EMMA results based on the fluorescent proxies presented only slight differences in the source contributions between the different phases of the event along with a slight increase of terrestrial sources during the event. In general, the balance between the different OM sources is likely to be controlled by two criteria that include (i) the catchment characteristics (e.g., size, morphology, and topography), and (ii) the rainfall event characteristics (Jeanneau et al., 2015; Tank et al., 2010). In the current study, the only difference in the rain fall characteristics between the two storm events is the rainfall intensity, which was two to three times more intense for event 2 than for event 1 in both watersheds. Thus, some explanations can be given for the unique observation for event 2. First, it is possible that the early rainy season might lead to the flushing of all possibly mobilizable terrestrial POM through rainfall-runoff but not for the other sources. The other factor to explain it may lie in the ecological response to the season. It was previously reported that every summer Korean rivers are hit by a severe algal bloom (Kim, 1989; Kim et al., 2017; Park, 2012; Srivastava et al., 2015). The hot and humid summer and the nutrient-rich condition due to the flush of nutrients during the early rain events may create an ideal system to promote the rapid algal growth in Korean rivers. Thus, the dominant and persisting algae contributions to the POM during the late summer rain event may be associated with the frequent algal bloom over the summer season, which scarcely occurs in the early summer period.

# 4. Conclusion

The EMMA based on fluorescence proxies showed relatively high contributions of terrestrial materials to the riverine POM for both rivers during the early summer storm event. In contrast, this trend was not obviously observed for the late summer storm event. The difference implies that the pulse-shunt concept may operate only for the beginning of the season. The export of the terrestrial POM from the upper catchments into the rivers seems to be timedependent during the summer rainy season. From this particular case, it can be concluded that an intensive and large discharge of terrestrial organic materials that occur at the beginning of the rainy season tended to become weakened with time during the season. This study also clarified the combined impacts of the land use/ surrounding areas, hydrology, and the seasonal factor on the evolution of the riverine POM sources. Both rivers would reveal the potential changes in the POM flux and reactivity according to the evolution of the different contributions from the end-member sources. This study provides an interesting insight into the impacts of the intense hydrological events on the export of the terrestrial POM and further on the potential modifications on the previously established carbon mass balance along the continuum land-river-ocean. This study also demonstrates the capability of the fluorescence proxies based EMMA to identify and quantify the different POM sources, which might be dynamic in the relative contributions during storm events.
