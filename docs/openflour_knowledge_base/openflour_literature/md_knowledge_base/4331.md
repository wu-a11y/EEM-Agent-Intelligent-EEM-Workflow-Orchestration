# ABSTRACT

Black carbon (BC) is a pyrolyzed product derived from incomplete combustion. A major fraction of BC produced by landscape fires is initially deposited onto onsite soils. Atmospheric deposition of soot is known to be an important source of soil BC, especially in watersheds that are not affected by landscape fires. The transport of the dissolved fraction of oxidized BC in soil, defined as dissolved black carbon (DBC), to streams is considered one of the important loss pathways of BC in soil, but the mechanism is not well documented. We measured the quantity and quality of DBC, determined by a benzenepolycarboxylic acid method, and the quantitative and qualitative parameters of bulk dissolved organic matter (DOM) in streams in Hokkaido, northern Japan, whose catchments were not affected by landscape fire for at least 110 years. DBC with relatively low polycondensed signatures occurred in the streams, irrespective of differences in watershed characteristics and seasons, suggesting that atmospheric deposition of soot into the catchment is probably a major source of stream DBC. The DBC concentration was linearly related to the dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentration, irrespective of the differences in watershed characteristics and seasons. Furthermore, the polycondensation degree of DBC was observed to correlate with the qualitative parameters of bulk DOM. Such quantitative and qualitative relationships between DBC and bulk DOM imply that the transfer mechanism from soils to streams of soot-derived polycondensed DBC is linked with that of higher plant-derived, high-molecular-weight aromatic DOM.

# 1. Introduction

Black carbon (BC) is a pyrolyzed product derived from incomplete combustion during biomass burning as well as fossil fuel combustion and includes soot, charcoal, and slightly charred biomass (Masiello, 2004). BC is chemically heterogeneous but has a high carbon content and condensed aromatic structures (Hedges et al., 2000; Masiello, 2004; Wozniak et al., 2020). Although BC produced under low charring temperatures, e.g., levoglucosan, is highly water soluble and biodegradable (Myers-Pigg et al., 2015), the major fraction of BC with polycyclic aromatic structures is considered to be resistant to biodegradation for at least thousands of years (Masiello and Druffel, 1998). The majority of biomass is completely oxidized to ${ \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ during a landscape fire, including wildfires, deforestation fires and agricultural burns, while $5 \mathrm { - } 1 5 \%$ of the biomass is converted to BC (Santín et al., 2016). A recent study estimated that $2 . 2 \mathrm { P g C }$ of $\mathbf { C O } _ { 2 }$ is emitted into the atmosphere, and $0 . 2 6 \ : \mathrm { P g C }$ of biomass carbon is converted to BC by annual landscape fires (Jones et al., 2019a). In addition, $0 . 0 0 2 \mathrm { - } 0 . 0 2 9 ~ \mathrm { P g C } ~ \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ of BC was estimated to be emitted into the atmosphere globally by fossil fuel combustion and biomass burning (Bond et al., 2013). This BC is eventually deposited on the land or ocean and impacts biogeochemical cycles as well as ecosystems (Mari et al., 2014, 2019).

A part of BC produced during landscape fires is emitted to the atmosphere mainly as soot, but the major fraction of BC is initially deposited onto onsite soils mainly as charcoal (Kuhlbusch, 1998; Santín et al., 2016). Otherwise, BC is redistributed to stream corridors or transported to rivers as particulate BC with overland flow (Wagner et al., 2015; Cotrufo et al., 2016). The riverine flux of particulate BC is found to be primarily controlled by erosion (Wagner et al., 2015; Cotrufo et al., 2016; Coppola et al., 2018). The annual global flux of riverine particulate BC to the ocean was estimated to be 0.017 to $0 . 0 3 7 \mathrm { P g C }$ (Coppola et al., 2018), which is equivalent to $6 . 5 \substack { - 1 4 . 2 \% }$ of $0 . 2 6 \ : \mathrm { P g C }$ , the annually produced BC by landscape fires (Jones et al., 2019a).

As BC incorporated in soils ages, BC becomes water soluble due to the introduction of oxygen- and nitrogen-containing functionalities (Knicker, 2011). The soluble fraction of BC in soil pore water is known to be exported to rivers (Hockaday et al., 2006, 2007). The long-term effects of slash and burn activities on the riverine export of dissolved BC (DBC, having condensed aromatic structures, detected by a benzenepolycarboxylic acid (BPCA) method), have been demonstrated (Dittmar et al., 2012). On the other hand, it has been pointed out that anthropogenic BC, associated with soot, is the most prominent source of DBC in glacial rivers (Stubbins et al., 2012; Ding et al., 2015). It has also been documented that BC atmospherically deposited onto vegetation is transferred to soil by throughflow and stemflow (Stubbins et al., 2017; Wagner et al., 2019b), and soot-derived BC deposited in watersheds is reported to be exported to rivers as DBC (Wang et al., 2016; Jones et al., 2017, 2019b). The relationships between riverine DBC parameters and land use variables showed that riverine DBC is generally less polycondensed in anthropogenically disturbed areas of the watershed, namely, urban and agricultural areas (Roebuck et al., 2018a). It was estimated that $0 . 0 2 7 \ \mathrm { P g C }$ of DBC is transported annually to the global ocean (Jaffe et al., 2013 ). The flux has been updated to 0.018 $\mathsf { P g C ~ y r } ^ { - 1 }$ (Jones et al., 2020). The riverine DBC flux is $\sim 1 0 \%$ of the annual BC production by landscape fires (Jones et al., 2019a) and is one order of magnitude larger than the annual atmospheric deposition of BC to the ocean $( \sim 0 . 0 0 2 \ \mathrm { P g C } )$ (Santín et al., 2016); therefore, the transport of DBC from the soil to rivers is a key factor shaping the distributional pattern of BC on the Earth’s surface. However, it should be noted that a recent study, which determined the $\mathfrak { F } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ of riverine and oceanic BPCA, revealed that oceanic DBC does not predominantly originate from rivers (Wagner et al., 2019a).

Recent studies have reported the coupling of the spatial distribution of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and DBC in fluvial systems with global (Jaffe et al., 2013  ), regional (Ding et al., 2015; Stubbins et al., 2015) and different catchment scales (Ding et al., 2013; 2014). Jaffe et al. (2013)  suggested that the coupling of DOC and DBC is possibly due to the same diagenetic processes driving the release of DOC and DBC from soils or the ability of DOC as a mobilizing agent of DBC through hydrophobic interactions. On the other hand, decoupling of DOC and DBC was observed for temporal variations in both parameters for streams and large rivers (Wang et al., 2016; Roebuck et al., 2018b; Bao et al., 2019). Such decoupling of the two parameters was explained by different source contributions, in particular anthropogenic sources (Wang et al., 2016; Roebuck et al., 2018b), or by the delayed recovery of soil pore water DBC stocks relative to pore water DOC stocks following rainfall (Jones et al., 2019b). As such, factors controlling DBC export from soil to rivers have not been well constrained (Wagner et al., 2018).

This study measured DBC in streams with different types of watershed characteristics in a transitional area from a cooltemperate zone to a boreal zone in Hokkaido, northern Japan. An important aspect of the watershed compared with other studies is fire history. The watershed was not affected by landscape fire for at least 110 years, and therefore, a major source of stream DBC is probably soot deposited in the catchment rather than charcoal produced by landscape fire. The bulk dissolved organic matter (DOM) parameters and their optical proxies were analyzed together with DBC quality and quantity determined using the BPCA method. The main purpose of this study was to assess the DBC export from the soil to the stream in a watershed not affected by landscape fires for the last 110 years. To achieve this purpose, we evaluated the quantitative and qualitative relationships between the parameters DBC and bulk DOM and clarified the linkage between the transport mechanisms of DBC and bulk DOM.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study site

The studied streams are located in Hokkaido University’s Uryu Experimental Forest (UREF), northern Hokkaido, Japan (Fig. 1). UREF is a part of the North Hokkaido Experimental Forest, a core site of the Japan Long Term Ecological Research Network (JaLTER). The climate is characterized by cold temperatures (minimum temperature is ca. $- 3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ and heavy snowfall $2 0 0 \mathrm { c m }$ or greater of maximum snowpack depth) in winter with a $4 . 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ mean annual air temperature and $1 4 6 7 \mathrm { m m }$ mean annual precipitation (Ozawa et al., 2001). Stream discharge reaches its maximum during spring snowmelt (Ogawa et al., 2006), and the plant-growing season is restricted to a short summer (June to September) (Seki et al., 2010). Vegetation in the forest area is characterized by a cool-temperate mixed forest composed of natural broad-leaved deciduous hardwood and evergreen coniferous species with dense Sasa dwarf bamboo as understory vegetation (Ogawa et al., 2006; Seki et al., 2010). Stream water samples were collected from the Dorokawa River (D), Butokamabetsu River (B), and Akaishi River (A) in the UREF (Fig. 1). The watersheds of the Dorokawa and Butokamabetsu Rivers are almost all natural forest areas, but that of the Akaishi River is partly in pastureland for dairy cattle farming (Hino et al., 2006). The upstream Dorokawa River is covered by natural forest, while there are several types of wetlands, i.e., spruce-swamp forests, open mire, and riparian wetlands, downstream (Ogawa et al., 2006).

There has been no record of landscape fire around the studied watershed since the establishment of the UREF in 1901. The UREF is located approximately $7 0 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ and $1 6 0 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ north of Asahikawa (population, 0.4 million) and Sapporo (population, 2 million), respectively (Fig. 1). Hokkaido is known to be affected by the longrange transport of BC from eastern Eurasia to the outflow region (Zhu et al., 2019). Therefore, atmospheric deposition of soot produced by fossil fuel combustion and biomass burning in Hokkaido and/or eastern Eurasia are probable major sources of BC in the UREF.

# 2.2. Field observations and sampling

Stream water samples for DBC analysis were collected from sites D, B, and A on June 10, 2011, October 24, 2011, January 24, 2012, April 17, 2012, and September 10, 2012. Additionally, water samples were collected from tributaries of the Dorokawa River, whose watersheds were covered by forest (DF) and wetlands (DW) (Fig. 1), on October 24, 2011, January 24, 2012, April 17, 2012, and September 10, 2012. Approximately $1 0 ~ \mathrm { L }$ of water was collected in an acidcleaned tank. Just after sampling, a water sample was transferred to the laboratory and filtered through a precombusted $( 4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C , 3 \mathsf h )$ Whatman GF/F filter $. 0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ nominal pore size), and the pH of the filtrate was adjusted to 2 with HCl. The acidified filtrate was then immediately subjected to solid-phase extraction (SPE) $^ { 1 } \mathrm { \bf ~ g } ,$ Bond Elut PPL, Agilent Technologies). The SPE cartridge was immediately dried under a $\Nu _ { 2 }$ stream, and the organic matter adsorbed on the SPE resin was eluted by methanol, according to Dittmar et al. (2008). The eluate was poured into a glass vial with a Teflonlined cap and then stored in the dark in a freezer.

The separate water sample was also collected in an acid-cleaned $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ polypropylene bottle for bulk DOM analysis. The sample was filtered using a precombusted Whatman GF/F filter. The pH of filtrate was not changed. DOC concentration, fluorescence spectrum, and absorbance spectrum were measured just after filtration, while samples for total dissolved nitrogen (TDN) and nutrient $( \mathsf { N O } _ { 3 } ^ { - } + \mathsf { N O } _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ , and $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ ) analyses were stored in the dark in a freezer. Samples for bulk DOM analysis were generally collected biweekly from June 2011 to October 2012, and then, DOC concentrations were determined.

Precipitation data observed at the headquarter office of UREF were obtained from the JaLTER Database (http://db.cger.nies.go.jp/ JaLTER/, Metadata ID: JaLTER-Hokkaido-Kita.16.9). Stream discharge was calculated from the recorded water level and the H-Q curve obtained at site D of the Dorokawa River and site B of the Butokamabetus River (Ogawa et al., 2006). Stream discharge was not determined during winter due to river freezing.

# 2.3. Chemical analysis

DBC analysis was performed by the BPCA method according to Dittmar (2008) with some modifications. In 2020, the methanol eluate that had been stored in a freezer in the dark, corresponding to $0 . 3 \mathrm { - } 1 . 5 \mathrm { ~ L ~ }$ of original water, was transferred into $2 \ \mathrm { m L }$ glass ampoules and dried on a centrifugal vacuum evaporator (CVE-3000, EYELA) at $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ Then, $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of concentrated $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ was added to the ampoule, and the ampoule was flame-sealed and kept in an oven at $1 7 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ according to Nakane et al. (2017). After oxidation, $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ was evaporated to dryness in a centrifugal vacuum evaporator at $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } .$ The oxidation residue in the ampoules was redissolved in $0 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of mobile phase A $. 4 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ tetrabutylammonium bromide, $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ sodium acetate, and $1 0 \%$ MeOH) to quantify BPCAs, namely, benzenepentacarboxylic acid (B5CA) and benzenehexacarboxylic acid (B6CA), using high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) with a photodiode array detector (1260 Infinity, Agilent). Analytical conditions of reverse-phase HPLC with mobile phases A and B (MeOH) were based on Dittmar (2008).

Benzenetricarboxylic acids (B3CA) and benzenetetracarboxylic acids (B4CA) were not determined in this study because these compounds were shown to be produced from molecules with no condensed aromatic functionalities, such as lignin (Kappenberg et al., 2016; Bostick et al., 2018).

The DBC concentration was estimated from the BPCA concentrations using equation (1) based on Stubbins et al. (2015).

$$
[ \mathrm { D B C } ] = 1 2 . 0 1 \times 0 . 0 8 9 1 \times \left( [ \mathrm { B 6 C A } + \mathrm { B 5 C A } ] \right) ^ { 0 . 9 1 7 5 }
$$

The units of [DBC] and [BPCAs] were $\mu \mathrm { g C } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ and nM, respectively. The molar ratio of B6CA to B5CA (B6CA/B5CA) was calculated as the polycondensation degree of DBC (Stubbins et al., 2015; Marques et al., 2017). The analytical error determined with replicate analyses $( n = 4 )$ of a sample from a stream in the UREF was $5 . 0 \%$ and $4 . 1 \%$ for the DBC concentration and the B6CA/B5CA ratio, respectively.

The DOC concentration was determined by a high-temperature catalytic oxidation method $\mathrm { \Delta T O C - V _ { C S H } , }$ Shimadzu). A standard curve with a series of glucose solutions determined daily was used for calibration.

Absorbance analysis of DOM was carried out using a spectrophotometer (UV-1800, Shimadzu) with a $1 \mathrm { { \ c m } }$ cuvette. The absorbance spectrum was determined from 250 to $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $0 . 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals under a medium scan speed. The absorbance spectrum of the sample was baseline-corrected by subtracting average values ranging from 700 to $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { \ n m }$ from the entire spectrum. The Napierian absorption coefficient at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , a254 $( \mathrm m ^ { - 1 } )$ , was reported as a quantitative parameter of chromophoric DOM (CDOM). Specific UV absorbance (SUVA) was calculated by dividing the Decadic absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , $\mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ $( \mathrm m ^ { - 1 } )$ , by the DOC concentration $( \mathrm { m g \ L ^ { - 1 } } )$ (Weishaar et al., 2003). The $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ value, the ratio of the spectral slope parameter (S) obtained from the two regions, $2 7 5 { - } 2 9 5 \ \mathrm { n m } \left( S _ { 2 7 5 { - } 2 9 5 } \right)$ and $3 5 0 \mathrm { - } 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \left( S _ { 3 5 0 \mathrm { - } 4 0 0 } \right)$ , was calculated according to Helms et al. (2008).

Excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence was determined with a fluorometer (FluoroMax-4, Horiba) according to Tanaka et al. (2014) with some modifications. Forty-one emission scans from 290 to $5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ taken at $2 { \cdot } \mathrm { n m }$ intervals were acquired for excitation wavelengths between 250 and $4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \mathrm { - n m }$ intervals. The bandpass was set to $5 \ \mathrm { \ n m }$ for both excitation and emission monochromators, and the integration time was set to $0 . 1 \ s .$ . The fluorescence spectra were acquired in $S / \mathbb { R }$ mode. Several postacquisition steps were involved in the correction of the fluorescence spectrum, including inner filter correction with absorbance spectrum, instrumental bias correction, and the subtraction of the EEM of Milli-Q water (Murphy et al., 2010). The fluorescence unit was converted to a Raman unit (RU) with a water Raman peak (excitation $= 3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ) of Milli-Q water analyzed daily (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009). The fluorescence index was calculated as the ratio of fluorescence intensities at $4 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $5 2 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ emissions at $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ excitation according to Cory and McKnight (2005). Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was conducted in MATLAB (Mathworks, Natick, MA) using the DOMFluor toolbox ((Stedmon and Bro, 2008) with 205 EEMs collected from streams in the UREF watersheds. A four-component model was validated according to (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Following Coble (2007), C1, C2, and C3 (Fig. S1) were assigned as humic-like components, and C4 (Fig. S1) was identified as a protein-like component.

TDN and nutrient $( \mathsf { N O } _ { 3 } ^ { - } + \mathsf { N O } _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ , and $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ ) concentrations were determined using a continuous flow analyzer (Hansen and Koroleff, 1999) with a built-in wet oxidation system by potassium persulfate (QuAAtro, Bran þ Luebbe Analytics). The dissolved organic nitrogen (DON) concentration was determined by subtracting the inorganic nutrient concentrations from the TDN concentration. The $\mathsf { C } / \mathsf { N }$ ratio of bulk DOM was calculated as the molar ratio of DOC concentration to DON concentration.

# 2.4. Statistical analysis

The differences in DBC parameters, i.e., the DBC contribution of DOC (DBC/DOC, $\%$ ) and the B6CA/B5CA ratio, among watersheds were examined. The Shapiro-Wilk test indicated that the DBC parameters in the three watersheds followed a normal distribution (Table S1). However, due to the small number of samples, we used a nonparametric Wilcoxon rank sum test as well as a parametric ttest for the comparison of DBC parameters. Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient and Kendall’s rank correlation coefficient were determined between DBC and the bulk DOM parameters. The results of Kendall’s rank correlation test can be found in Supplementary Tables and Figures. Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient and Kendall’s rank correlation coefficient were also determined for DBC and DOC vs. discharge and precipitation. The precipitation data obtained from April to October were used for the correlation analyses to eliminate snowfall. Since many values of precipitation were 0 during the observation of DOC, correlation coefficients were not determined between DOC and precipitation. A confidence level $( { \boldsymbol { \alpha } } )$ of $9 5 \%$ was used for all statistics. The Wilcoxon rank sum test, t-test, Spearman’s rank correlation test, and Kendall’s rank correlation test were carried out with R (version 3.5.1).

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Stream DBC characteristics and its origin

The DBC contribution to the DOC $( \% )$ ranged from $3 . 5 \pm 0 . 9 \%$ for pastureland to $4 . 8 \ : \pm \ : 0 . 7 \%$ for wetlands in streams of the UREF watersheds and was smaller than the $1 0 . 6 \pm 0 . 7 \%$ global average (Jaffe et al., 2013  ). DBC contributions have recently been determined to significantly differ among biomes (Jones et al., 2020), and the contributions observed in streams in the UREF watersheds were similar to those in temperate forests $( 4 . 4 \pm 2 . 5 \% )$ and boreal forests $( 5 . 4 \pm 1 . 1 \% )$ (Jones et al., 2020).

It has been documented that DBC is produced from not only fresh charcoal but also old charcoal (Abiven et al., 2011; Wagner et al., 2017), and DBC has been continuously released into rivers for a long time through the degradation of historically accumulated soil BC (Dittmar et al., 2012; Marques et al., 2017). It is possible that an ancient forest fire event contributed to the DBC in the studied streams, even though the sites were unburned for at least 110 years.

Recent studies have indicated that atmospheric deposition of BC to watersheds is a source of DBC in soil water (Santos et al., 2017) as well as riverine DBC (Jones et al., 2017, 2019b). It has also been documented that BC atmospherically deposited onto vegetation is transferred to riverine catchments by throughflow and stemflow (Stubbins et al., 2017; Wagner et al., 2019b). It is interesting to note that recent studies have suggested that the DBC derived from soot is less polycondensed than that from charcoal produced by a wildfire (Ding et al., 2015; Roebuck et al., 2018a; Coppola et al., 2019). Ding et al. (2015) reported that the DBC in the water soluble fraction extracted from atmospheric dust was less polycondensed than wildfire-derived DBC, and Roebuck et al. (2018a) suggested that atmospheric deposition of soot or polyaromatic hydrocarbons contributed to the less polycondensed DBC signature in streams in the urban area of the Altamaha watershed. A negative relationship between the polycondensation degree, namely, B6CA/ B5CA, in riverine DBC and the fossil fuel BC deposition rate integrated in corresponding subcatchments was also found in the Amazon River (Coppola et al., 2019). The B6CA/B5CA ratio of the DBC observed in streams in the UREF watersheds was generally lower than that of the DBC in an area of the Amazon River subcatchment (B6CA/B5CA of 0.26), which is the most affected by the atmospheric deposition of BC produced by fossil fuel combustion in addition to charcoal produced by landscape fires. The ${ \mathsf { B 6 C A } } /$ B5CA ratio of the DBC in streams of the UREF was also generally lower than the ratio of the water-soluble fraction extracted from atmospheric dust (B6CA/B5CA of 0.32, Ding et al., 2015). Additionally, the DBC concentration was not significantly correlated with precipitation for the UREF watersheds (Fig. S6), implying that soot and DBC in precipitation are unlikely to contribute directly to DBC in the streams. These pieces of evidence suggest that atmospheric deposition of soot on the soil of the catchment and subsequent mobilization and transport of soot-derived BC to the stream are probably the major sources of DBC in the UREF watersheds.

# 4.2. Factors controlling DBC export from soils to streams

The DOC and DBC concentrations were not significantly correlated $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ with stream discharge (Fig. 2, Figs. S3 and S5). Positive relationships between river (stream) discharge and DOC concentration (e.g., Holmes et al., 2008; Wilson et al., 2013) and DBC concentration (Dittmar et al., 2012; Stubbins et al., 2015;

Wagner et al., 2015), indicative of flushing of high levels of DOC and DBC from upper soil horizons under high flow conditions, have often been observed. On the other hand, in addition to the discharge, antecedent moisture conditions were determined to control the DOC concentration under high flow conditions (Inamdar et al., 2008; Oswald and Branfireun, 2014). The different effects of antecedent precipitation on riverine DOC and DBC concentrations due to the quick and slow rebound of porewater DOC and DBC concentrations after precipitation, respectively, were also noted for the Amazon River (Jones et al., 2019b). In our study, two spikes of DOC concentration were found in the small peaks of discharge with precipitation events after relatively long nonprecipitation periods (Fig. 2). These results suggest that spikes in the DOC and DBC concentrations observed in the UREF watersheds were possibly affected by antecedent precipitation. Further observations are needed to determine the factors controlling temporal variations in DOC and DBC concentrations in the UREF watersheds.

The DBC concentration was linearly related to the DOC concentration, irrespective of differences in seasons and watershed characteristics (Fig. 3a), even though the DBC/DOC $( \% )$ was different among the watersheds with different characteristics. Linear relationships between the two parameters have also been observed from the watershed scale to the global scale (Jaffe et al., 2013 ; Ding et al., 2013, 2014, 2015; Stubbins et al., 2015; Wagner et al., 2015). On the other hand, decoupling of the DBC concentration and DOC concentration, probably due to the different source contributions, particularly anthropogenic source contributions (Wang et al., 2016; Roebuck et al., 2018b), or the divergent effects of soil properties, temperature, rainfall, and aerosol deposition (Bao et al., 2019; Jones et al., 2019b), has also been reported. Interestingly, it has been pointed out that charcoal in soils supplies DBC to channels at baseflow, and atmospherically deposited soot contributes as an additional source of riverine DBC during high-flow regimes (Roebuck et al., 2018b; Jones et al., 2019b). Such a shift in DBC sources to channels was suggested to be a reason for seasonal decoupling of DOC and DBC (Roebuck et al., 2018b; Jones et al., 2019b). The major source of DBC in the stream in the UREF watersheds during the baseflow as well as spikes of the discharge was possibly mobilization from the soot deposited on the soil in the watersheds but was not direct contribution of soot and DBC in precipitation (Fig. S6). Therefore, the reason for the linear relationship between the DBC and DOC concentrations observed in this study might be the result of the coupled transport of soot-derived DBC and DOC from the soil to the stream.

The DBC/DOC $( \% )$ and B6CA/B5CA ratio values in streams with wetlands in the watersheds were significantly higher than those in streams in which watersheds were completely covered by forests and partly covered by pastureland (Fig. S7). In addition, a positive correlation was evident between the two parameters (Fig. 4b). A significant positive correlation was also observed between DBC/ DOC and B6CA/B5CA in small watersheds in the southeastern part of China and was considered a result of photodegradation of DBC (Bao et al., 2019). The streams observed in this study were near headwater regions that are heavily forested (Fig. 1), suggesting that a process other than photodegradation must be causing changes in DBC/DOC and B6CA/B5CA in this study.

Roebuck et al. (2018a) found that the DBC concentration and BPCA ratio in river water were positively correlated with wetland coverage but were negatively correlated with urban land use in the Altamaha River, a large subtropical watershed. Such relationships were considered to be the result of changes in the DBC sources along the river from fire-derived charcoal in wetlands to fossil fuel combustion-derived soot in urban areas (Roebuck et al., 2018a). Since the major source of DBC in the UREF watersheds is probably soot deposited on the soil of the watershed, the differences in the source may not solely account for the differences in DBC/DOC and B6CA/B5CA among the watersheds in the UREF. Kothawala et al. (2012) showed that larger aromatic compounds can be preferentially adsorbed in mineral-rich soils. Thus, a fraction having relatively highly polycondensed DBC mobilized from soot deposited onto soil may be preferentially retained in mineral-rich soils in forested and pastureland areas, resulting in a lower polycondensation degree of riverine DBC in forested and pastureland areas than in wetland areas.

The linear relationship between the DBC concentration and DOC concentration has been interpreted as the result of similar diagenetic processes driving the release of DOC and DBC from soils or intermolecular associations between DOC and DBC through hydrophobic interactions (Jaffe et al., 2013  ). In addition to the linear relationship between the two parameters, we found significant correlations between the polycondensation degree of DBC (B6CA/ B5CA) and the qualitative parameters of bulk DOM in the UREF watersheds (Fig. 4). SUVA is positively related to the aromaticity of DOM (Weishaar et al., 2003), while $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ is negatively related to the molecular weight of DOM (Helms et al., 2008). Therefore, positive and negative correlations between B6CA/B5CA and SUVA and $S _ { \mathrm { R } , }$ respectively, indicate that the environmental dynamics of polycondensed DBC are similar to those of high-molecular-weight aromatic DOM. The polycondensation degree of DBC was also positively correlated with the $\mathsf { C } / \mathsf { N }$ of bulk DOM but negatively correlated with the fluorescence index of bulk DOM. A high value of C/N and a low value of the fluorescence index indicate a higher plant origin of DOM (McKnight et al., 1997, 2001). In conclusion, the qualitative relationships between DBC and DOC observed in this study indicate that the transfer mechanism from soils to streams, namely, the release and subsequent adsorption and desorption processes, is similar between DBC derived from soot deposited on soil and higher plant-derived, high-molecular-weight aromatic DOM. This conclusion is supported by the significant positive correlation between SUVA and DBC/DOC (Fig. 4a).

The results of controlled DBC leaching experiments using charcoal and wildfire-influenced soil samples did not determine the effect of DOM as a mobilizing agent through hydrophobic interactions (Wagner et al., 2017). Wagner et al. (2017) also determined the octanol-water partition coefficients of modeled DBC and noted that a considerable portion of the DBC pool that has been quantified in aquatic environments is not truly dissolved. Knowledge of “DBC speciation” will help to better understand the biogeochemical mechanisms that control the transfer of BC from soils to streams. The soot from urban dust probably releases more DBC than charcoal produced from onsite soils, since the polycondensation degree of DBC dissolved from soot is lower than that from charcoal (Ding et al., 2015). Therefore, the soot/charcoal ratio in soils is possibly a factor that controls the mobility of DBC and shapes the coupling/decoupling of riverine DBC and DOC. DBC leaching experiments with charcoal as well as soils affected by atmospheric deposition of soot may clarify specific mechanisms of DBC leaching from various types of BC in soils.

# 5. Conclusions

This study investigated the quantitative and qualitative relationships among DBC and DOC in streams in a transitional area from a cool-temperate zone to a boreal zone in which catchments were not affected by landscape fire for at least 110 years. The relatively low polycondensation degree of DBC found in this study implies that atmospherically deposited soot on the soil in the catchment, which can preferentially release poorly polycondensed

DBC, is an important source of riverine DBC and is one of the important processes in the global BC cycle. A strong linear relationship was found between the DOC and DBC concentrations, irrespective of the differences in watershed characteristics and seasons. Furthermore, qualitative relationships were evident between DBC and bulk DOM. These quantitative and qualitative relationships suggest that for landscapes with dominant inputs of soot-derived BC, the transfer mechanisms of natural DOC and DBC are closely linked, while this linkage for wildfire charcoalderived DBC has been shown to be more complex.
