Soil organic matter (SOM) plays a dominant role in the functionality of agricultural soils and particularly so in organic farming. Yet, there is limited knowledge on the effect of organic management in which the soil is subjected to incorporation of a variety of organic residues on the composition of SOM and water-extractable organic matter (WEOM) in top and sub-surface soil layers. The general objective of this study was to quantify depth-related changes in the composition of SOM and WEOM in an organically managed soil subjected to multiple compost applications, using spectroscopic techniques requiring no or minimal soil sample pre-treatment. We collected soil samples across the top $6 0 \mathrm { c m }$ on October 2012 from an existing field experiment initiated in late 2009, which was organically fertilized by means of compost and green manure incorporation. Compost was applied at levels of 0 (control), 20, 40 and $6 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 }$ , with the control treatment being fertilized with urea and amended with green manure. The collected samples were then used to characterize (i) SOM by FTIR absorbance associated with hydrophilic SOM functional groups and aliphatic CHs, total organic C and N contents, and (ii) WEOM by dissolved organic C (DOC) concentration, UV–VIS absorbance and fluorescent components identified following parallel factor analysis. In general, for all the studied attributes the core data tended to (i) increase after compost addition, although differences among the compost doses could not always be identified, and (ii) decrease with soil depth for all compost doses as well as the control treatment. Compost addition enriched soil by hydrophobic organic matter and water-extractable aromatic and, specifically, humic-like components. In the compost-amended soil, SOM became depleted of hydrophilic groups and enriched by hydrophobic aliphatic CH-rich substances. However, the content of hydrophilic organic matter in SOM was elevated with increasing depth.

# 1. Introduction

Soil organic matter (SOM) has been considered a good indicator of a healthy soil system (Haynes, 2005) as it plays an important role in a wide range of soil properties and processes such as maintenance of soil structure (Oades, 1993), water-retention capacity (Lado et al., 2004), nutrient cycling (Goulding et al., 2000) and stimulation of biological activities (Jimenez et al., 2002). The addition of organic residues to soil increases SOM content over time (Schulten et al., 1992; Gregorich et al., 1996), modifies the chemical, physical and biological properties of the soil (Nardi et al., 2004; Adani et al., 2007) and increases its total N content (Marschner et al., 2003; Blackshaw et al., 2005) and C storage (Cabrera et al., 2009; Powlson et al., 2012), with the latter occurring directly from C inputs by amendments and indirectly from increased plant production (Eghball and Power, 1999; Ryals and Silver, 2013).

The role of SOM may become even more meaningful under organic management, as organic farming practices minimize chemicals inputs and rely on periodic incorporation of various organic residues and on reduced or no tillage, which in turn enhance SOM content (Doane and Horwath, 2004; Raviv, 2010). Increased SOM content enhances the activity and diversity of macro and microorganisms which are critical for the reduction of plants susceptibility to pests when pesticides are avoided (Raviv, 2010), as well as for successful crop nutrition that is based on nutrient cycling (Watson et al., 2002).

Concerning SOM-related biological functionalities, it is relevant to investigate labile soil organic C pools such as water-extractable organic matter (WEOM), microbial biomass and particulate organic matter that turn over quickly and can respond more rapidly to soil management practices (Haynes, 2005; Liang et al., 2011). Earlier studies have reported that organic matter extractable from soil is sensitive to the use of organic amendment (Leinweber et al., 1995; Chantigny et al., 2000). The application of composts to soil increased the stable organic matter content (Franko et al., 1997; Watson et al., 2002), and enhanced the activity and size of soil microbial communities (Saison et al., 2006).

Although the studies on the effects of organic amendments on organic matter dynamics in soils often focused on the upper soil layer $( 0 { - } 2 0 \thinspace \mathrm { c m } )$ (West and Post, 2002), some studies also looked at depth distribution of SOM and its components in relation to land use and management, concluding that deeper soil horizons are capable of sequestrating SOM due to its increased recalcitrance and turnover time (Lorentz and Lal, 2005; von Lützow et al., 2008). Subsoil horizons (below $2 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ ) are considered to contain large (and even major) amounts of soil organic C within the $0 { - } 3 ~ \mathrm { \textmu }$ depth thus affecting global C storage and dynamics (Jobbagy and Jackson, 2000; Janzen, 2005). A long term study of tillage effects in the $0 { - } 6 0 \mathrm { c m }$ soil profile reported a greater soil organic C in the upper $0 { - } 1 0 \mathrm { c m }$ layer under no tillage than under conventional tillage, but no effects on the labile C fraction below $1 0 \mathrm { c m }$ (Liu et al., 2014). Conversely, a long term application of manures increased dehydrogenase activity and organic C pools in 0– $6 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ depth relative to control (Liu et al., 2014; Simon and Czako, 2014). Several field studies have shown that concentration and fluxes of dissolved organic matter (DOM) in soil solution decrease significantly with soil depth (Kalbitz et al., 2000a). Subsoil SOM may be contributed more significantly by microbial-derived organic matter as compared with plant-derived material, and its degradation can be suppressed by SOM interactions with minerals, occlusion in soil aggregates and, possibly, unfavorable temperature, supply of nutrients and energy (Rumpel and Kögel-Knabner, 2011). Formation of organo-mineral complexes and occlusion in soil aggregates are important SOM protection mechanisms at different soil depths, but degradation dynamics of root litter may be different in top and subsoil (Sanaullah et al., 2011). Subsoil SOM was found to be horizontally stratified in adjacent compartments which should be taken into account in examining subsoil C sequestration (Chabbi et al., 2009). Yet, the importance of organic C in subsoils is underestimated, and more efforts are needed to better understand the mechanisms of soil organic C storage and turnover (SUBSOM - DFG).

The methodologies used to characterize SOM composition are often destructive (e.g., thermochemolysis and pyrolysis-field ionization mass-spectrometry; Lorentz and Lal, 2005; Erich et al., 2012), and may involve an aggressive pre-treatment, such as HF handling of soil samples for $^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ NMR measurements (Downie et al., 2011; Clouard et al., 2014) or base extraction for recovering humic substances. Alternatively, non-destructive FTIR spectroscopy has been used successfully for SOM characterization in bulk soil samples (Haberhauer and Gerzabek, 1999; Reeves et al., 2001; Tremblay and Gagné, 2002; Demyan et al., 2012; Voelkner et al., 2015). Recently, it was suggested to quantify the accumulation of certain SOM components in non-disturbed soil samples, by relating the absorbance of IR radiation by a given organic functional group to the absorbance by quartz (Bernier et al., 2013). The latter is commonly present in soils, its content is hardly sensitive to soil management and, hence, quartz could be used as an internal reference. Fluorescence spectroscopy is commonly used to examine fluorescing organic matter in aqueous solutions (Senesi et al., 1989; Zsolnay et al., 1999; Kalbitz et al., 2000b; Chen et al., 2003; Borisover et al., 2012; Cohen et al., 2014). Fluorescence spectroscopy (combined with thermal analysis) was used to study the effects of profile depth and organic amendment on the composition of labile and total SOM (Erich et al., 2012). UV-absorbance may provide a proxy for concentrations of aromatic DOM components soluble in water (Zsolnay and Steindl, 1991; Weishaar et al., 2003). Hence, based on ultraviolet–visible (UV/VIS) spectroscopy of WEOM, it was shown that the aromaticity and humification degree of WEOM increased with depth in the top $3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ of organic matter-poor sandy soil treated by different amendments (Daouk et al., 2015).

The fate of SOM and its water-extractable components in deeper soil layers may be different under organic management, in which C inputs are typically greater and the biological activity at the upper soil layer may be more intense, than under conventional management. However, the changes in the chemical composition of SOM and WEOM in sub-surface horizons of agricultural soils under organic farming practice have barely been investigated and are, thus, poorly understood. Therefore, examination of the composition of SOM/WEOM across a profile of organically managed soil is required, having the potential to provide original data and new meaningful insight for organic farming.

Thus, the general objective of this study was to quantify depth-related changes in the composition of SOM and its water-extractable constituents in an organically managed soil that was subjected to multiple compost applications. For that, spectroscopic techniques were selected, requiring no or minimal soil sample pre-treatment, including: (a) FTIRspectroscopic analysis of the whole soil samples (SOM characterization), and (b) UV/VIS spectroscopy and excitation-emission fluorescence of the water-extractable organic matter (WEOM characterization). FTIRspectroscopic analysis was enhanced by applying a novel approach using quartz as the internal soil reference (Bernier et al., 2013) which allows examining not only the SOM compositional changes but also the accumulation of separate SOM components in the whole soil sample. Examining WEOM fluorescence was carried out by measuring excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) of fluorescence, augmented with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) which provides the possibility to identify chemically sounding fluorescent components of organic matter (Smilde et al., 2004; Ohno and Bro, 2006). Consequently, a new set of data that had not been available before was obtained, and a better understanding of depth-related changes in organic matter composition under organic management may be expected.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study site, soil characterization and experimental design

The experimental field is situated at Newe Ya'ar Research Center, located in the Jezre'el Valley, northern Israel $( 3 2 ^ { \circ } 4 2 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { N } 3 5 ^ { \circ } 1 1 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { E }$ ). The contents of total organic C (TOC) and total N (TN) in the soil samples were determined by dry combustion with a Flashea™ 1112 NC elemental analyzer (Thermo Fi Elementar, Hanau, Germany).

# 2.2. SOM characterization by FTIR spectroscopy

FTIR analysis of the soil samples was carried out following the procedure described by Bernier et al. (2013). In brief, pellets were prepared by homogenizing a soil sample $3 \ \mathrm { m g }$ ) from each treatment with $3 0 0 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { K B r }$ (Fluka Analytical) pre-dried at $1 0 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } _ { \iota }$ , with a mortar and pestle. Similarly to Reeves et al. (2001), the soil had been dried at $1 0 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ beforehand to minimize interference in the FTIR spectra due to moisture. According to DSC analysis of clay-associated SOM (Plante et al., 2005), exothermic region associated with oxidation of organic matter appears only between $2 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and $5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ Based on XANES spectroscopy, drying soil at $1 0 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ had no effects on relative abundance of the aliphatic CH and the $\mathtt { C = } 0$ groups (Kiersch et al., 2012). When examining thermal alteration of soil physico-chemical properties, temperatures below $2 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ were reported to have a very little effect on quality or quantity of SOM (Araya et al., 2016). Therefore, in the comparative analysis carried out in the present work the effect of sample drying at $1 0 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ on soil (SOM) characterization by FTIR was considered minimal. Pellet preparations of the samples were made with the help of a PIKE AutoCrush IR hydraulic apparatus, by applying a fixed weight of 9 tons $\mathrm { c m } ^ { - 2 }$ maintained for $7 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The diameter of the pellets was $1 3 \mathrm { m m }$ Immediately after preparing the pellets, the spectra were measured by a Bruker Tensor 27 Spectrometer in the range $4 0 0 0 { - } 4 0 0 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ with $4 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ resolution, and 200 scans were performed on each acquisition. Each collected spectrum was measured in ambient air against that of pure KBr as a background reference. The data processing was carried out using the software OPUS 6.5 (similarly to Bernier et al., 2013) and included (i) correction for atmospheric ${ \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and water, (ii) baseline correction, (iii) use of the second derivative to smooth the spectrum and reduce its noise. The FTIR spectra were obtained for all 80 soil samples representing five replicate plots of each of the four treatments and sampling depths.

The effect of compost addition on SOM composition was evaluated by using the band areas at 2929, 2875, 1640 and $7 9 4 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ . The absorbances at 2929 and $2 8 7 5 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ are related to symmetric and asymmetric aliphatic CH stretching vibrations in methyl and methylene groups (Silverstein and Webster, 1997; Ellerbrock and Gerke, 2004). The relevant SOM chemical groups absorbing at $1 6 4 0 ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ contain O and/or N atoms and are relatively hydrophilic (or sensitive to the presence of neighboring hydrophilic groups as in the case of the double $\mathtt { C } = \mathtt { C }$ bonding; Silverstein and Webster, 1997). The absorbance at $1 6 4 0 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ may reflect $\mathrm { N H } _ { 2 }$ and NH bending in primary and secondary amides (amide II band), $\mathtt { C = } 0$ stretching vibrations in amides (amide I band), NH bending vibration of primary amines, asymmetric stretching vibrations of COO- , hydrogen-bonded $\scriptstyle { \mathsf { C } } = { \mathsf { O } }$ of carbonyl or quinone (as detailed in Bernier et al. 2013; see references therein). The absorbance at $8 0 0 { - } 7 9 5 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ associated with symmetric Si-O stretching is widely used as the diagnostic peak for quartz (Hanna, 1965; Reig et al., 2002). The band areas were determined after a straight baseline was drawn between the two defined wavenumber limits. The area above this line was then integrated.

The IR absorbance data analysis involved normalization of the band areas measured at 2929, 2875 or $1 6 4 0 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ by the band area of quartz absorbance. In this normalization the possible contributions of the outof-plane amide NH wagging and of the out-of-plane bending of the aromatic ring CH bonds in the area of the quartz peak were considered less important (Bernier et al., 2013). Since the quartz content in the soil layers is not expected to be affected by the addition of organic amendments and may only slightly vary with sampling depth, the quartz absorbance-normalized band areas allow quantitative monitoring of enrichment or depletion of certain SOM groups in soil affected by organic amendments.

# 2.3. Characterization of WEOM

# 2.3.1. Dissolved organic C (DOC) and UV–VIS absorbance measurements

Soil WEOM was obtained from each of the 80 samples by shaking $1 0 \mathrm { g }$ of the $< 2 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ soil samples with $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of de-ionized water for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ on a horizontal shaker at $1 4 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ (as modified after Maxin and KogelKnabner, 1995). The soil–water suspension was then centrifuged for

$1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at relative centrifugal force of 3430. The supernatant was filtered through a $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ polyethersulfone membrane filter (Bonna-Agela Technologies) and stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . The WEOM solutions were characterized for DOC concentration with Formacs HT Combustion TOC analyzer.

The UV–VIS spectra of the WEOM solutions were measured in the $2 0 0 { - } 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ wavelength range in a $1 \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cuvette with a Genesis 10UV (Thermo Scientific) spectrophotometer. The DOM in the WEOM solutions was also characterized by absorbance determined at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ $( \mathsf { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 } )$ which is considered as an acceptable representation of the content of aromatic structures in the overall DOM. The specific UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ (SUVA, $\mathsf { L } \operatorname* { m g } { \mathsf { C } } ^ { - 1 } \operatorname { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ) was calculated as follows: $\mathrm { S U V A } = 1 0 0 \times { \tt A b s } _ { 2 5 4 } / ( \mathrm { D O C } )$ ) where L is the optical path length (cm) and DOC is in $\mathrm { m g L ^ { - 1 } }$ .

# 2.3.2. Measurements and analysis of fluorescence of WEOM solutions

All WEOM solutions were characterized by EEMs of fluorescence. To minimize the “inner filter effect” (IFE) related to light absorption by the solution itself, solutions had been diluted when necessary by non-fluorescent high quality water. A dilution factor was chosen to obtain the solutions with absorbance $< 0 . 0 5$ (Lakowicz, 1999) at $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . At any wavelength ${ > } 2 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , the absorbance of a solution was even less. The pH of aqueous soil extracts prepared for fluorescence measurements varied in the 7.0–7.4 range. Fluorescence of each WEOM solution was measured in a $1 \mathrm { c m }$ fluorescence-free quartz cuvette using spectrofluorometer (Shimadzu RF-5301PC) equipped with 150-W Xenon lamp (Ushio Inc., Japan). The procedure used for obtaining the EEMs was similar to that described by Borisover et al. (2009). Briefly, EEMs were determined at $2 3 \pm 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ with the excitation and emission band-pass slit widths of $5 . 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . Fluorescence emission spectra between 220 and $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ were collected at $2 { \cdot } \mathrm { { n m } }$ increments, with excitation wavelengths ranging from 220 to $5 9 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $5 \mathrm { - n m }$ increments. Instrument stability was tested by monitoring the intensity of the Raman peak of pure de-ionized water at excitation/emission wavelengths of 270/ $2 9 8 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ (Borisover et al., 2009, 2011; Cohen et al., 2014), which was found to be stable (28.1 in arbitrary units) with standard deviation of 2.0 units over the analysis period.

Thereafter, the EEMs data were analyzed by means of PARAFAC (Andersen and Bro, 2003; Stedmon et al., 2003) by using the DOMFluor Toolbox-v1-7-2009 (Stedmon and Bro, 2008) for Matlab R2007b. The procedure of the data pre-processing included (a) assigning zero emission intensity values when the emission wavelength was less than the excitation wavelength, (b) considering the emission intensities in the Rayleigh scatter corridors as missing values, (c) converting any negative emission intensities, if found, to missing values, (d) subtracting water blank data, (e) normalizing the intensities in the pre-processed EEMs by the Raman scattering intensity of pure water at $2 7 0 / 2 9 8 \mathrm { n m }$ , determined at the same day of WEOM analysis (more details are given in Cohen et al., 2014). In order to reduce the impact of instrumental noise and IFE, only emissions obtained at excitation wavelengths exceeding $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ were finally considered. In the PARAFAC modeling, nonnegative constraints were applied for excitation and emission loadings. The procedures for selecting the number of determinable fluorescent components included, among others, split-half analysis and core consistency diagnostics, following the recommendations by Stedmon and Bro (2008). In the data set, one sample was removed as an outlier. Finally, emission intensities of components recovered from the EEM $^ +$ PARAFAC examination were multiplied by the dilution factor applied in preparation of WEOM solutions for fluorescence measurements.

# 2.4. Statistical analysis

The data presented are averages of the 5 field replicate plots established for each treatment. Correlation and linear regression analyses were carried out with Statistica 7.0 (Statsoft Inc. 2004). Only correlations characterized by $\left| \mathbf { r } \right| > 0 . 7$ (resulting in explained variability of a dependent property $54 9 \%$ ) are discussed in the text.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Soil organic matter

# 3.1.1. Total organic C and total N contents

The TOC and TN contents of the soil samples obtained from the four depths, following application of different amounts of compost, are shown in Fig. 1. Generally, the soil TOC and TN contents had a tendency to decrease with depth for all the treatments, although in multiple cases the changes, especially between the top 0–5 and the $5 { - } 1 5 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ layers, were not statistically significant. The positive effect of compost addition on TOC and TN contents, as compared with the control, was observed for each layer studied within the $0 { - } 3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ soil depth, however, this effect could hardly be statistically differentiated between different layers. The data shown in Fig. 1 suggest no significant effect of compost dose (20 to $6 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 }$ ) on TOC and TN contents. Presumably, TOC and TN were still critically affected by the history of these plots three years earlier, which included green manure incorporation (treatments i–iv) and one uniform application of compost at the highest dose $( 6 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 }$ ; treatments i–iii). Thus the cumulative applied amounts of C and N were in a ratio of 1:1.3:1.6 for treatments i, ii, and iii, respectively. Also, the lack of the effect of the compost dose (above $2 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { h } \mathsf { a } ^ { - 1 }$ ), at the background of earlier treatments, seems to fit the concept of soil C saturation (Six et al., 2002; Gulde et al., 2008; Stewart et al., 2009). Considering various mechanisms of SOM stabilization, this concept suggests that soil TOC may not respond to added organic matter due to a limited soil stabilizing capacity and a faster turnover of labile soil C fractions (Campbell et al., 1991; Stewart et al., 2008; Castellano et al., 2012). The lack of significant effect of compost addition on TOC and TN in the $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 \mathrm { c m }$ soil layer, as compared with the control, resulted from a shallow tillage, as mentioned in Materials and Methods and suggests a depth limit of organic treatment impact on bulk SOM.

For the samples taken from the $0 { - } 3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ depth, a statistically significant linear dependence characterized by $r = 0 . 8 3$ (at $p < 0 . 0 0 0 0 0 1 _ { . }$ ) between TN and TOC values was observed: $\mathrm { T N } = ( 0 . 0 1 5 ) + ( 0 . 0 9 3 4 ) \times \mathrm { T O C }$ , where all the field replicates were included in the analysis. The unique relationship between the TN and the TOC suggests that the total organic C and N demonstrate similar overall rates of accumulation, subsurface transport and degradation. Lack of effects of the treatments and soil depth could also be related, at least in part, to the pre-treatment history of the experimental field plots which might have resulted in a development of a SOM matrix that is homogeneous in terms of the C to N ratio. The observed higher C to N ratio in the $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 \mathrm { c m }$ layer compared to the shallower layers clearly indicates a depletion of N (relative to C) in that layer, regardless of the treatments studied.

Furthermore, the R values were additionally normalized by soil TOC contents. This normalization provides an index describing the relative changes of organic matter functional group concentrations in the SOM (Fig. 2b,d,f). A multiplying factor of 1000 is introduced in Fig. 2c,d,e,f for the sake of convenience.

The $\mathrm { R } _ { 1 6 4 0 / 7 9 7 }$ vs. depth data (Fig. 2a) suggests that soil content of hydrophilic SOM components was weakly affected, if at all, by compost addition or soil depth. However, the R1640/797 values normalized by soil TOC content data (Fig. 2b) clearly increase with depth in the $0 { - } 3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ layer. This increase indicates that SOM becomes enriched by hydrophilic components. Compost addition resulted in a decrease of the TOC-normalized R1640/797 values as compared with the control (Fig. 2b). This decrease indicates a relative depletion of the hydrophilic component fraction in SOM, caused by compost addition. The latter suggests that the hydrophobic components are contributed by compost addition within the $0 { - } 3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ layer but hardly reach the deeper $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ layer.

Absorbancies at 2875 and $2 9 2 9 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ are characteristic of aliphatic CH groups and therefore may be considered as proxies for estimating the changes in the concentration of hydrophobic aliphatic moieties (Capriel et al., 1995; Ellerbrock and Gerke, 2004; Ellerbrock et al., 2005; Nadav et al., 2013; Voelkner et al., 2015). Aliphatic components, absorbing at $2 8 7 5 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ , showed in general a decrease of the R2875/797 values with soil depth (Fig. 2c). This decrease suggests that the soil content of these components also decreases with increasing soil depth. When normalizing R2875/797 by soil TOC content, no distinct tendencies across the soil profile were noted (Fig. 2d). Hence, the SOM content of the aliphatic groups absorbing at $2 8 7 5 ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ did not show a trend with increasing soil depth.

Another index which is proportional to the content of aliphatic CH groups in soil is given by R2929/797. It decreased with soil depth (Fig. 2e), thus indicating that the content of the relevant aliphatic groups in soil also decreased. This trend became more pronounced with the increase in compost application rate. This trend may have important agronomic implications with respect to the possibility that organic additives in organic farming may increase soil hydrophobicity (Berglund and Persson, 1996; Doerr et al., 2000; Voelkner et al., 2015).

# 3.2. Water-extractable organic matter

# 3.2.1. Dissolved organic C (DOC) concentrations

Compost addition increased DOC concentrations in WEOM above that of the control in the entire $0 { - } 6 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ soil profile (Fig. 3), with the commonly expected effect of increasing compost addition rate being most distinct in the top layer. As anticipated, the top layer was enriched by DOC (for all treatments), compared with the deeper layers; yet, no further significant decrease in DOC with depth below $0 { - } 5 \mathrm { c m }$ was observed. When considering all the available replications, poor correlations $( r < 0 . 7 )$ were found between DOC concentration and soil depth or the amount of compost added.

# 3.2.2. UV absorbance and SUVA

Regarding compost addition, data on absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. 4a) demonstrated generally a trend similar to that observed for DOC concentrations (Fig. 3), i.e., excluding the $3 0 { - } 6 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ samples, higher ${ \tt A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were found in WEOM from soil sample amended by compost, compared with the control samples. The higher $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values suggest elevated concentrations of aromatic DOM in WEOM, due to compost addition. However, the concentration of aromatic DOM decreased with soil depth. Interestingly, not only the $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values but also SUVA values increased in the soil samples from the $0 { - } 3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ soil layers following compost application (Fig. 4b) which indicates that the fraction of aromatic organic matter in the whole pool of DOM increased too. However, in the deepest soil layer studied $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 { \mathrm { c m } } ,$ , this effect was hardly noted.

# 3.2.3. Fluorescent components of WEOM

The EEM $^ +$ PARAFAC analysis of WEOM from the samples studied resulted in a 4 component model. The fluorescence contours of all the components determined are shown in Fig. 5. Table 2 lists the positions of excitation and emission maxima of all the components presented in Fig. 5. Spectral localization of similar fluorescent components may vary among studies, depending on the specific ecosystem examined, the different instrumentation used, data collection protocols and methodologies of data analysis (e.g., including PARAFAC chemometrics or not). In this work an approximate description of fluorescent components is provided in Table 2, based on the critical analysis by Ishii and Boyer (2012).

Components 1, 2 and 4 shown in Table 2 refer to different types of humic-like substances. Component 3 seems to demonstrate a more complexed nature. It reminds a humic-like component with someway shifted maximums as compared with a similar one (Ishii and Boyer, 2012) but it exhibited also an emission shoulder at $3 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ which suggests that there is a contribution of tryptophan-like fluorescence typically observed in the 320–360 nm range upon the excitation at 275– $2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ (Vivian and Callis, 2001; Baker and Inverarity, 2004; Hudson et al., 2007). However, within the current dataset, it was not possible to separate component 3 into the sub-components.

The distributions of the fluorescence intensities (in Raman units, R.U.) of the four components in the soil profile for the various amounts of compost are presented in Fig. 6. The fluorescence intensities of the components, which are proportional to their concentrations, generally increased in response to compost application and decreased with soil depth. A significant decline in concentrations took place mostly between the upper layer $( 0 - 5 \mathrm { c m } )$ and the deeper ones. The distribution patterns of WEOM fluorescent components (Fig. 6) resemble the distribution of DOC concentrations (Fig. 3) and $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values (Fig. 4). Therefore, it is of interest also to examine how the fractions of the fluorescent components in the whole DOM would be affected by soil depth and compost addition. For that, fluorescence of each component in a given WEOM sample was normalized by DOC concentration and plotted against soil depth (Fig. 7).

It can be seen from Fig. 7 that for the control samples, the DOC-normalized fluorescence intensities of the four components (that are proportional to their fractions in the DOM) hardly change with the soil depth. Regarding WEOM from the compost-amended soils, the regular decline in fluorescence intensities (\~concentrations) of components observed with the increase in soil depth in the upper two layers (0–5 and $5 { - } 1 5 \mathrm { c m }$ ; Fig. 6) disappeared following the normalization of the data by DOC (Fig. 7). The DOC-normalized fluorescence intensities of the components either increased or showed no significant change with depth in these soil layers. Adding compost may increase the fractions of fluorescent components in the WEOM from different depths (except the $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ layer), but no a distinct effect of a specific amendment rate could be noted.

It is worth to note that for all soil samples, strong $( r \ge 0 . 8 7 )$ and significant $( p < 1 0 ^ { - 7 } \cdotp$ ) correlations existed between the various WEOM characteristics (Table 3). Fluorescent components undergo excitation in the UV range (Table 2) and, therefore, represent part of the compounds absorbing at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ and thus contribute to $\mathtt { A b } s _ { 2 5 4 }$ . Both UV-absorbing and fluorescent compounds contribute to the whole DOC concentration. Thus, the strong correlations between DOC concentration, $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and the presence of fluorescence components are not surprising; in fact, they were often reported in the literature (Yacobi et al., 2003; Zhang et al., 2007; Wu et al., 2007; Baker, 2005; Hur and Cho, 2012; Borisover et al., 2011; Cohen et al., 2014). At the same time, in WEOM obtained from various irrigated soils, no strong correlations were observed between DOC and concentrations of fluorescent components or $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Borisover et al., 2012). Considering that the r values shown in Table 3 are high $( r \ge 0 . 8 7 )$ , one may suggest that fluorescent components represent a significant part of UV-absorbing compounds, and, together, they make up a large portion of the DOC in the WEOM samples studied in this work.

Typically, no strong relationships were found between SOM and WEOM characteristics, except significant correlations between R2929/ 797 and fluorescence intensities of the four components F1–F4 characterized by correlation coefficients of 0.74, 0.77, 0.74 and 0.72, respectively. These correlations indicate that an increase in soil content of aliphatic CH-containing organic compounds is coupled with an increase in the concentration of the fluorescent humic-like substances in WEOM. In addition, statistically significant positive correlations with r values of 0.71, 0.72 and 0.71 were found between the TN content of soil samples and ${ \tt A b s } _ { 2 5 4 } ,$ F1 and F4 in WEOM, respectively. Such relations reinforce the concept that WEOM characterization may be useful also for describing SOM components not extractable by water although the links between WEOM and SOM constituents are far from obvious.

# 4. Conclusions

Our study demonstrated that when examining the $0 { - } 6 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ soil depths, the effects of compost addition on SOM during the tested period were limited to the $0 { - } 3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ depth. Compost addition may enrich soil content by hydrophobic organic substances and water-extractable aromatic and, specifically, humic-like constituents. Although differences among the compost doses could not always be identified, application of compost made SOM depleted of hydrophilic groups containing various heteroatoms and enriched by hydrophobic aliphatic CH-rich components. However, with increasing soil depth, SOM became generally enriched by hydrophilic organic components. All these factors may lead to reduced top soil wettability, a potentially negative effect on downward movement of water and nutrients that has to be investigated in further studies. In the context of organic farming, the observed structural changes in the pool of SOM may affect the role of essential soil microbiota. This, in turn, may affect the release rate of nutrients through soil biological activity, which is the main route for supplying crop demands in organic farming.
