# Abstract

Dredging is frequently implemented for the reduction of internal nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) loadings and the control of eutrophication. Residuals during dredging activities and external pollution loadings after dredging both commonly contribute to influence the effectiveness of dredging and have been widely discussed. In the current study, the exchanges of $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ across the sediment-water interface (SWI) to these two factors were compared in a six-month field incubation experiment. The results showed that the continuous deposition of external suspended particulate matter (SPM) led ammonium nitrogen $\mathrm { ( N H _ { 4 } ^ { + } { - } N ) }$ and soluble reactive phosphorus (SRP） fluxes across the newly formed SWI to increase by factors of 4.16 and 12.71, respectively, while residual material caused the same fluxes to increase by factors of 2.06 and 5.06. Both the deposition of external SPM and the residual matter led to higher increase of the fluxes of $\mathrm { P }$ across the SWI than those of the fluxes of N across the SWI after dredging. The SPM easily adsorbed $\mathrm { P }$ in the water due to extensive adsorption of water soluble organic mater (consisting primarily of easily-decomposed humic-like substances), iron, and aluminum. However, the decomposition of organic matter in the SPM after the deposition on the dredged sediment accelerated the dissolution of redox-sensitive P and organic $\mathrm { P }$ across the SWI after dredging. Both the increase in the fluxes of N and P across the SWI would further increase the concentrations of N and P in the overlying water and thereby aggravate the eutrophication status in lakes. More frequent dredging operations might be necessary to reduce the fluxes of N and P from the sediment due to the continuous influence of the external SPM and the residual matter.

# 1. Introduction

Although the importance of internal loadings in lake restoration has been recognized since the 197Os (Wood and Gibson, 1973）or earlier, dificulties in reducing internal loadings still affect eutrophic lake restoration worldwide. A series of internal loading reduction techniques have been developed and applied in recent decades, including dredging (Francingues et al., 2008; Recknagel et al., 1995), capping (Huser et al., 2016; Yin et al., 2017b), hypolimnetic oxygenation (Gerling et al., 2014), and phytoremediation (Horppila and Nurminen, 2003). Dredging has been applied since the 1970s and has been implemented in many lakes around the world during recent decades (Murakami, 1984; Sondergaard et al., 2007). However, the results of dredging have been inconsistent in different areas. Successful reductions of the release of $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ from the sediment have been discovered in a number of lakes, such as Lake Trehorningen in Sweden (Ryding, 1982) and Lake Wuli in China (Liu et al., 2016b), while the long-term reductions were not satisfactory in many other lakes due to insufficient reduction of external pollution loadings (Sondergaard et al., 20o7) or the continuous release of contaminants caused by resuspended sediments and dredging residuals (Bridges et al., 2010). Both of these factors might cause a lake to relapse to its previous eutrophic status within years of dredging or less (Sondergaard et al., 2007).

Both resuspended matter and residuals are generated during dredging operations. The resuspended sedimentssubsequently redeposit on the newly formed sediment-water interface (SWI) and become residuals along with the disturbed surficial sediments during dredging operations (Bridges et al.， 2010). Residual sediments often range from $2 \%$ t0 $1 1 \%$ of the total dredged sediment or, more specifically, $5 \%$ to $9 \%$ of the contaminant mass removed by dredging (Palermo and Hays, 2014), depending on dredging operations, sediment properties, site conditions, etc. (Bridges et al., 2010). Considering that residuals consist mostly of surficial soft sediment, the concentrations of contaminants in residual sediments are generally similar to those in the un-dredged surficial sediment (Reible et al., 2O03)，which accelerated the release of N and Pacross the newly formed SWI after dredging. External pollution loading is another important factor influence the release of N and P across the newly formed SWI after dredging (Recknagel et al., 1995). Most of the N and P from external sources is concentrated in suspended particulate matter (SPM) and subsequently deposited on surficial sediments (Sutula et al., 2004; Suzumura et al., 2004). Previous research in Lake Kasumigaura (Murakami, 1984) showed that 38 Oo0 tons of particulate matter were deposited on the dredged sediment every year, of which $5 7 \%$ was imported from inflowing rivers and $43 \%$ consisted of algae debris. The deposition of external SPM has been shown to increase the diffusive fluxes of P across the SWI after the application of geo-engineering materials such as lanthanum-modified bentonite and calcium-rich atapulgite (Yin et al., 2018). of N and P fluxes from the sediment in lakes.   
Previous laboratory studies have revealed rapid increases in the diffsive fluxes of soluble reactive P (SRP) across the SWI and increases in total nitrogen (TN) and total phosphorus (TP) concentrations in the surficial sediment after dredging (Liu et al.,2017a; Liu et al.， 2016a). However, field environmental conditions and SPM deposition are difficult to simulate in the laboratory, and the detailed mechanisms of the increase in the fluxes of $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ from the dredged sediment remain unclear. In addition, the influence of residuals, which are also of great importance during actual dredging operations, was not considered in the aforementioned study. Therefore, field incubation experiments and observations were performed in a river mouth with high influxes of contaminated external SPM. This research aims to compare the influences of external SPM and residual matter on the increase of N and P fluxes across the SWI in frequently-dredged river mouth areas， and to further reveal the detailed mechanisms for the increase of N and P fluxes across the dredged SWI. The fluxes of N and P across the SWI would influence the concentrations of N and $\mathrm { P }$ in the water to a great extent, which thereby influence the eutrophication status of lakes. Therefor, information acquired in this study might not only be used to improve the suppression of N and P release from the sediment by dredging in similar areas, but also be helpful to solve the eutrophication problems in lakes.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Sediment core sampling

The study was performed in Lake Chaohu in Anhui Province, China. Lake Chaohu is a typical large and shallow hypereutrophic lake with high external and internal N and P loadings and a high sedimentation rate (Duan et al., 2O17; Liu et al., 2017b). For the past two decades, this lake has been listed as one of the top-priority lakes of concern in China, and is in urgent need of restoration. The northwestern bay is the most seriously polluted area and features extended-duration harmful algal blooms throughout the year (Duan et al., 2017). Therefore, the sediment cores used during the study were sampled from the most heavily polluted site in this area $( 3 1 ^ { \circ } 4 2 ^ { \prime }$ $4 . 7 6 ^ { \prime \prime }$ Nand $1 1 7 ^ { \circ } 2 1 ^ { \prime }$ $4 3 . 4 9 ^ { \prime \prime }$ E; Fig. S1 in the Supplementary Material) (Liu et al., 2015）and incubated at the mouth of the Nanfei River $( 3 1 ^ { \circ } 4 1 ^ { \prime } 5 . 2 2 " \mathrm { ~ N ~ }$ and 117°23'45.78" E; Fig. S1), which is believed to be one of the most heavily polluted rivers flowing into the lake (Zhang et al., 2015） and contributes about $50 \%$ of the external pollution loading in Lake Chaohu (Yin et al., 2017a). On June 2016, eighteen sediment cores were sampled using a gravity corer $\mathrm { 9 0 ~ m m }$ diameter $\times 5 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ length; Rigo Co.， Ltd.， Saitama, Japan). Simultaneously, $^ { 2 5 \mathrm { ~ L ~ } }$ of overlying water was collected for the subsequent core incubation. All sediment cores and water samples were then transported to the river mouth without disturbance and treated within $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$

# 2.2. Field incubation and experimental design

sediment cores were dredged by removing the upper $2 5 \mathrm { c m }$ of contaminated sediment. The dredged sediment columns were then separated from the un-dredged sediment columns after the removal of the upper $2 5 \mathrm { c m }$ of contaminated sediment. According to a previous study in the area (Liu et al., 2015), the release of ammonium nitrogen $\mathrm { ( N H _ { 4 } ^ { + } { - } N ) }$ and SRP was efectively suppressed at this dredging depth. Both the sediment in the tubes (50 cm long) with and without dredging were adjusted to about $2 0 \pm 2$ cm high. The un-dredged sediment columns were adjusted to about $2 0 \pm 2$ cm high by removing the sediment below the surficial 20 cm and then sealed with rubber stopper. The overlying water collected from the sampling site was then added carefully to the columns with siphons to avoid disturbing the sediment. The other six sediment cores were preserved in their original conditions to ensure that the SWI remained undisturbed. Six subsequent treatments were implemented in the field incubation: (1) UD,un-dredged sediment cores without SPM deposition; (2) $\mathrm { D + R }$ ， dredged sediment cores with residuals and without SPM deposition; (3) D, dredged sediment cores without SPM deposition; (4) UD $^ { + }$ SPM, un-dredged sediment cores with SPM deposition; (5) $\mathrm { D + R + S P M }$ , dredged sediment cores with residuals and with SPM deposition; and (6) $\mathrm { D + S P M }$ ， dredged sediment cores with SPM deposition. Three rplicates were performed for each treatment. The amount of residual material added (up to $1 1 \%$ of the total dredged sediment) was derived from the residual levels reported by Bridges et al. (2010). Surface soft sediment $( 2 \mathrm { c m } )$ was separated from the dredged sediment, mixed, and then added to the dredged sediment as the residual.

Sediment cores with treatments UD, $\mathrm { D + R }$ ,and $\mathrm { D }$ were capped with 800 mesh $1 8 \mu \mathrm { m }$ mesh size) polyamide screens to prevent SPM deposition on the sediment (Fig. S2). Although some SPM components were smaller than $1 8 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ (Fig. S3), bio-membranes rapidly generated on the screens prevented the deposition of fine SPM. All of the sediment cores were fixed in stainless steel frames (Fig. S2) and then incubated at the bottom of the water column in the river mouth for six months, from June 2016 to December 2016. The water depth was ${ \sim } 3 \textrm { m }$ at the incubation site. The pH and temperature of the water were in the range of 7.5-8.0 and 10-30 □ during the incubation period.   
The deposition of the SPM was quantified by sediment traps at the incubation site. The SPM deposited in the sediment columns were mostly natural deposition because all the columns were incubated in the lake. And the environmental conditions were just the same as in the field. In order to quantify the deposition and analyze the phisico-chemical characteristics of the SPM we used a six-channel sediment trap (90 $\mathrm { m m } \times 5 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ ) deployed at the field incubation site during the study period. The trap tubes were made of polymethyl methacrylate and were fixed in a steel frame. The trapped SPM (Fig. S3) was mixed, preserved at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and transferred to the laboratory within 4 h for analysis.

# 2.3. Pore water profile analysis and diffusive flux calculations

At the end of the field incubation, the pore water in the sediment cores was sampled using a series of mini-peepers (Ding et al., 2010). Each peeper contained 30 vertically disposed dialysis cells at a vertical resolution of $4 \mathrm { m m }$ . The capacity of each cell is about $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . All of the peepers were inserted into the sediment and allowed to stabilize for two days (Fig. S4), after which the peepers were pulled gently from the sediment and flushed with oxygen-free deionized water before analysis for $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N, SRP, and ferrous ion $( \mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + } )$ . The concentrations of $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N, SRP, and $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ were analyzed in a microtiter plate reader (Biotek Epoch, Winooski， VT, USA) using miniaturized photometric methods (Laskov et al.， 2007） modified with Nessler's reagent method (Chinese EPA, 2002), the molybdenum blue method (Murphy and Riley,1962), and the ferrozine method (Stookey, 1970), respectively. Based on the analyzed pore water profiles, the diffusive fluxes of $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } ^ { + } { - } N } .$ SRP,and $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ across the SWI were calculated using Fick's first law of diffusion (Ullman and Aller, 1982). Details concerning the diffusive flux calculation method are described in the Supplementary Material. D

# 2.4. Chemical analysis

After the field incubation,all sediment cores were transported to the laboratory and segmented at 2 cm intervals. All of the sediment and SPM samples were freeze-dried under vacuum conditions, ground, and sieved through a 1OO mesh (0.150 mm) screen before analysis. The SPM particle size was analyzed using fresh samples in a laser optical particle-size analyzer (Mastersizer-2Oo0, Malvern Instruments Ltd., Malvern, UK). The concentrations of TN and labile $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N in the sediment and SPM were determined through extraction in alkaline potassum persulfate (Chinese EPA,

2002） and potassium chloride (Shannon et al., 2011),respectively, and subsequent spectrometric analysis. The concentrations of TP, aluminum (Al), iron (Fe),and calcium (Ca) in the sediment and SPM were analyzed using inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometry (ICP-AES, Perkin-Elmer DV4300, USA) after digestion. About 0.1235-0.1259g dried sampleswasdigestedina polytetrafluoroethylene tank by $1 \ \mathrm { m L }$ hydrochloric acid, $2 \textrm { m L }$ nitric acid, 2 mL hydrofluoric acid, and $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ perchloric acid at the temperature of 150-220 □. The fractions of $\mathrm { P }$ in the sediment and SPM were sequentially extracted according to Rydin (2000), whose methods separate P into labile P (Ex-P), redox-sensitive P (Fe-P), aluminum-bound $\mathrm { P }$ (Al-P), organic P (Org-P), apatite and other inorganic P (Ca-P), and residual P (Res-P). The water content was analyzed by drying the sediment at $1 0 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to a constant weight, and the porosity was calculated subsequently (Cermelj et al., 1997). The water soluble organic carbon (WOC) was extracted in ultrapure water for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and then analyzed using a total organic carbon (TOC) analyzer (Torch, Teledyne Tekmar, Mason, OH, USA). The proportion of sediment/water in the extraction was 1/20 (m/v) during the experiment. The TOC in the sediment and SPM was analyzed via the potassium dichromate volumetric method (Chinese EPA, 2002). In response to the ready adsorption of $\mathrm { P }$ on SPM (Sutula et al., 2004), the P adsorption capacities of the SPM and sediment were analyzed before and after dredging. The maximum $\mathrm { P }$ adsorption capacity, native adsorbed $\mathrm { P }$ on the SPM and sediment, and zero equilibrium phosphorus concentration $\mathrm { ( E P C _ { 0 } ) }$ were calculated using fitted curves; details regarding the $\mathrm { P }$ adsorption capacity analytical method are

described in the Supplementary Material.

Previous work revealed that high concentrations of organic matter accumulated on surface sediment can lead to a depletion of oxygen across the SWI and thereby influence the exchange of N and P across the SWI (Liu et al., 2O17a; Liu et al., 2016a). Therefore, the water soluble organic matter (WOM) was analyzed in the SPM and surface sediment before and just after dredging. The excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectrum of the extracted WOM was analyzed using a Fluorolog?-3 spectrometer (HORIBA Instruments Incorporated， Edison， NJ， USA). PARAllel FACtor (PARAFAC) analysis was used to decompose the EEM spectra into their underlying chemical components (Murphy et al., 2013). Detailed WOM extraction and analysis methods are described in the Supplementary Material; details concerning the PARAFAC procedure have been described in previous studies (Ohno et al., 2007; Stedmon and Bro, 20o8). In addition, the oxygen penetration profiles across the SWI were analyzed at the middle and the end of the incubation using a microprofiling system (Unisense, Aarhus, Denmark) with a vertical resolution of $0 . 1 \mathrm { m m }$

# 2.5 Statistical analysis

Data plots were produced using Origin 9.0 software (OriginLab, Northampton, MA,USA). PARAFAC analysis and contour figure generation were performed in MATLAB R2012a (MathWorks， MA， USA). The diferences between various treatments in pore water characters and other characteristics of the sediment were evaluated using one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) tests. # 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Basic physicochemical properties of sediment and SPM

The concentrations of TN and TP in the surface sediment before dredging reached up to $2 4 2 2 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ k g ^ { - 1 } }$ and $1 1 1 4 . 9 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ k g ^ { - 1 } }$ , respectively, and decreased to $1 2 0 7 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ $\mathbf { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ and $2 9 0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { \ m g ~ k g ^ { - 1 } }$ ， respectively, just after dredging (Table S1). Therefore, excessive $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ accumulated in the sediment could theoretically be effectively removed by the simulated dredging operation. The dredged sediment became more compacted, with low water content $( 3 8 . 4 8 \% )$ and porosity $( 6 0 . 9 9 \% )$ ，which likely suppressed the release of N and P from the sediment across the newly formed SWI (Cermelj et al., 1997). Significant decreases in WOC and TOC were also observed after dredging. The Fe:P mass ratio increased from 31.26 to 101.38, which was more favorable for P adsorption onto the sediment (Jensen et al., 1992). The labile $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N concentration increased from $1 2 8 . 2 4 ~ \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ to $2 9 6 . 7 5 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ , which was consistent with previous studies (Liu et al., 2017a; Liu et al., 2015). The reductive environment in the deeper sediment before dredging stimulated the formation of labile $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N (Brandes and Devol, 1997). Therefore, after the removal of the upper sediment by dredging, the concentration of labile $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N in the just dredged sediment was higher than that in the un-dredged surficial sediment.

The concentration of TN in the SPM was similar to that in the surface sediment before dredging (Table S1),while the TP concentration in the SPM was obviously higher than that in the surficial sediment of the un-dredged column. The SPM collected during this study consisted predominantly of fine particles with diameters less than $6 3 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ (Fig. S5), which was favorable for the adsorption of metals and organic matter (Campana et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2014). High concentrations of Fe,Al, Ca, and organic matter were detected in the SPM (Table S1), which also facilitated the adsorption of P by the SPM (Huser et al., 2016; Jensen et al., 1992). Therefore, it is logical that the TP concentration was higher in the SPM than in the surface sediment before dredging. The high P concentration in the SPM arose primarily from Fe-P, Al-P, and Org-P.Fe-P is redox sensitive and can be released into the water under variations in redox conditions (Hupfer and Lewandowski, 2008; Rydin， 2000). Org-P can be used by microorganisms and hydrolyzed by alkaline phosphatase from the sediment (Zhou et al.， 2008),which both could increase the release of P from the sediment. The SPM deposition reached up to $7 ~ \mathrm { k g }$ dry weight $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 }$ month'1 during the field incubation period. The high deposition rate of the SPM and the high concentrations of organic matter, nitrogen, and phosphorus in the SPM might influence the exchanges of $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ across the newly formed SWI after dredging.

Due to the high water content of the recently deposited SPM, a loose sediment layer with high water content formed on the newly created SWI. Residual material and some SPM that had leaked into the cores during incubation led to high water content and porosity in the uppermost $2 \mathrm { \ c m }$ of sediment in $\mathrm { D + R }$ and D (Fig. S6). While in the deeper sediment, the water contents and porosities of treatments $\mathrm { D + R }$ and D were significantly lower than those of other treatments. The water contents $( p =$ 0.782，ANOVA test） and porosities $( p = 0 . 6 6 8$ ，ANOVA test) in UD, UD+SPM, $\mathrm { D + R + S P M }$ , and D+SPM were similar and elevated. The deposited SPM formed loose sediment layers on the newly created SWI that reached thicknesses of up $5 \mathrm { { c m } }$ by the end of the field incubation period (Fig. S7). The loose SPM layer at the SWI increased the porosity of the surficial sediment. According to Ullman and Aller (1982), the diffusive fluxes of N and $\mathrm { P }$ are closely related to the porosity of the surficial sediment (Eqs.(1) and (2) in the Supplementary Material). Therefore, increases in porosity resulting from deposited SPM likely stimulated the diffusion of $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ across the newly formed SWI after dredging.

# 3.4 Increases in N and P concentrations influenced by SPM and residual matter

The TN concentrations in $\mathrm { D + R }$ and D were similar (Fig. 2, $p = 0 . 7 3 1$ ， ANOVA test), while treatments UD, UD $^ +$ SPM, $\mathsf { D } { + } \mathsf { R } { + } \mathsf { S } \mathsf { P } \mathsf { M }$ ,and $\mathrm { D + S P M }$ exhibited higher TN concentrations in the upper $4 \ \mathrm { c m }$ of the sediment. The high TN concentrations in the SPM (Table S1) led to the increase in TN in the surficial sediment at about 2.5 times $\mathrm { ( D + S P M ) }$ ） compared to the just dredged sediment.However, the profiles of labile $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } ^ { + } { - } N }$ were similar in all of the sediment cores $( p = 0 . 1 4 0$ ， ANOVA test). The anaerobic environment in the deeper sediment likely increased the concentration of labile $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N (Brandes and Devol, 1997). Therefore, the concentration of labile $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } ^ { + } { - N } }$ in the newly-dredged sediment was higher than that before dredging. The concentration of labile $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N in the SPM was similar to that in the sediment just after dredging (Table S1), leading to similar concentrations of labile $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } ^ { + }  – N }$ in all of the treatments.

The P concentrations exhibited more obvious increases under the influence of the deposited SPM (Fig. 3). 3.6. Responses of $\cdot N H _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ -N and SRP fluxes across the newly formed SWI to the external SPM and residual matter after dredging

The diffusive fluxes of $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N and SRP were significantly reduced in $\mathrm { D + R }$ and D six months after dredging (Fig. 5); the fluxes were slightly higher in $\mathrm { D + R }$ than in D. However, the fluxes increased significantly in $\mathrm { D + R + S P M }$ and $\mathrm { D + S P M }$ under the continuous deposition of external SPM on the newly formed SWI and were similar to or higher than those in the un-dredged sediments (UD and UD $^ { + }$ SPM). The fluxes of $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N and SRP in D+SPM were 4.16 and 12.71 times those in D,respectively, while those in $\mathrm { D + R }$ were 2.06 and 5.06 times those in D. Therefore, it is predicted from the above results that the deposition of these external SPM would lead to significant increase in the $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ fluxes across the newly formed SWI to the pre-dredging levels in the long-term. As revealed by previous studies (Birdwell and Thibodeaux, 2007; Lohrer and Wetz, 2003), resuspended and residual sediment can thwart attempts to reduce $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ fluxes from the sediment by intercepting external polution loading; the field observations in this study support these findings. However, the long-term increases in diffusive fluxes of $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ across the newly formed SWI after dredging due to the continuous deposition of external SPM were much more significant than those resulting from residual matter. Previous studies (Sondergaard et al., 2007) have already revealed that, without reductions in external pollution loadings, dredging cannot reduce internal $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ loadings effectively in the long term. External SPM is a dominant source of external N and P pollution inputs to sediment in river mouth areas (Sutula et al., 2004; Suzumura et al.， 2004). The results herein support the assertion that deposited external SPM, rather than residual matter, is the primary external cause for increased long-term N and P fluxes from the sediment after dredging in similar river mouth areas. Therefore, additional measures such as the reduction of external pollution sources or the estuarine wetland should be implemented to reduce the input of contaminated SPM and to reduce the $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ fluxes from the sediment thereafter, which would further control the eutrophication status in lakes. # 4. Conclusions

The exchanges of N and P across the newly formed SWI after dredging driven by the deposition of external SPM and residual matter were studied in field incubation experiments. The results indicate that long-term increase rates in the fluxes of N and P from the dredged sediment driven by the continuous deposition of external SPM were higher than those resulting from residual matter. The extensive adsorption of WOM (consisting primarily of easily-decomposed humic-like substances), labile $\cdot \mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N, Fe-P, and Org-P on the SPM was responsible for the increase in the fluxes of N and P. In addition, the increase rate of P flux was observed to be higher than that of N flux both influenced by the external SPM and residual matter in the long-term. The increased fluxes of N and $\mathrm { P }$ from the sediment might increase the concentrations of N and P in the overlying water and thereby aggravate the eutrophication status in lakes. Due to the influence of the external SPM and the residual mater, more frequent dredging operations might be necessary to reduce the fluxes of $_ \mathrm { N }$ and $\mathrm { P }$ from the sediment.
