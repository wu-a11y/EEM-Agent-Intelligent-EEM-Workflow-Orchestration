# Goose and hare faeces as a source of nutrients and dissolved organic matter for bacterial communities in the newly formed proglacial lake Ventisquero Negro (Patagonia, Argentina)

Evelyn N. Vega $\textcircled { 1 0 }$ . Marcela Bastidas Navarro $\textcircled { 1 0 }$ . Beatriz Modenutti $\textcircled { \scriptsize { 1 0 } }$

Received: 29 November 2019 / Revised: 16 January 2020 / Accepted: 5 February 2020   
$©$ Springer Nature Switzerland AG 2020

Abstract Dissolved organic matter (DOM) input is a key factor for freshwater ecology, since it regulates many aspects of aquatic ecosystem metabolism. Aquatic and terrestrial animals that inhabit or frequent aquatic environments also influence the DOM inputs via their faeces, supplying nutrients such as carbon (C), nitrogen (N), and phosphorus (P). Here, we analyse the response of a bacterial community in the newly formed proglacial Lake Ventisquero Negro (Mount Tronador) to the addition of dissolved nutrients from faeces leachate of the native goose (Chloephaga poliocephala Sclater, 1857; locally called ‘‘cauque´n’’) and the European hare (Lepus europaeus Pallas, 1778). A laboratory incubation experiment was carried out with unenriched lake water and lake water enriched with leachate from hare or goose faeces. The results showed that faeces and leachates of geese were richer in nutrients than those of hares. Spectrofluorometric analysis of the DOM also showed differences between the two sources.

Nutrient enrichment positively affected bacterial respiration and short-term carbon consumption. Thus, the faeces of these two animals may play an important ecological role by supplying allochthonous DOM and nutrients to this new ecosystem.

# Introduction

In aquatic ecosystems, dissolved organic matter (DOM) plays an important role in the regulation of lake metabolism (Rose et al., 2015). DOM is a mixture of organic compounds that constitute the main source of carbon (C) and other important nutrients such as nitrogen $( \mathrm { N } )$ and phosphorous (P) (Fisher & Likens, 1973; Meyers & Lallier-Verge\`s, 1999). Inland waters receive allochthonous DOM and nutrient input from different sources, including precipitation, surrounding soil, runoff water, and groundwater intake (Wetzel, 2001). These allochthonous inputs provide nutrients for food webs and influence water transparency and even the thermal stratification of water bodies (Rose et al., 2015). Aquatic and terrestrial animals that frequent aquatic ecosystems also influence DOM input (Hahn et al., 2008). In particular, the faeces of aquatic birds using these aquatic ecosystems for food, rest, or breeding sites (Portnoy, 1990) can represent the main source of nutrient input (N and P) in these environments (Manny et al., 1994; Mindl et al., 2007; Hahn et al., 2008). The role of mammals as nutrient vectors for aquatic ecosystems has recently been recognized. For example, in shallow lakes in the African Savannah, hippopotamus faeces contribute substantially to DOM and nutrients (Subalusky et al., 2015), and in Lake Superior in the United States, moose contribute to nutrient recirculation in streams through the trampling of soils and sediments (Bump et al., 2017). The presence of mammals and birds in glacial environments has been reported to contribute to nutrient input (Mindl et al., 2007; Anesio et al., 2010; Bradley et al., 2014). In particular, algal blooms have been observed following melt and fertilization by penguin and other bird guano (Hodson et al., 2008).

Proglacial lakes are interesting models for studying the effects of allochthonous DOM inputs on the early stages of bacterial assemblage formation. These lakes are limited in their primary productivity due to the strong influence of glacial clays, which increase turbidity (Sommaruga, 2015). Despite the low temperatures and high turbidity, these environments harbour trophic webs dominated by a wide variety of psychrophilic microorganisms (Franzetti et al., 2013; Sommaruga, 2015), including bacteria, archaea, yeasts, filamentous fungi, algae, and some metazoans (Margesin & Miteva 2011; Comte et al., 2018; Modenutti et al., 2018). Melting ice is the main source of DOM and nutrients for proglacial lakes (Hood et al., 2009; Slemmons et al., 2013; Sommaruga, 2015; Modenutti et al., 2018). However, the periglacial environment (soils, tributaries, surrounding vegetation, etc.) (Mladenov et al., 2011; Sommaruga, 2001; Turpin-Jelfs et al., 2018; Wojcik et al., 2018) and the faeces of birds and small mammals could have an important contribution to allochthonous DOM and nutrients (Mindl et al., 2007; Anesio et al., 2010; Bradley et al., 2014).

Lake Ventisquero Negro is a newly formed proglacial lake located in Tronador Mountain $( 4 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { ~ S ~ }$ Patagonia, Argentina). The lake has high turbidity and is largely colonized by bacteria (Modenutti et al., 2018). During spring and summer, the lake is host to migratory native geese of the genus Chloephaga, mainly C. poliocephala Sclater, 1857, locally called ‘‘cauque´n’’. In addition, throughout the year, the lakeshore is frequently visited by the European hare Lepus europaeus Pallas, 1778. Faeces of both species have the potential to release nutrients (C, N, and P) that can be transported by runoff towards the water body. Therefore, the objective of this study was to test the effect of this potential nutrient release in a bacterial community of the newly formed glacial lake. Birds, unlike mammals, excrete faeces and urine together; thus, based on these excretion differences, we hypothesized that faeces leachates would have different C:N:P ratios, which in turn would have different affects on bacterial respiration. To test this hypothesis, we carried out experiments to determine the response of bacterial respiration from proglacial Lake Ventisquero Negro to enrichment with hare or goose faeces leachate.

# Materials and methods

Study site and field procedures

Mount Tronador ${ } ^ { \prime 4 1 ^ { \circ } 1 0 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { { S } } }$ , $7 1 ^ { \circ } 5 2 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { W }$ ; $3 4 8 0 \ \mathrm { m }$ a.s.l.) is the highest mountain in the North Patagonian Andes and is shared by Argentina and Chile. The upper part of the mountain is covered by 11 glaciers that occupy a total area of about $6 4 ~ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , with the largest glacier extending its arm to $9 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ a.s.l., well beneath the local tree line of $1 7 0 0 \ \mathrm { m }$ a.s.l. at this latitude (Villalba et al., 1997; Masiokas et al., 2008). Ventisquero Negro is a debris-covered glacier situated in the south-eastern hillside of Mount Tronador and is fed by snow, ice, and debris avalanche from the steep slopes above (Modenutti et al., 2018). The rapid recession of Ventisquero Negro (Masiokas et al., 2008) has resulted in the formation of a new proglacial lake (Lake Ventisquero Negro) between the moraine and the glacier front (Bastidas Navarro et al., 2018).

We performed enrichment experiments in austral early summer (December 2016) with water and bacteria samples from the proglacial lake Ventisquero Negro. Lake water was collected on the same day as the initial experimental time point (13th December of 2016), using sterile 2-L bottles at $1 { - } \mathrm { m }$ depth in a sampling point located $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ from the shore. Faeces samples of $L$ europaeus and $C .$ . poliocephala for enrichment experiments were collected in sterile hermetic bags the same day. Faeces were collected in five different sectors of $1 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ (randomly selected) of the eastern forefield. Water and faeces were immediately transported to the laboratory in darkness in thermally insulated containers.

Laboratory procedures

# Hare and goose faeces

Particulate nutrient content in the faeces of each species was determined on collected faeces dried at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \ \mathrm { h }$ to 7 days (to achieve a constant weight) and crushed with mortar and pestle. Particulate $\mathrm { \bf P }$ concentration was determined by placing a known biomass of faeces in aluminium capsules, followed by combustion for $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Rosemond et al., 1993). Samples were then incubated for $3 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ with $1 \mathrm { \Delta N }$ HCl, and the P concentration was determined using the ascorbic acid reduction method (APHA, 2005). Particulate C and $_ \mathrm { N }$ concentrations were determined in a known biomass of dry faeces $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \ \mathrm { h }$ ) and analysed using a Thermo Finnigan EA 1112 CN elemental analyser (Thermo Scientific).

In order to carry out the enrichment experiments, faeces leachates were prepared following the protocol described by Mindl et al. (2007). Briefly, $2 0 ~ \mathrm { g }$ of faeces from each species was placed separately in sterile Erlenmeyer flasks with $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of Milli-Q water. The flasks were then placed in an orbital shaker for $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at $1 8 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ . The obtained solutions were filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ cellulose acetate sterile filter (Sartorius) in order to eliminate particles and any eventual bacterial cells. Finally, these solutions were used for the enrichment experiments and to determine the concentrations of dissolved nutrients (C, P, and N).

# Experimental design

In the laboratory, Lake Ventisquero Negro water (with naturally occurring bacteria) was filtered through Nuclepore polycarbonate filters $2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size) to remove eukaryotic cells. We ensured that all eukaryotic cells were eliminated by examination under epifluorescence microscopy, and we found no eukaryotic cells in the $2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filtrate. The experiment consisted in the incubation of $2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ -filtered lake water in flasks for 14 days at $2 0 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ under dark conditions, in three treatments: (1) LAKE (unenriched) $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of $2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ -filtered lake water; (2) GOOSE $\mathrm { 5 7 0 ~ m l }$ of $2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ -filtered lake water enriched with $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of goose faeces leachate, $5 \%$ total volume), and (3) HARE $( 5 7 0 ~ \mathrm { m l } ~ $ of $2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ -filtered lake water enriched with $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of hare faeces leachate, $5 \%$ total volume). Treatments were run in three replicates each. At the beginning and on days 2 and 14 of the experiment, the nutrient concentrations (DOC, TDN, and TDP) were determined in each replicate for each treatment, and the DOM was characterized by spectrofluorometry.

Bacterial respiration was analysed in the short term $( 4 8 \ \mathrm { h } )$ and in the long term (on days 2 and 14 of the experiment) (Guillemette & del Giorgio, 2011; Lapierre & del Giorgio, 2014). The concentration of dissolved oxygen was measured every $^ { 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ during the first 2 days with an optical oximeter (ProODO), which allowed for the measurement of dissolved oxygen from outside the flasks with a non-invasive oxygen fluorescent sensor (PreSens) located inside the flasks. DOC concentrations were then measured in $4 0 { \cdot } \mathrm { m l }$ samples every 4 days for the next 12 days in order to calculate DOC decay.

# Laboratory determinations

Total dissolved phosphorus (TDP), total dissolved nitrogen (TDN), and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations were determined on GF/F (glass fibre filter, Whatman)-filtered water samples (leachates and treatments). Samples for TDP determination were digested with potassium persulfate at $1 2 5 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ at 1.5 atm for $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , and the $\mathrm { \bf P }$ concentration was determined according to the ascorbate-reduced molybdenum method (APHA, 2005). DOC was measured in $4 0 { \cdot } \mathrm { m l }$ samples with a high-temperature combustion analyser (Shimadzu TOC-VCSH), and the TDN was determined using the TNM-1 unit on the Shimadzu TOCVCSH.

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) was characterized by spectrofluorometric analysis in faeces leachate at the beginning and on the 2nd and 14th days of the experiment. Measurements at specific excitation $( 2 4 0 - 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , in increments of $2 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ) and emission wavelengths $3 0 0 { - } 5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , in increments of $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ) were performed with a PerkinElmer LS-45 fluorescence spectrophotometer, equipped with a xenon discharge lamp. Measurements were made at constant ambient temperature of $2 0 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ in a 1-cm quartz fluorescence cell. Raman scattering was corrected by subtracting the pure water (Milli-Q) obtained on the same day. Excitation–emission matrices (EEMs) were constructed with these data.

A volume of $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of water from each replicate for each treatment at the beginning and on days 2 and 14 of the experiment was analysed for bacterial abundance and biomass determination. Samples were fixed with formaldehyde at a final concentration of $2 \%$ v/v, and bacterial cells were stained with fluorochrome $4 ^ { \prime } { , } 6 ^ { \prime }$ -diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) at a final concentration of $0 . 2 \%$ w/v (Porter & Feig, 1980). The bacterial abundance was measured on $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ black polycarbonate filters (Poretics) under an epifluorescence microscope (Olympus BX50) at $\times 1 2 5 0$ magnification using a UV light (U-MWU filter). The bacterial biovolume was estimated based on photographs obtained with Image-Pro software.

Data analysis

Differences in nutrient concentrations and their atomic elemental ratios (C:N and C:P) between particulate faeces (hare and goose) and leachates were analysed using the Student $t$ test. To analyse differences in nutrient concentration in the three experimental treatments at the initial time point, a one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was applied with a post hoc Fisher least significance difference (LSD) test. Differences in nutrient ratios (C:N and C:P) between the treatments during the experiment were analysed by repeatedmeasures two-way analysis of variance (RMANOVA) and subsequently with a post hoc Fisher LSD test. Normality and homoscedasticity were checked previously (Shapiro–Wilk test, $F$ test for equal variance).

Excitation–emission matrices (EEMs) were analysed with PARAFAC (parallel factor) analysis using the MATLAB DOMFluor toolbox (Stedmon & Bro, 2008; Murphy et al., 2013). The $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ consumption rates were estimated as the slope of the $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ concentrationversus-time (days) curve fitted to a least-squares regression. The oxygen consumption rates were converted to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ production using a respiratory quotient of 1 (McCallister & Del Giorgio, 2008), to provide short-term estimates of organic carbon consumption (STCC). The long-term carbon consumption rate (LTCC) was estimated as the slope of the DOCversus-time (days) curve fitted to a least-squares regression. To analyse differences in respiration rates between treatments, a one-way ANOVA was applied with a post hoc Bonferroni test. The same analysis was applied for differences between treatments in STCC and LTCC. The effects of C:P and C:N on bacterial respiration and carbon consumption were assessed by correlation analysis. Because the data were not normally distributed, a Spearman correlation was applied. A level of significance of $P = 0 . 0 5$ was used in all statistical analyses. All statistical analyses were performed using SigmaPlot 12.5 software.

# Results

Hare and goose faeces

Nutrient concentrations differed between hare and goose faeces. Concentrations of particulate phosphorus and nitrogen were significantly higher in goose than in hare faeces, by 1.7 and 1.6 times, respectively $( t$ test, $P = 0 . 0 1 9$ and $P < 0 . 0 0 1$ , for $\mathbf { N }$ and P, respectively). However, no significant differences in particulate carbon concentration were observed $\mathit { \Pi } _ { t }$ test, $P = 0 . 1 2 6$ ) (Fig. 1a). Thus, elemental ratios (particulate C:N and C:P) were significantly higher in the hare faeces than in the goose faeces $t$ test $P < 0 . 0 0 1$ and $P = 0 . 0 0 1 4 0$ , respectively) (Fig. 1b).

Differences in dissolved nutrient concentrations were also observed between goose and hare faeces leachate. Goose faeces leached 1.5 more C as DOC than did hare faeces (Fig. 2a), and the concentration of N as TDN and $\mathrm { \bf P }$ as TDP was significantly higher, 2.5 times on average, in the goose leachate ( $\mathit { \Pi } _ { t }$ test, $P < 0 . 0 0 1$ , for all nutrients). Elemental ratios of dissolved nutrients also showed significant differences for C:P $t$ test, $P = 0 . 0 0 6$ but not for C:N $\mathit { \Pi } _ { t }$ test, $\begin{array} { r } { P = 0 . 1 2 8 } \end{array}$ ) (Fig. 2b).

# Discussion

Our results showed that goose and hare faeces could be important sources of nutrients (carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus) in a proglacial lake. However, goose faeces were found to be richer in nutrients, with N and P concentrations on average 1.7 times as high in goose as in hare faeces. These differences can be explained by physiological differences, since birds release their products of renal excretion and faeces together (Skadhauge, 2012). In particular, geese of the Chloephaga spp. produce faeces that are very loose, in which the vegetation included in their diet can be easily recognized (Domı´nguez, 2018). On the contrary, lagomorphs produce two types of faeces: one soft, which is excreted first and still retains nutrients and minerals, and a second that is hard, composed mainly of fibre (Kuijper et al., 2004). Soft faeces are ingested again after egestion (caecotrophy) and undergo a bacterial fermentation process that enables optimal nutrient consumption. Laboratory experiments showed that caecotrophy enables the recycling of $5 0 \mathrm { - } 6 0 \%$ of $\mathrm { \bf P }$ and $3 5 \mathrm { - } 4 0 \%$ of N (Pehrson, 1983). This process would explain the lower nutrient content in hare compared with geese faeces. These physiological differences likely also affected the leaching process of dissolved nutrients. The dissolved $_ \mathrm { N }$ and P content in goose faeces leachate was higher than that in hare leachate. Although the particulate C content in faeces was similar between the species, the DOC resulting from the leaching process was different. During bacterial fermentation of the hare soft faeces, short carbon chains are used as an energy source, and the solubility of organic compounds decreases with increasing chain length and complexity of the molecule (Brown et al., 1988). Thus, the hard faeces could have a higher proportion of long-chain carbon compounds that are less soluble, and consequently the capacity for leaching of dissolved C is low. This hypothesis is also supported by the spectrofluorometric characterization of DOM: the content of humic-like compounds was higher in the HARE treatment. Humic acids are formed by the union of small associated molecules in a complex supramolecular structure whose solubility is low (Chiou et al., 1987; Piccolo & Conte, 2000). Thus, hare faeces were characterized by low leaching of DOM with high humic material content.

Mammal contribution to nutrient cycling in aquatic environments is generally overlooked. However, nutrient inputs from marine mammals via faeces contributes substantially to the primary productivity in surface waters (Roman & McCarthy, 2010), and the faeces of large terrestrial mammals, such as the hippopotamus, are an important source of nutrients in shallow lakes (Subalusky et al., 2015). Nevertheless, studies on the role of small mammals in nutrient cycling in freshwater ecosystems are scarce. In this sense, here we showed that nutrient contribution via hare faeces can positively affect bacterial respiration.

Bird faeces have been recognized as an important source of nutrients (Dessborn et al., 2016; Jensen et al., 2019), and thus they can potentially impact aquatic microbial ecology. Studies have shown that enrichment with nutrients from arctic goose faeces promoted bacterial production and abundance (Mindl et al., 2007), and that flamingo populations contributed to the formation of bacterial communities through the effects of guano input and bioturbation of sediments (Batanero et al., 2017). Our experiments showed that bacterial respiration and C consumption responded positively to faeces nutrient enrichment, in particular in the goose treatment with higher concentrations of dissolved nutrients. The C:N and C:P elemental ratios demonstrated a significant decrease between the initial and final experimental time points. In addition, we found a negative correlation between dissolved C:P ratios and respiration rate and C consumption (STCC and LTCC), suggesting the importance of $\mathrm { \bf P }$ concentration for bacterial metabolic variables. These results agree with those of other studies in Andean lakes. Bertoni et al. (2008) performed enrichment experiments with C and $\mathrm { \bf P }$ in an Andean glacial lake, observing an increase in bacterial production after P addition. A similar pattern was obtained in enrichment experiments with senescent leaf leachates and algal exudates (Bastidas Navarro et al., 2019) and with clean and debris-rich glacial ice (Modenutti et al., 2018). These studies and our data thus suggest that phosphorus can be an important factor controlling the carbon dynamics in glacial environments.

The composition of allochthonous DOM and its consumption and production dynamics are extremely important for the functioning of aquatic environments in the context of the current climate change scenario. Global warming and the associated changes in temperature and in rainfall and snow regimes (IPCC, 2014) will have significant consequences for aquatic ecosystems, particularly in DOM input (Adrian et al., 2009; Van de Waal et al., 2010). These changes can increase runoff (Milly et al., 2005), favouring the vegetation and soil leaching process, and consequently the contribution of nutrients from allochthonous DOM (Kominoski & Rosemond, 2011). Moreover, climate change is affecting the world’s glaciers, causing glacial recession and the formation of new environments, including lakes, rivers, and wetlands. The colonization of these new environments gives rise to both new aquatic communities and suitable habitat for birds and mammals. The present study shows that nutrient contribution from both goose and hare species promoted an increase in microbial respiration in the early stages of lake formation. Therefore, these new links between the freshwater and terrestrial environment during glacial recession and new lake formation should be further considered and analysed from a more global ecosystem perspective.
