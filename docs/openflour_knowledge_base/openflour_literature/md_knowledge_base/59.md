# Variations in high-latitude riverine fluorescent dissolved organic matter: A comparison of large Arctic rivers

Sally A. Walker,1 Rainer M. W. Amon,1 and Colin A. Stedmon 2 Received 22 February 2013; revised 1 November 2013; accepted 7 November 2013; published 11 December 2013.

[1] Given the pace of climate change, it is important to better understand dissolved organic matter (DOM) storage and cycling in high-latitude rivers and its subsequent export to the Arctic Ocean. To address this concern, excitation/emission matrix spectroscopy and parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) coupled with optical indices were used to characterize the optical properties of fluorescent DOM (FDOM) and colored DOM (CDOM) in five large Arctic rivers over two seasonal cycles. Five PARAFAC components were identified and proved useful indicators for the quantitative and qualitative descriptions of DOM sources and modification processes within Arctic watersheds. In comparing Pan-Arctic relationships between the optical properties and chemical properties of DOM, three components traced terrigenous biomarkers and two components are introduced as potential indicators for microbial processing. Conversely, no individual PARAFAC component could be directly linked to a specific plant source or river and simpler absorbance indices (i.e., $\mathbf { a } _ { 3 5 0 _ { , } }$ ) proved to be better suited to quantify dissolved organic carbon and lignin phenol concentrations. Differences in FDOM character between the rivers could be explained by general watershed characteristics, including vegetation, topography, and hydrology. Based on these differences, the influence of coniferous vegetation on DOM character is most apparent in the Lena and Yenisei Rivers and bog-derived FDOM sources are more important in the Ob River. This study illustrates how increased hydrological connectivity in the Ob catchment and the abundance of lakes within the Mackenzie watershed may influence FDOM concentrations and the microbial processing of DOM within these watersheds.

# 1. Introduction

[2] The Arctic region is currently warming at a rate twice as fast as the rest of the globe, where models project a mean annual high-latitude temperature increase of $3 . 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ by 2090 [Kattsov et al., 2005]. This accelerated warming has the potential to cause rapid changes in carbon storage and cycling in high latitudes and therefore the global carbon cycle [McGuire et al., 2009]. This is because large watersheds surrounding the Arctic basin contain roughly $50 \%$ of the global soil organic carbon (OC), where $\sim 5 0 \%$ of the total OC $( 1 4 0 0 { - } 1 8 5 0 ~ \mathrm { P g } ~ \mathrm { C } )$ is stored in vast permafrost regions, particularly in Siberia [Tarnocai et al., 2009]. Under a warming climate, substantial permafrost disintegration is projected by the year 2100, with a loss of $6 { - } 1 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ This represents an area containing $2 5 \%$ of the total carbon currently stored in Arctic soils [Euskirchen et al., 2009; Lawrence and Slater, 2008; Gruber et al., 2004]. At present, large Arctic watersheds are considered a net sink for $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ $( 2 0 0 { - } 4 0 0 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 } )$ [McGuire et al., 2009], but how carbon released from permafrost erosion will impact the biogeochemical cycling of organic carbon within large Arctic rivers and shelf seas and potentially influence atmospheric $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentrations [Bates et al., 2010; Fabry et al., 2009] and the amount of terrestrial carbon delivered to the Arctic Ocean and/or the North Atlantic [Amon et al., 2003, Hansell et al., 2004; Benner et al., 2005; Granskog et al., 2012] remains unknown. [3] Extreme seasonal changes in snow cover and air temperatures result in a large variability in Arctic river runoff during the year. During cold winter months, when precipitation rates are low, runoff to the Arctic is at the minimum. In contrast, warming temperatures, increasing precipitation, and extensive snowmelt during spring months create a pronounced peak in discharge, referred to as the spring freshet. Associated with these fluctuations are large changes in the characteristics of the terrestrial DOM (tDOM). During freshet, surface runoff is the predominant path for watershed drainage and mainly affects vegetation cover, surface litter, and top soils. Based on previous investigations using lignin monomer and $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -dissolved organic carbon (DOC) signatures, ${ \sim } 7 5 \%$ of lignin discharged to the Arctic occurs during spring freshet and is predominantly derived from an abundance of young, boreal-forest vegetation-derived leachates [Amon et al., 2012; Neff et al., 2006; Spencer et al., 2009; Raymond et al., 2007]. As a result, Arctic peak flow DOM is considered to be more bioavailable relative to base flow DOM [Hansell et al., 2004; Holmes et al., 2008; Manizza et al., 2009; Mann et al., 2012]. During midflow conditions, water percolates through deeper soil horizons, which introduces DOM that is older, more humified, with less aromatic and lignin phenol components [Neff et al., 2006, Amon et al., 2012; Raymond et al., 2007]. As a result, during lower flow regimes, soil- and peatderived DOC transported by ground water becomes more prominent, and lower DOC and lignin phenol concentrations are observed [Amon et al., 2012; Neff et al., 2006; Spencer et al., 2009].

[4] In an earlier study, we have shown how measurements of the light absorbing properties of colored DOM (CDOM) in six major Arctic rivers can be used to infer annual river loads of DOC and how changes in the absorption characteristics reflect the seasonal change in DOM composition [Stedmon et al., 2011]. More recently, lignin phenol analysis was used to describe the sources of tDOM in large Arctic rivers and is described in detail by Amon et al. [2012]. In the present study, we characterize fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM), using excitation/emission matrix (EEM) spectroscopy coupled with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC), and compare these results to established optical properties and previously reported lignin biomarker composition. We first explore the ability of PARAFAC components to (1) discriminate between various terrestrial FDOM sources, (2)

quantify tDOM (DOC and lignin phenol concentration) export to the Arctic Ocean, and (3) identify tDOM from individual rivers as tracers during transport and mixing within the Arctic Ocean. We then explore the ability of optical parameters to link certain watershed characteristics, including topography, hydrologic connectivity, and water residence time, to FDOM composition, FDOM sources (vegetation), and to the degree of microbial degradation of tDOM.

# 2. Methods

# 2.1. Sample Collection

[5] River data presented here were collected as part of the Pan-Arctic River Transport of Nutrients, Organic Matter, and Suspended Sediments (PARTNERS) project and include the Mackenzie, Lena, Kolyma, Ob, and Yenisei Rivers. Samples were collected from 2004 to 2005 near the river mouth at the northernmost discharge gauging stations as shown in Figure 1. To represent surface to bottom and cross-channel chemistry, samples were collected at five different locations along a cross-channel transect using a torpedo-shaped, Teflon-coated, $6 0 \mathrm { k g }$ , depth-integrated sampler (US D-96) and were homogenized using a Teflon churn. During winter months, water samples were collected through a hole drilled into the ice. Water samples were filtered $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ Pall Aquaprep 600 capsule filters) into acid-washed 1 L polycarbonate bottles and kept frozen until 2009 when analyzed in the laboratory. Optical samples presented for this study were collected at various times of the year, reflecting the changing seasonal hydrograph, and are presented as averages from three flow regimes (peak flow, mid-flow, and base flow conditions) within each river.

# 2.2. Optical Measurements

[6] Optical samples were warmed to room temperature and absorbance measurements were recorded on a Shimadzu UV2401PC/2501PC using a $5 \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cuvette. Absorbance spectra were measured from 200 to $8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $0 . 5 \mathrm { n m }$ increments and were blank corrected using Milli-Q water as a reference. Fluorescence measurements were made on a Photon Technologies International Fluorometer (Quanta Master-4 SE) using a 1 cm quartz cuvette with excitation and emission slit widths set to $5 \mathrm { n m }$ . Excitation emission matrix (EEM) scans for each sample were obtained by collecting a series of emission spectra ranging from 230 to $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ $2 \mathrm { n m }$ increments) at excitation wavelengths ranging from 220 to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (5 nm increments). To account for fluctuations in light source characteristics, the fluorescence signal was normalized to that from a reference detector during measurement;

emission and excitation correction files generated by the manufacturer were applied to each sample EEM to correct for instrument specific biases. Postacquisition, raw EEM’s were exported to MATLAB, Raman calibrated, corrected for Rayleigh and Raman scatter, and organized for the modeling process according to Walker et al. [2009].

[7] Samples presented in this study were analyzed twice, once in 2006 after samples were received and once in 2009. The initial analysis in 2006 was problematic due to high FDOM concentrations, which resulted in large inner filter effects compromising the fluorescence measurements. To avoid such interferences, the undiluted samples were reanalyzed in 2009 and then diluted with Milli-Q water to an absorbance value of 0.10 at $2 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and reprocessed. Dilution to this level has been shown to avoid inner filter effects and to ensure that absorption coefficients were low enough not to influence the excitation and emission lights in the cuvette [Ohno and Bro, 2006; Stedmon and $B r o$ 2008]. Conversely, freezing optical samples, particularly for longer storage times, has been shown to result in the fading of fluorescence and could create analytical artifacts [Spencer et al., 2007; Holmes et al., 2008]. This is because some of the more terrestrial humic-like components may not redissolve, particularly when DOC concentrations are high and mainly derived from soil and higher plants. As most of the samples were collected from extremely isolated locations, freezing of samples during transport was unavoidable and any alternative storage method would have resulted in larger uncertainties. A comparison of the absorbance at $2 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for undiluted samples measured in 2009 and in 2006 showed that the data varied from $- 1 0 \%$ to $+ 1 0 \%$ with an average decrease of $3 \%$ over the 3 year period. Further, as samples were warmed to room temperature, no residual particles were observed and based on sample comparisons and carbon mass balances during ultrafiltration, the loss of Arctic River DOC during long-term storage at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ does not seem to be problematic (R. M. W. Amon, unpublished data, 2004). We further suggest that even if freezing was a problem for this sample set, it might be difficult to determine because in situ freezing of river water is common in the rivers (particularly between October and April). We therefore believe that the effects of freezing should be at least consistent for the different rivers because waters sampled across the seasons have a different freezing history and because sampling protocol and handling were the same for all samples collected.

# 2.3. Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC) Modeling

[8] This study used the “DOMFluor Toolbox” in MATLAB according to Stedmon and Bro [2008] and outlier identification and model validation were performed according to Walker et al. [2009]. To ensure an even weighting between samples, the model was carried out on the diluted samples and dilution factors were then reapplied to component loadings post modeling. Initially, individual rivers were modeled independently, but there were too few samples for successful river specific models. The final PARAFAC analysis was conducted on 74 samples (including all rivers) and no outliers were identified using the leverage plots. A five-component model (Figure 2) was derived and validated using split-half validation and the model fit was assessed by residual analysis.

# 2.4. Calculations: Optical Indices

[9] Several optical indices were calculated to investigate CDOM/FDOM characteristics including absorption coefficients ranging from 200 to $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , spectral slope $\mathrm { ( S _ { ( 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 ) } ) }$ specific UV absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ (SUVA), and the biological/autochthonous index (BIX). The absorption coefficient at $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m } \left( \mathrm { a } _ { 3 5 0 } \right)$ is a quantitative measure for CDOM concentrations and was chosen based on its ability to estimate DOC and lignin concentrations and inputs of tDOM to the Arctic Ocean [Spencer et al., 2009; Stedmon et al., 2011]. Absorption coefficients were calculated using the equation $\mathsf { a } _ { \lambda } ( \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } ) { = } ( 2 . 3 0 3 \times \mathrm { A } _ { \lambda } ) / \mathrm { L }$ , where $\mathbf { A } _ { \lambda }$ is absorbance at a specific excitation wavelength and $\mathrm { L }$ is the cuvette path length in meters.

[10] $\mathrm { S } _ { ( 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 ) }$ differs between CDOM sources [Stedmon et al., 2011; Fichot et al., 2013] and has been shown to be inversely correlated to the average molecular weight of DOM [Helms et al., 2008]. In general, $\mathrm { S } _ { ( 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 ) }$ values of the high molecular weight fraction of the CDOM pool are lower and indicative of a tDOM sources, whereas S(275–295)

values of the low molecular weight fractions are generally higher and indicative of a more degraded and/or autochthonous source [Helms et al., 2008; Stedmon et al., 2011; Fichot et al., 2013]. Instead of the more commonly used broader wavelength range $( 3 0 0 - 7 0 0 \mathrm { n m } )$ , the narrow range of wavelengths between 275 and $2 9 5 \mathrm { n m }$ is used in this study because it can be measured with high precision and appears to be particularly sensitive to shifts in molecular weight and/or DOM sources [Fichot et al., 2013; Helms et al., 2008]. $\mathrm { S } _ { ( 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 ) }$ values were determined according to the approach in Helms et al. [2008].

[11] The specific UV absorbance (SUVA) at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ is used to estimate the degree of aromaticity in bulk CDOM [Weishaar et al., 2003; Mladenov et al., 2007]. Terrestrially derived DOM has a higher aromatic carbon content than autochthonous or microbially altered DOM, reflecting the presence of tannin-like and humic-like substances that originate from plant and soil organic matter [Battin, 1998]. Because aromatic compounds absorb more light in the UV-visible region of the spectrum, higher SUVA values indicate increased aromaticity from allochthonous inputs and lower SUVA values indicate a decrease in aromaticity and a more autochthonous or modified terrestrial CDOM source (i.e., microbial/autochthonous and soil-derived DOM). SUVA values were calculated using the equation $\mathrm { S U V A } { = } ( \mathrm { A } _ { 2 5 4 } / \mathrm { L } ) / [ \mathrm { D O C } ] ,$ , where $\mathbf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ is the absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ measured in the $5 \mathrm { c m }$ cuvette, L is the cuvette path length in m $( 0 . 0 5 \mathrm { m } )$ , and the concentration of DOC is measured in milligrams of carbon per liter $( \mathrm { m g \ C \ L ^ { - 1 } } )$ [Weishaar et al., 2003].

[12] The biological/autochthonous index (BIX) is used to assess the relative contribution of microbially altered DOM. In general, BIX is influenced by the presence of two commonly identified terrestrial (peak C) and microbial-like (peak M or a more diagenetically altered form of the peak M fluorophores) peaks within the fluorescence spectra [Burdige et al., 2004; Coble, 1996; McKnight et al., 2001]. BIX is calculated by taking the ratio of emission wavelengths at $3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that at $4 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , at a fixed excitation wavelength at $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { ( B I X = } \mathrm { [ ( e m } 3 8 0$ at ex310)/ $( \mathrm { e m } 4 3 0$ at ex310)]) [Huguet et al., 2009]. BIX values between 0.8 and 1.0 correspond to DOM of a microbial origin, whereas values $< 0 . 6$ are considered to be derived from allochthonous DOM sources [Birdwell and Engel, 2010].

# 2.5. CDOM Relationships to Dissolved Organic Carbon (DOC) and Lignin Biomarkers

[13] To interpret the optical properties determined in this study, we relate them to previously determined DOC concentrations, biomarker signatures of lignin phenols and p-hydroxybenzenes, and to the $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC age. These parameters were determined on the same sample set, where DOC, lignin phenol, and p-hydroxybenzene concentrations are reported by Amon et al. [2012] and $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC values are from Raymond et al. [2007].

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Fluorescence Characterization by PARAFAC

[14] Five unique fluorescent components were identified by the PARAFAC model, each with broad emission spectra and several excitation maxima (Figure 2). Component 1 has an excitation maximum below $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and at $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and a broad emission peak from 350 to $5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (emission (Em.) max at $4 3 2 \mathrm { n m }$ ). Component 2 has a primary excitation peak at $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and secondary peak at $2 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with a broad emission peak from 386 to $> 5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Em. max at $4 6 2 \mathrm { n m }$ ). Component 3 has an excitation peak at $< 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and at $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ with a broad emission peak from 416 to $> 5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Em. max at $5 4 6 \mathrm { n m }$ ). Component 4 has a primary excitation maximum at $2 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with a secondary peak at $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and a broad emission peak from 350 to $> 5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Em. max at $4 5 2 \mathrm { n m }$ ). The final component, component 5, has an excitation maximum below $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and at $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ with an emission peak at $3 4 4 \mathrm { n m }$ .

In comparing the rivers, the fluorescence for all components was highest in the Lena River during peak discharge months, whereas the highest fluorescence intensities for all components were observed in the Ob River during mid-flow and base flow conditions (Figures 3a–3c). Overall, the fluorescence of FDOM in the Mackenzie River across all flow regimes was low relative to the other rivers.

Among the remaining rivers, the fluorescence characteristics (distribution of components) of the organic matter were remarkably constant (Figures 3d–3f).

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. PARAFAC Component Source Identification

[21] The five fluorescent PARAFAC components identified in this study (Figure 2) are similar to those previously identified within surface waters of the Canadian Archipelago and Mackenzie River plume [Walker et al., 2009] and within the Kolyma River [Mann et al., 2012] as well as within other aquatic environments [Coble, 1996; Cory and McKnight, 2005; Mueller et al., 2012; Ohno and Bro, 2006; Osburn and Stedmon, 2011; Yao et al., 2011]. Based on previous investigations, PARAFAC components can be used to discriminate between fresh terrestrial FDOM sources (e.g., vascular plant sources), the addition of aquatic-derived FDOM (i.e., carbon fixed by aquatic plants and algal derived), and FDOM that has been recently altered by microbes (i.e., microbial degradation). The fluorescence character of the five components derived here can be used to illustrate such differences. The two broad humic-like peaks within the fluorescence landscape of components 1, 2, and 3 are in the traditional terrestrial-like peak A and C regions of the EEM landscape defined by Coble [1996]. Further, similar components have shown strong correlations to lignin phenol concentrations [Walker et al., 2009; Osburn and Stedmon, 2011] and are considered ubiquitous in aquatic environments. A component with similar spectral characteristics to component 4 has been identified as being introduced via microbial processing of algae-derived DOM [Ohno and Bro, 2006; Yao et al., 2011], but has also shown a positive correlation to lignin phenol concentrations [Walker et al., 2009]. Component 5 exhibits an emission peak in the UVA region, which overlaps that of tryptophan (peak T) [Coble, 1996].

[22] Despite the similarity in the spectra of the fluorescent components identified between studies, at present, little is actually known regarding their specific sources [Ishii and Boyer, 2012]. To date, the fluorescence components have mainly been associated with generic classes of molecules (i.e., humic and fulvic acids and DOC concentrations) [Cory and McKnight, 2005] and are only recently getting linked with more specific biomarkers present in environmental matrices (i.e., lignin phenol concentrations) [Walker et al., 2009; Osburn and Stedmon, 2011]. Here we relate PARAFAC components to other measurements made on the same samples as part of the PARTNERS program [Raymond et al., 2007; Amon et al., 2012]. This unique opportunity allowed us to investigate (1) if there is a potential to use PARAFAC components to discriminate between source contributions from fresh vascular plants ${ \Sigma } _ { 8 }$ and $\Lambda _ { 8 , }$ ), mosses and peat (P-yield), leachates from vascular plants and litter $( \mathrm { A d / A l } ) \mathrm { v }$ and $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C - D O C ) }$ , and/or microbially altered (decomposed) DOM (lignin yield and BIX), (2) if PARAFAC components can be used to distinguish tDOM from individual rivers once in the Arctic Ocean, and (3) if PARAFAC components can be used as a proxy for the degree of microbial degradation.

[23] The significant positive relation between components 1–3 and lignin phenol concentrations and P-yield values suggests that while vascular plant sources may be dominant, mosses and/or peat are also an important FDOM source in Arctic rivers (Table 1). Further, their strong relation to $( \mathrm { A d / A l } ) \mathrm { v }$ and to the $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C - D O C }$ age in the Lena and Yenisei (Table 1) suggests that they are also introduced as a result of leaching of surface litter during high-runoff periods when the DOC is predominately young [Amon et al., 2012; Raymond et al., 2007]. Based on these results, PARAFAC components are not specific to a particular terrestrial FDOM source, but rather, several sources contribute to each component, which are difficult to separate. This means that the relative weight of the sources determines the relative contributions of each component. While this data set is inadequate in size to address river specific differences (rivers could not be modeled independently), no specific terrestrial component could be isolated from an individual river. Therefore, when tracing tDOM in the Arctic Ocean,

[24] While PARAFAC components could not be linked to a specific terrestrial source, this approach allowed us to isolate fresh tDOM sources from those that have been recently altered by microbes. Not only were we able to confirm the terrestrial origin of components 1–3 but the overall lack of a relationship between components 4 and 5 to DOC concentrations and the terrestrial biomarkers (Table 1), suggests that these two components can be used as a proxy for other processes, including microbial degradation of FDOM and/or autochthonous FDOM sources (i.e., algal-derived FDOM). In a previous study on the elemental and isotopic composition of DOM in the Yenisei and Ob rivers, carbon to nitrogen ratios (C/N) suggest minimal algal-derived DOM and based on $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ signatures, only $\sim 6 - 1 6 \%$ of the total DOM pool was of a plankton origin in these rivers and their estuaries [Amon and Meon, 2004]. Similarly, in the Mackenzie River, the lack of a fresh, $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOM-enriched pool during freshet, relative to all the other rivers, indicates that aquatic primary production may not be a significant source of DOM in this river [Amon et al. 2012]. As a result, we suggest that if aquatic primary production is a major source of DOM in these rivers, then it seems to be degraded by the time it reaches the downstream sampling site. Overall, the much lower DOC concentrations and depleted $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOM, combined with the fact that components 4 and 5 are especially prominent in the Mackenzie (Figures 3a–3c), suggest that these two components more likely reflect greater microbial processing of terrestrial organic matter, rather than substantial contributions from algae-derived DOM.

# 4.2. The Use of FDOM/CDOM to Estimate DOM Export to the Arctic Ocean—Pan-Arctic Observations

[25] One of the goals of this investigation was to determine the usefulness of FDOM/CDOM to trace tDOM supplied by rivers to the Arctic Ocean. PARAFAC component 1 was the dominant fluorescent signal during all flow regimes (Figures 3d–3f) and is best related to DOC concentrations across all rivers (Figure 5a). A component with a very similar fluorescence spectrum has also been identified in coastal waters within the Mackenzie River plume [Walker et al., 2009], suggesting that it persists during coastal mixing. In this earlier study, highest DOC concentrations in the plume were approximately $3 \mathrm { m g / L }$ and the fluorescence component had an intensity of $0 . 2 \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ , which corresponds well with the overall relationship apparent in this river data set (Figure 5a). Although there was a correlation with component 1 (and often better within individual rivers; Table 1), component 2 proved to have the best Pan-Arctic correlation to the more specific terrestrial biomarker, lignin phenol concentrations (Figure 5b). While this relationship was not universal, it did encompass three large Arctic rivers, the Lena, Yenisei, and Kolyma. In the Mackenzie River, the deviation indicates an overall loss of lignin content (lower slope), whereas in the Ob River, the lack of a relation suggests sources other than vascular plant inputs are important (see discussion below). Based on these results, we suggest that of the three terrestrially derived PARAFAC components, component 1 is best suited to estimate riverine DOC inputs to the Arctic Ocean (Figure 5a), whereas component 2 might be used as a more specific proxy to trace lignin phenol concentrations on a Pan-Arctic scale (Figure 5b). Figure 6 is an example of how well the seasonal fluctuation in DOC and lignin concentrations in the Lena, Yenisei, and Kolyma is captured by the fluorescence of components 1 and 2. However, it should be noted that because this lignin phenol relationship varied among rivers (valid for the Lena, Yenisei, and Kolyma but not valid in the Mackenzie and Ob river), the extrapolation of this relation beyond the rivers studied here might not be suitable.

[26] Similar to the fluorescence components, $\mathbf { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ can be used to estimate tDOM concentrations. The relationship derived for DOC was reasonably constant across rivers and remarkably similar to that derived for the Yukon River (regression represented by grey line) (Figure 5c) [Spencer et al., 2008]. Further, this relationship explains a higher degree of DOC variability, relative to C1, and is a much more straightforward measurement, relative to PARAFAC analysis. Combined, these results suggest that $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ may be the better optical proxy when quantifying DOC export to the Arctic Ocean, where DOC concentrations can be estimated using the equation $\mathrm { D O C } = 0 . 4 0 5 9 \times \mathrm { a } _ { 3 5 0 } + 2 . 9 0 7$ (Figure 5c). As observed for the fluorescence, the lack of a universal relationship between $\mathbf { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ and lignin phenol concentrations (i.e., the data from the Mackenzie and Ob rivers deviated; Figure 5d) again discourages the extrapolation of this relation beyond the rivers studied. For example, this approach may be ideal for watersheds where the supply of fresh tDOM outweighs the removal by degradation (i.e., the Lena, Yenisei, and Kolyma Rivers), whereas it would overestimate in rivers where CDOM sources other than vascular plants are important (i.e., the Ob River; see discussion below). When comparing across studies, the relationship derived for the Yukon River (represented by grey line) [Spencer et al., 2008] deviated from that derived for the Lena, Yenisei, and Kolyma but was similar to that derived for Mackenzie River (Figure 5d). These differences may arise from methodological differences in the quantification of lignin phenol concentrations.

# 4.3. Implications of Microbial Consumption of Arctic River FDOM

[27] Aquatic conduits have been shown to be active microbial reactors of the organic matter they receive, where terrestrial organic carbon is decomposed by bacteria and readily converted to greenhouse gasses [Vonk and Gustafsson, 2013]. This is especially important for high-latitude regions undergoing extensive permafrost thaw where the mobilization of ancient terrestrial carbon, which has been shown to be highly biolabile [Vonk et al., 2013], has increased by $3 \mathrm { - } 6 \%$ since 1985 [Feng et al., 2013]. To investigate the extent to which microbial processing of tDOM occurs within the individual rivers, three property/property plots are evaluated. These include correlations between lambda and $\mathrm { S } _ { ( 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 ) }$ (Figure 7a), lambda and BIX (Figure 7b), and BIX versus the fluorescence of components 4 and 5 (relative to the other components; Figure 7c). For this analysis, lignin yields are used as an indicator for the early diagenesis of vascular plant-derived DOM [Opsahl and Benner, 1995] analogous to the use of amino acid and neutral sugar yields [Cowie and Hedges, 1994; Amon et al., 2001; Amon and Benner, 2003].

[28] Three mechanisms act to remove or alter Arctic river tDOM. These include sorption of the hydrophobic fractions of the DOM pool, which are rich in lignin phenols, to mineral particles [Kaiser et al., 2004; Kawahigashi et al., 2006], photochemical degradation [Benner and Kaiser, 2011], and microbial decomposition. Sorption of DOM can be addressed by comparing the $^ { 1 4 } \dot { \mathrm { C } }$ ages of DOM and POM, where the $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ age of POM will shift to reflect that of the DOM pool which is being sorbed. In large Arctic rivers, DOM is relatively young [Amon et al., 2012; Raymond et al., 2007], whereas particulate organic matter (POM) is considered to be much older, particularly in the Mackenzie River [Goni et al., 2005]. These findings suggest sorption is not a major removal pathway for tDOM. However, it is possible that sorption of DOM released from permafrost thaw may not be detected using this approach. This is because the DOC stored in permafrost is much older and cannot be differentiated from the old POM as it erodes. If sorption of permafrost-derived DOM would be of quantitative importance, then the DOM in rivers with little suspended matter (low sorption potential) should be older than in rivers with high suspended matter loads (high sorption potential), but we observe the opposite [Amon et al., 2012]. Therefore, we interpret the concurrent low lignin concentrations and the lack of $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -enriched DOM during snowmelt as a sign for degradation of fresh DOM, rather than selective sorption of permafrost DOM.

[29] $\mathrm { S } _ { ( 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 ) }$ values can be used to infer photochemical degradation [Stedmon et al., 2011; Fichot and Benner, 2012]. This is because photodegradation of DOM generally causes the average molecular weight of DOM to decrease, which subsequently causes S(275–295) to increase [Helms et al., 2008]. While the slight increase in $\mathrm { S } _ { ( 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 ) }$ from spring to summer months, when light is most available, could suggest a loss of terrestrial CDOM due to photobleaching, the highest S(275–295) values were observed during winter base flow conditions, under low/no light conditions (Figures 4b and 7a). This suggests that during this study S(275–295) values more likely reflect a shift in CDOM source, rather than photodegradation of DOM. Further, sunlight exposure does not seem to substantially degrade CDOM on Arctic shelves or in the Mackenzie River [Osburn et al., 2009], and the lignin biomarker composition in Arctic rivers does not indicate photochemical degradation [Benner and Kaiser, 2011].

[30] Provided that contributions from aquatic-derived DOM and losses via sorption and photodegradation are considered to be small in these rivers, the range in lignin yields results from a varying degree of microbial processing of tDOM, which can be traced using the negative correlations between $\mathrm { S } _ { ( 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 ) }$ and lambda $\bar { \mathrm { R } } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 8 \bar { 3 }$ ; Figure 7a) and BIX and lambda ( $\mathrm { \tilde { R } } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 8 9$ ; Figure 7b). Further, the significant positive relationship between BIX and the fluorescence of components 4 and 5 $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 8 3$ ; Figure 7c) suggests that these components can also be used to predict the degree of microbial degradation in these rivers. Lower average BIX $\displaystyle \left( < 0 . 6 \right.$ ; Figure 4d) across the rivers, excluding the Mackenzie, and different flow regimes suggest that Arctic river FDOM is mainly derived from fresh terrestrial sources with only minor contributions from microbial (or autochthonous) sources. All plots (Figure 7) clearly separate DOM sampled from the different discharge periods across all rivers, where there is a shift from a fresh tDOM source during peak flow to a more degraded, lower molecular weight tDOM pool during base flow conditions. These results are consistent with established optical properties, which suggest a seasonal shift from modern plant litter during spring melt to an older, more degraded soil organic matter during low flow conditions [Stedmon et al., 2011]. In comparing the rivers, the property plots clearly separate the Lena and Yenisei Rivers from the others and suggests that microbial degradation is not a major removal pathway for tDOM during peak discharge. More interestingly, microbial degradation of tDOM is most extensive in the Mackenzie River, particularly during lower flow regimes. Based on this analysis, microbial processing is a major transformation pathway for DOM in the Mackenzie River and can explain the lack of a $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -enriched DOM pool during freshet [Amon et al., 2012]. This can also explain the significantly lower concentrations of lignin phenols (Figures 5b and 5d) and the inherently different optical patterns observed in this river (Figures 3, 4, and 7). Although not to the same extent, a similar pattern is also apparent in the Ob and Kolyma Rivers (Figures 7a and 7b). The Ob further stands out in that there is more CDOM per unit lignin relative to the other rivers, particularly during base flow (Figures 7a and 7b), suggesting a shift in CDOM source independent from the microbial degradation of fresh vascular plant inputs.

# 4.4. Linking Watershed Characteristics to FDOM/CDOM Composition: A Comparison of Large Arctic Rivers

[31] Differences in optical patterns between the rivers can be related to their individual watershed characteristics. In the Lena and Yenisei, increased average a350 (Figure 4a) values and contributions from terrestrial-like fluorescence components during spring freshet (Figure 3a) underline the quantitative significance of these two rivers in terms of tDOM discharge to the Arctic Ocean. Based on total fluorescence, the Lena, Yenisei, and Ob contribute $\sim 9 3 \%$ of the combined FDOM discharged by large Arctic rivers. These results are comparable to that predicted using absorption properties [Stedmon et al., 2011] and are consistent with extremely high lignin phenol concentrations [Amon et al., 2012]. The Lena and Yenisei are also the only rivers where a positive relationship was observed between the terrestrially derived fluorescence components and $( \mathrm { A d / A l } ) \mathrm { v }$ ratios and the $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC age (Table 1). Further, overall higher average SUVA values $( > 3 . 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } \mathrm { g } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) were observed during peak flow conditions in the Lena, Yenisei, and Kolyma Rivers (Figure 4c). These three rivers are unique in that their watersheds compose a greater extent of continuous permafrost relative to the other rivers $7 7 \%$ in the Lena, $31 \%$ in the Yenisei, and $9 9 \%$ in the Kolyma compared to $1 3 \%$ in the Mackenzie and $1 \%$ in the Ob). Permafrost dominated watersheds not only limit the exchange of DOM with soil layers but also tend to have a greater extent of coniferous vegetation, which has a higher aromatic carbon content (hydrophobic compounds; $\mathrm { S U V A } { = } 3 . 0 ~ \mathrm { \ m } ^ { 2 } \mathrm { g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { \Omega } ,$ ) and decomposes slower relative to deciduous litter $( \mathrm { S U V A } { = } 2 . 4 ~ \mathrm { \ m } ^ { 2 } \mathrm { g } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) [Balcarczyk et al., 2009]. The optical patterns observed in these rivers therefore likely reflect increased contributions from coniferous vegetation [Shvidenko et al., 2011] and support the notion that leaching is one of the dominant sources of CDOM within these rivers [Amon et al., 2012].

[32] In the Kolyma, Ob, and Mackenzie, overall lower $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ , DOC, and lignin phenol concentrations (Figures 4a, 5a and 5b) suggest that vascular plant inputs during freshet were relatively lower in these rivers. In conjunction, microbial degradation of vascular plant DOM is more extensive in these watersheds (Figure 7), particularly during peak flow when the majority of DOM exported to the Arctic Ocean [Amon et al., 2012; Stedmon et al., 2011]. The Ob further stands out in that FDOM/CDOM quantity changes only slightly throughout the year (Figures 3a–3c and 4a) and there is a shift in FDOM source, particularly during base flow (more FDOM per unit lignin relative to the other rivers; Figures 5b and 5d). Further, only in the Ob River are the terrestrial components better related to the P-yield (a peat bog indicator) relative to lignin phenol concentrations (a vascular plant indicator) (Table 1). Collectively, these results suggest that Ob River FDOM switches from fresh plant leachates during freshet to a more peat bog-derived FDOM source during mid-flow and base flow conditions (Table 1). These results are consistent with the unique lignin composition observed in the Ob River, particularly during base flow, which suggest increased inputs from peat and/or moss DOM sources (indicated by relatively higher contributions of p-hydroxybenzenes) [Amon et al., 2012]. The Ob watershed is unique in that it experiences the mildest climate and has the least amount of permafrost within its catchment. Further, most of the lower reaches of the catchment are relatively flat with slopes between 0 and $2 \%$ , which results in enormous flood plains and the largest peat bog system in the world [Yang et al., 2004; Stolbovi et al., 1998]. The large peat bogs in this watershed can initially absorb a significant portion of the peak discharge enrich the freshet water with bog-derived organics and subsequently release the water back to the river channel after the hydrological pressure resides (during mid-flow and base flow conditions). This could produce a more stable supply of DOM to the Ob River and explain why we do not see a significant change in FDOM quantity over the different flow regimes (Figures $3 \mathrm { a } - 3 \mathrm { c }$ and 4a). This could also explain the more bog derived FDOM character observed in this river, where the fluorescence components are better related to the P-yield (Table 1). Moreover, increased hydrological connectivity and the extensive peat bog in this watershed likely result in longer DOM residence times, particularly during peak flow. This would increase the time allowed for microbial degradation to occur and explain the elevated microbial-like fluorescence signal observed in this river (Figures 3d–3f and 7a–7c).

[33] The Mackenzie River is the most unique in terms of DOM character. CDOM in this river has a lower molecular weight (Figure 4b) and is less aromatic (Figure 4c) and tDOM concentrations are much lower (Figures 5b and 5d) relative to the other rivers. These distinct differences can be attributed to the fact that a large fraction of tDOM in this river is removed or altered by microbes before reaching the Arctic Ocean (Figure 7). This is especially the case for base flow DOM, but even peak flow DOM has a greater microbial imprint relative to other rivers during lower discharge periods (Figure 7). The Mackenzie watershed is unique in that it contains a higher abundance of large lakes ( $1 0 . 3 \%$ of open water bodies have been reported in the Mackenzie watershed compared to 1.7, 2.1, 2.4, and $1 . 6 \%$ found in the Lena, Yenisei, Ob, and Kolyma). These open water bodies, which contribute more than $5 5 \%$ to the water flowing down the Mackenzie [Amon et al., 2012], tend to act as a buffer for hydrological events [Gibson and Prowse, 2002] and increase the residence times of DOM in this watershed, relative to the other rivers. This would also increase the time allowed for microbial degradation to occur and can explain why microbial degradation is most extensive in the Mackenzie River (Figures 7a–7c).

[34] While it is difficult to separate the various controls that determine the fate of permafrost carbon as it erodes, it is clear that the removal of tDOM in large Arctic rivers not only depends on its bioavailability but also on the amount of time the DOM pool is exposed to microbial processing; given a longer DOM residence time, there is a greater potential for microbial degradation of organic carbon to occur as it is laterally transported in large Arctic watersheds. Despite this fact, the lateral transport of permafrost carbon along the land to ocean conduit, which has the potential to translocate greenhouse gas release away from the thaw site, as well as carbon decomposition and conversion to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ , is rarely considered in the impact of thawing permafrost carbon on the global climate [Vonk and Gustafsson, 2013]. Given the pace of climate change in the Arctic [Kattsov et al., 2005], it is paramount that we begin to investigate how such changes will alter carbon cycling in high-latitude rivers and how this might contribute to climate change. For example, regions undergoing extensive permafrost thaw will result in the formation of thermokarst lakes. Based on optical trends observed in the Mackenzie River, the abundance of lakes could result in longer DOM residence times and therefore in a higher degree of degradation. On the other hand, the degree to which microbial degradation occurs is also strongly influenced by the topography of the watershed (e.g., hydrological connectivity). Based on optical trends observed in the Ob River, as the climate warms and precipitation rates increase in high-latitude regions, increased hydrological connectivity could also result in a higher degree of degradation, particularly for regions within the watershed that are relatively flat. Conversely, this may mean that for steeper watersheds, permafrost carbon may be discharged relatively unaltered to the shelf sea of the Arctic Ocean.

# 5. Conclusions and Implications

[35] In comparing Pan-Arctic relations between the PARAFAC components to DOC and terrestrial biomarkers, PARAFAC component 1 (Excitation $( \mathrm { E x . } ) 3 1 0 \mathrm { n m } , < 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m } $ Em. $4 3 2 \mathrm { n m }$ ) is better suited to estimate DOC discharged to the Arctic, offering what appears to be a Pan-Arctic relationship, whereas PARAFAC component 2 (Ex. $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , $2 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ; Em. $4 6 2 \mathrm { n m }$ ) can be used as a more specific proxy to trace lignin phenol concentrations. Conversely, PARAFAC components 4 and 5 can be used to indicate microbial degradation of tDOM (and potentially increased inputs from autochthonous FDOM sources). Based on optical patterns in Arctic rivers, the majority of FDOM in these watersheds appears to be largely derived from fresh vascular plant DOM sources, whereas during mid-flow and base flow, FDOM has a greater microbial imprint relative to peak flow conditions. While PARAFAC components were successful at addressing qualitative differences in terms of FDOM character between the rivers and across the seasons, simpler absorbance indices (i.e., a350) are better suited to quantify DOC export to the Arctic Ocean and to trace lignin phenol concentrations during transport and mixing.

[36] Differences in FDOM/CDOM character between the rivers can be explained by variations of vegetation, topography, and hydrology in the different watersheds. In the Lena and Yenisei Rivers, a larger contribution of conifer forests (mostly larches) results in a higher molecular weight, higher aromatic CDOM pool, which is largely derived from litter leachates during peak discharge months. Further, microbial degradation of DOM (and/or the supply of autochthonous DOM) is less important within these rivers and/or the supply of fresh tDOM outweighs the removal by degradation. In the Ob River, the lower slope of the drainage basin, which results in a large peat bog system likely leads to the removal of the most labile fractions of the FDOM pool and replacement by a more refractory, bog-derived FDOM source. In the Mackenzie River, the abundance of lakes allows for prolonged microbial degradation of vascular plant-derived DOM, which seems to be the driving mechanism governing FDOM quality in this watershed, rather than photooxidation and/or sorption processes. Overall, results from this study illustrate that the removal of tDOM not only depends on its bioavailability but also on the amount of time the DOM pool is exposed to microbial processing (i.e., the residence time of DOM within the watershed).
