# Effect of the use of Bacillus spp. on the characteristics of dissolved fluorescent organic matter and the phytochemical quality of Stevia rebaudiana grown in a recirculating aquaponic system

# Abstract

The effect of the incorporation of mineralizing Bacillus spp. on the characteristics of fluorescent organic matter (FDOM) in a recirculating aquaculture system (Nile tilapia-Stevia rebaudiana) was evaluated. EEM-PARAFAC analysis was used to determine the composition of the dissolved organic matter and to study its relationship with nitrogen transformation. The composition and antioxidant activity of Stevia leaves were used as indicators of the benefits of bacterial supplementation on nutrient absorption. Two systems were used, each consisting of a circular fish tank $( 1 . 7 ~ \mathrm m ^ { 3 } )$ and six units of the nutrient film $( 0 . 1 8 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } )$ ). One system was supplemented with bacteria (BS), while the other was used as control (NBS). The inclusion of Bacillus spp. facilitated mineralization and the availability of total phosphorus (TP), $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , and nitrogen, and also controlled the total ammonia nitrogen (TAN) for 56 days without water exchange. FDOM was modeled by four components (3-humic-like, 1-protein-like), which were good indicators of the process of mineralization. The fluorescence intensity in the biofilter was significantly correlated with TP, $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , temperature, and the absorption coefficient $a 2 5 4$ . The fluorescence index (FI) was a good indicator of the process of nitrification. Plants from BS required $4 6 . 4 \%$ less ${ \mathrm { N O } } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ and $4 7 . 8 \%$ less $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ compared to the control, and absorbed $4 5 . 1 \%$ more TP. BS-Stevia leaves produced $3 8 . 6 \%$ more reducing sugars, $2 8 . 6 \%$ more flavonoids, and $3 5 . 9 \%$ more glycosylated flavonoids than the control. The fish in the BS system reached a higher final weight than NBS, resulting in a $1 \mathrm { k g } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ higher gross yield. Even so, it will be necessary to reduce the $\mathrm { p H }$ of the water to increase the antioxidant scavenging capacity of the plants.

# Introduction

Aquaponics is a soil-less agricultural system that synergistically combines aquaculture with hydroponics in a closed cycle. Since a few years ago, aquaponics, as a sustainable production alternative to traditional aquaculture, has attracted increasing attention worldwide (Love et al. 2015; Greenfeld et al. 2019). The viable application of aquaponics as a sustainable production system faces some challenges. As aquaponics are systems involving a minimum exchange of water, they are generally rich in humic substances, as all or a part of the organic matter generated (feces and unconsumed food, decaying plant material, etc.) is contained within the system. As part of the recirculation, waste from the aquatic subsystem circulates through the subsystem of sedimentation and biological filtration, where the processes of mineralization and nitrification take place. Subsequently, water that is rich in inorganic nutrients acts as a natural food source for plants (Goddek et al. 2016). Achieving balance in integrated production systems has always been a challenge, mainly due to the imbalances that can alter water quality (Yang and Kim 2019). When recirculation systems retain large amounts of water, humic substances can comprise approximately $90 \%$ of dissolved carbon of dissolved organic matter (DOM) (Leonard et al. 2002). Excessive DOM affects the availability of nitrogen to plants and compromises the sustainability of the system (Wongkiew et al. 2017).

Mineralization in aquaponic systems occurs as a function of different groups of microorganisms that act synergistically, transforming organic matter to supply plants with ammonium and nitrate. Mineralization is a three-step process that involves ammonization, ammonification, and nitrification. Gram $( + )$ microorganisms are more efficient at converting organic matter to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ than Gram $( - )$ , and also optimize nitrification rates by maintaining a low concentration of ammonia (Elsabagh et al. 2018). The incorporation of Bacillus spp. in aquaculture systems has been shown to reduce organic carbon levels with excellent environmental benefits (Xie et al. 2013). Species such as Bacillus coagulans, Bacillus cereus, Bacillus licheniformis, and Bacillus subtilis are used in bioremediation processes, being effective in the transformation of organic matter (Divya et al. 2015). In integrated systems, the amount of humic substances increases the availability of inorganic nutrients available to plants, improving absorption, and therefore quality. Some authors have shown that the incorporation of Gram $( + )$ bacteria enhances the availability of phosphorus (P) to plants, due to the mineralization of organic $\mathrm { P }$ and the solubilization of precipitated phosphates (Da Silva Cerozi and Fitzsimmons 2016).

Given the accumulation of humic substances in recirculation systems and their effect on plants, the characterization of DOM in these systems has attracted great interest. Thus, fluorescence spectroscopy has become a useful tool for characterizing DOM in water, given its sensitivity (Hudson et al. 2007; Henderson et al. 2009). Its potential to detect and differentiate between various components of organic matter is based on differences in light emission after excitation of fluorophores at different wavelengths present in water. The fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEM), in combination with Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC), have been successfully used to quantitatively characterize changes in the composition of fluorescent DOM components in various types of aquatic samples (Stedmon and Bro 2008). The optically active fraction of FDOM has two main components: one that is similar to proteins $( \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } / \lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } 2 3 0 { - } 2 8 0 / 3 3 0 { - } 3 6 0 \mathrm { n m } )$ , and the other that is similar to humic and fulvic compounds (Coble 1996; Parlanti et al. 2000). Both components are routinely divided into several subdivisions: tyrosine, tryptophan, and phenylalanine fluorescence for protein-type FDOM, and UV and humic type for humic-type FDOM (Coble 1996). Some authors have reported that fluorescence spectra, as an early warning tool, could contribute to the successful operation of mineralization by controlling the exchange of water, and, thus, contribute to sustainability (Faassen and Hitzmann 2015). Absorption coefficients have been used as indicators of dissolved organic carbon concentrations (Osburn et al. 2016), while absorption ratios have been used to determine the chemical composition of CDOM (Li and Hur 2017).

Various species of plants and fish have been proven to be suitable for integrated production. Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) is widely used in the aquaculture subcomponent due to its adaptability and rapid growth (Gullian Klanian et al. 2018). In the case of Stevia rebaudiana (Bertoni), to the best of our knowledge, there have been no previous studies on this species in aquaponic with recirculation. Stevia is a plant in the family Asteraceae and has significant economic value. The leaves contain a natural source of diterpenic glycosides, due to which they are used as non-caloric substitutes to sugar (Ceunen and Geuns 2013). Stevia leaves are increasingly consumed not only for their sweetening properties but also for their antioxidant capacity (Zaidan et al. 2019).

Very few studies have applied fluorescence spectroscopy in conjunction with PARAFAC to track changes in organic matter content in an integrated culture system. The applicability of EEM-PARAFAC has recently been explored to characterize long-term changes in fluorescent DOM composition in aquaculture and zero-discharge aquaculture systems (Yamin et al. 2017). However, there is a lack of information on the characteristics and biodegradability of DOM and its impact on nitrogen transformations in aquaponic systems. The only available previous study successfully employed two types of optimization methods, including the aeration of hydroponic supplements, and the addition of polylactic acid, to promote nitrogen and DOM transformations for environmental and economic benefits (Wu et al. 2018). In the present study, we investigated the effect of the incorporation of mineralizing bacteria on the characteristics of DOM in a recirculating aquaponic system (Nile tilapia-Stevia), through the EEMPARAFAC analysis. We characterized the composition of DOM and evaluated the relationship between variations in DOM and nitrogen transformations. We used the composition and antioxidant activity of Stevia leaves as indicators of the benefits of bacterial supplementation on nutrient absorption.

# Materials and methods

This experiment was carried out at the Experimental Unit of the Marist University of Mérida $( 2 1 ^ { \circ } 0 5 ^ { \prime } 4 2 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { \Delta N }$ , $8 9 ^ { \circ } \ 1 6 ^ { \prime } \ 5 9 ^ { \prime \prime }$ W) during the dry season of 2019 (March 18th to May 6th, 33 $\pm 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ . Two recirculation aquaponic systems were designed to investigate the effect of bacterial bioremediation on mineralization, and the final quality of aquaponic plants. The conditions of the aquaponic systems are described below.

# Aquaponic systems

Both outdoor systems were designed with the same characteristics, using the nutrient film technique (NFT) for each hydroponic unit. The aquaponic systems consisted of a circular tank $( 1 . 7 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } )$ ) and six units of the nutrient film $\mathrm { \Delta } 0 . 0 3 \mathrm { \ m } ^ { 3 }$ each), which included PVC pipes (diameter of $1 1 \mathrm { { \ c m } }$ and length of $3 . 5 \mathrm { m }$ ). Each pipe contained ten perforated holes $3 0 \mathrm { c m }$ apart between each plant. The flow of water was circulated by gravity from the aquaculture tank to the three-tandem settling basin: the first and second areas contained $2 2 . 6 2 \mathrm { k g } ( 0 . 0 3 8 9 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } )$ and $2 9 . 5 4 \mathrm { ~ k g ~ } ( 0 . 0 4 2 7 \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { 3 } )$ of volcanic red rock or tezontle, respectively. The final area contained $1 0 . 4 2 ~ \mathrm { k g }$ of clay and ceramic $( 0 . 0 1 9 8 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } )$ ), and $2 . 3 8 ~ \mathrm { k g }$ of coal $( 0 . 0 1 3 4 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \cdot$ ). Following the in-line sedimentation system, water was decanted in a cylinder having a volume of $0 . 0 9 4 9 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ , containing $1 . 2 \mathrm { - k g }$ recycled polypropylene rope wires. After the mineralization, water was pumped into the drip biofilter using two submersible pumps (Kedsum 550 GPH, Xian, CN). The biofilter contained Kaldnes Bio Filter media (K1) with a capacity higher than $9 0 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ to promote nitrification. Finally, water flowed by gravity through the hydroponic pipes at the flow rate of $0 . 4 6 \mathrm { L } / \mathrm { m i n }$ , to return to the aquaculture tank and complete the recirculation circuit. Figure 1 shows the diagram of the aquaponic system and the sampling points.

At the start of the experiment, the system was filled with fresh groundwater, which was recirculated for 8 weeks, replenishing only the losses by evaporation $( < 1 \%$ weekly). The ionic profile in the fresh groundwater at the beginning of the experiment was as follows: $1 3 7 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ , $7 8 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ , $3 1 5 . 4 \mathrm { m g / L } \mathrm { C a C O } _ { 3 }$ for alkalinity, $3 5 4 . 0 \mathrm { m g / L C a C O _ { 3 } }$ of hardness, and $\mathrm { p H }$ of 7.9. An air pump was used to keep the dissolved oxygen concentration (OD) above $4 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ (1 HPSweetwater, Pentair Aquatic Eco-System, FL, USA).

# Aquaponic species

A total of 60 cuttings of $S .$ . rebaudiana (6 cm high) were seeded into the hydroponic pipes of each system. Rockwool medium (Grow Well, UK) was used in the net cups to provide physical support to the plants. Nile tilapia fingerlings ( $O _ { ☉ }$ . niloticus; $1 4 9 . 4 \pm 4 2 . 7 \ \mathrm { g }$ ) were raised in the aquaculture tank, at a density of $6 0 \mathrm { f i s h / m } ^ { 3 } ( 8 . 9 4 \mathrm { k g / m } ^ { 3 } )$ . The fish were fed thrice a day with pellets containing $35 \%$ crude protein (Winfish-Ziegler, PA, USA). The weekly feeding rate was reduced from $3 . 5 \%$ BW to $3 . 0 \%$ BW from the fifth week until the eighth week.

# Experimental design and sampling sites

The experimental period lasted 8 weeks, which corresponds to the total fish harvest of target commercial weight $( 3 0 0 \pm 2 0 \mathrm { g } )$ . For the aquaponic bacterial supplementation systems (BS), mineralization was stimulated by the daily supplementation of a bacterial mixture containing spores of Bacillus subtilis, Bacillus pumilus, and Bacillus licheniformis. The spores were inoculated daily at a concentration of $5 . 3 0 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ CFU/mL in the last part of the sedimentation system. The second aquaponics system served as a control system, and therefore, it was not supplemented with bacteria (NBS). Water quality was monitored throughout the experiment at the following sampling points: fish tank outlet (S1), three-tandem settling basin outlet (S2), biofilter inlet (S3), biofilter outlet (S4), and NFT system outlet (S5). The summary of the experimental and sampling conditions is presented as supplementary material (SM1).

# Water quality parameters

Water temperature and DO were measured thrice daily using an oximeter (YSI 200 St. Petersburg, FL, USA). The levels of ammonia $\left( \mathrm { N H } _ { 3 } \right)$ , nitrite $\left( \mathrm { N O } _ { 2 } ^ { - } \right)$ , nitrate $\mathrm { ( N O } _ { 3 } ^ { - } \mathrm { ) }$ , total organic carbon (TOC), total phosphorous (TP), $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 3 + }$ , total suspended solids (TSS), and $\mathrm { p H }$ were measured weekly. The water samples were collected in triplicate (200 mL/each) from the five sampling points every 7 days, and each replicate was analyzed in the laboratory using a benchtop spectrophotometer (Hach DR 2800, MO, USA). $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ -N was determined using the salicylate hypochlorite method (Reardon et al. 1966). $\mathrm { N O } _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ -N was measured using sulfanilamide in an acidic solution. ${ \mathrm { N O } } _ { 3 } ^ { - } .$ -N was also measured as $\mathrm { N O } _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ -N after its reduction on a Cd-Cu column (Strickland and Parsons 1977). TP was determined by acid persulfate digestion method (Gales et al. 1966). TOC was determined using the heated persulfate oxidation method (Clescerl et al. 2000) and TSS using the gravimetric method (WSLH 1993). $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ was evaluated using the tetraphenylborate method (Pflaum and Howick 1956). pH was measured daily using a $\mathrm { p H }$ meter (HANNA, HI98106; Carrollton, TX, USA). Nutrient consumption (NC, $\mathrm { { m g / h } ) }$ was calculated according to Khater et al. (2015).

# FDOM fluorescence measurements

Fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) and absorbance spectra were measured using an Aqualog $\textsuperscript { \textregistered }$ spectrofluorometer (Horiba Ltd., Japan). Samples were filtered before the analysis using precombusted Whatman GF/C glass fiber filters, collected in $2 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ disposable scintillation vials, frozen, and transported to the Geochemical and Environmental Research Group (GERG), Texas A&M University at College Station, Texas, for analysis. EEM and absorbance spectra of samples in a 1-cm quartz cuvette were recorded at intervals of $3 \ \mathrm { n m }$ in the excitation wavelength range of 240 to $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . Emission spectra were also recorded at intervals of $3 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . Fluorescence and absorption spectra were corrected by subtracting the corresponding values of a highly pure water sample (Raman Water Fluorescence Reference, Starna $\textsuperscript { \textregistered }$ ). Each EEM was corrected for inner-filter effects using the Aqualog software, and the fluorescence intensities were converted to Raman units (excitation at $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , emission from 371 to $4 2 8 \ \mathrm { n m } )$ using the package staRdom version 1.12 (Pucher et al. 2019) for R version 3.6.1 (R Core Team, 2019). The fluorescence spectra for each sample were corrected by subtracting the fluorescence response of the blank (Milli-Q water).

# Fish growth

All fish from each tank were weighed and measured individually every week. Fish were removed from each tank using a fence and returned to the tank after measurement. At the end of the experiment, all the fish were weighed and counted. The absolute growth rate (AGR, $\mathrm { g / d a y ) }$ ) was estimated as a function of $\mathrm { W } _ { f } - \mathrm { W } _ { i }$ over time $\mathbf { \rho } ( t )$ , where $\mathrm { W } _ { i }$ and $\mathrm { W } _ { f }$ were the initial and final weights, respectively. The specific growth rate (SGR, $\%$ /day) was estimated as $( \mathrm { l n W } _ { f } - \mathrm { l n W } _ { i } ) / 1 0 0 t .$ The gross yield $( \mathrm { k g } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } )$ was calculated as the sum of the individual weights of the harvested fish. Feed conversion ratio (FCR) was calculated as $c / ( W f - W i ) . 1 0 0$ , where $F c$ is the food provided $( \mathrm { k g } )$ per tank.

# Quality of Stevia leaves

Stevia plants were trimmed once a week to increase the number of stems and promote leaf density (Fig. 1). The maximum height of the plants did not exceed $2 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ . At the end of the eighth week, $5 0 ~ \mathrm { g }$ of Stevia leaves were collected from each aquaponic system. Two samples of Stevia grown in organic soil (OS) and inorganic soil (ISC) were used as controls and were subjected to the same extraction and drying processes. The leaves were dried using an oven at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ to remove the moisture (Convection Oven, Thermo Scientific OV701F, NC, USA). The dried materials (containing $1 5 . 4 \pm$ $1 . 1 \%$ of moisture) were ground to produce a powder, and the particle size $( 2 9 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ was measured using a vibrating sieve shaker to provide good surface area for the extraction process.

The extractions were carried out using an ultrasonic processor by a direct sonication method. A 130 W ultrasound probe operating at $2 0 \mathrm { k H z }$ was used for the ultrasound treatment (VCX 130PB, Sonics, Milford, CT, USA). All chemicals used were analytical grade and obtained from VWR. Specifically, $2 . 0 ~ \mathrm { g }$ of dry Stevia leaves was placed in a $5 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ glass tube containing $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $80 \%$ aqueous methanol and homogenized for $1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ at $2 2 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ . The amplitude of the ultrasound probe was set at $80 \%$ $7 4 \mathrm { W } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ for $1 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ at $3 0 \pm 2 { \ } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Gullian Klanian and Terrats Preciat 2017).

The content of reducing sugars was measured, according to Miller (1959). The absorbance was measured at $5 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ using a spectrophotometer (Vis-DR2700 Hach Company, Loveland, CO, USA). The concentration of reducing sugars was calculated as milligrams of glucose using a standard curve of glucose $( 0 - 2 0 0 \mathrm { m g / g ) }$ .

Total phenolic content (TPC) was measured according to Singleton and Rossi (1965). Absorbance was recorded after $2 5 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ of incubation at $7 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . TPC was calculated as units of milligram gallic acid equivalents (GAE) per gram, using a standard curve of gallic acid $( 0 { - } 2 0 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { G A E } / \mathrm { g } )$ .

The total flavonoid content (TFC) and glycosylated flavonoid content (GF) were determined using the method of Zhishen et al. (1999) and De Albuquerque et al. (2014), respectively. The absorbance was measured at $4 2 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ using a Multiskan spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific, Rockford, IL, USA). TFC and GFC were calculated as milligrams of quercetin equivalents (QE) per gram, using quercetin standard curve $( 0 { - } 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { Q E } / \mathrm { g } )$ .

# Data analysis

Permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) was used to compare the data between groups. The data were normalized before the analysis. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) at $5 \%$ probability level was used to compare the parameters of Stevia leaf quality between groups.

A PARAFAC model (Stedmon et al. 2003) was adjusted to the EEM fluorescence data using the “drEEM” toolbox version 0.51 for Matlab (Murphy et al. 2013). Non-negativity constraints were used for all three modes. During the exploratory phase, one sample was deleted due to high leverage, leaving a total of 69 samples in the dataset. The PARAFAC results for this sample were not included in any further analysis. A four-component model was adjusted to the data, with a core consistency of 54.4 and a fit of $9 9 . 4 \%$ . The model was validated by split-half analysis (Stedmon and Bro 2008). The PARAFAC components were compared to those in the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al. 2014), using Tucker’s congruence coefficient of at least 0.95 for both excitation and emission spectra.

Measurements of absorbance were converted to the Napierian absorption coefficients using the formula a $( \lambda ) =$ $2 . 3 0 3 \ a \ ( \lambda ) / l$ , where a $( \lambda )$ was the measured absorbance at wavelength $\lambda$ , and $\mathbf { \nabla } ^ { \ast } l ^ { \ast }$ was the cuvette path length in meters. The spectral slopes, spectral slope ratios, and fluorescence indices were calculated using the package “cdom” (Massicotte and Markager 2016). The packages “eemR” and “cdom” were executed with the statistical program R version 3.6.1 (R Core Team, 2019). The spectral slope ratio (SR) (Helms et al. 2009) was calculated as the ratio of the slope in the range $2 7 5 { - } 2 9 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ to the slope in the range 350–400 nm, while E2/E3 was determined as the ratio of $a 2 5 0 / a 3 6 5$ . The absorption coefficient at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m } \ ( a 2 5 4 )$ was taken as a proxy for the CDOM concentrations (Massicotte et al. 2017). The specific UV absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ (SUVA254) was calculated by dividing the UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ by the sample’s DOC concentration $\mathrm { ( m g / L ) }$ ), as an indicator of aromaticity, and was expressed as $\mathrm { L } / \mathrm { m g - m }$ (Weishaar et al. 2003).

Redundancy data analysis (RDA) was used to progressively evaluate the statistical effect of each water quality variable on fish weight. Canonical variant analysis (CVA) running with Hill’s scaling was used to provide an objective assessment of the similarity in water quality between the systems. The Monte Carlo test with 500 permutations was used to select the significant variables to include in the CVA and RDA (Ter Braak and Smilauer 2002). The predictors were accepted with a type I error probability estimated less than $\mathrm { { a / } }$ Nc (Bonferroni correction), where $\mathrm { N c }$ is the number of independent variables used in the analysis (Legendre and Legendre 1998).

The predicted trendline of fish growth was obtained by modeling it using the Gompertz equation: $W \left( t \right) = a e ^ { \wedge } ( b$ (1 $- e ^ { c } ) \operatorname { \nabla } t )$ ), where $a$ is the initial weight, $b$ is the relative growth rate, and $c$ is the fitting parameter number (Ricker 1975). Theil inequality coefficient $( U )$ was used to examine the reliability of the predicted curve for its likelihood and accuracy.

Statistical analyses were conducted using the software CANOCO (version 4.5, 2002) and XLSTAT (version 2016.1, Addinsoft).

# Results

# Fluorescence characteristics and identified components

The PARAFAC analysis indicated that the fluorescence signal could be broken down mathematically into four independently varying fractions, three of which were humic-like components (C1, C2, and C3), and one was a protein-like component (C4) (Fig. 2). The spectral characteristics of the four components were similar to those reported by the previous studies. Table 1 summarizes the interpretation of the components and references, according to the online database OpenFluor.

Component C1 showed two excitation peaks at $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , and a single emission peak at $4 4 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . This component has been associated with terrestrial humic-like compounds, and it has been particularly reported by Murphy et al. (2014) and Peleato et al. (2017) that the spectra of this component were identical to syringaldehyde, which is produced during the breakdown of lignin. Component C2 $\lambda _ { \mathrm { e x c } }$ $= 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m } / \lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } = 4 4 5 \mathrm { n m } )$ was associated with Coble’s peaks A and C (Coble 1996). Component C3 had two excitation peaks at 240 and $2 4 4 \mathrm { n m }$ , and a single emission peak at $5 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . This component was also reported in OpenFluor as being humiclike, and an intermediate of the photodegradation of humic compounds (Murphy et al. 2014). Component C4 had a single excitation and emission peak $( \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x c } } = 2 7 5 ~ \mathrm { n m } / \lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } = 3 2 5 ~ \mathrm { n m } )$ ), and although it could not be classified using the definition of the Coble Peaks (Coble 1996), it was interpreted as a proteinlike component in OpenFluor.

# Effect of bacterial supplementation on water quality parameters

The weekly variations in the parameters of water quality in each system are presented in Table 2. Both the systems were outdoors, but in some weeks, the BS system received higher exposure to the sun owing to its geographical position, as evidenced by a $1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ increase in temperature during this period. However, the $\mathrm { p H }$ and DO concentrations were higher in

NBS. In contrast, $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ and the total level of TP from the 5th week were higher in BS than in NBS. The high levels of nitrate concerning TAN and nitrite indicated that nitrification occurred in both the systems from the beginning of the trial. During the final 2 weeks, the level of TAN was $4 5 . 7 \substack { - 6 4 . 4 \% }$ higher in the NBS than in the BS, while the concentration of nitrite was $3 7 . 5 \substack { - 6 6 . 6 \% }$ higher. # Discussion

The findings of this study revealed that the dynamics of nutrients were affected by the inclusion of Bacillus spp. in water (Fig. 3). The low concentration of TAN and nitrite, as well as the accumulation of nitrate, indicated that nitrification occurred in both the systems, although BS presented better efficiency in mineralization. In the BS system, a high concentration of phosphorus and low concentrations of ammonium and nitrite were maintained until the end of the experiment (Table 2). The concentration of ammonia in NBS increased during the last 2 weeks, indicating the beginning of the maximum load capacity. In contrast, the level of organic carbon in the effluent of the BS-mineralization tank (S3), as well as the amount of TSS, was $1 3 . 6 \%$ and $50 \%$ lower than NBS (S3) (Table 3). Owing to the higher concentration of Bacillus spp., BS worked at a lower concentration of DO. PARAFAC analysis revealed that the fluorescence characteristics of water were represented by four components, which could be indicators of the process of mineralization due to microbial activity. These results partially agree with those of $\mathrm { W u }$ et al. (2018), who studied the characteristics of F D O M i n a n a q u a p o n i c s s y s t e m w i t h d i f f e r e n t intensification strategies and reported four components, including three humic-like components $( 8 3 - 8 6 \% )$ and one tryptophan-like substance $( 1 4 \mathrm { - } 1 7 \% )$ . The results obtained by these authors demonstrated that the intensities of fluorescence of the humic-like components decreased significantly after the application of intensification strategies, indicating that two attempts at improvement possibly affected humic-like fluorescence. In the present study, the intensity of fluorescence of the first three PARAFAC components (all humic- or fulvic-like components) significantly increased in the BS system compared to the NBS system during weeks 2, 3, and 4, whereas that of C4 (a protein-like component) did not change significantly. This fact suggests that the inclusion of Bacillus spp. increased the production of complex aromatic moieties, either from decaying plant material, feces, or unused feed), but not at the expense of proteins. The intensity of fluorescence of all the PARAFAC components increased significantly in the BS system at point S4 (biofilter outlet) (Table 5), which could be associated with the process of mineralization. Fluorescence intensity of C4, a protein-like component, was high at the input of the biofilter in NBS (S3) and decreased significantly to levels below those of BS at the biofilter outlet (S4). This suggested that there was more protein-like fluorescence at the input point of mineralization. The ability of aquatic microbial communities to contribute to FDOM has been previously documented (Asmala et al. 2014). Previous authors demonstrated that humic-like CDOM occurred during bacterial metabolism in freshwater (Kramer and Herndl 2004). Parlanti et al. (2000) have attributed these changes to the organic materials released by marine microorganisms because of their biologic activity, which is in line with the expected process of mineralization at S3 and S4 in the BS system. Since Bacillus spp. were inoculated in the sedimentation tank, an increase in the intensity of fluorescence was expected at the successive points (S3 and S4), as a product of microbial activity.

On the other hand, the PARAFAC components at S3 and S4 were highly correlated with TP, $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , and temperature. These results contrast with those reported by Hambly et al. (2015), who studied the FDOM characteristics of a recirculating aquaculture system. These authors observed positive correlations between the fluorescence components, as well as the dissolved organic carbon, with the feed charge. In our study, no significant correlations were observed between TOC and TSS with PARAFAC components, but there were significant correlations with the concentrations of $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , P, and the absorption coefficient at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m } \ ( a 2 5 4 )$ (Table 8). Furthermore, $a 2 5 4$ is the measure of absorbance per milligram of carbon present in the sample and is mainly correlated with the aromaticity of the CDOM (Weishaar et al. 2003). Particularly, the increase of P and $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , as products of mineralization, could be indirectly related to the intensity of fluorescence. Phosphorus, $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , and $_ \mathrm { N }$ are essential nutrients for not only plants but also phytoplankton. Hence, an increase in fluorescence at S3 and S4 could be attributed to an increase in the phytoplankton, as a consequence of the mineralization.

The information provided by the absorbance values is difficult to interpret and compare. The E2/E3 ratio during the third week indicated a change in the size of the particles of organic matter between the systems. The E2/E3 ratio in NBS was $1 3 . 5 \%$ higher than that in BS, indicating the release of molecules of lower molecular weight (Guo and Chorover 2003). Other authors (Helms et al. 2009) have used SR as an indirect measure of the average molecular weight of DOM. Although the values of SR remained relatively constant, in S2 (sedimentation basin outlet), particles released by the NBS system had lower molecular weights than those in BS, whereas the opposite was observed at the biofilter inlet (S3). Contrary to the absorbance indices, the fluorescence index (FI) proved to be a good indicator of the process of nitrification and is significantly correlated with a decrease in TAN, and an increase in nitrate (–0.66, 0.56, $P { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ ). FI acts as a marker of the origin of fluorescent CDOM (McKnight et al. 2001) and indicated that S3 was the origin of the increase in fluorescence in this study. According to McKnight et al. (2001), the FI value of 1.9 indicated the presence of fulvic acids derived from microbes. In the case of S4 in our study, the value of FI reached 2.30.

Plants from BS absorbed $4 6 . 4 \%$ less nitrate and $4 7 . 8 \%$ lower $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ than the NBS, and absorbed $4 5 . 1 \%$ more phosphorous (Fig. 4), suggesting that BS could withstand an even higher carrying capacity. A previous study by Da Silva Cerozi and Fitzsimmons (2016) has reported that the use of Bacillus spp. improved the availability of phosphorous and served as a promoter of plant growth in aquaponics systems.

The high rate of absorption of P explains the decrease of this nutrient in S5-BS, during its passage through the NFT (Table 3). Phosphorus is an essential requirement for plant growth, as it is involved in the development of the root. It also constitutes phospholipids of cell membranes and performs functions in cellular energy metabolism, photosynthesis, glycolysis, and fatty acid synthesis (Lorenzo-Orts et al. 2020). The higher rate of absorption of P by plants can also explain the increase in levels of glycosylated flavonoids and sugars of plants in BS (Table 10). Plants generally contain P at 0.05 to $0 . 5 \%$ of their dry weight, which constitutes a variety of organic compounds, including sugar phosphates, phospholipids, coenzymes, and nucleotides (Raghothama 1999).

Our data confirm that the compositions and antioxidant activities of Stevia leaves grown in both systems were different, indicating that variations in water quality had some effect on the composition of secondary metabolites in plants. Tavarini et al. (2015) demonstrated that the concentration of phenolic compounds in field-grown Stevia was negatively correlated with that of inorganic nitrogen. An increase in the level of nitrogen, applied as ammonium nitrate, decreased the concentration of flavonoids and total phenolics in plants. Our data showed that effluent from the NBS-NFT (S5) showed $5 3 . 4 \%$ more TAN and $1 1 . 1 \%$ more nitrite than BS, which indicates an excess of these nutrients. However, the NBS plants consumed a high amount of $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , which has been previously correlated with the production of total phenols (Shehata et al. 2019). These effects could explain the higher concentration of total phenols of NBS compared to BS, and in turn, decrease in the levels of total flavonoids (Table 10).

Due to the nature of the water source, the $\mathrm { p H }$ of the water was not ideal for Stevia. The $\mathrm { p H }$ in the soil system of Stevia varies between 6.5 and 7.5 (Goyal and Samsher 2010), which is lower than that observed in this study. This difference could explain the lower antioxidant capacity of aquaponics plants compared to the inorganic control (Table 10). Friedman and Jürgens (2000) reported that phenolic compounds are poorly stable at high pH. Chen et al. (2008) demonstrated that the total phenolic content was higher at $\mathrm { p H } = 5$ but gradually decreased with an increase in $\mathrm { p H }$ . Even so, the $\mathrm { p H }$ did not affect the concentration of total flavonoids (FT) and glycosylated flavonoids (FG). The BS treatment showed a high content of FT $( 9 9 . 2 \mathrm { m g / g } )$ and FG $( 9 7 . 2 \mathrm { m g / g } )$ , which were $2 8 . 6 \%$ and $3 5 . 9 \%$ higher than the NBS plants. Previous studies on experimental Stevia plants have reported FT values of $3 . 8 5 ~ \mathrm { m g / g }$ (Gaweł-Bęben et al. 2015), $2 1 . 7 3 \mathrm { m g / g }$ (Tadhani et al. 2007), and $4 8 . 2 9 - 6 0 . 3 3 ~ \mathrm { m g / g }$ (Zeng et al. 2013), which were lower than the values observed in this study. Our results of FT were within the range $( 7 3 . 9 { - } 1 1 3 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m g / g } )$ reported by Atas et al. (2018), for Stevia grown in the greenhouse while receiving different concentrations of nitrogen as fertilizer $\mathrm { ( 0 - 2 0 ~ k g / }$ day). Although there were no previous reports on total FG concentrations with which we could compare our results, our findings of $6 6 . 8 \%$ and $8 3 . 9 \%$ more FG in BS leaves than the organic and inorganic control, respectively, had a significant technological value. Bacillus spp. not only performs the functions of bioremediation but also acts as an additional source of microbial protein that is efficiently assimilated by Nile tilapia, positively impacting their health (Gullian Klanian et al. 2020).

In summary, the inclusion of Bacillus spp. favored mineralization, and hence the availability of free nutrients such as P, K, and N, in addition to controlling the level of ammonia for 56 days without the need for water replacement. A PARAFAC analysis revealed that the fluorescence characteristics of water were represented by four components, which proved to be good indicators of the process of mineralization as a consequence of microbial activity. The intensity of fluorescence of the four components at the biofilter was highly correlated with TP, $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , temperature, and the absorption coefficient $a 2 5 4$ . The FI was demonstrated to be a good indicator of the process of nitrification, as it was significantly correlated with the decrease in TAN, and the increase in nitrate. The BS plants required $4 6 . 4 \%$ less nitrate and $4 7 . 8 \%$ less $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ than the NBS and absorbed $4 5 . 1 \%$ more P. Under these conditions, the BS plants produced $2 8 . 6 \%$ more flavonoids and $3 5 . 9 \%$ more glycosylated flavonoids compared to NBS. The content of reducing sugars in BS was higher than that in the control and similar to that of Stevia grown in organic soil. Although the BS aquaponic system had a higher oxygen demand, the fish reached a higher final weight than the control, resulting in $1 ~ \mathrm { k g / m } ^ { 3 }$ higher gross yield. The carrying capacity of the BS could increase using the same technology. However, it will still be necessary to reduce the water pH to obtain better results of the radical-scavenging capacity of the plants.
