Abstract: Applications of aluminium (Al) salt or lanthanum (La) modified bentonite (LMB) have become popular methodologies for immobilizing phosphorus (P） in eutrophic lakes. The presence of humic substances, has been shown to inhibit this form of treatment due to the complexation with La/Al. However, the effects of other dissolved organic matter (DOM),especially that derived from phytoplankton (the dominant source in eutrophic lakes) are unknown. In this study, the interaction with La/Al of Suwannee River Standard Humic Acid Standard II (SRHA) and algaederived DOM (ADOM) were investigated and compared. Differed to SRHA which was dominated by polyphenol-like component ( $7 6 . 8 \%$ ， C1-SRHA)， majority in ADOM were protein-like substance, including $4 1 . 9 \%$ tryptophan-like component (C2- ADOM) and $2 1 . 0 \%$ tyrosine-like component (C3-ADOM). Two reactions of complexation and coprecipitation were observed between SRHA/ADOM and La/Al.

Complexation dominated at low metal inputs less than $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and coprecipitation was the main reaction at higher metal inputs. For ADOM, the tryptophan-like component (C2-ADOM) was the important component to react with metal. The reaction rate for C2-ADOM with La were about two-third of that for C1-SRHA, indicating that the influence of C2-ADOM was significant during the P immobilization by La/Al-based treatment in eutrophic lakes. The P removal data in the presence of ADOM confirmed the significant inhibition of ADOM. In addition, based on the composition of coprecipitates and relatively biodegradable character of tryptophan-like substances (C2-ADOM), the coprecipitation of ADOM was assumed to reduce the stability of precipitated P in eutrophic lakes. The release of P from the potential biodegradation of the coprecipitates and thus the possible decline of the performance of P immobilization by La/Al-based treatments is an important work in the future.

Main finding of the work:

The tryptophan-like component (C2） from algae-derived dissolved organic matter (ADOM) was reactive with La/Al. The complexation of C2-ADOM with La/Al inhibited the efficiency of P immobilization. The longevity of P immobilization was assumed to be suppressed due to the coprecipitation of C2-ADOM with La/P and the biodegradable character of tryptophan-like substances.

# 1. Introduction

The process of lake eutrophication, by which enrichment in nutrients (mainly nitrogen (N) and phosphorous (P)) results in excessive phytoplankton growth (Conley et al.， 2oo9) can have severe negative consequences for ecosystem function and services (Smith and Schindler, 2O09). Phosphorous has been considered a key factor in inducing eutrophication (Schindler et al., 2O08) and internal nutrient loading of P from the sediment has been found to maintain symptoms,even following the reduction of external loading from the watershed (Scheffer and Jeppesen, 2007). A widely used approach to the problem has been the suppression of internal loading by chemical immobilization of $\mathrm { P }$ using aluminium (Al) salt or, more recently,a lanthanum (La) modified bentonite (LMB) (Huser et al., 2016; Lürling et al., 2016). In the real application, Al salt was diluted with lake water, buffered with calcium hydroxide sand sprayed on the lake surface from a barge. La are presented as LMB, which were mixed with lake water and sprayed through the manifold on the water surface (Waajen et al.， 2016). Aluminum sulfate forms amorphous aluminum hydroxide, which binds irreversibly to $\mathrm { P }$ in the sediment (Cooke et al., 2005), while LMB acts by capture of phosphate to precipitate $\mathrm { L a P O _ { 4 } }$ , a mineral with an extremely low solubility $( \mathrm { K } _ { \mathrm { s p } } { = } 1 0 ^ { - 2 4 . 7 }$ to $1 0 ^ { - 2 5 . 7 } ~ \mathrm { m o l } ^ { 2 } { \cdot } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 2 } )$ (Liu and Byrme, 1997; Lurling et al., 2014).

In the natural environment, the coexistence of inorganic $\left( \mathrm { C O } _ { 3 } ^ { 2 - } \right.$ , dissolved silicon of $\mathrm { S i O _ { 4 } }$ or $\mathrm { S i ( O H ) _ { 4 } }$ $\mathrm { M g } ^ { 2 + }$ and $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ ) ions and dissolved organic matter (DOM) in lake water and sediment has been shown to affect the immobilization of ${ \mathrm { P O } } _ { 4 } ^ { 3 \cdot }$ (de Vicente et al.,2008b; Reitzel et al.,2013). Humic acid (HA) is found to reduce the effciency of $\mathrm { \ A l { ( O H ) } } _ { 3 }$ to bind $\mathrm { P O } _ { 4 } { } ^ { 3 \cdot }$ , thus more Al input is required to achieve desired $\mathrm { P }$ removal (de Vicente et al., 2008b; Guan et al., 2006). A negative effect of humic substances on the immobilization of phosphate by LMB has also been noticed (Dithmer et al., 2016; Lürling et al.， 2014). As suggested by Lürling et al. (2014), the complexation (chelation) of HA inhibited the phosphorous immobilization potential of LMB. Dithmer et al. (2016), found a significant negative effect of added HA or phthalate on the P sequestration of LMB in a short term (7 days), whereas a long-term P adsorption experiment revealed that the negative effect of HA was reduced with time.

DOM is ubiquitous in aquatic ecosystems and comprises a complex assemblage of heterogeneous organic molecules, including a mixture of aromatic and aliphatic hydrocarbon structures incorpoating amides， carboxyls, hydroxyls, ketones and a variety other minor functional groups (Hertkorn et al., 2016; Leenheer and Croué, 2003). Groups such as carboxylic and phenol groups are active sites for interactions with metals (Chen et al., 2014; Fujii et al., 2014; Guan et al., 2006). Owing to the heterogeneity of DOM, the potential for interactions with La or Al is variable. For example, DOM in pore waters from 16 Danish lakes had an inhibitory effect on phosphorus removal by LMB,but the magnitude of the effect did not correlate significantly with dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentration (Dithmer et al., 2016), reinforcing the importance of quality of DOM over quantity in this context. The character and composition of DOM is closely related to its origins. DOM in aquatic ecosystems can be either autochthonous or allochthonous. Autochthonous sources include exudates from phytoplankton and macrophytes and microbially transformed organic detritus from within the aquatic system， while allochthonous sources are most commonly the result of runoff or deposition from the surrounding terrestrial ecosystem (Findlay and Sinsabaugh, 2003). The majority of autochthonous plankton-derived DOM is made of carbohydrates， followed by N-compounds, including protein, polypeptides,and amino acids (Myklestad, 2000). Allochthonous DOM originating from forest or agricultural land, meanwhile, tends to be rich in humic substances (Shang et al., 2018; Yamashita et al., 2011a).   
During Al or LMB treatment, molecules of Al and La may contact DOM in pore

water and in the water column. Most current studies have focused on the influence of humic substances on P removal efficiency, but in eutrophic lakes, the dominant source of DOM is often phytoplankton (Yao et al.， 201l； Zhang et al.， 2009). To better understand the efficiency and stability of the $\mathrm { P }$ immobilization treatments using Al or LMB in eutrophic ecosystems, the effect of the alga-derived DOM (ADOM) should be considered.

Optical analysistechniquessuch asultraviolet-visible range (UV-Vis) spectroscopy and fluorescence spectroscopy have contributed substantial insights into the character and composition of DOM in diverse aqueous systems (Fellman et al., 2010; Lu et al., 2013; Osburn et al., 2011; Stedmon et al., 2007). Excitation emission matrix fluorescence with parallel factor analysis (EEM-PARAFAC) deconvolutes complex EEMs into discrete fluorescence components (which can be characterized as terrestrial/microbial humic-like, tyrosine-like or tryptophan-like） and allows the sources and reactivity of each component to be traced. In addition， due to other advantages of rapid, relatively inexpensive to undertake and precise, EEM-PARAFAC had been regarded as an ideally suited analytical tool for environmental applications of DOM (Cory and Kaplan, 2012; Fellman et al., 2010; Jaffé et al., 2014; Osburn et al., 2011). When DOM forms chemical complexes with metal ions, fluorescent emissions are either quenched or enhanced, depending upon the metals involved and the composition of DOM (Ohno et al., 2008; Yamashita and Jaffé, 2008). Previous studies have demonstrated that changes in fluorescence provide a valuable insight into the binding properties of EEM-PARAFAC components for different sources (such as water soluble organic matter, landfill leachate andnatural DOM in coastal ecosystem) with metal ions (Fe,Al, Cu and Cd) (Ohno et al., 2008; Wu et al., 2012a; Yamashita and Jaffé, 2008).

The objective of this study was to illustrate interactions between different forms of DOM and La or Al, and to infer the possible effects of DOM composition on the efficacy of Al or La-based treatments for immobilizing $\mathrm { P }$ in eutrophic lakes. To avoid artefacts resulting from the adsorption of DOM on bentonite (Bhatnagar and Sillanpäa, 2017),La ions were used instead of LMB. LMB is prepared through a simple cationexchange technique by adding 0.1M $\mathrm { L a C l } _ { 3 }$ into a purity bentonite and it was suggested that the active site/component of LMB for immobilize phosphorus is La ion (Douglas, 2002). Two different forms of DOM were investigated: ADOM and Suwannee River Standard Humic Acid Standard II (SRHA), the latter as a proxy for allochthonous DOM derived from humic material. La or Al were titrated into DOM solutions and interactions were illustrated by measuring the concentrations of dissolved metal ions,DOC and EEM-PARAFAC (excitation emission matrix fluorescence with parallel factor) components. Although the inhibition of HA has been shown during the immobilization of P by Al or LMB treatment, seldom studies have been reported that the reactivity of EEM-PARAFAC components of SRHA with La/Al. And to our best knowledge, the studies into EEM-PARAFAC components of ADOM with La/Al is limited. The tested hypothesis was that ADOM would interact with Al/La and inhibit their performance in P removal in a manner similar to that previously demonstrated for SRHA.

Two humic-like components and one polyphenol-like component were identified in SRHA. # 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Two interaction processes of SRHA/ADOM with La and Al

The observed changes in dissolved La and Al ion concentrations indicate that besides the complexation effect, a further interaction takes place between DOM and the introduced metal ions. At low inputs, there was a portion of La and Al ions complex with DOM, but with the addition of more metal, a further process of coprecipitation transforms these complexed ions into an insoluble solid state (Figure 2). The rapid decline in DOC levels and the changes in the UV-Vis spectra (Figure 3) indicate coprecipitation as a likely mechanism, resulting in much lower levels of soluble La at higher La doses.

Coprecipitation is defined as the drawing down of a normally soluble substance by inclusion, occlusion, or adsorption with another substance as it is precipitated (Chen et al.,2014; Du et al., 2018; Eusterhues et al., 2008; Riedel et al., 2013). For instance, the addition of $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 3 + }$ ions to a SRHA solution could lead to the formation of a

Fe-hydrophobic OM precipitate (Du et al., 2018). Coprecipitation of SRHA and La would explain the observed changes of in dissolved La and SRHA levels at high La doses. When La is added at more than $3 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ ， the precipitation of La draws down both SRHA and La complexed with SRHA. The area of the UV-Vis spectrum for SRHA solution following coprecipitation with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ La was only $6 . 9 \%$ of that with no La input (Figure 3(b)). Compared with removal of about $70 \%$ DOC by treatment with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ La (Figure 3(a)), it appears that chromophoric DOM (CDOM) in SRHA possesses a stronger ability to coprecipitate with La than non-chromophoric DOM.

There appear to be two reactions between SRHA/ADOM and metal (La/Al): complexation and coprecipitation. At low metal doses,although more than $80 \%$ percentage of metal transformed into solid, the amount of metal precipitates was small. Changes of DOC levels and UV-Vis spectra are found to be insignificant and complexation is regarded as the main reaction of metal with DOM. As metal doses increase however, coprecipitation becomes the dominant interaction， causing dramatically reduced DOC concentrations and altered UV-Vis spectra. The data of remaining DOC percentage vs metal inputs were fitted by the four-parameter logistic curve and the results were shown in Figure S2(a) and Table S2. In this model, the Botom could be regarded as the remaining DOC after the coprecipitation and the Hillslope may reflect the rate of coprecipitation. For reaction with La, $7 1 . 1 \%$ DOC in SRHA could coprecipitate with La, while only $1 7 . 0 \%$ DOC in ADOM could. The hillslopes of the curves were 6.3 and 1.8 for SRHA and ADOM with La, respectively, suggesting that the coprecipitation rate with La for SRHA was 3.5 times of that for ADOM. For the reaction of SRHA with Al, changes of DOC concentrations fitted well by the four-parameter logistic curve, with a $\mathtt { R } ^ { 2 }$ value of 0.99. The results showed that at most $7 2 . 1 \%$ of coprecipitated DOC with Al and the hillslope was 2.7 for SRHA solution. However, Changes of DOC concentrations in ADOM could not be fited by the four-parameter logistic curve.

Changes in the PARAFAC components of SRHA and ADOM solutions following La or Al treatment indicate the potential for complexation and coprecipitation between DOM and the investigated metal ions. Although DOC concentrations were almost unaltered by the complexation, the process is thought to change DOM fluorescence because ground-state metal-DOM complexes influence non-radiative processes including intersystem crossing that competeswith fluorescence (Lakowicz, 2006; Senesi, 1990). Similar complexation processes have previously been shown to involve the bonding of metal ions $( \mathbf { M } ^ { \mathrm { n + } } )$ with carboxylic/phenolic functional groups and electrostatic interactions between charged DOM molecules and $\mathbf { M } ^ { \mathrm { n + } }$ (Benedetti et al.， 1995; Dryer et al., 2008). On the other hand, transformation of DOM into solid form by coprecipitation leads to a decrease in the intensity of fluorescence. Fluorescence measurement has previously been used to assess the coagulation of humic acids reacting with iron ions (Cheng and Chi, 2002). The fluorescence changes of SRHA and ADOM treated with La (Figure 5(a) and (b) show that the polyphenol-like component C1-SRHA,and the terrestrial humiclike C2-SRHA are important in the complexation of La in SRHA，as is the tryptophan-like C2-ADOM in ADOM. This may be interfered by the weak but simultaneous coprecipitation.

4.2. Potential implications for the effciency and longevity of phosphorus immobilization by La/Al based treatment

In natural water, where phosphate $( \mathrm { P O } _ { 4 } { } ^ { 3 - } )$ and DOM coexist, the addition of metal ions $\mathrm { ( L a } ^ { 3 + }$ or $\mathsf { A l } ^ { 3 + }$ ） could initiate the formation of a ternary DOM-metal-P complex (Cheng et al., 2004; Gerke, 2010; Gerke and Hermann, 1992; Mikutta and Kretzschmar, 2011). It is also possible that binary complexation between DOM and metal (DOM-metal) is sufficient to inhibit the precipitation of $\mathrm { P }$ without $\mathrm { P }$ being chemically incorporated into the complex. Either of these two complexation scenarios could be sufficient to inhibit the immobilization of P and leave problematic levels of dissolved phosphate in the water.

The results of our PARAFAC modeling indicate that the polyphenol-like C1- SRHA and the terrestrial humic-like C2-SRHA components perform an important role in complexing with La/Al. It is noted that the tryptophan-like C2-ADOM also shows a strong ability to react with La. The water bodies in which LMB or Al treatments are applied are invariably ADOM-enriched, and so the consequences of this interaction are potentially significant (as shown in Figure 6). When the relative abundances of EEM-PARAFAC components are taken into consideration, the different abilities of SRHA and ADOM to inhibit $\mathrm { P }$ immobilization can be understood. The relative abundances of C1-SRHA and C2-SRHA in SRHA were $7 6 . 8 \%$ and $1 4 . 5 \%$ , while C2-

ADOM accounts for only $4 1 . 9 \%$ of ADOM. Thus, the inhibition effect of SRHA was stronger than that of ADOM. It is noteworthy that even in the absence of OM, treatment with $3 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ La results in sequestration of only $6 9 . 1 \%$ P (Figure 6(a)). Based on the theoretical ratio of SRP with La, $3 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ La should be sufficient to remove close to $100 \%$ of phosphate. It is further suggested that in a solution of pH 7.0, competition from OH' to combine with La may also hamper the removal of P (Reitzel et al., 2017).

When La or Al inputs increase beyond $3 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ， coprecipitation has a very distinct effect. At a La dose of $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ ， the removal percentages of $\mathrm { P }$ in the presence of SRHA $( 9 4 . 0 \% )$ and ADOM $( 9 7 . 9 \% )$ were similar to those achieved in the absence of DOM $( 9 7 . 7 \% )$ (Figure 6(a)). There are two possible explanations for this effect. One is that $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ La, which is more than adequate to settle $3 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ P, even with the interference of DOM. Alternatively, if a ternary DOM-La-P complex is formed, coprecipitation and settling could compensate for the inhibition of $\mathrm { P }$ removal by the complexation process. Further investigation into the removal mechanism of $\mathrm { P }$ at different La/Al concentrations lower than $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ is needed.

Besides P removal efficiency, the longevity of La or Al treatment is an important consideration. During the coprecipitation process，decreases in both DOC and phosphorus concentrations were observed (Figure 6). The stability of these coprecipitates is key to know the longevity of $\mathrm { P }$ immobilization achieved by La and Al treatments. The coprecipitation of DOM with metals was found to promote settling of organic carbon. The coprecipitation of organic compounds with iron (Fe) generates more organic-rich structures than the adsorption of DOM on iron oxides, where organic molecules are‘glued’ together by iron ions or nanophases of iron oxides (Lalonde et al., 2012). Precipitates of DOM and Al are subjected to microbial decay (Scheel et al., 2007) and resistance to decay decreases with less aromatic C content and smaller C/N. The protein-like substances including tyrosine-like and tryptophanlike components may serve as indicators of bioavailable DOM (Balcarczyk et al., 2009;Williams et al., 2010). As confirmed by the experimental data (as shown in Figure S3), about $60 \%$ of C2-ADOM was coprecipitated at a metal input of $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ during the $\mathrm { P }$ removal. The coprecipitates contained the bioavailable DOM of C2- ADOM, metal and P. Based on these, we assume that during P removal in eutrophic lakes by La/Al based treatments, the coprecipitates of DOM, Al/La and P could also be microbially degraded to some degree. Especially， coprecipitates containing the tryptophan-like component would be vulnerable. The stability of the coprecipitates and the longevity of La/Al-based treatment is a further interesting area for further study.

# 4. Conclusions

SRHA was dominated by polyphenol-like component $7 6 . 8 \%$ , C1-SRHA), while majority in ADOM was protein-like substance, including $4 1 . 9 \%$ tryptophan-like component (C2-ADOM） and $2 1 . 0 \%$ tyrosine-like component (C3-ADOM). Both SRHA and ADOM interacted with La/Al via two reactions. One is complexation and the other is coprecipitation. These two reactions always coexist. At low inputs of La/Al, interactions appear to be dominated by complexation processes， while at higher metal inputs, coprecipitation comes dramatically into play and levels of DOC decrease significantly. In general, compared to that of SRHA, the interaction of ADOM (both complexation and coprecipitation） with metal was weaker. But C2- ADOM was found have strong reactivity to complex with both La and Al, and to coprecipitate with La/Al. The complexation of SRHA and ADOM with metal reduced the efficiency of P removal. Meanwhile, the coprecipitation of C2-ADOM, metal and

P was assumed to decline the longevity of $\mathrm { P }$ removal based on the bioavailability character of tryptophan-like substance. The stability of coprecipitates containing the tryptophan-like component is a further interesting area for further study. Moreover, it is noted that, La ions was used in this study while in the real application,LMB is used. Although the active site/component of LMB for immobilize phosphorus is La ion, whether the coprecipitation is significant when using LMB is also needed to investigate further.

# Highlights

1. Complexation and coprecipitation occurred between SRHA/ADOM and La/Al.

2. A tryptophan-like component of ADOM complexed strongly with La.

3. The tryptophan-like component has strong ability to coprecipitate with La and Al.

4. The behaviors of PARAFAC components explained the inhibitory effect of SRHA/ADOM on P removal.

5. The stability of coprecipitates of DOM, Al/La and P should be noted.
