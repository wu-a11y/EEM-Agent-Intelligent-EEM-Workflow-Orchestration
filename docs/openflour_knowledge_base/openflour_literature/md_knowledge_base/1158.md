# Abstract

In order to understand the characteristics and dynamics of dissolved organic matter (DOM) in the sediment of rivers affected by impoundments, we examined the vertical profiles and the benthic fluxes of DOM in four diferent core sediments located at upstream sites of weirs in major rivers of South Korea. In three out of four sites, exponential accumulation of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) with depth was observed with the signature of seasonal variability. Except for the site displaying a below-detection limit of $\mathrm { F e ( I I ) }$ , the general accumulation trends of DOC with depth was concurrent with the increases of $\mathrm { F e ( I I ) }$ and $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ and the decrease of $\mathrm { P O } _ { 4 } ^ { 3 \cdot }$ ， signifying a close linkage of the DOM dynamics with anaerobic respiration via iron reduction, an important early diagenesis pathway. The estimated benthic fluxes from the cores revealed that the sediments likely serve as DOC, chromophoric DOM (CDOM), and fluorescent DOM (FDOM) sources to the overlying water. The benthic effluxes based on DOC were comparable to the ranges previously reported in lake and coastal areas,and those of CDOM and FDOM showed even higher levels. These findings imply that impoundment-affected river systems would change the DOM composition of the overlying water, ultimately influencing the subsequent water treatment processes such as disinfection byproducts production and membrane fouling. A simple mass balance model indicated that the impoundment-affected river sediments may operate as a net carbon sink in the environments due to a greater extent of sedimentation compared to the estimated benthic efflux and sediment biological respiration.

# 1. Introduction

Dissolved organic mater (DOM) in sediment pore waters plays a pivotal role in the global carbon cycle due to a large amount of the stored carbon and the benthic efflux of DOM to overlying water (Burdige et al., 1992; Burdige et al., 2004; Yang, et al., 2014; Chen and Hur, 2015). In both freshwater and marine environments, DOM in sediment pore waters exists in much higher levels compared to those of overlying waters (Chin and Gschwend, 1991; O'Loughlin and Chin, 2004; Ziegelgruber et al.， 2013; Burdige and Komada, 2015; Chen and Hur, 2015). The diference has been primarily attributed to the release of DOM during the degradation processes of particulate organic matter (POM) in sediments (Burdige and Komada, 2015; Burdige et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2016a) and/or to the reduction and dissolution of redoxsensitive minerals (Skoog et al.， 1996; Skoog and Arias-Esquivel, 2009). A recent report demonstrated using $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -labelled biodegradable organic compounds that microbial metabolism could mediate DOM-mineral interactions during re-mineralization of labile DOM, concomitant with the leaching of other non-labelled DOM (Hunter and Battn, 2016).   
Sediment DOM in inland freshwater ecosystems is considered an important compartment of global carbon budget because the carbon burial estimation is comparable to that of marine sediments (Cole et al., 2007; Tranvik et al., 2009). Anthropogenic activities (e.g., agriculture, industry,and urbanization) and/or the hydrologic alternation resulting from the construction of dams or weirs are known to exert great impacts on the trophic levels, sedimentation rates, and the quantity and the quality of DOM trapped in freshwater sediments (Hur, et al., 2007,2014; Nadon et al.,2015; Chen et al.，2016b). Considering that rivers are dubbed as the cradle of world civilization and are important water resources, their sustainable management should be of

particular interest with the main focus on water quality and related carbon and nutrient cycling. Although there is abundant literature on sediment DOM or riverine DOM, litle attention has been paid to downcore profiles of sediment DOM in rivers, the organic carbon flux, or related water quality issues, which are crucial especially under a warming climate due to increasing releases of carbon from sediments (Duan and Kaushal, 2013). R

There was an accelerating global trend in the construction of artificial impoundments over the last decades.Dams or weirs alter the hydrological flow as well as the way sediment is transported and the associated fluxes of carbon and nutrients. It was previously reported that the dams and weirs constructed along rivers worldwide have trapped substantial amounts of water and sediments,leading to upstream aggradation (Kondolf et al., 2014), and also that they have even resulted in sea level changes $\left( - 3 . 0 \ \mathrm { c m } \right)$ by impounding ${ \sim } 1 0 , 8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 }$ of water (Chao et al., 2008). A conservative estimate of sediments retained by these impoundment structures reached 4-5 Gt yr-1 (Vorosmarty et al., 2003)， corresponding up to ${ \sim } 2 5 { - } 3 0 \%$ of the total land-ocean transfer of sediment by rivers (15-20 Gt yr1，Walling, 2006). In addition, the impoundment of river waters may potentially pose water quality issues due to an increased in-situ primary productivity as well as the continuous release of DOM from sediments. As such, it is urgent to obtain further insight into the dynamics of sediment DOM in such impoundment-affected river ecosystems.

Due to the heterogeneous character of DOM, various types of analytical tools, including ultraviolet-visible (UV-Vis） and fluorescence spectroscopy, size exclusion chromatography (SEC), nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), and high resolution Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FT-ICR-MS), have been employed in characterizing DOM (Chin and Gschwend, 1991; Coble,1996; Hur et al., 2009; Chen et al., 2016c). Optical methods have the advantages of speed， high sensitivity，and solvent-free treatment. The optical active chromophoric DOM(CDOM) and fluorescent DOM (FDOM) have been widely and successfully used in tracing DOM dynamics in many ecosystems (Coble, 1996; McKnight et al.， 2001; Burdige et al.，2004; Maie et al.， 2007; Hur and Kim, 2009). The advent of parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) coupled with excitation emission matrix (EEM) has further advanced the usefulness of fluorescence spectroscopy for various environments (Stedmon et al., 2003; Cory and McKnight, 2005; Yamashita et al., 2010; Chen and Jaffe,2016).

Despite the presumably large amount of sedimentary DOM trapped by the impoundment structures worldwide, the studies of the DOM's characteristics and the dynamics are surprisingly sparse (Hur et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2016b). Enrichment of protein-like and microbial humiclike DOM and accumulation of bio-refractory DOM have been associated with dam-affected reservoirs (Hur et al.， 2014). In a study using size exclusion chromatography coupled with organic carbon detector (SEC-OCD),bulk DOC and high molecular sized humic substances generally co-varied with monthly precipitation, while smaller molecular weight fractions were found to be more abundant during dry seasons presumably due to in-reservoir photo- and biodegradation (Chen et al., 2016b). In an efort to fill the research gap for DOM dynamics as a function of depth as well as the benthic flux in impoundment-affected river sediments, we examined downcore profiles of sediment pore water DOM in the major river systems of South Korea. The study sites selected were 0.5 to $1 . 0 \ \mathrm { k m }$ upstream of large-scale weirs, in which the river water is not free-flowing or is partially stagnant, and thus the DOM sources are expected to be both allochthonous (transported from surrounding watersheds) and autochthonous origins (in situ primary productivity). The objectives of this study were to: (1） investigate the vertical profiles of DOM quantity and quality in the pore waters of river sediments affected by constructed impoundments,and (2) estimate the benthic flux at the water-sediment interface. The major focus of this work was to examine the vertical dynamics of DOM and the potential efflux in the sediments currently affected by weir impoundments, not to evaluate the changes of the U sediment DOM caused by the impoundments.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1 Site descriptions

There are four major rivers, including the Han, Nakdong, Geum, and Yeongsan Rivers, in South Korea, which serve as the main sources of drinking and irrigation water. In particular, the Han River flows through the capital metropolis, and it serves as the major drinking water source for more than 20 million people. From 2009 to 2011, sixteen weirs (or small dams)） were constructed as part of the so-called Four Major Rivers Restoration Project. The purpose was to enhance water resource management for flood control, irrigation, and power generation. All the weirs started to operate from 2O13,and water quality and flow rates are continuously monitored near the construction. For this study, four representative weirs in the mid- to down-stream of the rivers were selected from each major river system for sediment sampling, namely, Ipo weir in the Han River (HR), Gongju weir in the Geum River (GR), Gangjeong Goryeong weir in the Nakdong River (NR), and Juksan weir in the Yeongsan River (YR) (Fig.1). The multi-functional weirs are operated to maintain their designed water levels. The sampling sites are located about （204 $0 . 5 \mathrm { - } 1 . 0 \ \mathrm { k m }$ upstream of each weir. The river sections within $1 . 0 \ \mathrm { k m }$ upstream of weirs are expected to be river-reservoir systems due to the regulated hydrological regimes. The collcted sediments have been primarily built up after weir construction since the original river sediments were all dredged before the weirs were constructed. Summer stratification of the water column was observed at the NR site at ${ \sim } 1 \textrm { m }$ depth (Fig. S1a). The generally high chlorophyll $a$ (Chl a) concentrations in surface waters (with the ranges of $1 { - } 4 4 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ m } ^ { - 3 }$ ， $2 { \cdot } 1 1 7 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ m } ^ { - 3 }$ 心 $\mathsf { I } { - } 1 0 8 \ \mathrm { m g } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 }$ ， （204 $1 { - } 3 3 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 }$ and the monthly averages of $1 1 \pm 1 0 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 }$ ， $3 4 \pm 2 9 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 } , 4 0 \pm 2 3 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 }$ $1 2 \pm$ $8 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 }$ from 2013 to 2016 at sites of HR, GR, YR,and NR,respectively), monitored by the National Institute of Environmental Research (htp://water.nier.go.kr, Fig. S2), also imply an environment similar to typical river-reservoir systems. From the monitoring data of the aforementioned website, we also observed clear seasonal variations of Chl $a$ ， total organic carbon (TOC),and dissolved oxygen (DO) in the surface waters at the sampling sites, with general higher levels of Chl a and TOC in the wet season from June to September in contrast to the opposite trend for DO (Fig. S2-S3). However, Chl a and TOC at the YR site showed some exceptional trends with high levels in dry seasons (Fig. S2). The average annual precipitation is about $1 3 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m m } }$ in South Korea, with approximately two-thirds of the precipitation occurring in the summer monsoon season (June to September） according to data from the Korea Meteorological Administration (htps://www.kma.go.kr). Related land use information in the surrounding areas of the sites is available at the website (htps:/egis.me.go.kr) and is summarized in Table S1. The weekly- and quarterly- average water residence time (WRT) at the four sites, ranging from hours to days, showed a decreasing trend on the order of $\mathrm { N R } > \mathrm { Y R } > \mathrm { G R }$ $>$ HR based on the estimation using the water volume and the water flow rates measured at different time periods (Table S1). The WRT in the bottom layer of the NR site may be much longer than the estimation considering the observed summer stratification (Fig. S1a).

# 2.2 Sampling method

The samples were collected from July to August, 2016. Duplicate or triplicate sediment core samples were collected using acryl cores $( 6 \mathrm { c m i . d . } \times 2 5 \cdot$ cm length) by a professional scuba diver. The botom and top of the cores were capped with butyl rubber stoppers underwater, carefully retrieved onto the vessel, and cooled on ice until analyses in the laboratory. Separate surface sediments ( $( 0 { - } 2 \ \mathrm { c m } )$ ) were stored frozen at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until the measurements of TOC, total nitrogen (TN） contents,and Chl $a$ concentrations were made. The cores for measuring pore water constituents were transferred to an $\Nu _ { 2 }$ gas filled glove box, in which sub-samples were collected from 0-1 cm, $1 { - } 2 \ \mathrm { c m }$ ， $2 { - } 3 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ ， $3 { \mathrm { - } } 4 \ \mathrm { c m }$ $4 { - } 5 \ \mathrm { c m }$ , 5-6 cm, 6-8 cm and $8 { - } 1 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ depth intervals. The sediments were loaded into polypropylene centrifuge tubes in a $\Nu _ { 2 }$ -filled glove box. The tubes were tightly capped and centrifuged for $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ at $3 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ . After reintroduction into the glove box, pore water was sampled and filtered through a pre-cleaned $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ cellulose acetate syringe filter. The filtered pore waters samples were kept frozen until DOM analysis within two months. To determine the inorganic nutrient concentrations, aliquots of $1 { - } 2 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of pore water were fixed with saturated $\mathrm { H g } \mathrm { C l } _ { 2 }$ and then frozen until analysis. Aliquots of the pore water were acidified with $1 2 \mathrm { ~ N ~ }$ of HCl for ${ \mathrm { F e } } ( \mathrm { I I } )$ measurements and then stored in a refrigerator. HS" was precipitated with a $20 \%$ Zn acetate solution and then kept frozen at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for storage until further measurement.

Sediment porosity，density，and water contents were calculated based on the weight differences between the wet and the dried samples. The $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ concentration in pore water was measured by flow injection analysis with conductivity detection (Hall and Aler, 1992). The $\mathrm { N O } _ { \mathrm { x } } ^ { - }$ and $\mathrm { P O _ { 4 } } ^ { 3 - }$ concentrations in pore water were measured using an auto-analyzer (Proxima, Alliance, Inc.). Dissolved $\mathrm { F e ( I I ) }$ was determined using the colorimetric method with a ferrozine solution (Stookey, 1970).HS" and Chl $a$ were determined using the methylene blue method (detection limit: $0 . 0 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ ; Cline,1969) and the spectrophotometric method according to Parsons et al (1984), respectively.

Fluorescence EEMs were scanned with a Hitachi F-7000 luminescence spectrometer (Hitachi Inc.,Japan). The excitation/emission wavelengths were set at 200-500/280-550 nm. The excitation and emission slits were both $1 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . The scanning speed was set at $1 2 , 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m \ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ Excitation was at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ steps and emission at a $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ steps. Absorption spectra were scanned from 240 to $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ with a Shimadzu 1800 ultraviolet-visible (UV-Vis） spectrophotometer (Shimadzu Inc., Japan). A total organic carbon (TOC) analyzer (Shimazu TOC-VCPH) was used to measure DOC concentrations as non-purgeable organic carbon. All the samples were transferred into a capped cuvette in a nitrogen-filled glove box for the UV-Vis and EEMs measurements to minimize the redox potential changes.

The UV-Vis absorption data were used to calculate the Napierian absorption coefficient at 254 and $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ as follows (Blough and Del Vecchio, 2002):

$$
\mathrm { a _ { \lambda } } = 2 . 3 0 3 \times \mathrm { A _ { \lambda } } / \mathrm { p a t h l e n g t h }
$$

where ${ \bf a } _ { \lambda }$ is the absorption coefficient and $\mathbf { A } _ { \lambda }$ is the optical density. The absorption coefficient a254 reflects overall DOM absorption， whereas a35o is usually found to correlate with an abundance of lignin phenols (Benner and Kaiser,2011; Mann et al., 2016). The UV-Vis data were also applied for the inner filter effect correction. The correction procedures for postacquisition of EEMs data are available elsewhere (Chen et al., 2010). All the corrected DOM EEMs $( \mathfrak { n } \ ) \mathfrak { s } 3 \mathrm { ~ }$ ） were used for modeling. PARAFAC modeling was performed using the MATLAB (version 7.0.4) with the DOMFluor toolbox(Stedmon and Bro,2008). The number of components was determined based on the split-half validation. The corrected EEMs were then normalized by the integrated area of the Raman peak of daily measured Milli-Q water excited at $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Raman Unit: RU, Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009).

2.4 First-order exponential model for DOC and Fe(I) accumulation with depth

First-order DOC accumulation with depth is a common observation for marine sediments (Burdige and Komada, 2015; Chen et al., 2017). For this study, a simple first-order exponential equation (Eq. 2) was employed to fit the downcore trends of DOC and $\mathrm { F e ( I I ) }$ (OriginPro8） at most of our studied sites (GR, YR, and NR sites):

$$
\mathrm { C _ { d } } = \mathbf { a } \times \mathbf { e } ^ { ( \mathbf { k } \times \mathbf { d } ) } + \mathbf { b }
$$

where $\mathrm { C _ { d } }$ is the total concentration at depth (d), a and b are constants, e is the natural exponential constant, and k is the production constant with depth.

# 2.5 Benthic flux estimation

For benthic efflux estimation, Fick's first law of diffusion was used (Eq. 3) assuming the systems are diffusion-dominated and advections as wel as sediment resuspension are negligible:

$$
\mathbf { J } = \Phi _ { \mathrm { o } } \times \mathrm { D } _ { \mathrm { s } } \times ( \hat { \mathcal { X } } / \hat { \mathcal { O } } \mathbf { z } ) _ { \mathrm { o } }
$$

where J is the diffusion flux, $\Phi _ { \mathrm { { o } } }$ is the sediment porosity at sediment surface, $\mathrm { D } _ { \mathrm { s } }$ is the diffusion coefficient at water-sediment interface, and $( \overrightarrow { \mathrm { { o C } } } / \overrightarrow { \mathrm { { o z } } } ) _ { \mathrm { { o } } }$ is the concentration gradient at the watersediment interface. The concentration gradient was calculated by assuming $( \hat { \sigma } \mathbf { C } / \hat { \sigma } \mathbf { z } ) _ { 0 } \approx \Delta \mathbf { C } / \Delta \mathbf { x }$ in which $\Delta \mathrm { C }$ is the concentration in the top core section (at $1 \ \mathrm { c m }$ depth in this study) minus the concentration in the overlying water, and $\Delta \mathbf { x }$ is the mid-point of the section (i.e., $0 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ ,Burdige and Martens, 1990).

# 3.1 Sediment properties and water chemistry

The collected sediment cores are all $1 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ in length. The water depths at the four sampling sites were 2.1, 4.7, 4.6, and $1 0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ for HR,GR, YR, and NR,respectively (Table S2). The porosity of the surficial sediments was $0 . 7 4 , 0 . 8 6 , 0 . 7 9$ ， and 0.85, respectively (Fig. S1b). The porosity generally decreased with depth, reaching about 0.51, 0.39, 0.42, and 0.79 at $1 0 \ \mathrm { c m } \cdot$ depth for all four sampling sites. The DO levels in bottom waters showed suboxic to oxic conditions $( 1 . 7 – 8 . 0 \ \mathrm { m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ , Table S2) at the sampling time at the lower levels of the YR and NR sites (1.7 and $3 . 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ , respectively). Except for the HR site, the DOC levels showed increasing trends in upper sediments (Fig. 2). The details will be discussed in the following section. # 3.2 Comparison of DOM characteristics for different sites

Similarly, the absorption coefficients a254 and $\mathbf { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ were lowest at the HR site (average 66.O and $1 2 . 3 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ） and highest at the NR site (average 230.1 and $4 5 . 7 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ） $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ . Specific UV absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ (SUVA), an index of CDOM aromaticity (Weishaar et al., 2003), averaged 1.5, 1.5, 1.4, and $2 . 3 \mathrm { ~ L ~ } ( \mathrm { m g { - } C \ m } ) ^ { - 1 }$ at the sites of HR,GR, YR, and NR, respectively, with the highest range shown at the NR site $( p <$ 0.01). Absorption spectral slope ratio $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ : slope over 275-295 nm to slope over $3 5 0 { \cdot } 4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m } )$ isa proxy for molecular weight (MW) of CDOM with higher values associated with smaller MW (Helms et al., 2008). For this study, the higher values of $\mathrm { S _ { R } }$ (with the highest value ofup to 6.0 and an average of 2.3 at the HR site), compared with previous studies in a wetland and an ocean ${ \it \Omega } _ { \cdot } ( 0 . 8$ to 2.5,Helms et al., 2008) as well as in a coastal wetland ( ${ \sim } 0 . 9$ to 1.4, Chen et al., 2013), implies an episodic input of small MW CDOM in the site presumably due to algal blooms. The biological index (BIX) and humification index (HIX) are the proxies of recent autochthonous and biological contribution, and the degree of DOM humification, respectively (Huguet et al., 2009; Zsolnay et al. 1999). The much higher BIX( $( 1 . 5 \pm 0 . 5 )$ and lower HIX $( 0 . 8 \pm 0 . 2 )$ values at the HR site $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ support the considerable effects of algal blooms on the sediment pore water suggested from the $\mathrm { S _ { R } }$ data above.

For FDOM, four EEM-PRAFAC components were identified from the PARAFAC modeling (C1-C4, Fig. 4). C1 (Ex/Em maxima: $< 2 6 0$ (320)/440 nm) and C2 (Ex/Em maxima: $< 2 6 0 / 4 3 4$ nm） are traditionally termed terrestrial humic-like components, while C3 (Ex/Em maxima: 270/302 nm) and C4 (Ex/Em maxima: $3 0 0 / 3 3 8 \ \mathrm { \ n m }$ ） resemble free and bound protein-like components based on the spectral features previously reported (Cory and McKnight, 2005; Yamashita et al., 2010; Chen et al., 2010). C4 is typically observed in marine environments with abundant autochthonous origins (Stedmon et al., 2007; Murphy et al., 2008). The four identified components were slightly different from those found in the pore waters in the tributaries of the same watersheds, in which the terrestrial humic-like component was red-shifted compared to the C2 component of this study (Chen et al., 2015). The difference may reflect more solar irradiation effects exerted on the river-reservoir systems since this C2 has been reported to be a photoproduct and/or photo-refractory component (Stedmon et al., 2007; Chen et al., 2010; Chen and Jaffé, 2016). Similar to the DOC and CDOM trends, the terrestrial humic-like C1 was lowest at the HR site ( $[ 1 . 8 \pm 0 . 8 \mathrm { { R U } } ]$ , while it was highest at the NR site ( $1 2 . 5 \pm 4 . 0$ RU, $p < 0 . 0 1$ , Table S2).For the terrestrial humic-like C2, the values at the NR site were one to two orders of magnitude higher than those of the other sites ( $1 3 . 1 \pm 1 1 . 6$ vs. 0.2-0.5 RU). For protein-like C3, the value at the NR site also illustrated the highest level ( $\left. 1 2 . 8 \pm 1 0 . 3 \mathrm { ~ R ~ } \right.$ U) compared to the other sites (average values of 3.8-5.4 RU). The average values of protein-like C4 ranged from 4.3 to 6.5 RU with no significant differences among the four sites. The relative abundance of FDOM components,calculated with the percentage value of each component relative to the sum of entire components in the $\mathrm { F } _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ intensity，can represent the relative composition of FDOM. Although the relative abundance of one FDOM component depends on the changes of other components, the relative comparison among different sites is reasonable. The DOC generally increased with depth in Zigzag patterns (Fig. 2), consistent with the general increase with depth reported (below oxic-anoxic interface at ${ \sim } 7 \ \mathrm { c m }$ ） in a previous study on a lacustrine ecosystem (Fu et al., 2O06) and also suggesting strong seasonal variations in impoundment-affected river system (Kim et al., 2000), which were also observed in the water column for TOC, DO,and Chl a (Fig. S2-S3). It is more reasonable to infer that the downcore pattern corresponds to seasonal variations ( ${ \sim } 3$ -yrs) rather than to other temporal scales because all sediments had been dredged before weir construction in 2013 and the current sediments have been primarily built up since then (see section 2.1). The major factors shaping the seasonal Variations are probably associated with the strong seasonal variations in allochthonous inputs ascribed to the seasonal precipitation pattrn as well as the seasonality of in situ primary productivity. Approximately two-thirds of the precipitation occurring in the summer monsoon season from June to September in South Korea, which brings substantial loads of allochthonous inputs from surrounding watersheds (Chen et al.， 2016b). Moreover， the seasonality of allochthonous inputs can lead to higher sedimentation rates of POM in the summer monsoon season. Higher sedimentation rates of POM from in situ primary productivity is also expected in spring and summer growing season. Ultimately, DOM is likely to be released from POM into sediment porewaters. Although microbial degradation of labile DOM and the diffusion driven by concentration gradients are in part responsible for shaping the current depth profiles of DOM, non-steady could be maintained for the depth profiles in the weir-affected river systems due to the presumed fast sedimentation rates. Similarly， FI values illustrated general increasing downcore trends, indicating relatively more algal and/or microbial fulvic acid sources with sediment accumulation (McKnight et al., 2001), which might be caused by relatively higher algal inputs in deeper, i.e., “older", sediments. Absorption coefficients $\mathbf { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $\mathbf { a } _ { 3 5 0 }$ also demonstrated Zigzag shapes but without the general increasing trends as observed in a previous study (Fu et al., 2006), which might be caused by the greater contribution of algal and/or microbial fulvic acid source (so less terrestrial source) with depth as evidenced by the above-mentioned FI downcore profile. The general decreasing SUVA downcore profiles also support our inference for there being less terrestrial DOM sources with depth. The SUVA values at the HR and YR sites showed the maxima at ${ \sim } 1 . 5 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ depth, which might indicate an oxic-anoxic interface according to a previous study on lake sediments (Fu et al., 2006). The SUVA maxima were not observed at the GR and NR sites, suggesting the presence of oxic-anoxic interfaces at either depths shallower than $0 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ or at depths deeper than 10 cm. Considering the $\mathrm { F e ( I I ) }$ concentrations at the two sites (i.e., GR and NR), the former scenario seems more plausible, especially for the NR site where the bottom water DO reached $3 . 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ (Table S2). Although Fu et al (2006) reported the minima of DOC around the oxic-anoxic interface in a lacustrine ecosystem, such a trend was not pronounced for this study probably due to the strong seasonal efects in rivers. The absolute and relative abundances of FDOM components generally exhibited zigzag downcore shapes likely due to seasonal variations (Fig. 4 and Fig. S4).

For example, sulfate reduction instead of iron reduction was found to be a prevailing anaerobic respiration pathway during early diagenesis in marine sediments (Capone and Kiene, 1988; Chen et al., 2016a). Summer storms can flush a large amount of terrestrial sediments from surrounding watersheds into rivers,while autochthonous primary productivity tends to be elevated during the monsoon period (Kim et al., 2000). The superimposed seasonal variation, interfering with the DOM signature in the pore water by biological processes, might be partially responsible for the relative low $\mathtt { R } ^ { 2 }$ values (0.23 to 0.74) for the DOC data fitting to the first-order exponential model. 人

Although general increasing trends of DOC with depth have been commonly reported for sediments, the observation may be the case for anoxic environments only because the opposite trend was observed above the oxic-anoxic interface (Fu et al., 2006). Therefore, the production of DOC is most likely caused by anaerobic respiration (e.g., Fe(II) or sulfate reduction) of POM, which is supported by the concurrent production of $\mathrm { F e ( I I ) }$ and the general positive correlations between DOC and $\mathrm { F e ( I I ) }$ (Fig. 3 and Fig. 5). The amounts of DOC released from POM might be smaller than those consumed via respiration under aerobic conditions,and vice versa for anaerobic environments. It might also be associated with the release of DOM scavenged by $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 3 + }$ oxides or $\mathrm { M n ( I V ) }$ minerals during the reduction to Fe(II) or Mn(II). However, the exact reasons for this phenomenon are unclear at this stage and merit further investigation.

# 4. Conclusions

Here we observed the increasing trends of DOC with depth, concurrent with those of ${ \mathrm { F e } } ( \operatorname { I I } )$ at most of the studied sites with river sediments affected by constructed impoundments. Despite the potential superimposition of seasonal variations, it is interesting to observe the exponential variation of DOC with depth for the river-reservoir systems, similar to the previous reports on some marine sediment. Production of DOC in the sediments potentially occur via a biological pathway through POM anaerobic respiration using Fe(II) and/or sulfates as terminal electron acceptors, which was well supported by the observation of co-accumulation of DOC and $\mathrm { F e ( I I ) }$ and the positive correlations between the two. Our study revealed that the Fe(III) reduction might play a greater role in DOM production from the impoundment-affected river sediments, which was in contrast to marine sediments displaying more dominant roles of sulfate reduction. With the increasing human disturbances on river systems such as increased nutrient input and dam and weir construction, higher CDOM and FDOM efflux into the water column and more organic carbon burial in the impoundments-affected riparian systems are expected. Although rivers and other inland waters only occupy a smaller fraction of the Earth's surface area, the impoundmentaffected ecosystems may exert great impacts on the freshwater water quality by trapping a large amount of sediments and organic matter and subsequent benthic eflux to the water column.
