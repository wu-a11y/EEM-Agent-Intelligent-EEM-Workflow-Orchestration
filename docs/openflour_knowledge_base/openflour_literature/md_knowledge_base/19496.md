Elevated non-volatile dissolved organic carbon (NVDOC) concentrations in groundwater (GW) monitoring wells underoil-contaminated hydrophobic soils originating from a pipeline rupture at the National Crude Oil Spill & Natural Atenuation Research Site near Bemidji, MN are documented.We hypothesized the elevated NVDOC is comprised of water-soluble photooxidation products transported from the surface to the aquifer.We use field and laboratory samples in combination with complementary analytical methods to test this hypothesis and determine the biological response to these products. Observations from optical spectroscopy and ultrahigh-resolution mass spectrometry reveal a significant correlation between the chemical composition of NVDOc leached from photochemically weathered soils and GW monitoring wels with high NVDOC concentrations measured in the aquifer beneath the contaminated soil. Conversely,the chemical composition from the uncontaminated soil photoleachate matches the NVDOC observed in the uncontaminated wels. Contaminated GW and photodisolution leachates from contaminated soil activated biological targets indicative of xenobiotic metabolism and exhibited potential for adverse efects.Newly formed hydrocarbon oxidation products (HOPs)from fresh oil could be distinguished from those downgradient.This study illustrates another pathway for dissolved HOPs to infiltrate GW and potentially affect human health and the environment.

# 1．Introduction

Petroleum hydrocarbons (particularly polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons or PAHs) are toxic and carcinogenic causing short- and longterm impacts to human health and the environment [1-3].Biotic and abiotic processes strongly influence the fate and transport of petroleum hydrocarbons in surface waters [4-8],transforming them into complex polar hydrocarbon oxidation products (herein referred to as HOPs) that reside in the unresolved complex mixture [9].Dissolved HOPs are the fraction formed through these degradation processes that can readily partition into the aqueous phase and be transported long distances from their hydrocarbon source [1O-l2]. Soil contaminated with petroleum contains both low and high molecular weight petroleum hydrocarbons, which,like other sources of organic matter,can undergo chemical oxidation, forming HOPs [1,l3].Previous studies show the presence of HOPs after petroleum undergoes photochemical oxidation [1O,13,14].

Once in the environment,HOPs become bioavailable,posing a risk to human health and aquatic ecosystems. Studies have shown HOPs are more toxic than their parent compounds [1,l5-17].Therefore,it is important to focus on the formation,transport,and fate of HOPs in petroleum contaminated soil and how hydrological conditions and precipitation mobilize them into groundwater (GW).HOPs formation from abiotic processes in soil and subsequent transport into GW represents a previously undocumented pathway for exposure from spilled petroleum hydrocarbons residing in surface soils [18-23].In this study, we provide data supporting theories brought up in previous work by Thorn and Aiken that a fourth pathway of oil degradation exists at the Bemidji site [24].The fourth pathway,photodegradation,is for the oil present only in the surface soil over an area of about $7 5 0 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ called the spray zone (Fig.1） [25].Here,we provide evidence supporting their hypothesis and show that HOPs migrating into the GW activate human nuclear receptors.

Numerous studies show photodissolution of soil organic matter soil, humic-type materials,wood,plants,and sediment, herein referred to as particulate organic matter (POM),contributing dissolved organic matter into surrounding aqueous environments [21,23,26,27]． The main transformations governing the photodissolution of POM into nonvolatile dissolved organic carbon (NVDOC) are due to changes in chemical composition and molecular weight from biotic and/or abiotic oxidation processes [20-23,26]. The mechanism of POM desorption occurs during photolysis when high molecular weight POM is converted into lower molecular weight POM[28].The decrease in molecular weight induces a partitioning between the dissolved state and the adsorbed state,as shown by previous reports [29,3O].Mayer,Schick, Skorko and Boss [21] examined the photodissolution of POM into NVDOC in sediment collected from the Mississippi River.They observed a loss in POMwith a concurrent increase in NVDOC as a function of irradiation time [21]. Similar phototransformation processes were observed when soil organic matter was exposed to sunlight [21,27,31,32].Here we extend these studies to evaluate photodissolution of petroleum hydrocarbons from contaminated soil.

Soils containing petroleum hydrocarbons may be exposed to sunlight for several reasons.These include residual spilled oil left in place at the surface based on a low-risk finding,application of drilling wastes on agricultural land; or use of land farming for remediation.Land farming for the treatment of petroleum contaminated soil has been used since the 1980 s [33].Recommendations for land treatment include monitoring downgradient GW for water-soluble petroleum constituents [34]. However, these monitoring recommendations were developed for gas chromatography (GC)-amenable hydrocarbons [35] before recognition that partial HOPs are commonly associated with weathering of spilled petroleum hydrocarbons e.g.[e.g.11,24].The purpose of this study is to document the photo-transformation,dissolution,and mobilization of HOPs generated from petroleum contaminated soil and to evaluate the potential biological effects of these HOPs.

Former studies have linked early life stage fish toxicity to the sum of polyaromatic hydrocarbons $( \sum P A H )$ from the water-soluble fraction of crude oil e.g [36-39].However, there are conflicting reports on the issues associated with the conclusion that $\textstyle \sum P A H$ are the appropriate indicator for crude oil toxicity [4O].Meador and Nahrgang published a review urging researchers to consider the uncharacterized aqueous fraction as contributors to toxic responses especially the more polar compounds [36].They also state there are not enough experiments focusing on the toxicological response of the polar fraction of water soluble fractions of crude oil. Our goal is to contribute to this knowledge gap by focusing on the aqueous fraction collected in the field at an oil spill site and compare to the aqueous fraction produced in the laboratory to test whether or not they activate human nuclear receptors.

The objectives of this work are to (1) photogenerate NVDOC in a controlled laboratory setting from contaminated and uncontaminated soil over water as a function of irradiation time,(2) compare optical and molecular level composition of the NVDOC obtained from GW monitoring wells at the site with those produced in the laboratory,and (3) characterize biological activity and relative potency of the leachate and well samples via nuclear and G-protein coupled receptor,and transcription factor bioactivity screening.We hypothesize that the chemical composition of the NVDOC measured in the contaminated wells will match the chemical composition of the NVDOC measured from the contaminated soil photoleachate.Knowledge gained from this study will increase our understanding of the risk to human health and the environment when HOPs are transported into aquifers and other water systems at sites with oil contaminated soil.

# 2．Materials and methods

# 2.1． Site history

At a long-term crude oil study site in northern Minnesota,USA, spilled crude oil has been present in surface soils for over 40 years. On August 20,1979,a pipeline ruptured and released about 1.7 million liters of oil in a glacial outwash plain.Oil accumulated on the land surface and drained to a wetland to the south of the ruptured pipeline.A “spray zone"was created during the spill where oil sprayed over an area of about $7 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ [41].Initial cleanup efforts included excavating contaminated sediments,using vacuum trucks to recover oil pooled in trenches,and burning the spray zone to remove residual oil [42,43]. The excavated oily sediments (more than 1o,ooo cubic yards) were thinly spread in a 6-8 in.layer across about 15 acres of adjacent land and on the right-of-way of the pipeline [42,43].After initial clean-up efforts, two important mechanisms were documented that transported contaminants away from the source: evaporation [27] and dissolution of oil components [44,45]．After decades of biodegradation,a plume of elevated NVDOC comprising of partial hydrocarbon biodegradation products continues to expand in GW [46].Here we propose that photodegradation is another mechanism by which the solubility of oil components is increased and contributes to a GW NVDOC plume.

Today,parts of the spray zone and other locations where oily sediments were spread on the land surface are still sparsely vegetated and highly eroded.In these locations,the surficial soil is visibly contaminated with oil and has varying degrees of hydrophobicity or water repellency [47,48]．The water repellence is caused by residual oil coating the sand grains [47].Water-repellant soils reduce infiltration and increase runoff and erosion compared to wettable soils.In one experiment,runoff coefficients in water-repellant soils were 16 times higher compared to wettable soils [49].Water repellency also causes water to penetrate into the soil through preferential flow paths,which can lead to more rapid transport of solutes to GW compared to homogenous wetting of soils [5O]. The water table below the spray zone ranges from about $4 . 8 – 7 . 3  { \mathrm { m } }$ below the land surface.In 1998,Thorn and Aiken(1998） hypothesized that elevated NVDOC concentrations in wells beneath the spray zone was from photodissolution of the oil contaminated soil [24]. This study aims to link the high NVDOC concentrations in the wells beneath the spray zone to the NVDOC generated from the photodissolution of contaminated soil collected from the spray zone.Despite the soil hydrophobicity，we expect photodissolution mechanisms to be like those found for other organic carbon sources in the environment i.e.POM.We will also examine whether the high NVDOCvaluesmeasuredinmonitoringwellsbeneath petroleum-contaminated soils are from HOPs.

# 2.2. Site description and sample collection

In August 1979,a pressurized oil pipeline ruptured,spraying 440,00o gallons of crude oil over 1O acres of land in a remote location near Bemidji, MN, USA [25,41].About $7 5 \%$ of the oil was removed by pumping and burning,The remaining oil was to in the subsurface or excavated and spread on land surface to degrade through natural attenuation [41].In 1983,the United States Geological Survey (USGS)

established a long-term study of the fate of the spilled oil and eventually the site becameknown as The National Crude Oil Spill Fate and Natural Attenuation Research Site near Bemidji,MN, USA (herein referred to as the Bemidji site; https://mn.water.usgs.gov/projects/bemidji/). By 1998,hundreds of wells and testholes had been installed to evaluate the migration and degradation of hydrocarbons in the subsurface.The degradation of hydrocarbons has led to contaminated GW plumes of elevated NVDOC concentrations below and downgradient from the oil source zone [45,46,51,52].Throughout this paper,NVDOC is used rather than DOC to match previous studies at the Bemidji site [46,51,53, 54] in which the NVDOC measurement technique does not capture the volatile fraction of DOC.For this paper, the samples were collected 39 years after the spill(2O18) within the spray zone area of the Bemidji site (Fig.1). Information regarding oil composition can be found elsewhere [25].

The uncontaminated (control) soil samples were collected from a location that was vegetated, exhibited no water repellency (Fig.1) [48], and did not have a petroleum hydrocarbon smell.The contaminated soil samples were collected within an area containing severe water repellency [48],sparse vegetation,and apparent erosion (Fig.1). Samples were collected by removing the top $5 \mathrm { c m }$ of surface soil in a $2 0 \times 2 0 \mathrm { c m }$ area,followed by the collection of soil up to $2 0 \mathrm { c m }$ deep to ensure no previous sunlight exposure.Soil samples were stored in the dark at $0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until processing.

Groundwater samples were collected from 12 wells ranging in distance and depth from the spill site (Fig.1,circles).Detailed maps and site descriptions can be found in these papers [25,46,51,53,54] and well construction information is available in this data release [55].Wells were purged and samples were collected as described in previous studies [44] and summarized briefly here.Wells were purged at rates of less than one gallon per minute with a submersible 12-volt stainless steel Proactive SS Hurricane XL pump outfitted with polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) tubing.Samples were collected after purging at least three well volumes and stable field parameters (pH,dissolved oxygen, temperature,and specific conductance) were achieved.Field parameters were measured ina flow-through chamber ona YSI 682O V2 sonde.Samples were filtered through pre-combusted ( $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ 4h) $0 . 2 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ glass microfiber filters (Advantec) and stored in the dark at $0 \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C }$ until analysis.

# 2.3.Laboratory photodissolution procedure

# 2.3.1． Soil preparation

The laboratory experiment was set up based on the design described in previous studies [18,56].Briefly, soil samples were dried at $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ prior to being ground and sifted to increase surface area.After sifting，there were observable differences in each soil in that the contaminated soil was darker and smelled like hydrocarbons (See Supporting Fig.S1). All $8 0 0 \mathrm { m } \mathrm { g }$ of the contaminated soil floated over the water due to its hydrophobicity (Fig. S2b) compared to the uncontaminated soil (Fig. S2a).

# 2.3.2. Irradiation procedure

Jacketed beakers were thermostatically controlled with a circulating water bath keptat $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ Samples were subjected to simulated sunlight from 24 to $2 4 0 \ \mathrm { h }$ in triplicate batches for each period in a solar simulator (Atlas Suntest $\mathrm { C P S + }$ ，Atlas Material Testing Technology LLC).The irradiance was set at $7 6 5 \mathrm { W / m ^ { 2 } }$ ，which provided exposure conditions with one hour equivalent to approximately 6 days of noon sunlight (Bemidji irradiance $6 1 7 \mathrm { W } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ [https://www.solarenergylocal.com/sta tes/minnesota/bemidji/]) [57]. The solar simulator is calibrated annually by an external service.A batch of triplicate dark controls (zero light exposure) were also conducted on the control and spray zone soil at the longest time period of $2 4 0 \ \mathrm { h }$ ；these samplesare $\mathrm { \Delta T } = 0$ in all figures and data interpretations.The dark control beakers were wrapped in aluminum foil twice and kept inside a dark drawer. Once time frames were completed on the triplicate light and dark samples,the water was quantitatively measured and brought to $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ with nanopure water to correct for anyunwanted evaporation (if needed).Water samples were subsequently filtered through a pre-combusted $( 4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ 4h) $0 . 2 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ glass microfiber filter (Advantec GF75) and stored in the dark at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until analysis.All nanopure water throughout the experiment was from an Aeries nanopure system.

# 2.5.Optical analysis

Twenty-five laboratory generated and aquifer samples were adjusted to $\tt p H 8$ (as necessary） for excitation emission matrix spectroscopy measurements on a Horiba Aqualog fluorimeter (Horiba Scientific, Kyoto,Japan） [60]．A $1 0 \mathrm { m m }$ quartz cuvette was used to generate measurements at constant room temperature.Weekly checks were run to test instrument stability prior to analysis using a sealed water cell (Starna Raman Ultra-Pure Water,Certified Reference material $\#$ $\mathrm { R M } { \cdot } \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } ) ]$ to determine the Raman peak of water.Blanks were checked daily prior to sample collection.The excitation and emission wavelengths were scanned from 240 to $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ and $2 \mathrm { n m }$ increments, respectively. To reduce inner-filter effects (IFEs),nanopure water was used for dilution-correction to $A _ { 2 5 4 \mathrm { n m } } = 0 . 1$ as previously reported in Zito et al.,2O19 [56],Parallel Factor (PARAFAC) analysis was conducted to determine the underlying spectral properties of the excitation emission matrix (EEM) spectroscopy fluorescence spectra (Fig.S4) [61]. To correct for any remaining IFEs,a nanopure water blank was subtracted to remove instrumentbias,and the EEM spectral intensities were corrected for Rayleigh and Raman scattering during PARAFAC analysis [62].PARAFAC analysis was performed using drEEM and MATLAB [61, 63].PARAFAC components were uploaded onto the OpenFluor [64] database ata similarity threshold greater than $9 8 \%$ and compared to previously reported DOM datasets from other studies.

# 2.6. Ultrahigh resolution mass spectrometry

# 3.1．Photodissolution of oil contaminated soil produces high NVDOC concentrations

Nonvolatile dissolved organic carbon concentrations generated from the control (black) and spray zone (red) photodissolution microcosms are shown in Fig.2.NVDOC concentrations from the control soil leachate show a slight increase over time (Pearson's coeficient $\mathbf { r } = 0 . 4 0 3$ ）plateauing at $7 . 5 \pm 1 . 4 \mathrm { \ m g C / L }$ .The control soil leachate exhibited an overall weak,insignificant $( \mathbf { p } = 0 . 3 7 0 )$ ,positive correlation between NVDOC and time irradiated.Increases in NVDOC from the control soil corroborates previous studies in the DOM literature that POM contributes to elevated NVDOC concentrations as a result of photooxidation [20,21,26,27].In contrast, NVDOC concentrations in the leachate from the spray zone soil significantly increased (Pearson's coefficient $\mathbf { r } = 0 . 9 7 4$ $\mathbf { p } = 0 . 0 0 0 2 \mathrm { \mathrm { , } }$ from $6 . 1 \pm 0 . 6 \mathrm { { m g C / L } }$ to $5 1 . 6 \pm 1 . 2$ $\scriptstyle { \mathrm { m g C / L } }$ with irradiation time.Fig.2 shows that more organics in the spray zone soil are mobilized compared with the soil collected from the control site.Photodissolution from soil and POM is well reported; these findings support previous research in that oil residue on soil particles is photolabile 4O years after the pipeline rupture at the Bemidji site. Further, these results uncover additional transport mechanisms of HOPs through oil contaminated soil into GW.

Table 1 lists the NVDOC concentrations from wells located in the spray zone (contaminated) and outside the spray zone (uncontaminated).The areas within the extent of the severely water- repellant soils in the spray zone are sparsely vegetated and show evidence of substantial erosion (Fig.1) [47,48].The sandy soils in these areas are water repellent because the sand grains are coated with hydrophobic petroleum hydrocarbons that may have formed during early remediation efforts that included burning [47,72,73]. During rain events (see MP4 Video with supplemental information), the severely water repellant soils are resistant to infiltration and water quickly accumulates at the soil surface and begins to flow downslope toward the low-lying area marked in Fig.1.This surface runoff erodes the surface layer of hydrophobic sand and deposits it lower on the hillside or at the base of the hill in the low-lying area (Fig.1).The entire hillside is not severely water repellant [48] and preferential flow paths are known to develop in water repellant soils [5O],which means some water infiltrates into the soil on the hillside.During major precipitation events,runoff water accumulates in the low-lying area and focused recharge to the underlying aquifer occurs (Fig.1)[74]. The variability in soil-water repellency of the spray zone area promotes the transport of phototransformed petroleum hydrocarbons to GW.With each erosion event,a thin layer of soil is removed and a fresh underlying layer of sand grains coated in petroleum hydrocarbons is exposed to the sun.This cyclic process provides an ongoing source of hydrocarbons that are exposed to the sun and phototransformedintooxygenatedoilcompounds.Afterphototransformation，the hydrocarbons are water soluble and can be transported with water that infiltrates into the subsurface of the spray zone.The high concentrations and distinct composition of NVDOC in the lysimeters and the wells of the spray zone indicate that this is a plausible flow path for ongoing contributions of petroleum-derived NVDOC to GW.

Distinct differences between the chemical composition of the NVDOC in the contaminated versus uncontaminated soil photoleachates and well samples were revealed by UHR-MS.The UHR-MS data were used to assign a percentage of assigned formulae to each well and laboratory sample for CA(condensed aromatics),aromatic,UHO (unsaturated high oxygen),ULO,(unsaturated low oxygen） and aliphatic compositional classes (Table S1).A PCA model using loadings derived from UHR-MS data for laboratory photoleachate (circles） and well (diamond) samples explains $8 8 \%$ of the variance (Fig.4).As with the EEMs data, the major driver of variance within PCA space was whether samples contained HOPs (contaminated) versus those without HOPs (uncontaminated).For the wells (diamonds),the HOPs-contaminated samples have reduced (aliphatic) molecular features whereas the uncontaminated samples have molecular features related to more oxidized (unsaturated high oxygen） compositional classes.It is known that NVDOC changes in chemical composition with proximity to the oil source zone along the main GW plume transect at the Bemidji site [45]. # 4.Conclusion

These data provide insight on the phototransformation processes occurring when petroleum contaminated soil is exposed to sunlight. The possibility of photomobilization of HOPs is mentioned in Chapter 4 of ITRC[111] but without references.This study provides the first document occurrence of the effect [111].A combination of UHR-MS and optical data reveal clear compositional differences in the HOPs produced by phototransformation from contaminated versus uncontaminated soil in a laboratory solar simulator.Monitoring wells and suction lysimeters samples with elevated NVDOC concentrations provide an opportunity to track the transport of HOPs into the aquifer in the spray zone.The compounds produced in the laboratory are like those found in the unsaturated zone and in the GW below the contaminated soils, providing evidence that newly formed HOPs migrate vertically through the soil to the GW.Our observations indicate that erosion may be a factor in removing the photobleached soil layer after precipitation events.The exposure of fresh contaminated soil is a cyclic process contributing to the high NVDOC concentrations observed in the underlying aquifer.

The knowledge gained from this study provides insight into a new formation and transport pathway for HOPs from oil contaminated soil. Furthermore,this study provided evidence showing compounds generated in soil leachate photodissolution experiments exerted similar types and magnitudes of biological activity as the contaminated wells collected from the field.The results of this paper suggest that oil from terrestrial spills remaining at the land surface may provide an ongoing source of mobile HOPs to adjacent GW or surface water.This result has implications for the strategy of landfarming of crude-oil-contaminated soil.Some of the existing oil at the surface at the study site that is an ongoing source of HOPs was spread as part of a land-farming remedial strategy in the initial 1979 spill response.However,the fate of HOPs afterphotodissolution into GW is poorly known and methods for evaluating risk of HOPs are in flux.A discussion of existing methods to evaluate the risk of HOPs is provided in Chapter6 of (ITRC,2018)[111]. More research is needed to assess the bioavailability,fate,and toxicity of HOPs entering the wells under the contaminated surface soils.

# Environmental Implication

NVDOC leached from degraded petroleum is of emerging concern because its effects on human health and the environment are primarily unknown. Petroleum-contaminated soil undergoes environmental weathering processes such as photooxidation and erosion that form and mobilize water-soluble HOPs into underlying aquifers.Precipitation events erode the“photobleached’soil revealing“fresh”oil contaminated soil, thereby repeating the process.These cyclic processes,provide ample source material to contribute high NVDOC concentrations of HOPs,into the underlying aquifer.HOPs exhibit biological activity measured by bioassays,an indication of their potential risk to human health and the environment.
