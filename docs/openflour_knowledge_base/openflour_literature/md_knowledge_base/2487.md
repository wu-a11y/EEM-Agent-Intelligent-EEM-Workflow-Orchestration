# a b s t r a c t

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is a crucial driver in ecosystem services and a central part of the carbon transport and biological cycle in land-sea interaction. DOM exhibits characteristic environmental behavior in the coastal zone, but its sustainability is affected by expanding artificial topography (AT) construction. It requires combining analyses on AT-induced response of field fluorescent DOM (fDOM) and its quenching pattern under metal-complexation. Herein, we conducted systemic investigations into the spatiotemporal dynamics of fDOM compositions with further in-lab verification to study its Cu-binding capacity. We detected three humid-like fDOM components sensitive to AT. The total fDOM intensity was positively correlated with low molecular weight organic acid (LMWOA) extractable Cu and the Cu pools in above-ground biomass. The enriched fDOM serves as an ecological engineer by increasing the Cu mobility, confirmed by an in-lab fluorescence titration. The application of LMWOA greatly enhanced the intensity of one fDOM component, elevated its conditional stability constant, and decreased its quenched proportion, implying that LMWOA might extract part of Cu from fDOM complexation. The present work provides an “fDOM-LMWOA pump” explanation to suggest that fDOM is a novel ecological regulator on vegetation growth under the AT-induced matter accumulation.

# 1. Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) represents complex biogeochemical processes. As such, it reflects various environmental factors (Lehmann and Kleber, 2015) and plays a crucial and independent role in global ecosystem functionality (Prairie, 2008). DOM serves as the energy source for microbial activity (Ward et al., 2017), and therefore affects some critical ecological functions in aquatic ecosystems (Stanley et al., 2012). Microbial-enzyme modeling demonstrates that inputs with higher ratio of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) to sediment organic carbon (SOC) will attenuate the long-term organic carbon shortage under the influence of global warming (Allison et al., 2010). Sediments with higher DOC contents are also expected to release more greenhouse gases like $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ and ${ \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ into the atmosphere (X. Liu et al., 2017). Furthermore, DOM influences the bioavailability of nutrients and pollutants, which further impacts competition and biome survival. In S-rich anoxic environments, DOM can sequester arsenic and decrease its mobility through formation of covalent bonds with its organic sulfur groups (Langner et al., 2011). Thus, understanding DOM's driving factors provides deeper insights into critical environmental processes and aids in developing practical monitoring tools for ecosystem function promotion, water quality control, and climate change issues (Stanley et al., 2012).

Coastal ecosystems have been subjected to extreme impacts from human activities during the Anthropocene. Coastal communities require more land to support their large population densities and rapid urban expansion, and reclamation is, at times, the last resort. Reclamation projects in inter-tidal areas usually involve creating artificial topography (AT) in blocking the tide-derived matter transportation in exchange for land acquisition. Thus, coastal reclamation has raised many concerns regarding the ecosystems' sustainability (Ma et al., 2014; Huang et al., 2015). Direct ramifications of coastal reclamation include interruption (Li et al., 2014) of the natural physical environment—like hydrodynamics (Kuang et al., 2013), sediment trapping efficiency (Willemsen et al., 2016), and matter distribution (Yang et al., 2017, 2016; Cui et al., 2012; Hong et al., 2018); as well as ecological influences like habitat loss and growth strategy change (Hong et al., 2017). In addition, comprehensive land use continues to impact further pedogenesis in the reclamation area (Cui et al., 2012; Peng et al., 2013; Hung et al., 2013; Ding et al., 2017). Despite the current knowledge on the reclamation's environmental consequences, its specific impact on the DOM pool and properties is still poorly understood, particularly with respect to AT construction, which might have an individual impact different from blocking seawater inundation. Because development of artificial coastal flood protection is advocated to combat the rising flood risks due to climate change (Vousdoukas et al., 2018), the ramifications associated with AT still need to be fully clarified.

Due to its broad prospects and ease of application in long-term, routine monitoring, developments in technology to measure and decipher DOM's spectral characteristics have dramatically facilitated the exploration of DOM behavior from terrestrial water bodies to the deep ocean. By analyzing DOM's absorption and fluorescence spectrum, its important carbon-flux properties, e.g., molecular weight, composition, source, and processing (Bao et al., 2018), have been portrayed from a local to global scale. Moreover, fluorescent DOM (fDOM) characteristics have been applied to address some specific current concerns—such as allocation of pollutants (Liu et al., 2019), the binding affinity of hydrophobic organic contaminants (Hur et al., 2014) and heavy metals (Wu et al., 2011), identifying the contribution of marine biological pumps (Zhao et al., 2017), as well as tracing the estuarine influence (GonzálezOrtegón et al., 2018). Several previous studies have also demonstrated that land-use change and urbanization account for changes in the fDOM pool (Parr et al., 2015). However, ramifications of AT on the DOM pool's spatiotemporal drivers and its potential ecological functions, are still not sufficiently understood. Furthermore, hydrology and climate control the DOM pool's seasonal variation (Futter and de Wit,

2008), so temporal patterns must also be considered in order to evaluate the AT's full effects on the natural wetland.

Correlation between organic matter and metal is becoming one of the hot topics, especially in the regulation of trace metal(loid)s (TMs)- related ecological processes in wetland (Zhang et al., 2021). The effect of organic matter on the mobility and bioavailability of TMs is complex (Zhang et al., 2021). On the one hand, the low-soluble organic matter fraction in the sediment can participate in the sequestration of TMs. Humic substances, as one of the important low-soluble constituents of SOM, are a typical example. Some functional groups, like carboxyl and hydroxyl groups, are rich in the humic substances (Piccolo et al., 2019), and demonstrate high affinity to form complexes with metals (Kar et al., 2011). Internal hydrogen bonds also play a role in the immobilization of TMs. When the solubility of humus increases, the combining TMs should leads to the removal of TMs from the soil (Gusiatin et al., 2017). On the other hand, the organic matter during the decomposition or bioleaching could leave contrary effects on the TMs' mobility. For instance, DOM, released during these processes is strongly implicated in metal biogeochemical processes via acting as metal complexation compound and electron donor (Liu et al., 2020). The metal complexation ability was also influenced by natural and artificial factors, like salinity (Wufuer et al., 2014), agriculture (Waeles et al., 2013), and urbanization. The decreasing pH could also promote the release of TMs from the minerals (Ye et al., 2021). Chemically modified organic matter can also enhance the TMs' mobility (Xu et al., 2020). In one of our previous work (Hong et al., 2018), we found that Cu accumulation near an AT-impacted wetland, which could be well explained by a twofactor model considering both aluminosilicate and organic matter in the sediment. This finding is also consistent with other wetland researches (Warren and Zimmerman, 1994). However, unlike the aluminosilicate slope, which is similar between different sites, the slope of organic matter decreased in sites impacted by AT. Therefore, our hypothesis is that the accumulation of organic matter, triggered by AT, may increase the mobility of Cu. But this hypothesis still needs experimental support.

Fluorescent quenching is a powerful tool to study the dynamic mechanism of fDOM. Once released into the water column, the fluorescence signal of fDOM can be altered by complex environmental factors, such as TM-binding, light exposure, heat, and biological modification (Wu et al., 2011; Paerl et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2020; Carstea et al., 2014; Cuss and Guéguen, 2012). Monitoring the fluorescence signal of fDOM can help in the interpretation of the environmental processes of interest and their potential drivers. One of the common forms of fluorescent quenching is the one caused by binding with metals. There are a wide variety of metals that can cause fluorescent quenching, including Cu, Zn, Pb, Cd, As, Sb, and $\mathrm { H g }$ (Wu et al., 2011; Mandal et al., 2013; Cuss and Guéguen, 2014; Sheng et al., 2013). Generally, the TMbinding decreases the fluorescence signals of fDOM. Moreover, some metal-containing nanoparticles have also been reported to have fluorescence quenching behaviors (Manciulea et al., 2009). By fitting a nonlinear regression model (Ryan and Weber, 1982a), fluorescent quenching could help to calculate important TM-DOM complexing parameters, like complexing capacities, conditional stability constants, and residual fluorescence (Berger et al., 1984). Thus, the application of fluorescent quenching pattern could help explain the Cu removal in AT-influenced wetlands.

For a better understanding of DOM's ecological role, especially on the phytoavailability of TMs, at AT-impacted coastal wetlands, we carried out systemic investigations on fDOM pools, with further inlab verification to (1) investigate the spatiotemporal variability of fDOM in a typical AT-impacted salt-marsh; (2) evaluate the relationship between fDOM and metal(loid)s phytoavailability; and (3) demonstrate the fDOM's potential ecological roles with respect to its interaction with Cu. Ultimately, this study is intended for further understanding of fDOM's ecological function, especially in response to AT.

# 2. Methods and materials

# 2.1. Site descriptions, sampling, and data acquiring

The investigation was carried out in Rudong, a typical coastal county located in the eastern part of Jiangsu Province, China. Rudong's coastal ecosystems affect the regional and global ecological network, by supplying valuable habitat for birds traversing the East Asian—Australasian flyway (Ma et al., 2014), and jeopardizing the marine biome in the Northern Yellow Sea by generating Ulva macro-algal blooms (Huo et al., 2013). The reclamation projects in Jiangsu have raised some concerns, yet they continue unhindered. The seawall, where we conducted the field investigation, was built in 2010. However, the further reclamation was pending after the seawall construction; thus, direct interruption from the reclamation processes might be reduced when this study began in 2013.

Two sample sections (Section A and B) were designed in this study (Fig. 1). Section A, which consists of the site CL1, EA1, EA2, NV1, NV2, WE1, and WE3, was the main section to study the effect of AT. Section B was a reference sample section in an adjacent area to provide more robust validation about natural gradient. The orientation and history of the two sample sections were similar. Ten sites were selected and sorted into four groups. The three control (CL) group sites were located in the middle and high marsh with Spartina alterniflora. Two element accumulation (EA) sites were established around the seawall to estimate the effects of AT, with one site (EA1) seaward and the other site (EA2) landward. Tidal impact and plant colonization is also critical to form the spatial pattern of organic matters and TMs, so two sets of auxiliary sites were set up to assess these well-established natural influence and compare them with the AT effects. Three direct wave exposure (WE) sites were set at the margin of the S. alterniflora marsh with obvious tide impacts. Two no vegetation (NV) sites were close to the ones of CL and EA, but there was no plant colonization in these sites.

Sediment and plant samples were collected during low tide in April, July, and November of 2013. The response of plant growth might be different depending on stages, so the seasonal investigation is of great help on the description of complex response of S. alterniflora. S. alterniflora sprout and seed at April and November, respectively and its aboveground biomass is highest at July. Replicate samples were collected from three quadrats at each site, which were randomly selected and spaced ${ > } 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ apart. The composite sampling method was used in this investigation for sediment properties (Carter and Gregorich, 2006). Five subsamples, including four at the corners and one at the center of each quadrat, were collected and homogeneously mixed. More details on field survey and sample collection could be found in the appendix. The samples were freeze-dried then ground and passed through a sieve (100-mesh for sediment, or 60-mesh for plant), then stored at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ before analysis.

# 2.2. Fluorescence scanning of DOM

The DOM was extracted using a slightly modified method from SaidPullicino and Gigliotti (2007). Sediments were mixed with deionized water $( 1 { : } 5 \mathrm { ~ w / v ) }$ and stirred at $1 2 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ overnight on a reciprocating shaker at room temperature. Subsequently, the supernatant was separated by centrifugation (4500 rpm, $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , room temperature) and filtered with a Whatman $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m } \mathrm { G F / F }$ filter (Ward et al., 2019). Before filtration, the GF/F filter was pretreated at $4 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to remove possible organic matter contamination. The filtrate was collected and acidified using HCl/NaOH to obtain a final pH roughly equal to 3, in order to prevent potential interference from metal-complexation (Chen et al., 2003), then stored at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ before analysis. Biological inhibitors were not applied during the assay, as they might cause interference (Chen and Jaffé, 2014). As such, the spectroscopic analyses were completed within $2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ to prevent potential biodegradation. Excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) were obtained using a Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer (Agilent Varian, USA) at room temperature. The excitation wavelength ranged from 250 to $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ in $5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ increments, while the emission wavelength ranged from 280 to $5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in $2 \mathrm { n m }$ increments. The scan rate was $1 2 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . The absorbance between 200 and $8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ was obtained at $0 . 5 \mathrm { n m }$ intervals using an 8453 UV–Vis spectrophotometer (Agilent Varian, USA). Blank correction of EEMs was carried by subtracting the spectrum from the Milli-Q (Millipore) water (Lambert et al., 2016). The inner-filter effect was adjusted using absorbance data as mentioned by Ohno (2002). Then the EEMs were standardized to Raman Unit (RU) using the Ramet peak at $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009). PARAFAC modeling was performed following the DOMFluor Toolbox manual (Stedmon and Bro, 2008), employing the following equation:

where $x _ { i j k }$ is the fluorescence intensity of the ith sample at the jth excitation scan and the kth emission read. $a _ { i f } , b _ { j f }$ and $c _ { k f }$ represent sample scores, excitation loading, and emission loading on the fth components. $\varepsilon _ { i j k }$ is the residual noise not explained by the PARAFAC model. Four fluorescent components (Comp1–4) were determined by a classic split-half validation (Fig. S1).

# 2.3. Trace metal(loid)s in sediments and plant tissues

Prior to TM determination, the sediments were digested via closedvessel high-pressure digestion with 5 mL $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ , 2 mL $\mathrm { H C l O } _ { 4 }$ , and 1 mL HF. The plant tissues (leaf and stem) were digested with $5 \mathrm { m L }$ $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ , and 2 mL $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ . The digestion began with a 2-hour stage at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ then another 4-hour stage at $1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . The concentrations of TMs (As, Cu, Cd, Cr, Mn, Ni, Pb, & Zn) were determined via inductively coupled plasma - mass spectrometry (ICP-MS, Agilent $7 7 0 0 \mathrm { x }$ , Agilent Technologies, USA). We used certified reference materials (GBW-07314 & GBW07603, National Research Center for Standards, China) to assess the recoveries of digestion and variation between batches. The metals' recoveries ranged between $8 9 . 0 \%$ (Al) and $1 4 0 . 8 \%$ (As) for the sediment and $7 9 . 1 \%$ (Ni) and $1 1 5 . 3 \%$ (Pb) for the plant tissues (Table S1). We further used the standard samples in each batch to correct the bias between batches.

TM availability under the influence of the low molecular weight organic acids (LMWOAs) in the salt marsh was estimated by a simulation using representative LMWOAs as extractants (Mucha et al., 2005). During the simulation, $_ { 1 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ sediment was incubated with $5 \mathrm { m L }$ mixed LMWOA solution. The LMWOA level $2 5 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ oxalate, $5 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ succinate, $1 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ acetate) was established according to the field observation in pore water of salt marshes (Mucha et al., 2010). The incubation was conducted under rotation for $5 \mathrm { h }$ . Then TM contents were also determined via ICP-MS. Only four metal(loid)s in the extract (i.e., As, Cu, Cd, & Mn) showed a concentration higher than the ones in the LMWOAs solution.

# 2.4. Fluorescence titration

Fluorescence titration was carried out according to Wu et al. (2011) with minor modifications. Twelve representative sediment samples were selected for titration; their basic properties are provided in Table S2. In brief, $2 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ aliquots fDOM solution, which was extracted using the same protocol above, was titrated in brown vials using a calibrated syringe pump. Cu solution was prepared using $\mathrm { C u ( C l O _ { 4 } ) _ { 2 } }$ and the final concentration in titrant ranged from 0 to $5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ . Then the solutions were shaken overnight at room temperature to reach complexation equilibrium before undergoing a fluorescence scan using the same protocol in Section 2.2. After the fluorescence scan, the LMWOAs were added to the solution to the same level as plant accessible metal(loid) s extraction (Section 2.3, i.e., $2 5 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ oxalate, $5 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ succinate, $1 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ acetate). The fluorescence, perturbed by the LMWOAs, was recorded under a new equilibrium established overnight. Three fluorescent components (qComp1–3) were determined by the split-half validation (Fig. S1b). The nonlinear complexation models were fitted using the Ryan - Weber model (Ryan and Weber, 1982b):

$$
\begin{array} { l } { \displaystyle \frac { I } { I _ { 0 } } = 1 - \bigg ( \frac { f } { 2 \alpha } \bigg ) \bigg ( 1 + \alpha + \beta - \sqrt { \left( 1 + \alpha + \beta \right) ^ { 2 } - 4 \alpha \beta } \bigg ) , } \\ { \alpha = K _ { M } \cdot C _ { L } , \beta = K _ { M } \cdot C _ { M } , } \end{array}
$$

where $I _ { 0 }$ and $I$ are the fDOM intensities for each component, at the beginning or at a specific Cu level; $f$ is the quenched fluorescent proportion when it reached complete quenching $( f = 1 - I _ { \mathrm { f u l l q u e n c h e d } } / I _ { 0 } )$ , respectively; $C _ { L }$ , $C _ { M }$ represent the ligand and Cu concentration, respectively; and $K _ { M }$ is the conditional stability constant for Cu binding. The parameters $K _ { M } ,$ and $C _ { L }$ were estimated using R's minpack.lm package (Elzhov et al., 2016).

# 2.5. Data analysis

Statistical analysis was performed using R (version 3.6.2), and the significance criterion was 0.05. Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) tests (Gelman and Hill, 2006; W. Liu et al., 2017) were conducted using lme4 (Bates et al., 2015) to analyze the spatial and temporal influences, considering site and season as potential random effects, and the partial effect size were calculated using effects package (Fox and Weisberg, 2019). Redundancy analysis (RDA) was conducted by R's psych package (Revelle, 2018) to explore the relationship between sediment element and fDOM proportion, using Akaike information criterion (AIC) to determine factor capacity. Visualization was conducted using R's ggplot2 package (Wickham, 2016).

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Spatial variations of fDOM components around AT-affected marsh

The PARAFAC modeling determined four fDOM components with $9 9 \%$ explained variance of the fluorescence spectra. These fluorescent components were annotated (Table S3) by comparing them with the autofluorescence organic compounds in the OpenFluor library (Murphy et al., 2014). As shown in Fig. 2a, all fluorescent components were inferred to be humic-like substances, although they were determined to have different origins. Comp1 and Comp3 matched terrestrial humic-like compound spectra in previous reports (Lambert et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2017), and these components were found related to the photo-chemically mediated production or photo-refractory (Lapierre and Del Giorgio, 2014; Amaral et al., 2020). Comp2 were related to the natural soil fulvic compounds (Chai et al., 2019) with only one match found from the OpenFluor library. In contrast, previous studies have identified Comp4 as marine, allochthonous humic-like matter, and microbial contribution is vital for this fDOM component (Chen et al., 2018; Wünsch et al. 2017).

Comp1, Comp2, Comp3, and Comp4 contributed $3 3 . 2 \pm 0 . 4 \% 1 3 . 7 \pm$ $0 . 4 \%$ , $3 7 . 6 \pm 0 . 7 \%$ , and $1 6 . 6 \pm 0 . 6 \%$ of the total fDOM, respectively. Regardless of the fDOM intensity were mainly controlled by the spatial factors (Fig. 2b), the fDOM proportions co-regulated by spatial and temporal factors (Fig. 2c). The Comp1, Comp2 and Comp3 exhibited a proportion change of $4 . 1 \%$ , $3 . 1 \%$ and $- 8 . 7 \%$ at the EA sites. We also found fixed effects of WE (Comp1, Comp2, and Comp3) on the fDOM composition, which represent the natural gradient between CL and WE sites. However, both of these effects were weaker compared to the ones of EA. Moreover, the proportions of Comp4 decreased $2 . 9 \%$ at the lategrowing season (November). The fDOM composition of EA sites was clearly different from that of control sites (CL and WE, Fig. S7), which also supports the results of GLMM.

Changes in fDOM composition represent the environmental processes (Lehmann and Kleber, 2015) in AT-impacted wetlands, which might be related to element heterogeneity. Thus, we carried out an RDA to illustrate the general pattern between fDOM composition and environmental parameters. Al, Cd, Cu, Mn, Ni, As, SOC, N, and S were selected as environmental variables, since they have an established background (Hong et al., 2018, 2017). Al and SOC represented the independent accumulation of aluminosilicate and organic matter, respectively, thus they were considered the main drivers behind element distribution, while S and Cd exhibited different responses at the ATimpacted wetland. Moreover, N, S, Cu, Mn, Ni, and As were related to the growth traits of S. alterniflora, and were therefore involved in controlling the plant growth.

AIC-based modeling finally reached a three-element (SOC-N-S) result on the component intensity (Fig. S8a) and another three-element (SOC-S-Cd) result on the component proportion (Fig. S8b). The SOC was both critically correlated to the fDOM component and proportion, which implies SOC's important role in regulating the fDOM pool. Organic matter in the sediment was correlated with the $F _ { m a x }$ via different coefficients (Comp1: $6 1 7 \pm 2 7$ RU, Comp2: $3 0 1 \pm 1 4$ RU, Comp3: $3 6 9 \pm 2 5$ RU, Comp4: $2 6 3 \pm 1 5$ RU). # 3.2. Dynamic of LMWOA-extractable TMs

To access the TM contents available for plant utilization, we used LMWOAs to simulate the TM release under root exudates. Among eight TMs (i.e., As, Cu, Cd, Cr, Mn, Ni, Pb, & Zn) examined using ICPMS, only four of them (As, Cu, Cd, & Mn) reported positive release from the sediment compared to the solution background. The LMWOA-extractable metal(loid)s (Fig. 3a) decreased following the order of Mn $( 7 3 9 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } ) > \mathrm { C u } ( 9 0 . 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } ) > \mathrm { A s }$ $( 2 3 . 6 \mu \mathrm { g } \ k g ^ { - 1 } ) >$ Cd $( 2 . 1 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ ). The LMWOAs extracted more Cu, As, and Mn from sediments in EA sites (Fig. 3b). For instance, the LMWOA-extractable Cu in EA $( 2 5 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ ) sites was notably higher than that in CL $( 8 3 . 7 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ), WE $( 2 5 . 1 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } .$ ), and NV $( 2 3 . 7 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } .$ ) sites. Besides, the seasonal factor was critical in determining the pattern of LMWOAextractable Cd and As. Hence the LMWOA-extractable TM could be supplied by the sediment gross TM pool; thus, we further examine the proportion of LMWOA-extractable TMs (Fig. 3c). The proportion decreased following the order of Cd $( 0 . 9 9 \% ) > \mathsf { C u } ( 0 . 5 1 \% ) > \mathsf { A s } ( 0 . 1 2 \% ) > \mathsf { M }$ $( 0 . 1 2 \% )$ . As indicated by the GLMM fitting (Fig. 3d), the LMWOAs could also extract more proportion of Cu in the EA sites $( 1 . 0 2 \% )$ , which was much higher than that in CL $( 0 . 6 1 \% )$ , WE $( 0 . 2 8 \% )$ and NV $( 0 . 1 8 \% )$ sites. The LMWOA-extractable TMs also showed a coupling pattern with the total intensity of fDOM (Fig. S10). Cu and Mn had more strong correlation among metal(loid)s, both on their extractable concentration and ratio. Arsenic correlated with fDOM intensity on its extractable concentration, but not on its extractable ratio. None correlation was found between LMWOA-extractable Cd and fDOM.

# 3.3. Cu utilization of S. alterniflora

S. alterniflora is well known as an N-limited coastal herb (Qing et al., 2015), and we also found that LMWOA-extractable Cd does not respond significantly to fDOM (Fig. S10). Therefore, N and Cd were used as positive and negative controls, respectively, to reveal the critical role of Cu on S. alterniflora growth in the AT-affected wetland (Fig. 4). In both leaves and stems, the N pool was higher in the EA sites than in the CL sites - this difference was much higher than the natural gradient between the CL and WE sites. On the contrary, the Cd pool did not show a clear distinction between EA and CL sites. The Cu pool in spring and autumn shows a similar phenomenon to the N pool, i.e., the difference between EA and CL is significantly larger than the natural gradient between CL and WE. The pattern was also supported by the significant positive correlation with fDOM intensity in spring and autumn (Fig. S11). In spring, the slope on the leaf was much higher than that on the stem, which may demonstrate a strategy regulating Cu allocation during early growth.

Cu stocks in both the leaf and stem correlated well with their N stocks, indicating that the vegetative growth of S. alterniflora resulted in co-uptake of Cu and N. The seasonal variation of tissue Cu content v.s. population biomass (Fig. 4) further confirmed the dependence of vegetative growth. Cu content in leaf tissues have a seasonal relocation within all groups (CL: $F = 2 5 6 . 5$ , $p < 0 . 0 0 1$ , EA: $F = 3 7 . 8$ , $p < 0 . 0 0 1$ , WE: $F =$ 19.1, $p < 0 . 0 0 1 .$ ). These results imply that Cu is more likely to affect the leaf's growth as opposed to the stem's.

We chose average tiller height (H), average base diameter (BD), and total above-ground biomass (AgB) to evaluate the vegetative growth of S. alterniflora. The vegetative growth of S. alterniflora was well correlated with both total intensity of fDOM (Fig. S12c–e) and the proportion of some specific compounds (Comp1, Fig. S12f–h). These results demonstrate the close relationship between fDOM and saltmarsh primary production.

# 3.4. Cu-induced quenching pattern on representative fDOM samples

The combination of fluorescent quenching titration and PARAFAC modeling identified three fDOM components (i.e., qComp1–3) that were sensitive to the increasing Cu concentration (Fig. 5a). These components were all labeled as humic-like compounds. The quenching of these components could be fitted by the Ryan - Weber model (Fig. 5b). The three $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { m }$ values of the Cu sensitive fDOM components were similar (qComp1: 5.55, qComp2: 5.33, qComp3: 5.58), and they were in good agreement with the $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { m }$ reported for Suwannee River Natural Organic Matter (Yan et al., 2013). A relatively stable Cu complexation was confirmed with slightly higher $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { m }$ values compared to that reported in lake sediments (Xu et al., 2013). However, the Cuquenching potential was quite different between components (f, qComp1: $3 2 . 7 \%$ , qComp2: $7 1 . 7 \%$ , qComp3: $2 5 . 9 \%$ ). The $f$ values of qComp1&3 were much lower than the reported Suwannee River standard samples (Yan et al., 2013), but comparable to the ones from the lake sediments (Xu et al., 2013). These results demonstrate the complexity of field samples and again reinforce the need to discuss AT's impact on DOM pools.

Interestingly, the applying of LMWOAs changed the fluorescent quenching behavior, especially on the qComp2. After the LMWOAs were added into the solution, the three $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { m }$ values of qComp2 rose to 6.25, while the other two components remained stable or slightly increased (qComp1: 5.72, qComp3: 5.58). The existence of LMWOAs increased the $f$ values of two components (qComp1: $3 8 . 6 \%$ , qComp3: $3 0 . 8 \%$ , while the $f$ of qComp2 decrease two-third from $7 1 . 7 \%$ to $2 3 . 5 \%$ .

# 4. Discussion

In conducting this study, we attempted to connect the AT-induced fDOM changes with their potential ecological functions on Cu phytoavailability. First, we describe the general properties of fieldobserved fDOM to evaluate its spatiotemporal driving factors. Next, we compare the pool of elements in plant tissue and the sediment LMWOA-extractable TMs, to prove that enhanced Cu phytoavailability was related to fDOM. Finally, we used in-lab fluorescence quenching verification to demonstrate the ecological roles of fDOM to regulate the Cu supply.

# 4.1. Elevated phytoavailability of Cu in AT impact wetland

Extensive researches have been dedicated to modeling the growth traits of wetland plant under abiotic gradients, based on several factors that were well known as controlling parameters—i.e., annual temperature, nitrogen content, tidal range, salinity, and redox statue (Liu et al., 2016; Osgood and Zieman, 1993; McKee and Patrick, 1988). To determine the role played by Cu during vegetative growth, we compared its stocks in living stand with that of N (Fig. 4), since the latter is well known as a limiting nutrient for the growth of S. alterniflora. Both Cu and N have higher stock in EA sites, which demonstrates that Cu is also an essential nutrient that supports the rapid S. alterniflora bloom.

An element content - biomass diagram (Fig. 4), is another way to test the Cu supply requirement. S. alterniflora has a lower light saturation point than other C4 plants (Kathilankal et al., 2011). As such, more abundant leaf biomass results in promoting growth statues and competitive advantages for S. alterniflora. Thus, the high fluctuations in its biomass as a function of seasonal variation provide an opportunity to explore its dependence on a specific nutrient. A sharp decline in the leaf Cu was observed at the EA sites during the growing season, while the leaf Cu changed slightly at the CL sites (Fig. 4). This could be explained by a more abundant leaf biomass and Cu requirement at the EA sites, which again supports the notion that Cu is also a crucial element controlling S. alterniflora growth, especially under the ATinfluence. The WE sites, like the EA sites, showed a similar decline, but the leaf Cu at the WE sites was lower than that at the EA sites. We surmise this was related to the Cu limitation by relatively lower Cu levels in sediment, which is due to the tide washing away a lot of nutrition. The Cu contents in the leaf tissue underwent a decrease from growing season to late-growing season, in concordance with the previous record, in which S. alterniflora transports Cu out of its leaves in the late-growth season (Windham et al., 2003).

LMWOAs, which originates from root exudation, litter decomposition, and metabolites produced by plant-associated microbes (Shahid et al., 2012; Rajkumar et al., 2012), have complex and sometimes contrary effects on the phytoavailability. The release of LMWOAs could improve the uptake of some nutrients. On the other hand, LMWOA exudation could be stimulated by some TMs (Rocha et al., 2014), which could be interpreted as one of the potential defense mechanisms to metal(loid)s stress via organic complexation (Xie et al., 2013). In the presenting study, four out of eight TMs (As, Cu, Cd, & Mn) were released under LMWOA stimulation, meaning that LMWOA plays critical roles in regulating the metal(loid)s phytoavailability. LMWOA-extractable Cu and Mn were elevated in EA sites (Fig. 3), supporting a higher metal (loid)s stock in the above-ground biomass (Fig. 4). The LMWOAs level is reported to be positively related to the phytoavailability of some TMs (Cieśliński et al., 1998). In this study, the LMWOA level was lower than the ones in the rhizosphere (Mucha et al., 2010); the continuous secretion and biodegradation (van Hees et al., 2005) could form an LMWOA gradient. Thus, we speculate that the LMWOAs could act as an “LMWOA pump” linking the solubilization and uptake of specific TMs like As, Cu, Cd, and Mn.

# 4.2. Coupling between fDOM and bio-availability under AT impact

A series of biogeochemical processes control the conversion of SOC to fDOM. The increasing DOC and fDOM intensity resulted from the elevated SOC pool (Fig. S9) instead of the conversion ratio between organic compounds. EA sites were predicted to have $1 3 8 \%$ higher SOC, $1 8 8 \%$ higher DOC, and $1 0 1 \%$ higher total fDOM intensity compared to CL sites, while WE sites were expected to reduce these compounds by $3 6 . 4 \%$ , $3 7 . 3 \% ,$ , and $4 5 . 8 \%$ , respectively. Comparing the effect size between the EA and WE group provided results that suggested AT had relocated the SOC pool in the wetlands and would further promote the fDOM release into pore water. Similar enrichment patterns were observed where hydrogeomorphic mechanisms play a central role in the accumulation of organic matter, like depressional wetlands (Daugherty et al., 2019). This data is in agreement with reports that show S. alterniflora wetland has a higher carbon sequestration capacity (Gao et al., 2016; Cheng et al., 2006).

Previous studies have shown that climate, hydrology, atmospheric deposition, and sorption/desorption processes are critical to control the DOM stock (Monteith et al., 2007; Futter and de Wit, 2008; Allison et al., 2010). Results from this study imply that AT, which was shown to induce remarkable litter trapping, reshaped DOM distribution in the inter-tidal wetland. Although the fDOM intensity showed a good linear regression with SOC (Fig. S9a), suggesting that AT did not interrupt the equilibrium between these two variables, both spatial and temporal factors determined the fDOM composition (Fig. 2). Human activities substantially impact the global carbon biogeochemical cycle, and significant effort has gone into demonstrating the ramifications of carbon stocks during land-use change (Stanley et al., 2012). Seawall construction altered soil carbon and nitrogen dynamics, as well as the soil microbial biomass in a salt marsh invaded by S. alterniflora in eastern China (Yang et al., 2017). The presenting results show a novel influence of anthropogenic activity in the form of AT-induced matter trapping, which emphasizes the importance of reconsidering hydraulic characteristics and relocation of plant litter during the design and construction of coastal engineering.

Our findings suggest that the relocation of plant litter could change the distribution pattern of fDOM in a typical S. alterniflora salt-marsh. Plant litter in the inter-tidal zone usually undergoes a rapid leaching process, followed by a slower decomposition process (Gao et al., 2016), which continuously leaches DOM into the pore water, and finally exports it into seawater (Stanley et al., 2012). The tidal cycle causes regular changes in water content, further accelerating the release of DOC (Chow et al., 2006). The fDOM pool can be more dynamic, significantly dependent on detailed biological processes, or determined by physical and chemical conditions. Moreover, some fDOM components would be highly degradable, and thus under a rapid turn-over (Nimptsch et al., 2015). In short, the fDOM composition might be changed via further biogeochemical processes after litter accumulation caused by AT. Herein, we chose Cu for our experiment since it was determined to be the most relevant element with respect to changing fDOM proportion.

# 4.3. fDOM: an ecological regulator of Cu bio-availability

Our results demonstrate that the AT-impacted wetland facilitates vegetative growth, which might require more Cu supply. To confirm the role of fDOM on vegetative growth, we still needed to show its relevance to Cu mobility. Considering previous works reported that TMs show a distinguishable affinity and quenching pattern on fDOM components (Lü et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2011; Sheng et al., 2013; Xu et al., 2013), herein we quantified Cu quenching in connection with changing fDOM composition under AT.

The combining of ${ \mathrm { C u } } ^ { 2 + }$ increased its mobility and accessibility (Fig. S13), which further facilitated S. alterniflora growth. The fluorescent quenching result is in agreement with the higher Cu content detected in the leaching of EA sediments (Fig. S13). The quenching characteristics were relatively stable, which might be associated with the ecological engineering effect of S. alterniflora. Although the longterm evolution of vegetation adaptation to the changing environment is not the focus of this study, as a species with invasive behavior that is observed worldwide (Strong and Ayres, 2013), S. alterniflora, may have the ability to regulate the supply of elements through litter production, which further enhances its competitive advantage.

The applying of LMWOA greatly enhanced the fluorescence of qComp2, elevated its conditional stability constant $K _ { M }$ but decreased its quenched proportion $f ,$ implying that LMWOA might extract part of Cu from fDOM complexation. Hence the extracted Cu is available to promote plant growth via the “LMWOA pump,” as discussed above. So we arrive at an “fDOM-LMWOA pump” explanation that special metal (loid)s like Cu could improve their phytoavailability via the fDOM complexation and LMWOA activation. The LMWOA usually have a high turnover rate (van Hees et al., 2005). Therefore it cannot act on the metal(loid)s activation for a long time. On the contrary, part of fDOM could be recalcitrant (Jiao et al., 2011), which means that they could maintain relative stability in the environment and have long-term ecological functions on the metal(loid)s activation (Chen et al., 2020). It should be noted that the promotion of growth traits would, in turn, accelerate the accumulation of nutrients in the sediment, finally form a positive feedback loop with more litter production and matter-trap efficiency. These findings indicate that the relationship between inter-tidal vegetation growth and fDOM is worth exploring.

The fDOM components observed in the field investigation and the fluorescence quenching results show different spectral characteristics (Figs. 1a & 5a). The EEM components were separated by three emission peaks (qComp1: 413 nm, qComp2: 329 nm, qComp3: $4 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ); two of them had double excitation peaks. Wu et al. (2011) observed similar patterns in their previous work. The PARAFAC components of fluorescence quenching provided meaningful insights into fDOM and Cu's complexation, other than that from field investigation. We speculated that quenching components represent the molecular change determining the fluorescent emission.

Independent PARAFAC fits have the advantage of distinguishing the response to TMs from the spatiotemporal characteristics of the EEM. On the other hand, more evidence was required to sort out each chemical group's specific changes in DOM during the binding with TMs. Calculating the fDOM components' scores directly from the existing loadings will improve the understanding of spatiotemporal variation in metal(loid)s complexation and connected the individual components between the field observations and the fluorescent quenching experiment. Therefore we suggest that extensive comparison between lab experiments and field observations should be the focus of the following works. Also, there are some metal(loid)s that bind to DOM without significant change in fluorescence intensity, as well as some others prone to precipitation, thus limiting the application of fluorescence titration in the study of DOM binding process of these metal(loid)s. Other techniques, such as Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy or X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, may be able to solve these troubles and thus can be considered in subsequent studies.

Although this study was conducted in AT-impacted wetlands, it is important to note that litter trapping should also play critical roles in natural inter-tidal wetlands. Litter trapping is a common feature in the plant kingdom, having a long evolutionary history (Zona and Christenhusz, 2015). The overall ecological function of litter trapping has not been fully explored, which is somewhat attributable to the fact that this phenomenon is so widespread that it is easy to be ignored. AT enhances litter trapping (Fig. S14a) and thus provides opportunities to indicate the ecological role of litter trapper in the inter-tidal ecosystem. On the other hand, inter-tidal vegetation can also trap some artifacts (Martin et al., 2019), especially plastics, and AT has been reported to enhance the accumulation of these artifacts (Martin et al., 2019), which also exacerbates long-term human environmental impacts. It may be a long time before the long-term ecological impacts of such trapping behaviors are gradually manifested.

In this study, we reveal the mechanism that DOM complexation could improve the Cu mobility and availability of wetland sediments via an fDOM-LMWOAs pump. It need to be noted that other mechanisms, for instance, microbial activity supported by the liable DOM, might also facilitate the release (Liu et al., 2020) and sequestration (Hong et al., 2020) of TMs. On the other hand, the microbial function would release new DOM compounds or modify the structure of existing DOM. Thus, more works were called for to reveal the DOM's biogeochemistrial property and its function on TM's mobility.

# 5. Conclusion

Herein, we report the seasonal variation of fDOM in a typical wetland impacted by artificial topography (AT). The increasing SOC contents, which resulted from litter trapping, continuously supported the fDOM's production and release throughout the growth of aquatic vegetation. Four fDOM components were identified, among which three were enriched by AT, while Comp4 showed a highly enriched distribution in the early- and mid- growing season. The total fDOM intensity was positively correlated with Cu support and vegetative growth. Apart from the role played by S. alterniflora as an ecological engineer, we also found evidence that fDOM components could chelate Cu, and thus increase its mobility, which likely facilitates a positive feed-back loop established by self-fertilization of wetland vegetation. The application of LMWOA greatly enhanced the intensity of one fDOM component, elevated its conditional stability constant, and decreased its quenched proportion, implying that LMWOA might extract part of Cu from fDOM complexation. In all, the present work provides an “fDOMLMWOA pump” explanation into considering the fDOM dynamics as a novel ecological regulator, which could leave hallmarks on the floral population structure under the AT-induced matter accumulation.
