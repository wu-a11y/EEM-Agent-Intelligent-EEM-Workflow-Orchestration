# Abstract

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is a critical component in aquatic ecosystems, yet its seasonal variability and reactivity remain not well constrained. These were investigated at the land-ocean interface of a subtropical river (Minjiang River, SE China), using absorption and fluorescence spectroscopy. The annual export flux of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) from the Minjiang River (5.48 $\times ~ 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } ~ \mathrm { g ~ y e a r ^ { - 1 } ) }$ was highest among the rivers adjacent to the Taiwan Strait, with $72 \%$ occurring in spring and summer. The freshwater absorption coefficient $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ , DOC-specific UV absorbance $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and humification index HIX were higher, while the spectral slope $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and biological index BIX were lower in summer than in winter. This suggests intensified export of terrestrial aromatic and high molecular weight constituents in the rainy summer season. Six fluorescent components were identified from 428 samples, including humic-like C1–C3, tryptophan-like C4 and C6, and tyrosine-like C5. The freshwater levels of four components (C1, C2, C4, and C6) were lower while that of C5 was higher in the wet season than in the dry season, suggesting contrasting seasonal variations of different constituents. Laboratory experiments were performed to assess the effects of photochemical and microbial degradation on DOM. Photo-degradation removed chromophoric and fluorescent DOM (CDOM and FDOM) effectively, which was stronger (i) for high molecular weight/humic constituents and (ii) during summer under higher solar radiation. Microbial degradation under laboratory controlled conditions generally showed little effect on DOC, and had smaller impact on CDOM and FDOM in winter than in summer. Overall, this study showed notable seasonal changes in the chemical composition and reactivity of DOM at the land-ocean interface, and demonstrated the significant effects of photo-degradation.

# Introduction

Dynamics of dissolved organic matter (DOM) are key factors in the biogeochemistry and ecosystem function at the landocean interface (Battin et al. 2008; Chen and Jaffé 2014; Zhuang and Yang 2018). Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) is discharged to the ocean by global rivers, which represents an important flux $( \sim 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 8 } \mathrm { t C y e a r } ^ { - 1 } )$ in the global carbon cycle and has profound implications for coastal marine ecosystems (Ludwig et al. 1996; Dai et al. 2012). DOM is also a key factor determining the biogeochemistry of nutrients and inorganic/ organic pollutants (Stedmon et al. $2 0 0 7 \mathrm { a }$ ; Chen et al. 2018). DOM is the major substrate for heterotrophic respiration and microbial growth, fueling the microbial food web (Fuß et al. 2017). A fraction of DOM can absorb UV-visible light and is thus termed chromophoric DOM (CDOM), which strongly affects the aquatic optical properties, primary production and the penetration of harmful UV radiation (Coble 2007; Nelson and Siegel 2013). In the populated watersheds and coastal zone where treated river water is one of the major sources of drinking water supply, the formation of harmful disinfection byproduct from the interaction between DOM and disinfectants is a major challenge (Nguyen et al. 2013; Yang et al. 2015a). Therefore, it is of great importance to characterize the quantity and quality of DOM at the dynamic land-ocean interface, to provide a basis for understanding the biogeochemical processes and ecosystem functions and for ensuring the water quality.

The dynamics and environmental effects of DOM at the land-ocean interface are dependent on a variety of factors such as land cover, hydrological and more generally environmental conditions, and in situ biogeochemical transformations (Hong et al. 2012; Chen and Jaffé 2014; Singh et al. 2017). In particular, the transition between rainy and dry seasons can change greatly the water flow path and the mobilization of DOM from different sources such as terrestrial plants, leaf litter, soils, groundwater, and anthropogenic sources (Inamdar et al. 2012; Singh et al. 2014; Yang et al. 2015b). Such seasonal inputs of various DOM sources, combined with the seasonal changes in the production of marine DOM, may lead to a notable variability of aquatic DOM concentration and chemical composition (Fellman et al. 2009; Hur et al. 2014; Powers et al. 2018). Furthermore, microbial and photochemical degradations are important for further modifying the quantity and quality of DOM and can release large amounts of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ which may be outgassed to the atmosphere (Battin et al. 2008; Wang et al. 2009). In particular, the relationship between the chemical composition and reactivity of DOM remains poorly understood (Kellerman et al. 2018). Absorption and fluorescence spectroscopy are widely used techniques for characterizing the chemical composition of DOM for a variety of aquatic environments (Helms et al. 2008; Stedmon and Bro 2008; Murphy et al. 2014). They could provide insights into the DOM quality-reactivity relationship through testing the effects of microbial and photodegradation on the optical indices of DOM, although they could not provide information on DOM at the molecular level.

Therefore, seasonal changes in the chemical composition and reactivity of DOM were tested at the land-ocean interface of a subtropical river (Minjiang River, SE China) in this study. The Minjiang River shows notable seasonal variability of precipitation and freshwater discharge under the influences of East Asia Monsoon and heavy rainfalls brought by typhoons. The distribution of DOM in the estuary is further affected by the tidal mixing and biogeochemical processes. However, little is known about the dynamics of DOM in the lower Minjiang River-Estuary zone, and the impacts of seasonal hydrological variation have not yet been examined. We aimed to (1) provide a quantitative analysis of the annual flux of DOC from the Minjiang River, based on the DOC concentration and freshwater discharge in the four seasons of 2017; (2) examine the seasonal variations of DOM composition, by comparing the characteristics of CDOM and fluorescent DOM (FDOM) in different seasons; and (3) determine the biogeochemical reactivity of different DOM constituents in the Minjiang river-estuarine system, by testing the responses of DOM properties to photochemical and microbial degradation.

# Materials and methods

# Study area, sample collection, and degradation experiments

The Minjiang River is a subtropical river in southeast China with a length of $5 7 7 \mathrm { k m }$ , a watershed area of $6 0 9 9 2 { \mathrm { k m } } ^ { 2 }$ and a long-term annual freshwater discharge of $6 . 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ . It is the largest river adjacent to the Taiwan Strait which is the only channel directly connecting South China Sea and East China Sea, two large marginal seas in the world. Thus, the discharge of DOM from Minjiang River is important for determining the regional carbon budget and may have notable effects on the coastal ecosystem. In addition, the lower Minjiang River serves as a major drinking water source for a population of 7.66 million in the Fuzhou City; thus, DOM in the river is a critical factor for determining not only the aquatic ecosystem but also the drinking water safety. In particular, both precipitation and freshwater discharge show notable seasonal variability in the Minjiang River, under the influences of East Asia Monsoon and heavy rainfalls brought by typhoons. In fact, $73 \%$ of the annual freshwater discharge occurred during March–August in 2017 (www.weather.com.cn).

Surface water samples in the lower Minjiang riverestuarine zone were collected from 17 stations on April 15th–16th, 2017, and from 18 stations on July 8th–10th, October 21st–22nd, and December 17th–18th, 2017 (Fig. 1). The sampling periods are representative of different seasons, based on the rainfall and discharge data (Fig. 1c). The sampling locations spanned from M03 at the Zhuqi Bridge near the major hydrological control station of the Minjiang River to M18 for collecting coastal seawater. Temperature, salinity, dissolved oxygen, and $\mathrm { p H }$ were measured in situ using a handled Thermo Scientific Orion Star A Meter (Online Resource 1). Samples were filtered through pre-combusted GF/F filters, and the filtrates were transferred into pre-combusted (at 450 $^ \circ \mathrm { C }$ for $3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ ) brown glass bottles. The filtrates were stored in frozen and dark for DOC measurements and were stored in cold $( 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ and dark for optical measurements.

Photochemical degradation of DOM was measured in July and December for four typical stations (M03, M05, M11, and M18), following the procedure described previously (Helms et al. 2008; Guo et al. 2012). Samples were filtered sequentially through pre-combusted GF/F filters and acid-rinsed $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ Millipore polycarbonate filters. The filtrates were transferred into a series of pre-cleaned and combusted $1 2 5 \mathrm { - m L }$ quartz tubes. Three tubes of samples were measured for each station at the beginning of the experiment. Half of the remaining tubes were wrapped in aluminum foils as the dark group for comparing with the irradiated group. Thereafter, all tubes were placed in a shallow water tank under natural sunlight on the roof of our college building. Samples were taken for measurements in triplicate after 1- and 3-day irradiations for all stations, and the irradiation time was extended to 6 days for three stations in winter (M03, M11, and M18) due to the lower solar radiation in this season.

Microbial degradation of DOM was determined for 16 stations in July and for 18 stations in December, following the procedure described in detail previously (Vonk et al. 2013; Yang et al. 2015c). The bottles were loosely capped to allow free exchange of air, and all incubations were carried out in triplicate. After the incubation, DOC and optical properties were measured, and the results were compared with the initial values.

# DOC, absorption, and fluorescence measurements

The DOC concentration was measured using the hightemperature catalytic oxidation method after removing inorganic carbon by acidification and oxygen purging, using a Shimadzu TOC-L analyzer (Yang et al. 2015a, b). Absorption spectra of CDOM were scanned using a Techcomp 2600 UV-Vis spectrometer over $2 4 0 { - } 8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with Milli-Q water as the blank and were baseline-corrected by subtracting the mean absorbance over $7 0 0 { - } 8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (Yang et al. 2013). The absorption coefficient at $2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m } \ ( a _ { 2 8 0 } )$ , DOC-specific UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { ( S U V A _ { 2 5 4 } ) }$ , and the spectral slope over $2 7 5 { - } 2 9 5 ~ \mathrm { n m } ~ ( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ were calculated following procedures described previously (Weishaar et al. 2003; Helms et al. 2008). $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ is an index of the CDOM level in aquatic environments, while $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ correlate with the aromaticity and average molecular weight of DOM (Weishaar et al. 2003; Helms et al. 2008).

Fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) were scanned using a Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer at excitation/emission $\left( \mathrm { E x / E m } \right)$ wavelengths of 240–450/280– $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , with increments of excitation and emission wavelengths of 5 and $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ respectively, following the procedure described previously (Yang et al. 2013, 2015a). Each EEM was corrected for inner-filter effects based on absorbance data (McKnight et al. 2001) and subtracted the fluorescence response of blank (Milli-Q water). A total of 428 EEMs, including those for samples from both field investigation and degradation experiments, were modeled with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) in MATLAB 7.0 with the DOMFluor toolbox 1.7 (Stedmon and Bro 2008). The number of PARAFAC components was determined with split-half validation, and the fluorescence intensity $( F _ { \mathrm { m a x } } )$ of each component was used to represent its level (Stedmon and Bro 2008). Humification index (HIX) and biological index (BIX) were also calculated from EEMs (Zsolnay et al. 1999; Huguet et al. 2009). HIX was the ratio of the fluorescence over $\mathrm { E m } 4 3 4 - 4 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that over $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 6 \mathrm { n m }$ (at $\operatorname { E x } 2 5 5 \mathrm { n m } )$ , and BIX was the ratio of the fluorescence at $\mathrm { E m } 3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that at $4 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (at $\operatorname { E x } 3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ). HIX is an index for the humic content of DOM, with high values $> 1 6$ for strong humic character and low values ${ < } 4$ for lowest humic content (Zsolnay et al. 1999; Huguet et al.

2009). BIX is an index for DOM source, which is $< 0 . 7$ for important terrestrial contribution and is $> 1$ for biological/ aquatic bacterial origins (Huguet et al. 2009).

# Fluorescence results

The humification index HIX changed greatly from 0.6 to 6.2 in the lower Minjiang river-estuarine zone and decreased with increasing salinity in the estuarine zone (Fig. 3e, f). The freshwater HIX was much higher in July than in other months (Fig. 3e). BIX was $0 . 6 { - } 1 . 2$ i n the study area and increased with increasing salinity in the estuarine zone (Fig. 3g, h). The freshwater BIX was lower in April and July than in other months (Fig. 3g).

Six fluorescent components were identified using EEMPARAFAC in this study (C1–C6, Fig. 4, Online Resource 2) and were compared with those in the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al. 2014). Component C1 showed two Ex maxima at 245 and $3 4 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , and one Em maximum at $4 4 1 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . It resembled a humic-like component mainly from terrestrial sources (C1 in Stedmon et al. 2007b; C1 in Gueguen et al. 2014). Component C2 had Ex/Em maxima at (245, 300)/383 nm, similar to a microbial humic-like component (C4 in Yamashita et al. 2010; C2 in Murphy et al. 2011; C3 in Lambert et al. 2016). Component C3 displayed Ex/Em maxima at $( 2 5 0 , 3 9 0 ) / 5 0 4 \mathrm { n m }$ , resembling a terrestrial humic-like component (C2 in Stedmon and Markager 2005; C1 in Shutova et al. 2014). C4 had Ex/Em maxima $( 2 7 5 / 3 1 3 \ \mathrm { n m } )$ ) similar to a tryptophan-like component (C6 in Osburn and Stedmon 2011). C5 had $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maxima at $2 7 5 / 2 9 7 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , and it was similar to a tyrosine-like component (C7 in Graeber et al. 2012; C4 in Kowalczuk et al. 2013). C6 had $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maxima at $( \leq 2 4 0 , 2 8 5 ) / 3 5 2 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , which resembled a tryptophan-like component (C5 in Murphy et al. 2011; C5 in Yamashita et al. 2013). The difference in $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maxima between C4 and C6 might be related to the fact that tryptophanlike materials could exist as either free amino acids or being bound within peptides and proteins (Fellman et al. 2010).

Tryptophan and tyrosine can also exist within the same protein, and the energy transfer between them could affect their $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ wavelengths (e.g., C4; Kowalczuk et al. 2013).

The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of humic-like C1-C3 correlated significantly with each other $( r ; 0 . 7 0 - 0 . 8 6 , p < 0 . 0 1$ , Table 1). There was also a strong correlation between the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ values of tryptophan-like C4 and C6 $( r = 0 . 9 7 , p < 0 . 0 1$ , Table 1). The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1–C3 all decreased linearly with salinity in this study (r: between − 0.64 and − 0.83, $p < 0 . 0 1$ ), while those of protein-like $\mathrm { C 4 - }$ C6 showed weak correlations with salinity and were nonconservative in the estuary (r: between − 0.21 and 0.10, Table 1, Fig. 5, Online Resource 3). The contribution of protein-like components to the total fluorescence generally increased with salinity in the estuary (Online Resource 4). The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1, C2, C4, and C6 in the freshwater zone were lower in April and July than in other months, while C3 showed no notable seasonal variation (Fig. 5, Online Resource 3). The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of tyrosine-like C5 did not correlate significantly with any other component, and was elevated in April and July (Fig. 5e). In addition, all PARAFAC components except for C5 $( r ; 0 . 2 4 - 0 . 5 2 , p < 0 . 0 5 )$ correlated weakly or moderately with DOC, while $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ correlated positively with C1, C3, C5 $( r ; 0 . 3 2 \ – 0 . 5 8$ , $p < 0 . 0 1 $ ) and negatively with C4 and C6 $( r = - 0 . 3 0 , p < 0 . 0 5$ , Table 1).

# Photo- and microbial degradation results

In the photo-degradation experiment, the changes of DOC generally ranged between $- 1 5$ and $7 \%$ after 3 days in summer and after 6 days in winter, except for a higher decrease of − $36 \%$ for site M03 in summer (Table 2). The changes of $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ were between − 36 and − $51 \%$ after 3 days in summer and were between − 19 and − $30 \%$ after 6 days in winter, except for a smaller decrease of − $4 \%$ for the coastal seawater site M18 (Table 2, Fig. 6). The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ sums of humic-like (C1–C3) and protein-like components (C4–C6) decreased by $- 7 \%$ to $- 7 5 \%$ (mean − $5 9 \% \pm 2 0 \%$ ) after photo-degradation (Table 2, Fig. 6). In addition, $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ increased by $9 - 7 6 \%$ (mean $4 0 \% \pm 2 4 \%$ ), while HIX decreased by − 9 to − $58 \%$ (mean − $34 \% \pm 1 7 \%$ after photo-degradation for all stations in both seasons (Table 2, Fig. 6). BIX decreased by $- 1 1 \% \pm 8 \%$ after photo-degradation for freshwater samples, while it increased by $43 \%$ in summer and by $8 \%$ in winter for the coastal site M18 (Table 2).

The DOC concentration changed by $- 9 \% \pm 1 2 \%$ and − $5 \% \pm 1 6 \%$ after 28-day bio-degradation in summer and in winter, respectively (Online Resource 5). $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ decreased by − 19 to − $40 \%$ in the freshwater stations M02-M12 after bio-degradation in summer, while it changed little for other stations in summer and for most stations in winter (Fig. 7; Online Resource 5). The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ sum of humiclike C1–C3 generally showed little change both in summer $( 1 0 \% \pm 1 1 \%$ ) and in winter $( 1 \% \pm 4 \% )$ , although it increased for four stations in summer (M02, M03, M17, and M18). The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ sum of protein-like C4–C6 changed by $- \ 1 1 \% \ \pm \ 2 5 \%$ in summer with evident decreases for four stations (M02, M06, M07, and M18) and notable increases for two stations (M03 and M17). It showed limited change for most stations in winter $( 0 \% \pm 7 \% )$ . In addition, $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ increased by $4 \mathrm { - } 1 7 \%$ for freshwater samples (M02–M12) after bio-degradation in summer, while $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ showed little change for other stations in summer and for most stations in winter (Fig. 7; Online Resource 5). HIX increased by $2 7 \% \pm 3 6 \%$ after biodegradation in summer, and showed little change in winter $( - \ 1 \% \ \pm \ 9 \%$ , Online Resource 5).

# Discussion

# Export flux of DOC from the Minjiang River to the coastal sea

The DOC concentration was generally low at the land-ocean interface of Minjiang River $( 7 2 - 1 2 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , although a higher value of $1 6 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ indicated potential anthropogenic pollution at the urban site M05 in December (Fig. 2). They were in the lower end of the broad range of DOC concentration for global rivers ( $4 2 { - } 4 9 8 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ , mean $\sim 4 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ , Ludwig et al. 1996; Huang et al. 2012) and were low among the values for rivers adjacent to the Taiwan Strait $1 4 { \ - } 6 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ , Yang et al. 2013). The low DOC concentration in the Minjiang River might be caused by the low wetland coverage, dilution effect due to the high freshwater discharge, microbial and photochemical degradation during the long-distance transport, adsorption to suspended particles, and/or an overall limited anthropogenic pollution in the watershed (Battin et al. 2008; Wang et al. 2009; Yang et al. 2013; Singh et al. 2017).

The export flux of DOC was estimated for the first time for Minjiang River, the largest river adjacent to the Taiwan Strait. The results showed an annual DOC flux of $5 . 4 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } \mathrm { ~ g ~ }$ year−1 in 2017, with $72 \%$ occurring in spring and summer. This estimate was conservative since the freshwater discharge in 2017 $( 4 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } )$ was $21 \%$ lower than the long-term average value of $6 . 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ . The DOC yield of Minjiang watershed $( 0 . 9 0 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { y e a r } ^ { - 1 } .$ ) was lower than the mean value of global rivers $( 1 . 4 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { y } \mathrm { e a r } ^ { - 1 } )$ but was higher than that of Oceania rivers $( 0 . 7 6 \ \mathrm { g \ m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ , Huang et al. 2012). # Temporal variations of CDOM and FDOM in the lower Minjiang river-estuarine zone

The DOM parameters were plotted against longitude to show the spatial and seasonal variations in both the river and estuarine zones, which showed notable variations of CDOM and FDOM in the four seasons (Figs. 2, 3, and 5, Online Resource 3). The $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ values for freshwater samples were much higher in July than in other seasons (Fig. 2c), indicating intensified flushing of CDOM from terrestrial surface and subsurface sources in the rainy summer (Spencer et al. 2010; Singh et al. 2014). Correspondingly, the freshwater $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and HIX were higher in July than in other months (Figs. 3a, e), suggesting elevated aromaticity and humic content of DOM in the rainy season (Yoon and Raymond 2012; Yang et al. 2015b). In addition, the freshwater $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and BIX were lower in April and July than in other months (Figs. 3c, g), indicating a higher average molecular weight and terrestrial contribution of DOM in the wet season (Helms et al. 2008; Huguet et al. 2009).

PARAFAC components correlated weakly with DOC and $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ (r: between − 0.30 and 0.58, Table 1), suggesting the spatial and temporal variations of PARAFAC components were probably different from those of DOC and $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ . The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1, C2, C4, and C6 in the freshwater zone were lower in April and July than in other months, suggesting their temporal variations were dominated by significant dilution effect in the wet season due to high precipitation and freshwater discharge (Figs. 5a, c, Online Resource 3a, e). In contrast, the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of tyrosine-like C5 was elevated in April and July (Figs. 5e), which might be related to increased production from algae in spring and summer. The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C3 showed no notable seasonal variation. Therefore, the PARAFAC results demonstrated contrasting seasonal variations of different DOM components, which were probably dependent on variable inputs of different DOM sources including terrestrial sources, autochthonous production, and anthropogenic inputs (Inamdar et al. 2012; Yang et al. 2015b) and different responses of PARAFAC components to biogeochemical transformations such as preferential removals by photo-degradation, microbial degradation, and particulate sorptiondesorption (Stedmon et al. $2 0 0 7 \mathrm { a }$ ; Guo et al. 2012; Chen and Jaffé 2014).

The sources of CDOM and FDOM could be in part inferred from their mixing behavior in the estuarine zone (Stedmon and Markager 2005; Guo et al., 2011; Yang et al. 2013; Santos et al. 2016; Arguelho et al. 2017). In the estuary, DOC, CDOM absorption, and the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of fluorescent components will show strong linear correlations with salinity under conservative mixing between freshwater and seawater. In contrast, they will deviate towards higher or lower values due to addition or removal processes (i.e., non-conservative behavior; Huguet et al. 2009; Guo et al. 2011; Yang et al. 2013; Arguelho et al. 2017). In this study, the DOM parameters for estuarine samples (to the east of $1 1 9 . 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ) were plotted against salinity to show the mixing behaviors of DOM (Figs. 2, 3, and 5, Online Resource 3). $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ and the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1-C3 all decreased linearly with increasing salinity in the estuarine zone (Figs. 2d and 5b; Online Resources 3B, D, 6), suggesting they mainly originated from inputs from the watershed and were dominated by the conservative mixing between CDOM-rich freshwater and CDOM-depleted coastal seawater. Similarly, terrestrial-derived compounds are important sources for CDOM and humic-like fluorescence in other estuaries and the freshwater discharge is a major factor affecting CDOM dynamics in the estuary (Guo et al. 2011; Yang et al. 2013; Santos et al. 2016; Arguelho et al. 2017). In contrast, the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of protein-like C4–C6 generally showed weak correlations with salinity, suggesting they were affected by nonconservative processes in the estuarine zone (Fig. 5d, f; Online Resources 3F, 6). The fluorescence intensity of C4 and C6 were elevated at some estuarine stations, and the contribution of protein-like components to the total fluorescence generally increased with salinity in the estuary (Online Resource 4). These results indicated inputs of protein-like materials from additional sources such as desorption from suspended particles, autochthonous production from phytoplankton, microbes and coastal plants, and/or anthropogenic inputs (Romera-Castillo et al. 2010; Guo et al. 2011; Yang et al. 2013; Santos et al. 2016; Arguelho et al. 2017; Powers et al. 2018). Moreover, the tyrosine-like C5 and DOC were also non-conservative in the estuarine zone (Figs. 2b and 5f), which might be affected by removal processes such as photochemical and microbial degradation (Helms et al. 2008; Santos et al. 2014; Arguelho et al. 2017). The diverse source and estuarine behavior of CDOM and different FDOM components probably resulted in the changes of DOM quality indices (e.g., $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , HIX , $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ , and BIX) in the estuary. $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and HIX are indices for the aromaticity and humic content of DOM respectively, while higher $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and BIX are indicative of lower average molecular weight and lower terrestrial fraction of DOM respectively (Weishaar et al. 2003; Helms et al. 2008; Huguet et al. 2009). $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and HIX decreased while $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and BIX increased with increasing salinity in the estuarine zone, indicating seaward decreases of aromaticity, humic content, average molecular weight, and terrestrial fraction of DOM (Fig. 3, Online Resource 6; Helms et al. 2008; Huguet et al. 2009).

# Photo-degradability and bioavailability of DOM at the land-ocean interface of Minjiang River

The DOC concentration changed little for most stations in the photo-degradation experiment, although it decreased for two stations M03 and M05 in July ( $- 3 6 \%$ and − $11 \%$ , respectively, Table 2). $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ decreased notably for most stations both in summer $( - ~ 3 6$ to $- 5 1 \%$ ) and in winter (− 19 to $- 3 0 \%$ , Table 2, Fig. 6). Therefore, the molecular structures of CDOM were destructed effectively by solar radiation into either non-chromophoric DOM or inorganic carbon. One exception was the smaller decrease of $a _ { 2 8 0 } ( - 4 \% )$ for the coastal seawater site M18, which might be partly due to the weakest light absorbance with a lowest $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ of $1 . 7 4 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Guo et al. 2012). In addition, the significant decreases in the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ sums of humic-like and protein-like components $( - \ 5 9 \% \pm 2 0 \% )$ 0 suggested that photo-degradation was also effective for removing fluorescent components. Furthermore, $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ increased by $4 0 \% \pm 2 4 \%$ while HIX decreased by − $34 \% \pm$ $17 \%$ after photo-degradation in both seasons, indicating preferential removals of high molecular weight and humic constituents of DOM (Helms et al. 2008; Santos et al. 2014). Finally, the changes of optical parameters $( a _ { 2 8 0 }$ , $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ , humic-like and protein-like $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ sums) after 3 days were generally slightly stronger in summer than in winter and were stronger for the freshwater sites than for the coastal site M18. This might be partly caused by the higher average molecular weight of DOM in the freshwater and in summer as indicated by lower $S _ { 2 7 5 - }$ 295, due to the preferential breakdown of conjugated high molecular weight constituents. The intensified photo-degradation of CDOM and FDOM in summer was probably in part due to stronger solar radiation. CDOM and FDOM were further removed by extending the irradiation time to 6 days in winter.

Overall, our results demonstrated that photo-degradation removed CDOM and FDOM effectively, and the process was dependent on seasonal changes in both the DOM quality such as molecular weight and environmental factors such as solar radiation intensity and radiation time. It is noteworthy that the photo-degradation rate may be decreased under field conditions of lower irradiation intensity due to the light scattering by suspended particles, while the photo-degradation time could be longer in the field.

The limited changes of DOC after bio-degradation in summer $( - 9 \% \pm 1 2 \% )$ and in winter $( - 5 \% \pm 1 6 \% )$ suggested an overall low bioavailability of DOM during both wet and dry seasons, although this might be in part related to the difference between the laboratory-controlled and the field conditions. Similarly, both $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ and $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ showed little change for M13–M18 in summer and for most stations in winter, indicating limited effects of microbial degradation on CDOM. However, $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ decreased by − $19 \%$ to − $40 \%$ after biodegradation in the freshwater stations $_ { \mathbf { M 0 2 - M 1 } 2 }$ in summer, which probably indicated inputs of bioavailable CDOM constituents from the watershed in the rainy season such as those derived from rainfall, leaf litter and upper soils (Fig. 7, Fellman et al. 2009; Yang et al. 2015b). The increase of $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ after bio-degradation for M02–M12 in summer further suggested a decrease in the average molecular weight of DOM and thus a preferential microbial removal of high molecular weight constituents (Benner and Amon 2015). Changes of DOM composition by bio-degradation was also reflected in the different responses of PARAFAC components. The humic-like C1–C3 generally showed little change both in summer and in winter, except for the increase at four stations in summer. The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ sum of protein-like C4–C6 showed evident decreases for M02, M06–M07, and M18 but notable increases for M03 and M17, while it showed limited change for most stations in winter $( 0 \% \pm 7 \% )$ . Overall, humic-like components could be produced by microbial transformation of DOM, while protein-like components could be either consumed by microbial utilization or produced as a by-product of microbial activity (Lønborg et al. 2010; Yang et al. 2015c). Correspondingly, the relative importance of humic-like materials generally increased in summer as indicated by an increase of HIX $( 2 7 \% \pm 3 6 \% )$ ). Our results also showed a weaker effect of microbial degradation on CDOM and FDOM in winter than in summer, which was probably in part due to lower average temperature in winter than in summer $1 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ vs. $2 9 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ) and thus less active bacterial activity in winter. CDOM and FDOM in the river-estuarine system are affected by both photo- and biodegradation in the field. It should be noted that the biodegradation experiments were performed under laboratory controlled conditions, which were not representative of in situ conditions since the filtration removed bacteria associated with suspended particles. However, some bacteria can pass the filter and the bacterial biomass could increase largely within a few days based on previous studies (Romera-Castillo et al. 2011). The bioavailability of DOC has also been determined by measuring DOC after incubations of filtrates from other regions, and is effective to reveal low bioavailability for some rivers $( < 2 0 \%$ , Wiegner et al. 2009) but high bioavailability for glaciers (up to $69 \%$ , Spencer et al. 2014) and permafrost (up to $34 \%$ , Vonk et al., 2013).

# Conclusions

The export of DOC from Minjiang River was the largest source of river DOM adjacent to the Taiwan Strait, which should be considered in the studies of regional carbon cycle and ecosystem function. The optical indices of DOM at the land-ocean interface of Minjiang River showed notable seasonal variability, indicating elevated aromaticity and average molecular weight in the rainy season and contrasting seasonal changes of different PARAFAC components. CDOM and FDOM in the lower Minjiang river-estuarine zone were susceptible to photochemical degradation, a process depending on seasonal variations of both DOM quality and solar radiation. The bulk DOC was generally refractory to microbial utilization under laboratory controlled conditions in both wet and dry seasons, and CDOM and FDOM also showed little response to biodegradation in winter. The results have implications for understanding the dynamics of DOM at the land-ocean interface, demonstrating notable seasonal variability of fluvial DOM quality. The results also indicated higher photo-degradability of CDOM and FDOM than non-CDOM constituents and preferential removals of high molecular weight/humic constituents by photo-degradation. The increase of $S _ { 2 7 5 } ~ \textrm { -- } 2 9 5$ and HIX after microbial degradation further suggested decreasing average molecular weight and increasing humic content of DOM. Photo-degradation and bio-degradation both occur in the field and these results are important for understanding the microbial and photochemical reactivity of different DOM constituents at the land-ocean interface.
