Stratification of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) for aggregated anammox microorganisms

ABSTRACT: Sludge aggregation and biofilm formation are the most effective approaches to solve the washout of anammox microorganisms. In this study, the structure and composition of EPS (extracellular polymeric substances) were investigated to elucidate the factors for the anammox aggregation property. Anammox sludge taken from 18 lab-scale and pilot-scale reactors treating different types of wastewater was analyzed using EEM-PARAFAC (excitationemission matrix and parallel factor analysis), FTIR (fourier transform infrared spectroscopy) and real-time PCR combined with multivariate statistical analysis. The results showed that slime and TB-EPS (tightly bound EPS) were closely related with water quality and sludge morphology, and could be used as the indicators for anammox microbial survival ability and microbial aggregate morphology. Furthermore, slime secreted from anammox bacterial cells may be exhibited higher viscosity to the sludge surface and easily formed the gel network to aggregate. Large amounts of hydrophobic groups of protein in TB-EPS promoted the microbial aggregation. The mechanisms of anammox aggregation explored in this study enhanced the understanding of anammox stability in wastewater treatment processes.

Keywords: Anammox, Extracellular polymeric substances (EPS), Aggregation behavior, Uronic acids, $\beta$ -sheet.

# Introduction

Application of anammox in wastewater treatment plants has been extensively studied, due to its high efficiency, no need of oxygen and additional carbon source and low sludge output 1 . However, the anammox bacteria have slow growth rate with doubling time of two weeks 2 , which hinders the adoption of this technology. Maintaining high biomass concentrations and preventing the loss of anammox biomass is critical for this process. To date, the most feasible and effective method to improve the bacterial accommodation capacity are granulation, biofilm formation and immobilized biomass 3 . EPS (extracellular polymeric substances) plays a crucial role in sludge granulation and biofilm formation as well as the maintenance of the structural integrity 4 . Their physicochemical properties and spatial distribution structure can dramatically affect the structure and function of microbial aggregate. For example, proteins, humic acids and uronic acids in EPS contributed to the hydrophobicity of activated sludge while carbohydrates contributed to the hydrophilic nature 5 . The $\beta$ -polysaccharides forms the outer layer to support the mechanical stability of aerobic granules 6 . Certain protein secondary structures promoted bioflocculation aggregation, adsorption and biofilm formation 7, 8 . Although EPS in activated sludge and biofilms have been extensively studied, the key mechanism of aggregation ability of anammox microorganisms remains unknown. Since the unique properties of anammox sludge including density dependent 9, high aggregation ability 10 , aggregation morphology of broccoli shape 10, strong tendency to form granules 11, 12 and the ability to secrete large amounts of EPS 13 , the EPS of anammox sludge is different from that in conventional activated sludge. Therefore, a new approach to elucidate EPS production in anammox sludge should be explored.

To date, only a few studies have focused on EPS in anammox-dominated mixed culture, and mainly limited to the content of protein and polysaccharides using colorimetric method 13, 14 . Meanwhile, many factors including different treatment processes, operational conditions and water quality 15 influenced the properties of EPS (fractions, structure, composition, etc.), whereas the previous studies only compared one anammox sludge sample with that of nitrifying sludge or denitrifying sludge sample 7, 10 . Therefore, these obtained results related to the aggregation of anammox cannot suitable for other anammox processes. In addition, EPS had different stratified structure in sludge and each fraction possesses different physicochemical characteristics of organic matters16, such as slime layers markedly impeded the sludge dewaterability16 , LB-EPS (loosely bound EPS) might inhibit bioflocculation, sedimentation and sludge dewatering17 , and cause the irreversible fouling in membrane bioreactors18 . On the other hand, TB-EPS (tightly bound EPS) had the gel-like characteristics and strong elasticity and might benefit flocculation16, 19, 20 . Furthermore, since anammox bacteria have not been cultivated as a pure culture 9 , it is so significant to determine the origin of EPS from different microbes in anammox-dominated mixed culture. Therefore, to elucidate the effect of EPS on anammox microbial aggregation, the structure and composition of stratified EPS should be investigated at the microscopic level.   
The objective of the study was to elucidate the relationship between the properties of stratified EPS (fractions, structure, composition, etc.) and the properties of anammox biomass (macrostructure, activity, microbial composition, etc.) by studying a variety of anammox-dominated biomass samples collected from lab-scale and pilot-scale systems using multivariate statistical analysis (principal component analysis and Pearson correlation analysis). This study devote to explore the critical factors for anammox microbes aggregation, which provide a reference to

enhance the understanding of anammox stability in wastewater treatment processes.

# Materials and methods

# Sludge samples

Anammox sludge samples were collected from 18 different lab-scale and pilot-scale reactors, which treated wastewater from synthetic wastewater, domestic sewage, landfill leachate and reject water and possess different sludge morphology (flocs, biofilm and granules). The characteristics of these sludge samples were shown in Supplementary Table S1. The collected samples were stored at $4 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ immediately after sampling for further analysis.

# Bacterial activity test

To elucidate the activities of ammonium oxidizing bacteria (AOB), nitrite oxidizing bacteria (NOB) and anammox bacteria, a series of batch experiments were carried out. The sludge samples were washed three times by oxygen-free water to avoid the oxygen inhibition, and then injected into a $5 0 0 \mathrm { m l }$ flask to measure AOB, NOB and anammox activity. The initial pH was 7.5 and temperature was maintained at $3 5 \pm 1 ~ \mathrm { ~ \textdegree C }$ . Initial concentration of ammonia and nitrite was $3 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ for anammox bacteria. As for AOB and NOB, ammonia and nitrite was respectively added to $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ and the average DO was maintained at $3 . 0 \pm 0 . 5 \mathrm { m g / L }$ .

# EPS extraction protocol

The slime and LB-EPS extraction protocol was modified according to the research of Yu et al.16 . In brief, $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of sludge sample was placed in a $5 0 \mathrm { m l }$ centrifuge tube and centrifuged at 2000 g for $1 5 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ , then the bulk solution was collected as slime. The bottom sediments were collected and resuspended to their original volumes with PBS. After that, the suspensions were centrifuged at $5 0 0 0 \mathrm { g }$ for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ and the bulk solution was collected as LB-EPS. The TB-EPS extraction protocol was according to our previous work 21 . Briefly, the bottom sediments were collected and resuspended to original volumes with PBS. The ultrasonic method was employed to extract the TBEPS from resuspended sediments at $2 0 \mathrm { k H z }$ and $5 . 5 9 \mathrm { W / m l }$ for $1 \mathrm { m i n }$ . The extracted solutions were centrifuged at ${ 2 0 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { g } }$ for $2 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Organic matter in the bulk solution was comprised of the TBEPS. Polytetrafluoroethylene membranes with a pore size of $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ were used to remove the particulates present in the slime, LB-EPS and TB-EPS solutions.

# EEM fluorescence spectroscopy and PARAFAC analysis

To avoid any inner-filtering effects, the EPS sample was diluted with deionized water to ensure that their absorbance at $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ was lower than 0.05 22 . EEM fluorescence spectroscopy measure method was according to our previous work 21 . The fluorescence spectral parameters of fluorescence index (FI) and humification index (HIX) value were calculated as the report of Gabor et al.23 . The PARAFAC is a three-way method that was used to model the EEM fluorescence data. The principle of the PARAFAC could be found elsewhere 24 , so there is no need to provide a detailed description on it. The PARAFAC process was carried out in MATLAB 8.3 (MathWorks, Natick, MA) with the DOMFluor Toolbox (http://www.models.life.ku.dk). To avoid the impact on the component score caused by varying EPS concentrations in different samples, the EEMs were normalized by dividing the spectra by the corresponding DOC concentrations 25 after removal of the Rayleigh and Raman scatters by Delaunay triangulation method and subtract the control Milli

Q water, respectively. The outliers were examined by comparing the leverage. The number of fluorescence components was determined by a validation method, including split half analysis, residual analysis and visual inspection.

# Quantitative real-time PCR Assay

Genomic DNA was extracted from about $0 . 2 \mathrm { \ g }$ of freeze-dried sludge using the FastDNA SPIN Kit for Soil (QBIOgene Inc.,Carlsbad, CA, USA), with a beating time of 30 s and a speed setting of 5.0. The concentration of the extracted DNA was determined with Nanodrop ND-1000 ultravioletvisible spectrophotometry (Thermo, USA). Quantitative real-time PCR were performed in MX3000P Real-Time PCR system (Stratagene, La Jolla, CA) with SYBR Premix Ex TaqTM (TAKARA, Dalian, China) and the assays were carried out in a volume of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction mixtures, including $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of SYBR Premix Ex Taq, $0 . 4 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of ROX Reference Dye, $0 . 3 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of each primer $( 1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , and $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of tenfold-diluted DNA template $\left. 1 - 1 0 { \mathrm { ~ n g } } \right)$ ). The program of protocol of each consisted of the following steps: $3 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $9 5 \mathrm { ~ \ } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , followed by 40 cycles of $3 0 ~ \mathrm { s }$ at $9 5 \mathrm { ~ \ } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 30 s at corresponding annealing temperature, and 45 s at $7 2 \mathrm { ~ \textdegree C }$ . The standard curves for each bacteria gene copies were constructed from a series of 10-fold dilutions of the plasmid DNAs. The results with amplification efficiency in a range from $9 0 \% { - } 1 1 0 \%$ and correlation coefficient above 0.9 were employed. Primers and corresponding annealing temperature are listed in Supplementary Table S2.

# Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy

The EPS samples were lyophilized and ground with the infrared grade KBr and molded into a disc to characterize the major functional groups in the EPS using a FTIR spectrometer (IRPrestige

21, Shimadzu, Tokyo, Japan). IRsolution (v1.10) operating software was used to generate and process the FTIR Spectra. The amide I region $( 1 7 0 0 - 1 6 0 0 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) in the EPS was further analyzed to extract information regarding protein secondary structures. Furthermore, the second-derivative spectra and deconvolution spectra of amide I region were carried out to resolve the overlapped peaks with the minimum residual using Peakfit software (version 4.12, Seasolve Software Inc.). Moreover, a ratio of $\mathfrak { a }$ -helix/( $\beta$ -sheet $^ +$ random coil) was used to describe the tightness degree of protein structure (Supporting Information (SI) Table S5) 10 .

# Chemical analysis

Proteins (PN), humic acid (HA), polysaccharides (PS) and uronic acids (UA) were measured according to the research of Badireddy et al.8 . The DNA content was measured by the diphenylamine colorimetric method 26 . The dissolve organic carbon (DOC) in the filtrate was analyzed using a Vario TOC analyzer (Elementar Analysensysteme Hanau, Germany). The $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ was measured with a spectrophotometer UV-4802 (UNICO, Shanghai, China). Specific ultraviolet light absorbance (SUVA) was calculated as $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 } / \mathrm { D O C }$ to characterize aromaticity 27 . SCOD, TSS and VSS contents of sludge were analyzed according to the Standard Methods 28 . Ammonium, nitrite, nitrate were analyzed using Lachat QuikChem8000 Flow Injection Analyzer (Lachat Instrument, Milwaukee, USA). DO, pH and temperature were measured by oxygen, pH and temperature probes (WTW 340i, WTW Company). All standards were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, USA), and the chemical analyses were carried out in duplicate using chemicals of analytical grade.

# Data Analyses and Statistical Methods

To display variation in samples of a statistical population, the boxplot was used for data visualization (Fig 1a and e). The relationships between anammox sludge samples and EPS components characteristics were analyzed using the principal component analysis (PCA) method in CANOCO 5.0 software. Pearson correlation analysis was showed in heatmaps using the Euclidean distances and hierarchical cluster analysis in the “pheatmap” package of R 3.1.0 software.

# Results and discussion

# Chemical characterization of EPS components

Variations on the contents of the three spatial scales EPS components were examined in 18 samples taken from different reactors (Fig 1a), with significant difference in water quality and sludge morphology (the detailed data are presented in Fig.S1). Compare to slime $( 1 5 . 6 1 \pm 9 . 9 2 \% )$ and LB-EPS $( 6 . 4 6 \pm 3 . 0 2 \% )$ ), TB-EP $( 7 7 . 9 3 { \pm } 1 0 . 3 \% )$ ) was the major component of total EPS, which was similar to previous findings. 29 . Polysaccharides and humic acid were the most widely distributed substance in slime. The predominant substance in LB-EPS and TB-EPS was humic acid and protein, respectively. The ratios of protein to polysaccharides, protein to DNA and protein to humic acid were 2.10±1.07, $6 . 3 8 { \pm } 7 . 8 8 $ and $1 . 4 8 { \pm } 0 . 5 7 $ , respectively. The SUVA of EPS was: TBEPS $( 0 . 6 2 { \pm } 0 . 1 2 ) >$ slime $( 0 . 5 5 { \scriptstyle \pm 0 . 4 1 } ) > ]$ LB-EPS $( 0 . 3 9 \pm 0 . 3 1 ) $ ). Higher SUVA values indicate a higher concentration of organic matters with carbon-carbon double bonds, such as lignin, humic acid, PAHs (polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon), PCBs (polychlorinated biphenyls), aromatic protein, etc 30 . TBEPS should contain large amounts of aromatic protein and other two scales contained a lot of humic acid in DOC.

EEM fluorescence spectra of three EPS fractions in different anammox samples was investigated using PARAFAC model (Fig.1 b-d). The decomposed three components were identified as the characteristic peaks at Ex/Em of (215, 275)/342 nm, (205, 240, 305)/398.5 nm and (225, 240, 335)/434.5 nm, which represented the protein-like substance (C1), heme-like substance (C2) and humic-like substance (C3) fluorophores, respectively (peak recognition of EEM fluorescence spectra are presented in Supporting Information (SI) Text 1). C1 was the most widely distributed substance in all three spatial scales EPS ( $4 5 . 1 9 { \pm } 1 6 . 2 9 \%$ , $4 6 . 8 8 { \pm } 1 8 . 4 2 \%$ , $7 4 . 2 6 { \pm } 1 8 . 4 0 \%$ ) (Fig. 1e and the detailed data are presented in Fig.S1). Unlike the TB-EPS, there was approximately the same magnitude of C1 to C3 in slime and LB-EPS. The fluorescence intensity scores showed the relative abundance of EPS components in three spatial scales was inconsistent with the results of colorimetric method, which was caused by extremely high sensitivity of fluorescence spectroscopy than chromatic spectrum 31 . In addition, FI values in the three spatial scales EPS varied in a range of 1.59±0.11\~1.72±0.13, with relatively moderate values (1.4\~1.7), indicating that the DOC in the EPS was originated from both microbial metabolism and waste water 32 . Except for sample F6/L with a strong humic content of landfill leachate $\mathrm { ( H X > 6 ) }$ , the HIX values in slime and LB-EPS ranged from $1 . 3 7 { \pm } 0 . 5 9 $ to $1 . 5 7 { \pm } 0 . 7 3 $ , representing the character of weak humic content and high microbial metabolic activity 33 that was consistent with the high SUVA values in slime and LB-EPS. Low levels of TB-EPS $( \mathrm { H I X } { = } 0 . 4 9 { \pm } 0 . 1 6 $ ) illustrated the TB-EPS components were originated from from bacterial secretion rather than exogenous source.

# Quantification of EPS components using PCA method

The compositional characteristics of EPS based on the chemical and spectral analysis was correlated with the activities of AOB, NOB and anammox bacteria using PCA method. By projecting the sample points onto the arrows of component concentration, samples were centered satisfactorily on the basis of water quality (Fig.2 a-3). Synthetic wastewater samples (bottom-left) were separated from domestic sewage (upper-right). The C1, C2 and PN had a relatively high abundance in the domestic sewage, while PS, FI, DOC, UA and HIX were more abundant in synthetic wastewater (Fig.2 a-1). In addition, landfill leachate and reject water samples were located in the upper-left side (Fig.2 a-3), showing a strong humification depended upon high content of SUVA, DNA, HA, UV254 and C3 (Fig.2 a-1). However, LB-EPS samples were scattered in PCA scatterplot of both water quality and sludge morphology (Fig.2 b-2 and b-3), whereas there was a clear clustering of the TB-EPS samples based on sludge morphology (Fig.2 c-2). Biofilm samples clustered on the upper-middle side of the plot (Fig.2 c-2), which had high levels of UA, C1 and PS (Fig.2 c-1), while granule samples located in the bottom-left (Fig.2 c-2) that were characterized by high SUVA and UV254 richness (Fig.2 c-1). Furthermore, flocs samples were apart from other two types of sludge and occupied a large area in the middle of the scatterplot (Fig.2 c-2), so that the distribution of each component in flocs was comparatively uniform.

# Fig.2 here

PCA results indicated that different clusters represented different types of water quality and sludge morphology. The slime layer was correlated with water quality type due to its outermost spatial scales location. This might be helpful for identifying microbial survival environment. The

TB-EPS layer was associated with microbial aggregates morphology, since the gel-like property of TB-EPS was tightly bounded to the cell surface and played an important role for flocculation 34 . Bacterial activity (the Supporting Information Table S1) showed that nitrification activity of AOB and NOB were higher in the flocs, whereas the anammox activity was higher in the granules (Fig.2 c-1), which was consistent with previous findings that nitrification mostly occurred in small flocs while anammox was found in large aggregates 35 . Since loose structure of the flocs have lower mass transfer resistance, ammonium and oxygen was easily penetrated in to the flocs, which benefited the growth of nitrifying bacteria (AOB and NOB). While large aggregates had high retention capability to accommodate microorganisms with slow growth rates (e.g. anammox bacteria) and provided anoxic micro-environment.

# Pearson correlation analysis of anammox sludge aggregation

Relationships of functional microorganisms and EPS parameters were established to explore the aggregation behavior of anammox sludge using Pearson correlation analysis (Fig.3). The realtime PCR and FTIR results showed many positive and negative correlations (Supporting Information Text 2, Table S4 and S5). In this study, we only focused on the relationships associated with anammox microorganisms. For slime layer, the abundance of anammox were positively correlated with the level of UA and DOC but was negatively correlated with SUVA, indicating that the more abundance of anammox in sludge, the more slime was secreted that was characterized by high UA content and low carbon-carbon double bonds (Fig.3(a)). For TB-EPS layer, anammox abundance was positively correlated with the protein secondary structures of $\beta$ -sheet in TB-EPS layer (Fig.3(c)). For the first positive correlation between anammox and UA in slime (Fig.4 a-1), anammox bacteria were able to synthesize UA. Given the metagenome of Candidatus Kuenenia stuttgartiensis, one gene named kustb0219 was found to encoded GDP-mannose dehydrogenase synthesis (algD) 36, which was known as crucial enzymes for alginate biosynthesis 37 . Hence, anammox bacteria have the ability to synthesize UA. In addition, the FTIR spectra of slime demonstrated typical bands of UA (Fig.4 a-2 and Table 1): the bands at $1 6 5 5 ~ \mathrm { c m ^ { - 1 } }$ and $1 4 0 2 ~ \mathrm { c m ^ { - 1 } }$ were corresponded to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibration of COO- attributed to the presence of UA. The band at $1 5 4 0 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ was assigned to the $\mathrm { C } { = } \mathrm { C }$ in pyranose ring, which might be caused by the $\beta$ -elimination effect of alginate lyases. The band at $1 2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { c m ^ { - 1 } }$ was assigned to the presence of O-acetyl ester for bacterial alginates 38. The bands at $9 5 2 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ showed a weak absorbance that might be caused by the existence of nucleic acids 8 . From a structural point of view, UA is the unique component in alginate, which is an unbranched exopolysaccharide composed of random arranged 1, 4-linked uronic residues of $\beta$ -d-mannuronate (M) and $\mathfrak { a }$ -l-guluronate (G) 39 , and typically occurred as $( - \mathrm { G } - ) _ { \mathrm { n } } ,$ (– $\mathrm { M } - { \bf \epsilon } ) _ { \mathrm { n } }$ and $( - \mathrm { M G } - ) _ { \mathrm { n } }$ blocks (Fig.4c) 40 . The $( - \mathrm { G } - ) _ { \mathrm { n } }$ blocks are a rod-like polymer yielding an array of coordination sites that benefit divalent cations in their cavities and provide gel-forming capacity (Fig.4c). In contrast, $( - \mathbf { M } - ) _ { \mathrm { n } }$ and $( - \mathrm { M G } - ) _ { \mathrm { n } }$ blocks are fiber-like chain necessary for the flexibility of

the chains, the connection of $( - \mathrm { G } - ) _ { \mathrm { n } }$ blocks and the network structure during gelation 38 . Alginate was involved in the development of microcolonies and responsible for the mechanical stability of biofilms 39 . In addition, bioflocculation, settling and dewatering properties were substantially affected by UA 5, 8 . Since the viscosity of slime was increased with alginate concentration, UA in the slime of anammox bacteria may be showed stronger viscosity, and plays a key role for the adhesive ability and architecture structures of anammox sludge 38, 39, 41 .   
For the second positive correlation between anammox and $\beta$ -sheet in TB-EPS (Fig.4 b-1), secondary structure of protein in EPS played important roles in aggregation, adhesion, bioflocculation and biofilm formation 7, 8 . Previous studies showed that $\beta$ -sheets promoted bioflocculation 8, and $\beta$ -sheet contributed to high aggregation ability of anammox sludge 10 . Due to the twisted and pleated sheet structure of $\beta$ -sheet (Fig. 4c), large amounts of inner hydrophobic groups of amino acids were more easily to be exposed and express the hydrophobic property of anammox sludge 10 . Increasing the hydrophobicity of cell surfaces promoted cell-to-cell aggregation 42, and the cell surface hydrophobicity was the triggering force for bio-granulation 43 . The FTIR spectra showed more hydrophobic functional groups in TB-EPS (Fig. 4 b-2 and Table 1): the peak at approximately $1 6 6 8 ~ \mathrm { c m ^ { - 1 } }$ and $1 6 2 3 ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ were mainly assigned to $\mathrm { C } { = } \mathrm { C }$ and $\mathrm { C } { = } \mathrm { O }$ in proteins. The band at $1 3 8 4 ~ \mathrm { c m ^ { - 1 } }$ was attributed to the bending vibration of C-H in -CH3. There were fewer hydrophilic functional groups (especially for high polarity of N-H, -OH and COO-) in TB-EPS. Relative content of $\beta$ -sheet in TB-EPS proteins increased with the abundance of anammox bacteria (Fig. 4 b-1), implying that TB-EPS secreted from anammox bacteria had a strong hydrophobicity to promote microbial aggregation.

Overall, the mechanisms of anammox aggregation might be contain three stages. (1) Initial attachment of anammox cells to the surface: Free anammox bacteria swimming in close proximity to abiotic solid surface until find an nutritious habitat for initial contact by secrete small amounts of sticky UA in bulk water; (2) Production of UA resulting in more firmly irreversible adherence: Then they come to rest on the surface and contact with other cells. In this moment, many anammox bacteria began to secrete a lot of UA. The gelation properties of UA can play a role in net capturing to prevent cells detachment due to its three dimensional network structure. (3) Development of microcolony architecture: After that, during the growth and reproduction of anammox bacteria, more EPS is produced and tightly-wrapped on cell surface. Due to the $\beta$ -sheet secondary structure in extracellular protein, inner hydrophobic groups were more easily to be exposed, which makes the surface of anammox community showed higher hydrophobic property. It can promote cell-to-cell aggregation and form strong microcolony architecture, which make them undergo further adaptation to life in large aggregates.

# Significance of this study

The identification of the key role of stratified EPS is of importance to understand the mechanisms of anammox microbial aggregation and enhance anammox efficiency through biofilms and granules formation. By examining anammox-dominated mixed culture samples from 18 anammox systems, this study for the first time elucidated the characteristics of slime and TB-EPS composition and their correlation with water quality and sludge morphology. UA (in slime) and $\beta$ -

Sheet (in TB-EPS) level were positively correlated with anammox abundance (Fig. 4a). To improve the accommodation capacity of anammox biomass, mature anammox sludge should be added in order to promote the release of UA or directly added alginate to make the flocs quickly form granules or biofilm. Another option will be adding accelerants (e.g. quorum sensing molecules 45, 46 ) or pretreatment (e.g. ultrasound 47 and magnetic field 48 ) to enhance anammox bacterial metabolic activity that may promote anammox bacterial secrete more UA. In addition, Verrier et al. found that hydrophobic surfaces are favor adhesion of hydrophobic bacteria 49 , Based on the above result, the high hydrophobic characteristic of anammox can be utilized to develop the special carriers or small particles (acting as crystal nucleus) with high hydrophobicity (eg. polytetrafluorethylene, polypropylene or polyethylene 49 ) that can priority selection bond anammaox bacterial cells to form biofilms or granules 50 . Future studies should be extracted alginate-like exopolysaccharides from anammox sludge and verified its gel networks by gel formation experiments and further elucidated the role of stratified EPS on the anammox aggregation behavior by proteomics and glycomics.
