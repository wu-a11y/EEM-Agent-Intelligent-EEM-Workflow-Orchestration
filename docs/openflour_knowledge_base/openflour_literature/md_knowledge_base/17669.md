# ABSTRACT

To assess how the quality and properties of the natural dissolved organic carbon (DOC) could drive different effects on gill physiology, we analysed the ionoregulatory responses of a native Amazonian fish species, the tambaqui Colossoma macropomum, to the presence of dissolved organic carbon (DOC; $1 0 \mathrm { m g } 1 ^ { - 1 } .$ ) at both $\mathsf { p H } 7 . 0$ and $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ in ion-poor water. The DOC was isolated from black water from São Gabriel da Cachoeira (SGC) in the upper Rio Negro of the Amazon (Brazil) that earlier been shown to protect a non-native species, zebrafish Danio rerio against low $\mathsf { p H }$ under similar conditions. Transepithelial potential (TEP), net flux rates of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ and ammonia and their concentrations in plasma and $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ ATPase; v-type $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ ATPase and carbonic anhydrase activities in gills were measured. The presence of DOC had negligible effects at $\mathsf { p H } 7 . 0$ apart from lowering the TEP, but it prevented the depolarization of TEP that occurred at $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ in the absence of DOC. However, contrary to our initial hypothesis, SGC DOC was not protective against the effects of low pH. Colossoma macropomum exposed to SGC DOC at $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ experienced greater net $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ losses, decreases of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ concentrations in plasma and elevated plasma ammonia levels and excretion rates, relative to those exposed in the absence of DOC. Species-specific differences and changes in DOC properties during storage are discussed as possible factors influencing the effectiveness of SGC DOC in ameliorating the effects of the acid exposure.

# 1 | INTRODUCTION

When exposed to acidic freshwaters, most fishes experience disturbances in their ionic homeostasis, caused by inhibition of active $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ uptake, stimulation of passive diffusive ionic losses and reversal of the transepithelial potential (TEP) at the gills (Wood, 1989; Gonzalez et al., 2005; Nelson, 2015). These responses can lead to ionic imbalances and consequent decreases in plasma concentrations of major ions, which in turn may lead to death (Milligan & Wood, 1982). However, several Amazonian fish species seem to have developed mechanisms to avoid these ionic disturbances at low pH conditions (Gonzalez et al., 2005). Among these species, the tambaqui Colossoma macropomum (Cuvier 1816) (Teleostei, Characiformes, Serrasalmidae), is considered one of the most tolerant species to low pH. In aquaculture, it grows well at $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ (Aride et al., 2007) and in the wild, it migrates in the high-water period from basic (pH c. 7.0) white water into acidic (pH c. 4.0) black water in order to feed in flooded forests (Val & Almeida-Val, 1995; Araujo-Lima & Goulding, 1998). The ability of the acidophilic $C .$ . macropomum to tolerate acidic environments is related to avoiding ionic imbalance, which has been described in at least four previous studies (Gonzalez et al., 1998; Wood et al., 1998, 2017; Wilson et al., 1999). For example, other Amazonian fishes less resistant to low $\mathsf { p H }$ , such as Brycon hilarii (Valenciennes 1850) and Hoplosternum littorale (Hancock 1828), as well as temperate salmonid species that have been studied extensively (Wood, 1989; Nelson, 2015), presented increases in net loss rates of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ , reduced levels of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ in plasma and enhanced plasma protein and red blood cell concentrations (indicative of ionic disturbance (Milligan & Wood, 1982) during gradual exposure to $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ and $\mathsf { p H } 3 . 5$ , disturbances which were quantitatively much greater than in the acidophilic $C .$ . macropomum (Wilson et al., 1999).

The Amazon acid black waters have unique physical and chemical characteristics, including low dissolved ion concentrations (particularly, $\mathsf { N a } ^ { + } c . 1 6 \mu \mathrm { M } , \mathrm { C l } ^ { - } c . 1 9 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + } c . \ 1 0 \ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ and low pH (3–6) which is attributed to the high amount of humic substances (HS; Furch, 1984). These comprise a heterogeneous combination of higher molecular weight humic acids (HA) and lower molecular weight fulvic acids (FA; Al-Reasi et al., 2013), accounting for $50 \mathrm { - } 9 0 \%$ of the total dissolved organic carbon (DOC) content (Leenheer, 1980; Hedges et al., 1994). There is mounting evidence that the DOC can play an important role in ameliorating the effects of the low pH on the osmoregulatory systems of aquatic organisms (Wood et al., 2003, 2011; Duarte et al., 2016). This was initially proposed by Gonzalez et al. (1998, 2002) and first demonstrated by Wood et al. (2003) though in an indirect manner, in endemic freshwater stingrays Potamotrygon spp.. In comparison with water of very similar ionic composition but lacking in DOC, the natural Rio Negro black water was able to ameliorate the effects of the acid exposure by decreasing the passive efflux rates of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l } ^ { - }$ ions across the gills and by reducing the inhibition of the active influx rates of both $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ . However, the first direct evidence that the DOC isolated from Amazonian black water can exert some protection against the effects of acidic pH was recently published by Duarte et al. (2016). For zebrafish Danio rerio (Hamilton 1822), a non-native Amazonian species used as a model organism, the DOC isolated from the São Gabriel da Cachoeira (SGC) region of the upper Rio Negro reduced the diffusive losses of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ and provided a stimulation of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ uptake, demonstrating an almost full protection against the

ionoregulatory failure imposed by $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ in waters of low ionic strength (i.e., low $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , Cl– and $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ concentrations; Duarte et al., 2016). Until now, it remains unknown whether these protective effects for $D$ . rerio exerted by the native SGC DOC will be extended to a native fishes. Moreover, there is evidence that the capacity of the DOC to interact with biological membranes, which may alter their permeability (Vigneault et al., 2000; Galvez et al., 2008) as well as other basic physiological functions (Wood et al., 2003; Galvez et al., 2008; Duarte et al., 2016), is associated with the spectroscopic characteristics of the DOC molecules. Properties of DOC that promote these biological activities include its aromaticity, as indicated by its specific absorbance coefficient at wavelength 340 nm $\mathrm { S A C } _ { 3 4 0 } \mathrm { ) }$ and the proton binding capacities (PBI[ Galvez et al., 2008; Al-Reasi et al., 2013, 2016; Duarte et al., 2016; Holland et al., 2017).

With this background in mind, in the present study we characterised the spectroscopic properties of natural SGC DOC that had been isolated from black water. We tested the hypothesis that this natural SGC DOC would have protective actions against the unfavourable effects of acid water $\mathrm { ( p H 4 . 0 ) }$ exposure on the ionoregulatory homeostasis of native $C .$ . macropomum. Therefore, to address this issue, we assessed the TEP, $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ net losses, ammonia excretion rates, concentration of major ions $( \mathrm { N a } ^ { + } , \mathrm { C l } ^ { - } )$ , ammonia and urea in plasma and the specific activity of gill ionoregulatory enzymes $\left( \mathrm { N a } ^ { + } \right.$ , $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ ATPase; v-type $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ ATPase, carbonic anhydrase) of $C .$ . macropomum under two $\mathsf { p H }$ conditions $\mathrm { \ p H } 7 . 0$ and 4.0), in the presence and absence of SGC DOC.

All experimental and holding procedures followed Brazilian animal care guidelines and were previously approved by the National Institute for Research of the Amazon (INPA) animal care committee (registration number 047/2012).

# 2.1 | Water collection and DOC concentration and characterisation

Water samples for DOC collection and concentration were obtained from São Gabriel da Cachoeira (SGC) black water $( 0 ^ { \circ } 0 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { S } , 6 7 ^ { \circ } 0 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ ; dissolved oxygen $6 . 7 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { l } ^ { - 1 }$ ; temperature $2 9 . 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; conductivity $1 3 . 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { S } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ ; DOC $9 . 9 \mathrm { m g } 1 ^ { - 1 }$ ; pH 4.4; $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + } 1 4 . 3$ ; Cl– 19.2; $\mathrm { K } ^ { + } 9 . 5$ $9 . 5 ; \mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + } 2 0 . 5 ; \mathrm { M g } 2 . 4 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , on the upper Rio Negro. The black water was collected and concentrated in 2013, as described by Duarte et al. (2016). After that, the concentrate was treated with a cation exchange resin (Amberlite IR – 118 (H), Sigma-Aldrich; www.sigmaaldrich.com) to remove the influence of the cations, which were concentrated together with the DOC by reverses osmosis. Lastly, the concentrate was filtered with a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ membrane (Pall Acrodisc; www.pall.com) and stored in sealed dark polyethylene bottles (Nalgene; www.nalgene.com) at $4 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ until the physical-chemical characterisation and fish experiments were performed.

The SGC DOC was first optically characterised shortly after collection in 2013 and the results were reported by Duarte et al. (2016). However, it was then stored at $4 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ for c. 2 years until the performance of the present experiments in 2015, so we re-analysed it, using the same equipment (SpectraMax M2 fluorescence spectrophotometer, Molecular devices; www.moleculardevices.com; Agilent Cary Eclipse spectrophotometer; www.agilent.com) used in 2013, prior to use in the present study. The concentrated SGC solutions were at the same pH (7) and DOC concentration $( 5 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C l ^ { - 1 } } )$ ) for the optical characterisation in both analyses (2013 and 2015). The $\mathrm { S A C } _ { 3 4 0 }$ , an indicator of aromaticity (Curtis & Schindler, 1997)

and the fluorescent index ( $\mathrm { \Delta } I _ { \mathrm { F } }$ ; a simple ratio of emission intensity at $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ per emission intensity at $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ; both taken at an excitation wavelength of $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ), an indicator of origin (allochthonous v. autochthonous) were both determined according to McKnight et al. (2001); and the ratio of absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ to that at $3 6 5 \ \mathrm { n m } \left( { \mathrm { A b s } } _ { 2 5 4 / 3 6 5 } \right)$ , an indicator of the molecular weight, was determined according to Dahlén et al. (1996). Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC; Stedmon & Bro, 2008) was applied to determine the relative abundance of fluorescent components of DOC, representing the humic-like, fulvic-like and proteinaceous materials (e.g., tryptophan-like and tyrosine-like compounds), in the fluorescence excitation emission matrices (FEEM). For FEEM matrices, fluorescence was determined using excitation wavelength between $2 0 0 { - } 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ in $1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ increments and for each excitation wavelength the emission wavelength was scanned between $2 5 0 { - } 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ increments. For excitation-emission fluorescence measurements, standard solutions were scanned together with the samples as a control procedure to check for instrument drift, where the standard solutions were, tryptophan $( 0 . 5 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ and tyrosine ( $ { \mathrm { ~ \textrm ~ { ~ ~ } ~ } }  { \mathrm { [ 1 . 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } } }$ ; Sigma Aldrich) and a mixed solution ( $\mathrm { 1 0 ~ m g }$ of carbon $1 ^ { - 1 }$ of a well characterised DOM isolate plus $0 . 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ tryptophan and $1 . 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ tyrosine), in Milli-Q water (Merck-Millipore;   
www.merckmillipore.com). Scattering was removed by replacing it with not-a-number (NaN) in the data matrix. Inner filter corrections were not applied since the absorbance was found to be below the threshold, where inner-filter corrections are necessary (Ohno, 2002). The spectral FEEMs were modeled using the PLS Toolbox from Eigenvector Research Inc. (www.eigenvestoc.com) running on a Matlab platform (MathWorks; www.mathworks.com). PARAFAC assigned the fluorescence on a percentage basis based on the a priori assumption that there were four components (humic-like, fulvic-like, tyrosine-like and tryptophan-like) (Al-Reasi et al., 2012, 2013).

# 2.4 | Branchial transepithelial potential measurements

For the TEP measurements, fish ${ n = 8 }$ ) were cannulated intraperitoneally with catheters as described by Wood & Grosell (2008). Before surgical procedures, fish were anesthetised with buffered MS-222 (Sigma-Aldrich) in IPW $\mathrm { \ p H } 7 . 0$ or $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ ). A saline-filled PE50 catheter (Clay-Adams; Becton–Dickinson; www.bd.com) was inserted $1 { - } 2 \mathrm { c m }$ through the peritoneal wall into the coelom via a puncture site made with a 19 gauge needle, just lateral and anterior to the rectum. A 1 cm PE160 sleeve, heat-flared at both ends, was glued to the PE50 with cyanoacrylate resin and anchored to the body wall with several silk sutures, to prevent the catheter from changing depth. The fish were then transferred to individual 4 l chambers served with flowing, aerated INPA IPW, where they were held overnight, prior to TEP measurements. To minimise stress, the fish was gently transferred to a 2 l plastic chamber in which TEP measurements were made.

TEP measurements were first made in fresh IPW which was then replaced by four exchanges of the test solution (IPW pH 7.0; IPW pH 7.0 + DOC; IPW pH 4.0; IPW pH 4.0 + DOC). TEP measurements were taken $2 \mathrm { m i n }$ after the solution change. TEP was measured using 3 M KCl–agar bridges connected by Ag–AgCl electrodes to a high impedance electrometer (pHM 82 meter, Radiometer; www.radiometer.com). The measurement bridge was connected to the coelomic catheter (out of the water) and the reference bridge was placed in the surrounding water. The electrodes were checked for symmetry. TEP values $( \mathrm { m V } )$ were expressed relative to the water side as $0 \mathrm { m V }$ after correction for junction potential.

# 2.5 | Experimental set-up for flux measurements and terminal plasma and gill sampling

Fish ( $n = 8$ in each of the four treatment groups) were individually transferred to glass aquaria covered with black plastic and filled with 4 l of aerated experimental solution. These were either IPW $\mathrm { p H } 7 . 0$ , or IPW $\mathsf { p H } 7 . 0 + \mathsf { D O C }$ , or IPW $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ , or IPW p $\mathrm { H } 4 . 0 + \mathrm { D O C }$ . Fish were allowed to settle for $^ { 1 \mathrm { h } }$ in the experimental glass aquaria before starting the experimental series. At the beginning of the experiment, $1 5 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of water was removed with a pipette from each aquarium. This same procedure was repeated after 3, 9 and $2 4 \mathrm { { h } }$ of exposure to the experimental solution, corresponding to 0–3; 3–9 and 9–24 flux periods, respectively. The flux chamber was aerated throughout and the pH was monitored hourly and adjusted as necessary by the addition of 0.01 N KOH or $0 . 0 1 \mathrm { N H N O } _ { 3 }$ . The average pH values (mean $\pm$ SE) in the chambers over the $2 4 \mathrm { { h } }$ of experiment were $6 . 9 \pm 0 . 0 2$ for both IPW pH 7 and IPW $\mathsf { p H } 7 + \mathsf { D O C }$ and $4 . 1 \pm 0 . 0 1$ for both IPW pH 4 and IPW $\mathsf { p H } 4 + \mathsf { D O C }$ . Immediately after collection, water samples were stored at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until analysis of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ and ammonia concentrations. At the end of 24 hours of exposure, fish were anesthetised with buffered MS222 as above in IPW $\mathrm { \ p H } 7 . 0$ or $\mathrm { p H } 4 . 0$ ). Blood was sampled with a heparinised syringe from the caudal vein and then centrifuged $( 2 0 0 0 g , 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } , 5 \operatorname* { m i n } )$ ) for plasma separation, which was stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until analysis of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ , ammonia and urea concentrations. Immediately after blood collection, the fish was euthanised by cervical dislocation and then gill filaments were collected, frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until analysis of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ ATPase, v-type $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ ATPase and carbonic anhydrase specific activities.

# 2.6 | Plasma sodium, chloride, ammonia and urea concentrations

For determination of both sodium and chloride and urea concentrations, the plasma samples were diluted 1:1000 and 1:100, respectively. All dilutions were performed in Milli Q water (Milli-Q Integral 3, Millipore). The readings of sodium and chloride were performed by the same methods used for the water samples while plasma urea was measured by the colorimetric method of Rahmatullah & Boyde (1980). For ammonia, an enzymatic method was employed (Raichem commercial assay, Cliniqa Corporation; www.cliniqa.com) using the plasma without dilution. For all assays, measurements were made in triplicate, employing appropriate blanks, using certified commercial standards (Radiometer for $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ , Cliniqua for ammonia and Sigma-Aldrich for urea).

The net flux rates $( J _ { \mathrm { { n e t } } } )$ of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ and total ammonia ( $T _ { \mathrm { a m m } } { = } \mathrm { N H } _ { 3 } + \mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } { ^ { + } } )$ were calculated as: $J _ { \mathrm { n e t } } { = } ( X _ { 1 } { - } X _ { 2 } ) V ( T M _ { \mathrm { T } } ) ^ { - 1 }$ where $X _ { 1 }$ and $X _ { 2 }$ werethe initial and final concentration of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ or total ammonia $( \mu \mathrm { m o l } 1 ^ { - }$ 1) in the water during the flux period, respectively, $V$ was the volume (l) of the chamber, $T$ was the duration of the flux period in hours and $M _ { \mathrm { T } }$ was the fish mass in kg.

Sodium concentration was read on a Perkin-Elmer model 3100 atomic absorption spectrophotometer (AAS; www.perkinelmer.com). Chloride and total ammonia concentrations in water were measured by colorimetric methods described by Zall et al. (1956) and Verdouw et al. (1978), respectively. All measurements were made in triplicate using appropriate blanks and the same commercial certified standards as for plasma analyses.

# 2.8 | Enzyme activities in gill filaments

# 2.9 | Statistics

All data are reported as means $\pm$ SE $( n = 8 )$ ). Statistical significance was accepted at $\mathrm { P } < 0 . 0 5$ . Significant differences among treatments in TEP were determined through one-way repeated measures ANOVA followed by the a posteriori Tukey multiple comparison test. Significant differences among treatments in $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { C l } ^ { - }$ and ammonia net fluxes, their plasma concentrations, as well as $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ ATPase; v-type $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ , ATPase and carbonic anhydrase specific activities in gill filaments were determined through a one-way ANOVA, followed by a posteriori Tukey multiple comparison test. In the case of a failed normality test, a nonparametric Kruskal-Wallis test was performed. All statistical analyses and graphics employed Sigma Stat 3.5 and Sigma Plot 11.0 software (Jandel Scientific; www.systatsoftware.com).

The TEP was c. $- 1 7 \mathrm { m V }$ in neutral IPW $\mathrm { ( p H } 7 . 0 )$ but decreased significantly to $- 2 3 \mathrm { m V }$ during exposure to SGC DOC under neutral conditions $\left( \mathsf { p H } 7 . 0 + \mathrm { D O C } \right)$ . Colossoma macropomum exposed to IPW $\mathsf { p H } 7 . 0$ were in slight negative balance for $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and did not experience any significant alterations in $J _ { \mathrm { N a . n e t } }$ over time (0–3 h, 3–9 h and $9 { - } 2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ ; Figure2). However, the presence of DOC had negligible other effect on $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ balance at neutral pH. Exposure to acidic conditions in the absence of DOC (IPW pH 4.0) caused overall increases in $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ net losses which were significant relative to the IPW pH 7.0 treatment only in the first $3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ of exposure; again, $J _ { \mathrm { { N a . n e t } } }$ was significantly reduced by $9 { - } 2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ (Figure 2). # 3.4 | Ammonia excretion rates

In contrast to $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ loss rates, which tended to become smaller over time (Figures 2, 3), ammonia excretion rates tended to become greater over time, an effect that was significant by $9 { - } 2 4 \mathrm { h }$ in both DOC treatments (Figure 4). Colossoma macropomum exposed to neutral $( \mathrm { I P W } \ p \mathrm { H } \ 7 . 0 )$ and acidic (IPW pH 4.0) water in the absence of DOC did not experience any alterations in $J _ { \mathrm { a m m . n e t } }$ in any flux period and there were no significant differences between the two treatments. On the other hand, DOC exposure in neutral $( \mathrm { I P W } \ p \mathrm { H } \ 7 . 0 + \mathrm { D O C } )$ and acidic $( \mathrm { I P W \ p H \ 4 . 0 + D O C } )$ were associated with time-dependent increases in $J _ { \mathrm { { a m m . n e t } } }$ . In the latter treatment, the presence of DOC at low pH significantly increased the net ammonia excretion at both $0 { - } 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ and $9 { - } 2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , relative to both the (IPW pH 7.0) and IPW $\mathsf { p H } 7 . 0 + \mathsf { D O C }$ treatments; indeed at $9 { - } 2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ , the elevation in ammonia net negative flux seen in the IPW pH $4 . 0 + \mathrm { D O C }$ treatment was significant relative to all other treatments (Figure 4). In summary, exposure to low $\mathsf { p H }$ alone had negligible effect on ammonia excretion, whereas the presence of SGC DOC resulted in elevated $J _ { \mathrm { { a m m . n e t } } }$ at $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ , but not at $\mathsf { p H } 7 . 0$ .

# 3.5 | Plasma sodium, chloride, ammonia and urea concentrations

In accord with the increased net losses of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ , the exposure to SGC DOC in IPW pH 4.0 caused marked significant decreases in plasma concentrations of these electrolytes in $C .$ . macropomum after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of exposure (Table 3). There were no significant differences among the other three treatments. The absolute decrement in plasma $[ \mathrm { C l } ^ { - } ]$ $( c . 4 3 \mathrm { m m o l } 1 ^ { - 1 } )$ ) was larger but more variable than the absolute decrement in plasma $\mathrm { [ N a ^ { + } ] } ( c . 1 8 \mathrm { m m o l } \Gamma ^ { - 1 } )$ , in contrast with the pattern of net fluxes over $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ (Figures 2, 3). The increased ammonia excretion rates seen in $C .$ . macropomum exposed to SGC DOC in IPW pH 4.0 were accompanied by a significant increase in plasma total ammonia after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ (Table 3). Again, there were no significant differences among the other three groups. The plasma urea concentration was not altered in C. macropomum in any treatment group (Table 3).

# 3.6 | Enzyme activities in gill filaments

No significant differences in the specific activities of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ ATPase or v-type $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ , ATPase were found in gills of $C .$ . macropomum among the different experimental treatments (Table 3). The specific activity of v-type $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ , ATPase was consistently higher (by six to nine-fold) than that of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ ATPase. On the other hand, $C .$ . macropomum exposed to acidic water in the presence of DOC ( $\mathrm { I P W \ p H \ 4 . 0 + D O C } )$ had a higher value of the specific activity of carbonic anhydrase in the gills than fish exposed to neutral water either with DOC (IPW pH $7 . 0 + \mathrm { D O C } )$ or without DOC $( \mathrm { I P W } \ p \mathrm { H } \ 7 . 0 $ ) (Table 3).

# 4 | DISCUSSION

# 4.1 | DOC characterisation

In the present work, the SGC DOC molecules displayed optical properties (Table 1) indicating lower molecular weight (higher Abs254/365 ) and lower aromaticity (lower $\mathrm { S A C } _ { 3 4 0 }$ ) in comparison with the values reported by Duarte et al. (2016) for the same DOC, but measured shortly after collection. The latter values were highly unusual for freshwater DOC in general (Duarte et al., 2016, table 1), indicative of very high aromaticity and very high molecular weight and were thought to reflect conditions in the upper Rio Negro close to major terrigenous sources of this allochthonous DOC. However, in both analyses, the $I _ { \mathrm { F } }$ values were quite low confirming the allochthonous origin (Mcknight et al., 2001). Furthermore, in both studies, the PARAFAC analysis of the relative contributions of major components to total fluorescence were similar, indicating a predominance of humic-like (51– $5 9 \%$ over fulvic like moities $( 2 8 - 3 6 \% )$ as the two major allochthonous components, but the tryptophan-like $( 8 \% )$ and tyrosine-like compounds $( 5 \% )$ were not insignificant, suggesting some autochthonous input.

As the techniques used for the two analyses were identical, the most likely explanation for the differences would be degradation over the period of time that the DOC was stored in the dark at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , resulting in loss of aromatic compounds and reduction in mean molecular weight. Changes in DOC quality occurs spatially and temporally in nature between sites and even within the same sites in the Rio Negro (Holland et al., 2017). Indeed, three recent investigations on Rio Negro DOC quality for samples collected $7 0 0 { - } 8 0 0 \mathrm { k m }$ downstream from the SGC collection site have reported Abs254/365 and $\mathrm { S A C } _ { 3 4 0 }$ values much closer to those of the stored SGC used in the present study (Holland et al., 2017; Johannsson et al., 2017; O.E. Johannsson, unpubl. data).

# 4.2 | Branchial transepithelial potential (TEP), ion fluxes and effects of DOC

The TEP in freshwater fish can be interpreted as a diffusion potential predominantly regulated by the relative permeabilities of the gills to positively (mainly $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ ) and negatively (mainly Cl–) charged ions (McWilliams & Potts, 1978; Galvez et al., 2008). Under control conditions the negative potential in IPW pH 7.0 (Figure 1) would be expected to counter the preferential efflux of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , which has a higher permeability than $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ . In the present work, acid exposure alone $( \mathrm { I P W } \ p \mathrm { H } 4 . 0 )$ caused a rise in TEP by about $1 4 \mathrm { m V }$ in relation to neutral water, indicating that the relative permeabilities became more equal to one another at low pH. This response in TEP was very similar to, but in smaller magnitude, than those reported by Wood et al., (1998) for $C .$ . macropomum exposed to $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ in IPW without DOC and also to brown trout Salmo trutta L. 1758 exposed to low pH in water with low $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ concentrations (McWilliams & Potts, 1978). This shift in potential is expected, since freshwater fish challenged by acidic waters usually display a large stimulation of diffusive losses of both $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ , in addition to an inhibition of active $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ uptake and thereby experience disturbances in both TEP and ionic regulation (Wood, 1989; Gonzalez et al., 2005; Wood et al., 2011). Thus, the rise in TEP seen in $C .$ . macropomum at IPW pH 4.0 might be explained by the stimulatory effects of increased $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ on both $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { J } } ^ { \mathrm { N a } } _ { \mathrm { n e t } }$ and $\mathrm { \Delta J ^ { C } l _ { n e t } }$ of fish (Figures 2, 3), as seen as by increased $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + } \left( 0 { - } 3 \mathrm { h } \right)$ and $\mathrm { C l } ^ { - } \left( 3 { - } 9 \mathrm { h } \right)$ net losses in $C .$ . macropomum at low pH in the absence of DOC. Interestingly, $C .$ . macropomum showed a pattern of decreasing loss over time, resulting in cumulative net $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + } ( - 2 2 5 6 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } )$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ losses $( - 2 1 4 3 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } )$ about equal, which were not enough to promote disturbance in the plasma concentrations of both $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ in $C .$ . macropomum after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ under acidic conditions in the absence of DOC (IPW pH 4.0; Table 3). This was very comparable to the pattern reported by Wilson et al. (1999) in $C .$ . macropomum exposed to IPW pH 4.0 under similar conditions and indicates an ability to control ion losses over time, as seen in many acidophilic fish (Wood, 1989; Nelson, 2015).

The presence of SGC DOC clearly offered protection against the depolarising effect of low pH exposure in $C .$ . macropomum (Figure 1). At $\mathrm { p H } 7 . 0$ , the presence of DOC caused hyperpolarisation of the TEP by about $- 7 \mathrm { m V }$ , a phenomenon previously reported in rainbow trout exposed to other allochthonous DOCs sources (Galvez et al., 2008). More importantly, the presence of DOC at $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ completely prevented the depolarisation of the TEP which occurred at low pH in the absence of DOC, allowing internal TEP to remain about $1 2 \mathrm { m V }$ more negative in relation to fish at IPW $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ , an effect that would be expected to preferentially help to retain $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ . However, the $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ net losses were markedly stimulated when native $C .$ . macropomum were exposed to low pH in the presence of SGC DOC (IPW p $\mathrm { H } 4 . 0 + \mathrm { D O C }$ ) (Figs 2 and 3). Surprisingly, this occurred despite the protective effect of SGC DOC against the depolarisation of the TEP caused by low pH exposure (Figure 1). The exacerbation of ion losses by SGC DOC in the present study is completely different from the great protection against ion losses at low pH by freshly collected SGC DOC reported by Duarte et al. (2016) for non-native $D$ . rerio and by Wood et al. (2003) for native potamotrygonids, where natural DOC-rich Rio Negro water provided great protection against influx inhibition and efflux stimulation by low $\mathsf { p H }$ , relative to similar IPW lacking DOC. Nevertheless, it is similar to the results of Glover et al. (2012) who reported that native DOC did not protect $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ balance and indeed exacerbated the inhibition of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ uptake at low pH in the inanga Galaxias maculatus (Jenyns 1842), a fish native to acidic waters in New Zealand, as well as the report of Wood et al. (2003) that a commercial DOC (Aldrich humic acid) exacerbated the negative effects of low pH on potamotrygonids.

DOC molecules are known to directly interact with gill membranes and are also known to affect the permeability of cell membranes (Vigneault et al., 2000), where the interaction has been shown to become more intense at low pH (Campbell et al., 1997). How they might prevent or exacerbate ion losses under acid conditions, however, remains

unknown. Using biogeochemical modelling, Wood et al. (2003) concluded that DOC could not protect the fish gill against $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ binding but would reduce epithelial $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ binding, so they hypothesised that DOC might actually substitute for $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ in stabilising paracellular junctions, reducing tight junction permeability and thereby reducing diffusive ion losses (Wood et al., 2003, 2011; Gonzalez et al., 2005). Additionally, they suggested that DOC might concentrate $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ ions (by complexation) locally at the apical membrane of the gill in a similar manner to mucus (Handy, 1989), preferentially delivering the ions to the active uptake sites. In previous studies with $C .$ . macropomum (Wood et al., 1998) and S. trutta (McWilliams & Potts, 1978), the addition of $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ to the IPW attenuated the potential shift caused by low pH exposure, while at the same time reducing the accompaning diffusive ion losses. However, it did the former by raising the TEP to a less negative value at $\mathsf { p H } 7 . 0$ , while having little effect on the value at $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ . Therefore, the actions of DOC in the present study were different from those of elevated $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ , because the TEP values at both $\mathrm { p H } 7 . 0$ and $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ were both made more negative. Furthermore, ion losses at low pH were not reduced but rather exacerbated, as discussed above. From this, we conclude that the mechanisms of action of SGC DOC and $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ in controlling the diffusive ionic losses at low $\mathsf { p H }$ differ from one another. Overall, no clear picture yet emerges, in accord with the conclusions of a recent review (Nelson, 2015).

Although the unexpected large stimulation in both $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ was seen mainly in the first $^ { 9 \mathrm { { h } } }$ of exposure to acidic conditions in the presence of SGC DOC, the ionic losses of $C .$ . macropomum were quite similar than those seen in fish following $9 { - } 2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ exposure to all other treatments, demonstrating that the fish were able to control ionic losses over time. However, at acidic conditions when SGC was present $( \mathrm { I P W } \ p \mathrm { H } 4 . 0 + \mathrm { D O C } )$ the cumulative net $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + } ( - 8 4 5 5 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } )$ and $\mathrm { C l } ^ { - } \left( - 5 3 6 0 \mu { \mathrm { m o l } } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } \right)$ losses of $C .$ . macropomum were markedly higher over $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , where the net loss of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ exceeded net $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ losses by $5 7 \%$

(Figures 2, 3). Nevertheless, there was a modest reduction in $[ \mathrm { N a } ^ { + } ]$ but a three-fold greater fall in $[ \mathrm { C l ^ { - } } ]$ in plasma of $C .$ . macropomum at IPW pH $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } 4 . 0 + \mathrm { D O C }$ (Table 3). These data suggest that acid exposure was accompanied by a substantial redistribution in ions between intracellular and extracellular compartments and that this redistribution differed in the presence and absence of DOC. In future, it will be of interest to measure tissue ion levels during low pH exposure in C. macropomum .

Through the analysis of the DOC quality (discussed earlier), it is apparent that the SGC DOC after the storage became less aromatic (lower $\mathrm { S A C } _ { 3 4 0 }$ ) and the molecules became smaller (higher Abs254/365; Table 1), which could change the way these molecules interact with biological membranes. Overall, our data suggest that the stored SGC DOC used here does not have an ameliorative effect to protect $C .$ . macropomum against ionoregulatory disturbances promoted by low $\mathsf { p H }$ exposure, as seen with fresh SGC in $D$ . rerio by Duarte et al. (2016), but rather it exacerbates the ionic losses of both $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ in this species. The greater loss of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ than of $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ is in accord with the more negative TEP. Although the exposure to stored SGC DOC at low $\mathrm { p H }$ helps fish to maintain a more negative TEP, it seems not to limit the overall gill permeability to $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ . These findings are contrary to McWilliams & Potts (1978) and Wood (1989), suggesting that the positive shift in TEP may not be the principal factor driving net $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ losses at low $\mathsf { p H }$ in this acid-tolerant fish species.

# 4.3 | Ammonia excretion, plasma ammonia and urea concentrations and branchial enzyme activities

The present data (Figure 4) confirm previous findings that $C .$ . macropomum are highly resistant to stimulation of $J _ { \mathrm { A m m . n e t } }$ under exposure to acidic waters (Wood et al., 1998, 2018), in contrast with many other freshwater fishes (Gonzalez & Dunson, 1989; Wood, 1989;

Gonzalez & Wilson, 2001; Kwong et al., 2014; Duarte et al., 2016). In most freshwater fish, increased ammonia excretion at low $\mathsf { p H }$ is explained by the increased passive diffusion of $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ , which is facilitated by the acid-trapping of $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ in the boundary layer of the gills (Wilkie, 2002). The almost negligible $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ partial pressures in the ambient water serve as a perfect sink for excretion via $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ diffusion from blood to water (Gonzalez et al., 2005). Additionally, plasma ammonia levels often rise associated with increased metabolic production of ammonia when fish are stressed by acidity (Kwong et al., 2014). However, the plasma concentrations of ammonia in $C .$ . macropomum at IPW $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ did not increase and were kept at levels similar to those of animals under neutral $\mathsf { p H }$ (in either the presence or absence of DOC) and close to values previously reported to $C .$ . macropomum during exposure to IPW pH 4.0 (Wood et al., 1998). Therefore, the lack of elevation in $J _ { \mathrm { A m m . n e t } }$ seen in $C .$ . macropomum exposed to IPW pH 4.0 probably reflects a lack of elevation in ammonia gradient and a lack of elevated production rate in these acidophilic fish.

In contrast, increased ammonia excretion was seen in $C .$ macropomum exposed to SGC DOC under acid conditions, particularly during 9–24 h of exposure. Increases in ammonia excretion were also reported in potamotrygonids of the Rio Negro and exposed to low pH in natural black water (Wood et al., 2003). Interestingly, in that study, increases in ammonia net fluxes were facilitated in reference water by the addition of $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ , demonstrating that native DOC molecules may interact with gill membranes in the same manner as $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ ions with respect to ammonia excretion mechanisms (Wood et al., 2003). In $D$ . rerio, a stimulation in $J _ { \mathrm { A m m . n e t } }$ was seen in fish exposed to $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ in IPW in the presence of two different sources of DOC, which was thought to be essential for animals to keep $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ uptake coupled to ammonia excretion under acidic conditions (Duarte et al., 2016, 2018). This coupling was thought to be associated with an upregulation of the Rh-protein– ${ - } \mathrm { N a } ^ { + } { - } \mathrm { N } \mathrm { H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ exchange metabolon in the gills (Weihrauch et al., 2009; Wright & Wood, 2009). It is

unknown whether this metabolon is operative in the C. macropomum . In C. macropomum exposed to $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ in IPW with SGC DOC, there was a marked increase in plasma total ammonia concentration (Table 3), which suggests an increment in oxidative metabolism towards enhanced proteolysis and amino-acid oxidation, resulting in elevated ammonia production (Sinha et al., 2013). Notably, the plasma concentration of urea, which comes largely from uricolysis rather than from protein–amino acid oxidation (Wood, 1993), was not elevated (Table 3). Colossoma macropomum are unusual in largely relying on protein–amino acid oxidation to maintain their aerobic metabolism (e.g., between $6 0 { - } 7 0 \%$ support; Pelster et al., 2014; Wood et al., 2017). Altogether, our data indicate that under low pH exposure in the presence of SGC DOC, C. macropomum enhanced ammonia production, resulting in both ammonia accumulation in plasma and increased ammonia excretion.

In three acidophilic characids of the Amazon Basin, tested in naturally acidic, DOCrich water, $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ uptake appeared dependent on ammonia excretion and the mechanism appeared to be strongly dependent on intracellular carbonic anhydrase and basolateral $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ ATPase in the gill epithelium (Wood et al., 2014). In the present study, the activities of both $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ ATPase and v-type $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ , ATPase in the gills of $C .$ . macropomum were very similar in all four treatment groups (Table 3), suggesting that neither acidic conditions nor the presence of SGC DOC affected these enzymes. However, as seen for the increased ammonia excretion and concentration in plasma, the activity of carbonic anhydrase in the gills of $C$ . macropomum was significantly stimulated in IPW $\mathrm { 9 H 4 . 0 + D O C }$ . Although the specific mechanisms for apical $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ uptake in $C .$ . macropomum are unknown, the present results suggest that SGC DOC may act to increase both carbonic anhydrase activity and ammonia excretion order to generate the required electrochemical gradient to drive $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ uptake under ion-poor acidic conditions. Further exploration of this topic is an important question for future examination in the $C .$ . macropomum and other acidophilic Amazon fishes.

Thus, in the present study, stored SGC DOC did not protect the native Amazon species Colossoma macropomum against the deleterious effects of low pH exposure. The positive shift in the TEP of $C .$ . macropomum exposed to SGC DOC at $\mathsf { p H 4 . 0 }$ demonstrated that this may not be an important factor driving net $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ losses at low pH. Indeed, the $C .$ . macropomum under these conditions experienced greater net $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ losses, decreases of $\mathrm { N a } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ concentrations in plasma and elevated plasma ammonia levels and excretion rates, relative to those exposed in the absence of DOC. These findings could be related to species-specific differences and changes in DOC properties during storage. In future studies, it will be preferable to use DOC that has been freshly collected from the wild, rather than stored DOC. In addition, it will be of interest to investigate whether protective elements in the DOC are simply lost over time in storage, or whether they degraded into molecules that have the opposite action. Also, future studies should address the effects of DOC with different physico-chemical characteristics on the ionic and osmotic homeostasis of freshwater fishes under short-term acidic exposure and during acclimation to low pH, contributing to a better understand of the mechanistic action of DOC molecules on the gill physiology of freshwater fishes.
