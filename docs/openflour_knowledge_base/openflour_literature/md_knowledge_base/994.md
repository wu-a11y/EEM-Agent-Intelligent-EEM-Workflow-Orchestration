# a b s t r a c t

Mangroves are increasingly recognized as an important component of regional and global carbon cycles especially for their high carbon storage capacity. Global estimation of mangrove soil organic carbon (SOC) storage requires detailed regional studies, but estimates of SOC data in deep soils are currently missing in many countries. Furthermore, little is explored on the molecular composition of mangrove SOC. Here, we assessed the SOC stock in a Trat mangrove forest (Thailand) by collecting deep soils $( 3 . 5 \mathrm { m } )$ and analyzed the SOC composition for better understanding its potential sources and influencing factors. The Trat mangrove forest had four times higher SOC stock than has been considered for Thai mangrove forests, with the per-area SOC stock of nearly $1 0 0 0 \mathrm { M g C h a ^ { - 1 } }$ which rivals that of Indo-Pacific mangrove forests. # 1. Introduction

Mangrove forests effectively store organic carbon primarily in the underneath soils as soil organic carbon (SOC), making them one of the most carbon-dense ecosystems on Earth (Donato et al., 2011). Despite occupying no more than $0 . 0 6 \%$ of the Earth's surface (Hamilton and Casey, 2016), mangroves contribute substantially to coastal biogeochemical cycles. Carbon stored beneath mangroves, together with salt marshes and seagrass, is termed Blue carbon, and has been a target of conservation efforts (McLeod et al., 2011). However, like many other ecosystems, the ongoing climate change (e.g., extreme climatic events and sea level rise) and anthropogenic disturbances (e.g., conversion to agriculture or aquaculture and clearing) pose a great threat to mangroves (Adame et al., 2021; Richards and Friess, 2016). In order to understand and quantify the consequences of such impacts, the global mangrove SOC stock has been investigated in the last decade (Atwood et al., 2017; Ouyang and Lee, 2020).

Despite the high SOC storage potential of mangroves, little is known why mangrove SOC persists and accumulates over the long period (Kida and Fujitake, 2020; Li et al., 2018; Yarwood, 2018). In terrestrial soils and marine sediments, it is generally accepted that SOC persistence is the integrated consequence of SOC's inherent recalcitrance (molecular composition), physical protection, and chemical interactions with minerals and metals (Sollins et al., 1996). However, related discussion is scarce among mangrove soil studies (Kida and Fujitake, 2020). The molecular composition of mangrove SOC is much less studied (Kida et al., 2019b; Ono et al., 2015; Prasad et al., 2017) compared to dissolved organic matter (DOM) in water, the latter frequently studied with spectroscopic methods such as absorption and fluorescence spectroscopy (Maie et al., 2007; Yamashita et al., 2010). As such, it is unknown, for instance, how difference in SOC composition influences its response to disturbances. SOC in a mangrove soil is derived from a range of sources, such as mangrove roots and litter, benthic mats within mangrove forests, primary production in river- and seawater, and terrestrial organic matter derived from catchments (Bouillon et al., 2008; Kristensen et al., 2008). The overall composition of SOC reflects the relative contribution from such sources. Studying SOC composition is fundamentally important because it can give information on its sources and because it largely controls SOC's biogeochemical reactivity and ultimately persistence in the environment (Kida et al., 2019b; Roth et al., 2019).

The primary factor that determines a SOC stock per unit area in mangrove forests is arguably the soil depth (Donato et al., 2011). Other factors such as temperature (Atwood et al., 2017), forest age (Alongi, 2018; Lunstrum and Chen, 2014), salinity (Kida et al., 2017), and coastal environmental settings (Twilley et al., 2018) become important when the soil depth is normalized (e.g., $1 \mathrm { m }$ ). Because there is generally a clear inverse relationship between SOC contents $( \% S O C )$ and soil bulk density, mangrove forests with peat do not necessarily have a higher per-area SOC stock compared to those with a mineral soil (Atwood et al., 2017). A recent review reveals that half of the studied countries were missing estimates of SOC data deeper than $5 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ (Atwood et al., 2017). This means that regions with only shallow SOC data most likely underestimate their potential SOC stocks. One of these underestimated mangrove forests are those of Thailand, where only two studies sampled soils deeper than $1 \textrm { m }$ (Alongi, 2012; Bukoski et al., 2017). Reassessing the potential SOC stock in Thailand mangrove forests is thus warranted. The aim of this study was two-fold: (1) to reassess the potential SOC stock in Thailand mangrove forests by collecting and analyzing deep soils and (2) to better understand potential sources and controlling factors of mangrove SOC by analyzing its composition.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study site

The study was conducted in a secondary mangrove forest in the estuary of the Trat River, eastern Thailand $( 1 2 ^ { \circ } 1 2 ^ { \prime } 1 5 . 5 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N }$ , $1 2 0 ^ { \circ } 3 3 ^ { \prime } 5 5 . 0 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E }$ Fig. 1). Since the 1980s, timber and charcoal production at the site has ceased, and Department of Marine and Coastal Resources of Thailand has managed this area. The regional climate of Trat is monsoon tropical with distinct dry (November–April) and rainy (May–October) seasons. The average air temperature from 2009 to 2013 was $2 8 . 0 \pm 0 . 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C .$ The mean annual precipitation is $5 2 0 7 \pm 8 1 6 \mathrm { { m m } }$ . The sum of the precipitation in the rainy seasons from 2009 to 2016 was $8 7 . 7 \%$ of the total (Trat Meteorological station, Thai Meteorological Department). Majority of the land use are Para rubber (Hevea brasiliensis), oil palm (Elaeis guineensis) and fruit trees (Land Develop Department, Thailand).

A permanent study plot $( 5 0 \mathrm { m } \times 1 2 0 \mathrm { m } )$ ) was established in 2008 (Poungparn et al., 2009; Umnouysin et al., 2017) (Fig. 1). The study plot clearly showed four vegetation zones from the creek to the inland forest, although Rhizophora species is relatively evenly distributed throughout the site (Fig. 1). At the creek's edge, some Sonneratia caseolaris and Avicennia alba were dominant $0 { - } 1 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ from the river, “Sonneratia–Avicennia zone”). The second zone ( $1 0 { - } 4 5 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ from the river, “Avicennia zone”) was dominated by A. alba, which has a high density of short, slim pneumatophores (so called pencil roots), with some Rhizophora apiculata and R. mucronata. The third zone $4 5 { - } 9 0 \mathrm { m }$ from the river, “Rhizophora zone”) was primarily dominated by R. apiculata and R. mucronata with some A. alba, Bruguiera gymnorhiza, Ceriops tagal, and Xylocarpus granatum. The Rhizophora species has many stilt roots penetrating into soil approximately $5 { - } 1 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ deep. A dense root necromass existed below that depth (Poungparn et al., 2009). The last zone $9 0 { - } 1 2 0 \ \mathrm { m }$ from the river, “Xylocarpus zone”) was dominated by $X .$ granatum, with R. apiculata, B. gymnorhiza, and C. tagal as minor constituents (Fig. 1). X. granatum has small buttress roots, and large amounts of root necromass were densely accumulated in soil (Poungparn et al., 2009). The net primary productivity of the forest ranged between 21 and $2 9 \mathrm { ~ t ~ } \mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { ~ y r } ^ { - 1 }$ during 2008 to 2018 (Poungparn et al., 2020).

The zonation occurred because of differences in the elevation from the river to the inland plot, which caused variations in the inundation period (Poungparn et al., 2009), where the inundation period decreased with increasing distance from the river. The relative elevation compared to a datum point at the river edge was 0.1–0.2, 0.2–0.4, 0.4–0.6, and $0 . 6 { - } 0 . 8 \ \mathrm { m }$ for the Sonneratia–Avicennia, Avicennia, Rhizophora, and Xylocarpus zones, respectively. The Sonneratia–Avicennia and Avicennia zones had a relatively long inundation period of more than $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ per day, while in the inland zones (the Rhizophora and Xylocarpus zones), the inundation period was shorter (5–11 h) (Poungparn et al., 2009). The Trat estuary is semi enclosed, and the study site is affected by diurnal tidal cycles (once per day) rather than waves. The highest tidal level in the wet season is typically around 1.5, 0.9, 0.7 and $0 . 3 \mathrm { m }$ in the Sonneratia, Avicennia, Rhizophora, and Xylocarpus zones, respectively (Poungparn et al., 2009).

# 2.2. Sampling

Soil sampling was conducted in July 2016 using an open-face peat sampler (430-mL volume), which minimized disturbance and compaction during the sampling process. Samples were taken from three vegetation zones (Avicennia, Rhizophora, and Xylocarpus zones) in triplicate, every $5 0 \mathrm { c m }$ down to $3 . 5 \mathrm { m }$ $[ n = 6 2$ , one of the deepest samples is missing in the Rhizophora zone, Table S1). The samples were sent back to the laboratory in the dark, dried in an air-dryer at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , and weighed. Subsamples of each air-dried soil were collected and dried at $1 0 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to constant mass for bulk density determination, determined as the mass of the dried core soil divided by the volume of the peat sampler. Air-dried soil samples were gently ground and all passed through a $2 { \cdot } \mathrm { m m }$ sieve; no samples had gravels $> 2 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ . Roots retained on the sieve (including those of the subsamples for bulk density determination) were washed with tap water, dried at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ and weighed. A portion of sieved air-dried samples was hand-picked of remaining roots using tweezers and by electrostatic attraction (Kaiser et al., 2009) and passed through $0 . 5 \mathrm { - m m }$ sieve for SOC concentration determination and alkali extraction. We did so with the intention to minimize a contribution from fresh plant debris such as roots to the SOC analysis and to improve sample homogeneity. The exclusion of fine roots from soil samples for the elemental analysis also minimizes the double-count of fine root contributions to SOC stock, those measured by the elemental analysis of soils and those previously estimated by a core method (Chalermchatwilai et al., 2011) (Table 1).

# 2.3. Bulk soil analysis and alkali extraction

The $0 . 5 \mathrm { - m m }$ sieved samples were analyzed for C concentration using an elemental analyzer (FlashEA 1112, Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA), with or without HCl treatment (2 M HCl overnight). The elemental analysis was conducted in duplicate using $4 { \mathrm { - } } 7 { \mathrm { m g } }$ of dry sample per measurement. The analytical precision was $0 . 0 6 \%$ for C and $0 . 0 1 \%$ for N, respectively, and average values are presented. Because C concentration did not change whereas N concentration decreased by $1 0 \%$ with the HCl treatment, which was likely due to inorganic N dissolution, we used the results obtained without HCl treatment for this study. Thus, reported values are $\% S 0 C$ and $\% \mathrm { T N }$ (total N). Particle size was determined in air-dried soils by the pipette method after $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ digestion. Soil C density was obtained by multiplying soil bulk density by $\% S 0 C$ . Soil C pools at each sampled depth were obtained by multiplying C density with the depth interval $( 0 . 5 \mathrm { m } )$ . The per-area SOC stock was calculated by summing up soil C pools at each sampled depth down to $3 . 5 \mathrm { { m } }$ . To obtain the biomass C stock, we multiplied previously reported biomass (Chalermchatwilai et al., 2011; Umnouysin et al., 2017) by commonly used C proportions of 0.47, 0.39, and 0.50 for aboveground trees, belowground roots, and woody debris biomass, respectively (Murdiyarso et al., 2015). Standard error in the total ecosystem C stock in each vegetation zone was computed by propagating standard errors of component C pools.

# 2.4. UV–vis and fluorescence spectroscopy combined with PARAFAC

UV–vis absorption spectra $2 4 0 { - } 6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , 1-nm increment) were measured using a UV–visible spectrophotometer (V-630, JASCO, Tokyo, Japan) with 1-cm path length quartz cuvette at ambient temperature. Measurements were baseline-corrected using the same solvent as samples. Spectral slope $( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ was calculated using a linear fit to the natural log-transformed Napierian absorption coefficient $( \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) spectrum in the ranges of $2 7 5 { - } 2 9 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Helms et al., 2008). $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ has been related to the degree of photodegradation (Helms et al., 2008; Kida et al., 2019a) and used as a source differentiation (terrestrial versus marine) tool in estuarine studies (Fichot and Benner, 2012). Typically, a higher (steeper) $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ value suggests a decrease in aromaticity and an increase in the degree of photodegradation of DOM (Fichot and Benner, 2012; Helms et al., 2008). DOC-specific UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { \Delta S U V A _ { 2 5 4 } } ,$ ), an index of DOM aromaticity (Weishaar et al., 2003), was calculated from absorbance and DOC data of the corresponding samples after correction for dilution factors.

Fluorescence Excitation-emission matrix (EEMs) spectroscopy were collected with a spectrofluorometer (F-2700, Hitachi HighTechnologies, Tokyo, Japan) equipped with a $1 5 0 \mathrm { W }$ xenon arc lamp at room temperature using $5 \mathrm { n m }$ excitation (Ex) and emission (Em) slit widths, scan speed of $1 5 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ , an Ex range of $2 4 0 { - } 5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ at $1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ increments, and an Em range of 280–600 nm at 2 nm increments. The EEMs were corrected for all necessary steps to remove spectral biases and normalized in Raman units (RU) (Murphy, 2011; Murphy et al., 2013). EEMs were analyzed by parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) to separate overlapping fluorescence signals into distinct fluorescent components with independent spectral characteristics (Supporting Information, S1) (Murphy et al., 2013). The obtained four components were named according to their rounded fluorescence emission maximum: $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 2 5 }$ , C470, $\mathsf { C } _ { 5 4 0 }$ , and $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 3 0 }$ , with variance explained by each component decreasing sequentially from $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 2 5 }$ to $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 3 0 }$ (Fig. 4, Fig. S1).

# 2.5. Statistical analysis

All statistical analysis was conducted using R statistical language (version 4.1) (R Core Team, 2019). Least-square regression analysis was conducted with sampling depth as explanatory and SOC stock as dependent variables. Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed on basic soil properties (%SOC, %TN, and particle size distribution) and the four fluorescence components to identify the parameters most associated with soils from each vegetation zone and depth. Because the data used for PCA were all compositional data (CoDa), which carry relative information (Egozcue and Pawlowsky-Glahn, 2019), we conducted a CoDa version of PCA (CoDa-PCA). For CoDa

PCA, the original CoDa in a $D$ -dimensional constrained space was centered logratio (clr)-transformed by:

$$
\operatorname { c l r } ( \mathbf { x } ) = \left( \begin{array} { l }  \ln { \frac { x _ { i } } { g ( \mathbf { x } ) } } \right) _ { i = 1 , \ldots , D } \operatorname { w i t h } g ( \mathbf { x } ) = { \sqrt [ { \object Object] { x _ { 1 } \cdot x _ { 2 } \cdots x _ { D } } } } , \end{array}
$$

using the robComposition package (Templ et al., 2011). Here, the three compositions (SOC and TN, particle size, and relative abundance of PARAFAC components) were taken as a single composition to compute the clr. Subsequently, PCA was performed on clr-transformed data. We refer to the literature for the special interpretations of CoDa-PCA biplots (Aitchison and Greenacre, 2002; Egozcue and Pawlowsky-Glahn, 2019).

Other variables (depth, root abundance, soil bulk density, soil C/N ratio, soil C density, $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ , and $\mathrm { S U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ) were fitted to the PCA ordination space using envfit function in the R package vegan with $P -$ -values calculated over 999 permutations (Oksanen et al., 2019). $\mathsf { S U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ was not associated with the sample distribution in the ordination space at the 0.01 significance level. In addition, we used additive models to fit a response surface for C density to the PCA ordination space to inspect the distribution of C density in the ordination space using the ordifurf function (Oksanen et al., 2019). A smooth 2-d function of the sample scores on the first and second PCA axes (PC1 and PC2) was generated using default thin-plate regression splines and used to model the individual measured C density. These a posteriori fittings of external variables are often used for other ordinations techniques but have never been used for CoDa-PCA. Nevertheless, their application to the CoDaPCA ordination space should be appropriate because these functions use axis scores and the compositional nature of the original data does not play a role anymore. However, discussion regarding the (quantitative) relationships between variables used for constructing CoDa-PCA (clr variables) and external variables projected by the mentioned functions should be avoided. We therefore discuss only the associations between samples and respective variables regarding Fig. 5.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Soil C stock of Trat mangrove forest and its mangrove contributio

We found that Trat mangrove forests have almost four times higher SOC stocks $( 9 6 1 . 7 \pm 2 6 . 6 \mathrm { M g C h a } ^ { - 1 }$ , $n = 9$ cores) than the average of previous estimates of Thai mangrove forests $( 2 5 1 \pm 1 2 2 \mathrm { \ : M g C \ : h a ^ { - 1 } } ;$ ) (Table 1) (Alongi, 2012; Ouyang and Lee, 2020). The SOC stocks varied little between vegetation zones (Avicennia, $9 4 3 . 6 ~ \mathrm { { \ M g C } } ~ \mathrm { { h a } } ^ { - 1 }$ Rhizophora, $9 4 9 . 2 \mathrm { M g C h a } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Xylocarpus, $9 9 2 . 2 \mathrm { M g C } \mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 }$ ) (Table 1). Combined with the previously reported C pools of ecosystem compartments (above- and belowground biomass and woody debris) (Chalermchatwilai et al., 2011; Umnouysin et al., 2017), the Trat mangrove forest had total ecosystem C storage of $1 1 1 3 . 2 \pm 6 3 . 8 \mathrm { M g C h a } ^ { - 1 }$ (Table 1). Soil was by far the largest C pool, accounting for $8 5 \% { - } 9 0 \%$ of total ecosystem C pool (Table 1), corroborating the past findings (Donato et al., 2011; Murdiyarso et al., 2015; Ouyang and Lee, 2020). Smaller pools of C were stored in aboveground biomass $( 7 \% - 1 1 \% )$ , in belowground biomass $( 3 \% - 6 \% )$ , and in woody debris $( 0 . 1 \% - 0 . 3 \% )$ .

It's worth noting that $\% S 0 C$ remained relatively high $( > 1 . 5 \% )$ even below 1-m depth where mangrove roots are sparse, while bulk density of deep soils tended to be higher than surface soils (Fig. 2ab). Consequently, C density did not decrease as clearly as $\% S 0 C$ did (Fig. 2c). This finding means that soil sampling depth is critical for accurate estimation of mangrove belowground C storage. We found a very clear correlation between sampling depth and estimated SOC stock in the Trat mangrove forest, irrespective of the vegetation zones (Fig. 3). Limiting the sampling depth to $5 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ and $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ , SOC stock would be 176 $\mathsf { M g C h a } ^ { - 1 }$ and $3 2 1 \mathrm { M g C h a } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively (Fig. 3). With many countries missing SOC data deeper than $5 0 \mathrm { c m }$ (Atwood et al., 2017), regions with only shallow SOC data highly likely underestimate their potential SOC stocks. The clear linear correlation between sampling depth and SOC stock when samples are collected from multiple vegetation zones (Fig. 3) is a promising finding as it enables estimation of SOC stock in deeper $\left( \sim 3 . 5 \mathrm { m } \right)$ uncollected soils in mangrove forests and strongly supports past studies that extrapolated SOC stock estimated with shallower soils to $1 \mathrm { m }$ (e.g., Atwood et al., 2017; Ouyang and Lee, 2020). Contrarily, most roots $( > 2 \mathrm { m m }$ ) were present within the top $1 \mathrm { m }$ in the Rhizophora and Xylocarpus zones and $0 . 5 \mathrm { m }$ in the Avicennia zone (Fig. 2f, Table S1). The $\% S 0 C$ distribution of the Rhizophora and Xylocarpus zones reflected the distribution of root abundance (Fig. 2af). A recent work also shows that a sizeable fraction of fine root production occurs deeper than $3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ (Arnaud et al., 2021). As fine root abundance was estimated based on $3 0 – \mathrm { c m }$ deep core (Chalermchatwilai et al., 2011), the reported fine root stock in this study is likely conservative (Table 1).

It is possible to conservatively estimate the mangrove contribution to the SOC pool based on the clear $\% S 0 C$ depth distribution (Fig. 2). Here, we tentatively assumed that the sharp increases in $\% S 0 C$ in the surface soils of the Rhizophora and Xylocarpus zones were solely due to inputs from mangroves (Fig. 2af) and that there was no mangrove contribution deeper than $1 \textrm { m }$ . Using the average $\% S 0 C$ values of the soils deeper than $1 \textrm { m }$ in the Rhizophora $( 1 . 9 0 \% )$ and Xylocarpus $( 1 . 8 9 \% )$ zones, and considering the C density (Fig. 2c), the mangrove contribution to the SOC pool in the Rhizophora and Xylocarpus zones was estimated as both $2 7 \%$ . This simple estimation, however, could not be applied to the Avicennia zone because of its relatively uniform $\% S 0 C$ depth distribution (Fig. 2a). Mangrove roots were found throughout the $3 . 5 \mathrm { m }$ cores (Fig. 2f, Table S1), thus the actual mangrove contribution must be larger than this conservative estimate. The depth distribution of the soil fluorescence components (Fig. 4) as well as roots (Fig. 2f) indicates that the influence of mangrove-derived organic matter remained at least until $2 \mathrm { m }$ depth.

# 3.2. Soil fluorescent components and implications for estuarine SOC composition and persistence

We analyzed the composition of Trat mangrove SOC by EEMPARAFAC after the extraction of the alkali-extractable fraction and found that four fluorescence components constituted the fluorescent fraction of SOC in the studied mangrove forest (Fig. 4, Fig. S1). In order to deduce sources and biogeochemical reactivity of the fluorescent components, we compared the spectral characteristics of components with the published organic fluorescence spectra in an open-access spectral library (OpenFluor) (Murphy et al., 2014) (Table S2). $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 2 5 }$ and $\mathsf { C } _ { 5 4 0 }$ have been assigned as a terrestrial humic-like component (Yamashita et al., 2010) (Table S2). Santín et al. (2009) called them a fulvic acid-type and humic acid-type component, respectively, because they were enriched in fulvic and humic acid fractions extracted from Spanish estuarine soils and sediments (Santín et al., 2009). These humic-like components ( $\cdot C _ { 4 2 5 }$ and $\mathsf { C } _ { 5 4 0 }$ ) have been considered as ubiquitous, meaning these are found in almost all terrestrial and water environments and derived from both plant (soil) and microbial sources (Kida et al., 2019a; Wünsch et al., 2017). $\mathsf { C } _ { 5 4 0 }$ was also related to lignin phenol concentrations (Walker et al., 2013). $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 7 0 }$ was reported as a terrestrial humiclike component that was dominant in headwaters and soil leachates (Garcia et al., 2018) (Table S2). As for ${ \sf C } _ { 3 3 0 }$ it is difficult to assertively assign this UV-wavelength component, because fluorescence of a wide range of substances such as aromatic amino acids, lignin phenols, and mangrove tannins is all associated in this region of EEM $\mathrm { E m } < 3 5 0$ nm) (Albinsson et al., 1999; Hernes et al., 2009; Maie et al., 2007). High $\% C _ { 3 3 0 }$ was associated with Rhizophora/Xylocarpus surface soils but not with Avicennia surface soils where proteinaceous materials were assumed to be enriched due to its vicinity to the river (Figs. 4,5). Thus, this component may represent a mangrove-derived aromatic compound (Table S2). A poor association with root abundance may indicate that $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 3 0 }$ was derived from other parts of mangroves, such as twigs and leaves (Fig. 5). The possible mangrove origin of this component, as well as overall mangrove contribution to the SOC pool, was supported by exceptionally high net primary productivity of the studied mangrove forest $( > 2 0 \mathrm { t h a } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ) (Poungparn et al., 2020).

The existence of the same (or spectrally indistinguishable) components ( $\yen 425$ and $\mathsf { C } _ { 5 4 0 }$ ) in a similar estuarine environment (Santín et al., 2009) is worth noting because the vegetation was very different; the Spanish salt marsh was colonized by sea purslanes (Halimione portulacoides) and rushes (Juncus maritimus) compared to mangroves in this study. This congruence may be derived from common environmental processes that take place in similar conditions (estuarine saline soils). Santín et al. (2009) found that these components were always present in different chemical fractions of alkali-extractable SOC. These findings suggest that SOC in estuarine soils and sediments may consist of fluorescent components that give rise to the highly similar fluorescence properties irrespective of vegetation or chemical fractions.

Molecular characterizations of estuarine SOC such as nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy and pyrolysis-gas chromatography/mass spectrometry also found highly similar structural characteristics of SOC across estuaries with an enrichment of aliphatic moieties (de la Rosa et al., 2011; Dodla et al., 2012; Santín et al., 2008). A contribution from lignin remains is another important feature that influences estuarine SOC structural characteristics (de la Rosa et al., 2011; Dodla et al., 2012; Kida et al., 2019b). The detection of lignin-derived signals indicates that a part of the estuarine SOC structure is derived from vascular plants. Because partially oxidized lignin contributes several visible wavelength humic-like fluorescence (Boyle et al., 2009; Senesi et al., 1989), the reoccurring humic-like components $\yen 425$ and $\mathsf { C } _ { 5 4 0 }$ ) may represent lignin-derived moieties that underwent biogeochemical processes that are common to estuarine soils and sediments. The rest of humic-like components $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 7 0 }$ , which was absent in the Spanish salt marsh, may be a fluorescence component unique to mangroves. A joint analysis of fluorescence components and stable isotopes $\cdot ^ { \mathrm { { \delta } } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { { C } } }$ and $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N }$ ) and specific biomarkers may give a detailed understanding of the origin of this component.

The vertical profile of fluorescence components revealed the selective preservation of components of mangrove SOC (Fig. 4). The sharp decline of the relative abundance of the mangrove-derived aromatic compound $\left( \mathsf { C } _ { 3 3 0 } \right)$ indicated that this component was labile relative to the other fluorescence components. Contrarily, $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 2 5 }$ was persistent considering its relative increase with depth (Fig. 4). The persistence nature means that molecules responsible for this fluorescence are continuously found in soil either because the molecules are resistant to biodegradation or because they are constantly recycled through the microbial degradation network. The reoccurring, ubiquitous humic-like components ( $\mathbf {  { C _ { 4 2 5 } } }$ and $\mathsf { C } _ { 5 4 0 }$ ) made up $5 7 \mathrm { - } 6 7 \%$ of the fluorescent fraction of SOC (Fig. 4). These persistent components may represent the bulk of SOC that resided for presumably the long-term in the deep mangrove soil $\mathrm { > } 1 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ , Fig. 2a).

# 3.3. Factors determining the vegetation- and depth-wise mangrove soil characteristics

The $\% S 0 C$ ranged from $1 0 . 7 \%$ in a Xylocarpus surface soil to $1 . 0 \%$ in Avicennia subsurface soil $( 0 . 5 { - } 1 \mathrm { m } )$ ) (Fig. 2a; Table S1). The $\% S 0 C$ correlated strongly with $\% \mathrm { T N }$ $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 9 3$ ), while negatively with bulk density (exponential, $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 8 6$ ), as is often found in mangrove soils (Donato et al., 2011; Kida et al., 2019b). The C/N ratios showed a clear spatial variation, with higher values (up to 29) found in surface soils of the Rhizophora and Xylocarpus zones (Fig. 2g, Table S1), indicating a strong influence of mangrove litter (Kida et al., 2019b). However, the generally low C/N ratios in the Avicennia zone (13–19) suggest that mangrove litter or other woody tissue did not contribute substantially to the SOC in this vegetation zone (Fig. 2g). The low C/N ratio in the surface soils of the Avicennia zone may indicate a fast carbon-selective loss during early diagenesis of mangrove litter due to passive leaching or microbial decomposition (Dittmar and Lara, 2001), or inputs of marine organic matter with low C/N ratios due to the longest inundation period. Almost invariant C/N ratios in the soils below $2 \textrm { m }$ depth in the respective vegetation zones imply similar decomposition rates for carbon and nitrogen during the subsequent organic matter degradation in the soil, as was previously observed in an Amazonian mangrove (Dittmar and Lara, 2001). A clear difference in C/N ratios in the deep soils between vegetation zones likely reflects different organic matter sources. Particle size distribution (soil texture) varied largely (Sand $1 9 \% - 8 3 \%$ , Silt $6 \% - 2 6 \%$ Clay $6 \% { - } 6 4 \%$ , Fig. 2i–h, Table S1) and coarser particles tended to be more dominant inward (Avicennia zone $<$ Xylocarpus zone). SOC characteristics evaluated by UV–vis absorption $\mathrm { S U V } \mathrm { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and S275–295) and fluorescence spectroscopy (EEM-PARAFAC) also revealed a high spatial variation (Fig. 2de, Fig. 4, Table S1). The generally high $\mathsf { S U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and low $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ values suggest a limited contribution from marine microbial organic matter in general (Fichot and Benner, 2012; Helms et al., 2008; Kida et al., 2019a; Weishaar et al., 2003).

Factors that determine vegetation- and depth-wise soil characteristics were revealed by CoDa-PCA, which explain $8 5 . 6 \%$ of the compositional variance in the overall data (Fig. 5). We found a clear separation between soils from the Avicennia zone and the other zones and between surface and deeper soils (Fig. 5). Surface soils in the Rhizophora and Xylocarpus zones clustered at the positive end of PC1 and they were associated with a high abundance of organic matter (root, SOC, TN), high C density, high C/N, low bulk density, and low humic-like fluorescence proportions. Surface soils in the Avicennia zone at the lower left part of the ordination space were associated with steep spectral slope (S275–295), fine soil particles (clay and silt), and low $\% C _ { 3 3 0 }$ . In contrast, deeper soils in the Rhizophora and Xylocarpus zones mainly clustered at the negative end of PC1, and they were associated with high humic-like fluorescence proportions and low abundance of organics. Deeper soils in the Avicennia zone were clustered around the center of the ordination space, meaning that they had intermediate soil properties (Fig. 5).

The response surface shows that C density systematically increases from the negative to positive end of PC1, albeit not linearly (Fig. 5). C density ranged from $1 2 . 5 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ $. \mathrm { A } 3 0 . 5 { - } 1 \ \mathrm { m } )$ to $4 6 . 9 \ : \mathrm { m g } \subset \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ $\mathrm { R 1 } ~ 0 { - } 0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m }$ ). The percentage of variance in C density explained by the response surface of the PCA axis scores was 67.5 $P < 0 . 0 0 1$ ). The highest C density was observed in Rhizophora and Xylocarpus surface soils $( 0 { - } 0 . 5 \ : \mathrm { m } )$ followed by their subsurface soils $( 0 . 5 { - } 1 \mathrm { m } )$ , while the lowest was observed in Avicennia subsurface soils $( 0 . 5 { - } 1 \ \mathrm { m } )$ . The low C density in the Avicennia surface soils was compensated for by high C density in the Rhizophora and Xylocarpus surface soils, leading to a clear correlation between sampling depth and estimated SOC stock in the Trat mangrove forest (Fig. 3). C density was most strongly associated with root abundance (Fig. 5). In the Avicennia zone, roots existed in high abundance only in the surface soils $\left( 0 { - } 0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m } \right)$ because of the Avicennia's shallow root morphology (Fig. 2f, Table S1). Consequently, Avicennia subsurface soils $( 0 . 5 { - } 1 \mathrm { m } )$ did not necessarily receive higher root inputs than deeper soils. The difference in surface soil C density between Avicennia and the other two zones indicates that mangrove species influences surface soil SOC accumulation. However, C density in deeper soils was higher in the Avicennia zone (Figs. 3, 5), suggesting that understanding the variation in deep soil C pool requires factors other than recent vegetation such as historical sedimentation processes and decomposition after sedimentation. As clear differences in fluorescence components’ abundance were observed in deep soils (Fig. 4), linking these components to specific biogeochemical components or processes may decipher historical sedimentation processes.

Lower C density of the upper $1 \textrm { m }$ of soil compared to deeper soils in the Avicennia zone may be related to its location. Because the Avicennia zone was closest to the river and had the longest inundation period, the Avicennia surface soils likely received fine particles in river- and seawater and accumulated them actively (Furukawa et al., 1997). Steeper $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ values are typically associated with marine origins and an increase in the degree of photodegradation (Fichot and Benner, 2012; Helms et al., 2008). Association of Avicennia surface soils with steep $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and high clay contents suggest the inputs of marine and/or photodegraded organic matter brought together with fine mineral particles (Fig. 5). Such particle inputs will inevitably dilute the $\% S 0 C$ of the existing soil if organic C contents of the particles are low.

Detailed examination of the CoDa-PCA inferred factors and processes that impacted SOC abundance and composition (Fig. 5). Sand, clay, and $\% S 0 C$ proportionally varied greatly considering the longest arrows (Fig. 5). Consequently, these variables were those having high relative variations (logratios), reflected by long links between the vertices of the arrows of clr-variables (Fig. 5) (Aitchison and Greenacre, 2002; Egozcue and Pawlowsky-Glahn, 2019). On the one hand, the long links between the vertices of the arrows of sand, clay, and $\% S 0 C$ and humic-like fluorescence components indicate that the ratios of a member of the former group and a member of the latter group varied greatly throughout the dataset (Fig. 5). This suggests that the relative abundance of humic-like fluorescence was not dependent on SOC contents and soil texture. On the other hand, the long links among the vertices of sand, clay, and $\% S 0 C$ indicate that their ratios also varied largely. These results collectively suggest that the soil particle size (sand versus clay) was not a controlling factor of either SOC content or quality. Meanwhile, samples associated with high $\% S 0 C$ and low humic-like fluorescence proportions (Rhizophora and Xylocarpus surface soils) were also associated with high root abundance, and vice versa (Fig. 5). Therefore, SOC characteristics in the Trat mangrove forest were significantly impacted by mangrove roots rather than soil textures. This is contrary to past findings reporting moderate positive correlations between $\%$ SOC and finer particle size (Ouyang et al., 2017; Xiong et al., 2018). There could be several reasons behind this difference: first, some of the past studies did not consider root abundance in their analysis (Ouyang et al., 2017; Xiong et al., 2018); secondly, the sampling depth of the past studies examining influencing factors of %SOC in mangroves only ranged between 0.1 and $1 \textrm { m }$ (Ouyang et al., 2017; Xiong et al., 2018), while the sampling depth of this study was much deeper $( 3 . 5 \mathrm { m } )$ and influencing factors can vary by depth (Xiong et al., 2018). Our results highlight the importance of mangrove roots as a source of SOC in the Trat mangrove forest.

# 4. Conclusion

This study shows that the Trat mangrove soils are clearly categorized according to their organic matter characteristics. Root abundance is a major controlling factor that affects SOC composition and C density, while soil grain size hardly plays a role in determining SOC properties. Molecular composition of SOC should simultaneously be investigated together with mangrove C pool estimates to give information on sources and controlling factors of mangrove soil C pool. SOC compositional changes after mangrove clearing or regeneration should also be investigated to better understand the response of the mangrove ecosystems to such human activities. EEMPARAFAC and multivariate statistics provide a fast and informative means for this purpose. Finally, this study reveals the possibility to extrapolate SOC stock estimation based on shallow soils $( < 1 \mathrm { ~ m } )$ t o deep soils (up to $3 . 5 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ ) in mangrove forests when samples are collected from multiple vegetation zones, thereby contributing significantly to a refined estimation of global SOC stock in these vegetated coastal ecosystems.
