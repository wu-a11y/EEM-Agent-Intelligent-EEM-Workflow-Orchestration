Advanced oxidation processes (AOPs) are a promising technology for removing emerging organic contaminants (EOCs) from wastewater, while the coexisting dissolved organic matter (DOM) significantly hinders effective oxidative removal of EOCs. Understanding the effects of DOM on EOCs removal in real water matrices is crucial for designing more efficient AOPs. Herein, a pilot-scale integrated in-situ ozonation/ultrafiltration process was conducted to remove EOCs from hospital wastewater. Furthermore, the ozonation reactivity of DOM and EOCs was contrastingly analyzed at the molecular level. Our integrated process selectively removed $8 5 ~ \%$ of EOCs at a relatively low oxidant dosage of $0 . 4 4 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { O _ { 3 } }$ per mg dissolved organic carbon (DOC). Using Fourier-transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FT-ICR MS), we reveal that ozone selectivity for EOCs arose from their overall reductive characteristics of being more aromatic and unsaturated in molecular composition compared to DOM. Additionally, specific DOM compounds potentially facilitate EOCs removal. This work provides pilot-scale experience in EOCs removal from hospital wastewater and in-depth understanding of DOM’s role in the integrated process, guiding the development of effective AOPs for selective removal of emerging contaminants.

# 1. Introduction

Emerging organic contaminants (EOCs) [1], such as pharmaceuticals and personal care products (PPCPs) [2,3], endocrine-disrupting chemicals (EDCs) [4], and other substances [5,6], pose significant threats to human health and ecosystems due to their frequent detection and reported toxicities. Various EOCs removal technologies have been developed, including membrane filtration, physicochemical adsorption, advanced oxidation processes (AOPs), and biological degradation [7]. However, due to their trace concentration and persistence in real wastewater, individual treatment processes merely concentrate or partially degrade EOCs, rather than achieving complete, efficient treatment [8].

Integrating membrane filtration with heterogeneous catalytic ozonation simultaneously has proven to be a promising technology for the efficient oxidative degradation of enriched EOCs within membrane pores [9,10]. Numerous pilot-scale studies have demonstrated the excellent performance of integrated ozone-based AOPs for EOCs removal in wastewater and drinking water treatments [10–12]. A common challenge in these practical applications is that mg/L levels of dissolved organic matter (DOM), coexisting with trace EOCs at ng/L or $\mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ levels, tend to consume oxidants more readily than EOCs, resulting in limited EOCs removal efficiency and necessitating higher oxidant dosage [13]. However, the detailed inhibition mechanism is still unclear due to the complex structure and diversity of DOM in real water [14]. Thus, the lack of thorough understanding of DOM’s roles in AOPs hampers the optimization of processes for high-efficiency EOCs removal, limiting the widespread industrial application of AOPs in various real water matrices [15]. Recently, Fourier-transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FT-ICR MS) has enabled molecular-scale characterization of DOM composition and transformation with ultrahigh resolution [16,17]. This technique serves as a powerful tool for investigating the effect of DOM on EOCs removal.

Hospitals generate approximately $7 5 0 \mathrm { L } /$ day of wastewater per bed, containing various pollutants including organic matter, PPCPs, EDCs, and pathogenic microorganisms [18]. Therefore, hospital wastewater (HWW), as a typical point source of EOCs, can introduce high concentrations of EOCs into municipal sewerage systems if not specifically treated [19–21]. This can strain EOCs treatment in subsequent sewerage networks and wastewater treatment plants [22], ultimately reducing the economic viability of EOCs treatment at the municipal scale and hindering water reuse. Few pilot-scale studies have employed AOPs to remove EOCs from real HWW, demonstrating that ozone [23], or ozonebased integrated process [24,25], can effectively remove EOCs. However, the complex and high concentration of DOM in HWW resulted in up to tens or even over $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ of ozone consumption to achieve approximately $9 0 ~ \%$ removal efficiency of target EOCs [24], which is neither cost-effective nor sustainable. There are still theoretical and practical knowledge gaps in overcoming the limitations imposed by DOM, and achieving strategic selective removal of EOCs in real HWW to

In this work, we conducted a pilot-scale integrated process mainly basing on in-situ ozonation/catalytic membrane ultrafiltration for EOCs treatment at a large hospital in China, achieving effective and selective removal of EOCs with a low ozone dosage in real HWW. Detailed analysis of DOM composition and transformation illustrated the interaction of DOM with EOCs and the origin of selective removal of EOCs in ozone-based AOPs. Overall, this work provides molecular-level insights into EOCs selective removal mechanisms in pilot-scale HWW treatment, and is expected to guide the theoretical optimization of AOPs for better selective removal of target organic contaminants in real wastewater.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Pilot-scale integrated process configuration

An integrated pilot plant (Fig. 1) with a capacity of $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } / \mathrm { d }$ was established inside a large 2450-bed general hospital in Zhuhai, Southern China, and operated continuously for 45 days. During operation, feed water of the integrated system was the effluent normally discharged to municipal sewerage network, disinfected with sodium hypochlorite administered by the hospital to meet local discharge standards. General water quality of the raw HWW during the study is listed in Table S1.

As shown in Fig. 1a, the pilot process mainly consisted of three treatment units: coagulation-sedimentation (C-S), in-situ ozonation/ catalytic ceramic membrane filtration $\left( \mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 } \right)$ , and carbon filtration (Filter) in series. The integrated $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit contained catalytic Mn–Al flat ceramic membranes with a total effective filtration area of $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ (Fig. 1b), under membrane module allowing for in-situ ozone aeration during membrane filtration. Two parallel filtration tanks, each with dimension of $0 . 0 5 \times 0 . 0 5 \times 1 . 3 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ , were filled with adsorbents to a height of $_ { 1 . 0 \textrm { m } }$ .

Polymeric aluminum chloride (PACl) solution $( 3 . 0 ~ \mathrm { w t \% } )$ ) was injected to raw water through the pipeline mixer at an optimal dosage of 25 $\mathrm { { \ m g / L } }$ . The settled water passed through the sedimentation tank and then flowed into the membrane tank by gravity. Membrane filtration was conducted at a constant flux of $4 0 \mathrm { L } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { h }$ (LMH). The ozone-containing gas was directly supplied to the bottom of the membrane module via a titanium alloy aerator, facilitating in situ ozonation. Considering the best EOCs removal performance, in-situ ozone dosage was finally set at $1 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ after 15 days of optimization, corresponding to $0 . 4 4 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ per mg dissolved organic carbon (DOC). The membrane module was backwashed with the final filter effluent for $_ { 6 0 ~ s }$ every $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ of filtration with a flux of $1 0 0 \ \mathrm { \ L M H }$ to remove membrane fouling and recover membrane filterability. Two filters were filled with granular activated carbon (GAC, HuaHui, China) and activated anthracite (AA, HuaHui, China), respectively. GAC has a particle size of $0 . 6 { - } 2 . 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , a specific surface area of $1 0 5 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { g } .$ , and an iodine adsorption value of $9 0 0 \mathrm { m g } / \mathbf { g } ,$ , while AA has a slightly lower iodine adsorption of $7 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / g }$ . At a filtration rate of $4 \mathrm { m / h }$ , the empty bed contact time (EBCT) for the GAC and AA filter was controlled at $1 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , and the filter effluent finally flowed into a reclaimed water tank by gravity. For simplicity, the pilot influent, coagulation-sedimentation tank effluent, and in-situ ozonation/ membrane tank effluent are abbreviated as raw water, C-S, and ${ \mathrm { C M } } / { \mathrm { O } } _ { 3 } ,$ respectively. GAC and AA filtration tank effluents are collectively referred as “filter” due to their similar removal performance (see raw data for each filter effluent in Table S2). With the optimal operating conditions, the integrated system was operated stably and continuously for 30 days from March to April 2024 to evaluate its long-term performance in HWW treatment.

# 2.2. Catalytic ceramic membrane

The Mn-doped catalytic ceramic membranes were fabricated through a solid-state sintering method. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Text S1.1) images revealed that membrane layer has a sub-100 nm pore size (Fig. 1c). The membrane is primarily composed of ${ \tt O } { \tt - } { \tt A } { \tt l } _ { 2 } { \tt O } _ { 3 } ,$ , with the membrane layer doped with manganese oxide as a catalyst (atomic ratio of $0 . 8 8 ~ \%$ for Mn, Table S3). The X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Text S1.1) results of Mn $\boldsymbol { 2 } \mathsf { p } _ { 3 / 2 }$ (Fig. 1d) was wellfitted to two distinct peaks at 641.1 eV and $6 4 3 . 0 \ \mathrm { e V }$ , corresponding to the binding energies of $\mathrm { M n ^ { 3 + } }$ and $\mathrm { M n } ^ { 4 + }$ , respectively [27]. The multiple coordination environments of Mn enhance catalytic activity of the membrane [11,28], enabling it to efficiently activate ozone and generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) like hydroxyl radicals (⋅OH) and singlet oxygen $( ^ { 1 } 0 _ { 2 } )$ under space confinement [9,29–31] (see Text S1.2 for further discussion).

# 2.3. Conventional water quality analysis

The pH and turbidity of water samples were measured in-situ using portable instruments. Specifically, turbidity was measured with a turbidity analyzer (TL2310, HACH, USA), and pH was measured by a pH analyzer (HQD, HACH, USA). Additionally, water samples were collected in triplicate from different treatment units of the integrated process, sealed in polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) containers, and then transported to laboratory within $2 4 \ \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ for subsequent analysis. The concentrations of chemical oxygen demand $\left( \mathrm { C O D } _ { \mathrm { C r } } \right)$ , total nitrogen (TN), $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ -N and total phosphorus (TP) were measured according to the Chinese National Environment Protection Agency standards (China). Water samples were filtered with a $0 . 4 5 – \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter (Whatman Uniflo, USA) before the detection of DOC and florescent compounds. The concentrations of total organic carbon (TOC) and DOC were determined using a TOC-VCPH (Shimadzu, Japan).

# 2.4. 3-D EEM and PARAFAC analysis

The three-dimensional excitation-emission matrix (3-D EEM) of water samples was measured by fluorescence spectroscopy (F-7000, Hitachi, Japan). The scan speed for emission wavelengths were $2 , 4 0 0$ $\mathrm { n m / m i n }$ . Other operating parameters are same with our previous work [17].

Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was used to identify the main components of the EEMs [32] on combined EEM datasets $( n = 7 3$ , performed with the DOMFlour toolbox [33] in MATLAB R2024a (MathWorks, USA). Split-half validation and residual analysis were used to identify and validate the fluorescent DOM component, resulting in four identified components: C1, C2, C3, and C4 (Fig. 2c).

# 2.5. EOCs analysis

EOCs in water samples, including 37 types of PPCPs and 6 types of EDCs, were measured by high-performance liquid chromatographytandem mass spectrometry (HPLC-MS/MS, Shimadzu, Japan) according to the methods from our previous work [12] (see details in Text S2). All the target compounds of EOCs are listed in Table S4 including their abbreviations, classifications, and physicochemical properties such as molecular weight (MW), pKa, charging state, LogKow and Log D (pH 7.4) values. The potential environmental implications of detected EOCs were calculated in terms of risk quotient (RQ) [12] (see details in Text S3).

# 2.6. FT-ICR MS analysis

The FT-ICR MS (solariX, Bruker Daltonics, Germany), equipped with a 15.0 Tesla superconducting magnet and an electrospray ionization source in negative ion mode, was used to characterize the DOM in water samples. The acquired datasets were analyzed using DataAnalysis 5.0 (Bruker) and Composer 1.5.6 (Sierra Analytics, USA) software. The DOM was classified based on the matched molecular formulas, and various descriptive parameters of DOM, including the double bond equivalent (DBE), the modified aromaticity index $\scriptstyle ( { \mathrm { A I } } _ { \mathrm { m o d } } )$ , and the nominal oxidation state of carbon (NOSC), were calculated. For each sample, weighted values for each descriptor were calculated based on the relative intensities of the mass spectral peaks. More details on the operating parameters of the FT-ICR MS, the molecular formula assignment, the basis of DOM classification, and the specifics of each DOM characteristic are provided in Text S3. Given that all samples originated from the same hospital wastewater matrix, relative intensities were used for the semiquantification of compounds, which enabled the analysis of DOM transformations in the integrated process [34]. For the DOM of effluent samples from neighboring units in the integrated process, molecules were classified into removed, produced, and resistant molecules according to a cutoff of $\pm ~ 3 0 ~ \%$ relative intensity change [17].

# 2.7. Statistical analysis

All quantifications were independently performed with at least three replications. Sample-based standard errors were calculated using the built-in STDEV.S function in Excel 2016 and are shown as error bars in Fig. 2a, 2d, 3c, & 4a. The relationships between EOCs and DOM were analyzed by Spearman correlation coefficients (Fig. 5a, 5b, Fig. S20). All correlation matrices were computed using OriginPro 2021. Significance was judged based on the $p$ -value from variance analysis, with $p < 0 . 0 5$ indicating a significant difference at the $9 9 ~ \%$ confidence level.

# 3. Results and discussions

# 3.1. Long-term operational performance

During long-term operation, the integrated system not only effectively reduced the turbidity of HWW but also removed organic contaminants. Specifically, turbidity was reduced from $5 1 . 2 \mathrm { N T U }$ in the raw water to $0 . 0 8 \mathrm { N T U }$ in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ effluent, attributed to the excellent interception effect of ceramic ultrafiltration membranes (Fig. S1b). The integrated system effluent exhibited a mean reduction of $5 3 . 6 \%$ in $\mathrm { C O D } _ { \mathrm { C r } } ,$ $7 4 . 0 \%$ in TOC, and $7 0 . 7 \%$ in DOC compared to the raw water (Fig. 2a). Due to the limited settling efficiency of the sedimentation tank, some unsettled flocs flowed into the membrane tank, resulting in an abnormal increase in turbidity (average 306 NTU, see Figs. S1b, S2) and organic contents (Fig. S3) in membrane tank, posing additional challenges for EOCs removal and maintenance of the $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit. However, in-situ ozone aeration effectively mitigated membrane fouling through direct oxidation of organic foulants and bubble-induced hydraulic flushing. The transmembrane pressure (TMP) of ceramic membrane filtration stabilized at $- 5 . 8 \pm 0 . 7$ kPa over the 30-day operational period, with only minor fluctuations observed later (Fig. 2b), reflecting the good impact resistance of the integrated process.

Moreover, the DOM in HWW were also effectively removed through the integrated process. Three-dimensional fluorescence excitationemission matrix (3D-EEM) spectra demonstrated the gradual elimination of DOM throughout the treatment process (Fig. S4). The total fluorescence intensity decreased by $2 . 2 ~ \%$ , $1 7 . 2 ~ \%$ , and $7 8 . 2 ~ \%$ in C-S, $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ and filter units, respectively (Fig. 2d). Further parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis differentiated four characteristic components through fluorescence spectra (Section 2.3), designated as C1, C2, C3, and C4 (Fig. 2c). Due to the limited studies of DOM from HWW, we determined the potential classification of these four compounds based on reported DOM from natural or anthropogenic matrix. According to the excitation/emission $\left( \operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m } \right)$ wavelengths of each peak (Fig. S5), C1 and C2 are considered to be associated with terrestrial [35,36] and microbial sources [37] of humic-like compounds, respectively. C3 can be ascribed to a protein-like or tryptophan-like substance [37,38]. C4, with a very low Ex/Em wavelength $\mathrm { ( E x / E m } = 2 2 5 \ \mathrm { n m / 2 8 7 \ n m ) }$ , is identified as a tyrosine-like constituent [39].

The proportion variation of four PARAFAC components throughout the integrated process is illustrated in Fig. 2d. Tryptophan-like substances (C3) and terrestrial humic-like substances (C1) were most prevalent in raw water, followed by microbial humic-like compounds (C2). The high levels of C3 and C2, in conjunction with the optical index, suggest active microbial and bacterial metabolism in the raw HWW (Fig. S6, Text S4). The removal of humic-like compounds (C1 and C2) was not significant in either the C-S unit or $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit. In contrast, the GAC and AA filter appeared to remove microbial sources of humic-like compounds more effectively (Figs. S7a, b). This may be due to the greater utilization of these substances by microorganisms growing in the filter media [40] (Fig. S8). Notably, the proportion of tyrosine-like constituents (C4) sharply increased from $1 9 ~ \%$ in raw water to $5 2 ~ \%$ in filter effluent, and was also elevated in C-S effluent, suggesting that it may be released during coagulation (Fig. S7d). These findings suggest that tyrosine-like substances are more recalcitrant than other DOM components, possibly containing to persistent organic compounds unique to hospital wastewater.

# 3.2. Long-term removal performance of EOCs

The large amount of detected EOCs in HWW generated during medical activities are the target contaminants of this study. The total concentrations of PPCPs and EDCs in the HWW were $1 8 3 . 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ and $2 7 . 3 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ , respectively (Fig. 3a, b). These EOCs are categorized into 10 types according to their medical uses (Table S4), and the most abundant pharmaceuticals in the raw water were antibiotics, nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs (NSAIDS), analgesics, and dermatology drugs; whereas the detected EDCs were all estrogens (Fig. 3c, S9).

The integrated process showed excellent performance in removing EOCs from HWW. PPCPs in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit were reduced by $5 1 \%$ , and then further removed by nearly $9 0 \ \%$ after treatment with carbon filters (Fig. 3a). In comparison, the total concentration of EDCs rose to $3 1 . 3 \mu \mathrm { g } /$ L after C-S unit, and were subsequently removed by $1 7 . 2 ~ \%$ by $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit, finally with $5 3 \ \%$ removed by filters (Fig. 3b). The integrated process also effectively reduced the environmental risk levels originated from these compounds (Fig. S10). Specifically, analgesics, cardiovascular drugs, neurohormonal drugs, stimulants, and diuretics could be almost completely removed by the integrated process. Antibiotics, dermatological drugs, and NSAIDS were removed with efficiencies of 91 $\%$ , $8 5 \%$ , and $8 2 \%$ , respectively (Fig. 3c). The four most abundant EOCs in the raw water—ofloxacin, acetaminophen, ibuprofen, and salicylic acid, were significantly removed with efficiencies of $9 7 ~ \%$ , $9 9 \%$ , $8 5 ~ \%$ , and $8 6 ~ \%$ , respectively (Fig. 3d, e, f, & g). Additionally, morphine, a potent opioid analgesic [41], was detected in HWW at a concentration of $3 6 . 7 ~ \mathrm { n g / L }$ , indicating the release of anesthetic drugs from hospitals (Fig. S11 and Text S5). Over $8 7 \ \%$ of morphine was oxidized and decomposed in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit, with its concentration in filter effluent falling below the detection limit $\mathrm { \Omega } [ 0 . 1 \mathrm { n g / L } ]$ , demonstrating the complete removal of morphine by our integrated process.

Overall, the integrated process achieved a nearly $9 0 ~ \%$ removal of PPCPs in HWW with an ozone dosage of less than $0 . 4 4 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { O } _ { 3 } / \mathrm { m g }$ DOC. In contrast, previous pilot-scale studies demonstrated that ozone-based AOPs typically require an ozone dosage of 0.8 to $1 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { O _ { 3 } / m g }$ DOC to achieve a comparable removal efficiency of pharmaceutical compounds in HWW [23,42,43] (see Table S5). The $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit in our study exhibited several-fold improvements in oxidant utilization, resulting in significant chemical cost saving. This efficient ozone utilization was attributed to the high activation efficiency of ozone by doped manganese oxide catalysts, as well as the high available specific surface area and space confinement of the sub- $\cdot 1 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ catalytic membrane pores. An analysis of equipment assets and operating costs (Text S6, Table S6) estimated the unit cost of the integrated treatment at $0 . 3 8 4 ~ \mathrm { R M B } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ . The low ozone dosage and long service life of ceramic membranes made this cost significantly lower than other reported costs for treating EOCs in HWW [24,44], and comparable to costs incurred for EOCs removal from WWTPs secondary effluent using similar treatments [11,12]. In addition, the removal efficiency of EOCs by $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit was more than three times higher than that of DOC removal. This difference indicates that the integrated process achieved high selective removal of EOCs through in-situ ozonation even under the adverse influence of organic matters in real HWW treatment.

Furthermore, significant differences in the sensitivity of ozone to different EOCs were observed. For instance, ofloxacin and acetaminophen demonstrated $7 2 \%$ and $8 7 ~ \%$ removal in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit, respectively (Fig. 3d, e), while ibuprofen and salicylic acid exhibited only $2 4 \%$ and $2 9 \ \%$ removal, respectively (Fig. 3f, g). These discrepancies may be primarily due to differences in the molecular structures of EOCs, such as specific types of functional groups and heteroatoms. Ofloxacin has O and/or N-containing heterocycles and other heteroatoms like fluorine; while acetaminophen contains an amide group, where nitrogen electrons can be delocalized into the ring. Similarly, morphine, which has unsaturated rings, including O-containing heterocycle and aliphatic amine structure, was $8 6 . 7 \%$ removed under in-situ ozonation in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit (Fig. S11). Since lone electron pair on the nitrogen is susceptible to electrophilic attack by ozone, and simultaneously substituents further increase the electron density of nitrogen, in this way resulting in rapid reactions with ozone of ofloxacin, acetaminophen and morphine [45,46]. In contrast, ibuprofen and salicylic acid lack such reactive sites, similar to them are also ethinyl estradiol and estriol, which were removed by only $1 2 ~ \%$ and $2 \%$ under $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ treatment (Fig. S12). Pearson correlation analysis also showed a moderate positive correlation between the removal efficiencies of EOCs in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit and the presence of N-containing heterocycles and heteroatoms in their corresponding chemical structures (Fig. S13). These results in real HWW treatment were consistent with previous studies on ozone oxidation kinetics in synthetic wastewater, indicating that chemical compounds containing heterocycles or ionizable nitrogen exhibit strong ozone reactivity [45–47]. Accordingly, the selective ozonation of different structural EOCs likely accounts for the observed removal differences of EOCs in aqueous matrices with high concentrations of DOM, such as HWW.

# 3.3. Molecular composition and transformation of DOM

DOM is recognized as a significant limiting factor in oxidizing trace EOCs by AOPs during water and wastewater treatment industries. The substantial influence of EOCs molecular structure on their ozonation removal efficiencies inspired us to investigate the DOM’s roles in oxidation of EOCs. To this end, we thoroughly characterized and analyzed the molecular composition and transformation of DOM during HWW integrated treatment using FT-ICR MS analysis. As control, the membrane effluent without ozonation and the subsequent filter effluent were identified as CM and CM-Filter, respectively. The intensity of mass spectra (Fig. S14a) varied consistently with trends in DOC concentration and fluorescence intensity (Fig. 4a, S14b, and S15). They all showed only a slight decrease after ceramic membrane filtration with or without in-situ ozonation, suggesting that DOC removal in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit was limited and primarily attributed to membrane filtration. In contrast, the crucial role of ozonation was reflected in the significant removal of EOCs, especially PPCPs removal, as discussed in former sections.

Fig. 4b shows the categorization of DOM under different bases (Text S3.3) and their corresponding proportions calculated by relative intensities. DOM in raw water of HWW was mainly lignins-like substances, lipids, aliphatics (Aliph), and unsaturated hydrocarbons (UnHy) compounds (also see Fig. S16a). In terms of elemental composition, CHO molecules constituted the highest proportion of DOM in raw HWW $( 3 0 . 4 ~ \% )$ , followed by CHON $( 2 6 . 4 ~ \% )$ , CHOS $( 2 4 . 8 ~ \% )$ and CHONS $( 1 8 . 3 ~ \% )$ ). Following membrane filtration with or without in-situ ozonation, the proportions of condensed aromatics (CoAr), aliphatics, and proteins increased, while those of lipids and lignins-like compounds decreased. Subsequently, the $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ effluent passed through the filter, and the proportions of lignins-like compounds and tannins in DOM increased, while the proportions of lipids and UnHy decreased greatly. The proportions of CHONS and CHOS notably decreased, suggesting potential desulfurization processes. Carboxy-rich alicyclic molecules (CRAM) made up $2 3 . 4 \%$ of all DOM molecules in the raw water (also see Fig. S16b), rising to $4 2 \%$ in the filter effluent.

The van Krevelen diagrams (Fig. 4c) based on $_ { \mathrm { O / C } }$ and $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C }$ ratios illustrate the distribution and transformation of DOM throughout the integrated process. In raw water, DOM was mainly distributed in the upper left section of the van Krevelen diagram, characterized by lower O/C and significantly higher H/C $\mathrm { ( O / C _ { w a } = 0 . 4 4 }$ , $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C } _ { \mathrm { w a } } = 1 . 4 6$ , see Table S7). The H/C ratio in HWW was higher than reported characteristics of natural water bodies such as lakes [48], rivers [17]and groundwater [49] $\mathrm { ( H / C _ { w a } }$ ranging from 1.09 to 1.32). Meanwhile, the modified aromaticity index $\scriptstyle ( { \mathrm { A I } } _ { \mathrm { m o d } } .$ , see Text S3.4) of raw water was extremely low at only 0.05. In comparison, the reported $\mathrm { \bf A _ { \mathrm { m o d } } }$ values for natural water range from 0.24 to 0.33 [17,48,49]. Thus, the DOM in HWW contains a little amount of unsaturated and aromatic structures such as double bonds and unsaturated rings, which are commonly considered as crucial reactive sites for ozonation [46]. The remaining panels in Fig. 4c depict the individual and coupled effects of ozonation, ceramic membrane, and filter on DOM, with changes in overall intensity and element class corresponding to the trends shown in Fig. 4a and 4b.

To reveal potential chemical reaction pathways in the integrated process, DOM molecules were further classified as removed, produced, or resistant molecules [34] (Section 2.5). Compared to membrane filtration without ozonation, more molecules were removed and produced in the $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit, suggesting more active chemical reactions during membrane filtration with in-situ ozonation (Table S8). Moreover, molecules with low $_ { \mathrm { O / C } }$ ratios were effectively removed during in-situ ozonation in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit (Fig. 4d), forming compounds with higher $_ { \mathrm { O / C } }$ together with lower $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C }$ ratios [17]. Wherein lignins-like substances accounted for the highest proportion of the removed compounds after being treated by $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit, followed by CoAr and UnHy. The most produced compounds were lignins-like compounds, aliphatic, and proteins. The resistant molecules contained relatively most CRAM, indicating their greater difficulty of removal (Fig. 4e).

The saturation and redox properties of the molecules in the $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit were evaluated via the nominal oxidation state of carbon (NOSC) and different oxygen extracted double bond equivalent per carbon ((DBE-O)/C) [50] (Fig. 4f, Text S3.4). Most removed molecules were reduced and unsaturated compounds, making them more susceptible to oxidation by ozone. Correspondingly, the produced molecules exhibited higher saturation. Resistant molecules were mainly located in the center of the coordinate, with some reduced and unsaturated compounds in the upper left region. Notably, after being treated by filter tank, the intensity of resistant DOM in the center region significantly decreased (Fig. S19c), suggesting these disappeared molecules may represent biologically assimilable organic carbon generated during ozonation in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit. DOM in the upper left portion remained recalcitrant, indicating potential association with the tyrosine-like constituents C4, which possessed very limited removal efficiency by filter (Fig. S19).

# 3.4. Interactions between EOCs and DOM in AOPs

To further reveal the interactions between DOM and EOCs during ozone-based AOP treatment of HWW, the correlations between EOCs concentration in water samples and the corresponding DOM characteristics weighted by relative intensity were analyzed (Fig. 5a). In addition, the correlations between the removal efficiencies of EOCs in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit and organic matters (Fig. S20) were nearly opposite to those in Fig. 5a. EOCs showed positive correlations with total fluorescence intensity and four PARAFAC compounds, particularly terrestrial humics (C1) and protein-like substances (C3). The tyrosine-like compounds C4 in HWW was significantly positively correlated with dermatologic drugs, stimulants, and diuretic (primarily salicylic acid, theophylline, and caffeine), suggesting that C4 has similar structural or molecular characteristics to these pharmaceuticals.

EOCs were positively correlated with the $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C }$ ratio and significantly negatively correlated with the O/C and NOSC values of DOM. This suggests that EOCs had higher concentrations in integrated process when DOM had relatively more hydrogen atoms or fewer oxidizing functional groups, indicating a more reduced state of DOM. Specific to DOM category, EOCs were positively correlated with lipids and UnHy. This is predictable because: (1) several EOCs (e.g., antibiotics, NSAIDS, and estrogens, etc.) themselves are structurally classified as lipids-like compounds; (2) most EOCs in the HWW are pharmaceutical compounds, which are designed to readily diffuse into the cell membrane (i. e., lipophilicity) and target intracellular proteins [51]. Remarkable negative correlations with EOCs concentration were observed mainly for CRAM, aliphatic, and tannin-like DOM. Since CRAM belongs to persistent organic compounds; aliphatics are believed to originate from lipids breakdown, and tannins are antioxidant compounds. Therefore, compared to these DOM compounds, EOCs are more susceptible to oxidization by ozone and ROS.

Among 2813 shared molecules, $2 0 ~ \%$ $( n = 5 7 3$ ) were significantly correlated $( r = \pm 1 )$ with the total concentration of EOCs (Fig. 5b), mainly including CHO, CHON, lignins-like, aliphatic and tannin compounds. The vast majority $( 9 2 . 7 ~ \% )$ of the compounds were negatively correlated (blue dots) with the total EOCs concentration, and were concentrated in the upper-middle region of the van Krevelen diagram, where $4 2 \ \%$ belonging to CRAM. Positively correlated molecules (red dots) were concentrated in regions with very low O/C $( < 0 . 1 5 )$ and high $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C } \left( > 1 . 2 5 \right)$ , mainly corresponding to UnHy, lignins-like, and aliphatic compounds. These DOM molecules may facilitate the EOCs removal by cross-coupling with EOCs, illustrating the trade-off effect of DOM [14,52]. The top six EOCs detected in HWW (occupying $7 3 . 2 \%$ of total EOCs concentrations) were marked with stars in Fig. 5b, and were mainly located in the lignins-like region. Note that there were almost no shared DOM molecules near the marked EOCs positions, meanwhile DOM transformation process showed a high removal tendency in this region (Fig. 4c, 4d, and S18a). Evidently, DOM molecules with similar O/C and H/C ratios to EOCs exhibited stronger ozone reactivity, which reflected ozone’s selectivity towards EOCs and specific DOM fractions.

To further understand the selective oxidation mechanisms of DOM and EOCs during real HWW treatment by catalytic ozonation in $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit, the concentration-weighted molecular parameters of EOCs in raw water were calculated and compared with DOM (Fig. 5c). EOCs in HWW were significantly much more aromatic (higher $\mathrm { \sf A I _ { m o d } } )$ and unsaturated (i.e., higher DBE-O and (DBE-O)/C) than DOM, and overall exhibited a more reductive characteristic (i.e., low O/C). Compared to DOM with multiple complex natural sources, artificially selected or synthesized EOCs reasonably contain more specific functional structures to pass through the cell membrane and bind with designated targets (e.g., nitrogen sites on heterocycles are widely designed in pharmaceutical compounds for hydrogen-bonding with protein targets [53,54]), thereby exhibiting significant differences in molecular composition and properties. Above favorable characteristics resulted in the EOCs benefiting from the competition with DOM for oxidative species, achieving their selective removal in real HWW treatment. Unlocking the comprehensive structural discrepancies between EOCs and DOM molecules will contribute to developing better selective removal strategies for EOCs and promote practical industry applications.

Molecular-level insights into the interactions and differences between DOM and EOCs in ozone-based AOPs have enhanced the understanding of the efficient selective EOCs removal by the $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit. When processing real HWW, a synergistic effect of in-situ ozonation and confined catalytic membranes was formed, as shown in Fig. 5d. Under cross-flow filtering, organic polymers (i.e., DOM with high molecule weight, or DOM-HMW) are readily intercepted by ceramic ultrafiltration membranes with pores smaller than $1 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , leading to membrane fouling during filtration [10]. Meanwhile, DOM with low molecule weight (DOM-LMW) and EOCs can enter through the membrane pores during filtration. In this study, in-situ ozonation effectively controlled membrane fouling through hydraulic flushing and direct oxidation of organic membrane foulants (Fig. 2b). In HWW, EOCs had more unsaturated and aromatic structures than the rest of DOM, moreover, partial EOCs owned N, O-containing heterocyclic structures. Therefore, within the catalytic ceramic membrane pores, EOCs were preferentially oxidized, while only a small portion of reductively unsaturated DOMLMW was removed. Additionally, some ozone molecules could be adsorbed on the Mn-based catalyst and activated to generate more ROS, achieving non-selective degradation of adsorbed EOCs within membrane [30,55]. Overall, direct ozonation and non-radical oxidation of heterogeneous ozonation contributed to the selective removal observed, whereas hydroxyl radical-dominated oxidation could enhance removal of both DOM and EOCs, although quantifying the contribution of each mechanism remains challenging [56]. In this way, the $\mathrm { C M } / \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ unit successfully removed $6 3 \ \%$ of the EOCs from membrane effluent compared to that in influent, however, only removed $1 2 \%$ of the DOM, eventually revealing efficient removal selectivity in real HWW.

# 4. Conclusions

In summary, we report a pilot-scale study for efficient and highly selective removal of EOCs from real hospital wastewater. An integrated in-situ ozonation/ultrafiltration process achieved stable and selective removal of $8 5 ~ \%$ of EOCs at an oxidant dosage of $0 . 4 4 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { O } _ { 3 } / \mathrm { m g }$ DOC during long-term HWW treatment. We further describe the complex interactions between DOM and EOCs presence and removal, along with molecular-level mechanism of ozone selectivity. Compared to DOM, the more aromatic and unsaturated molecular composition of EOCs results in an overall reductive characteristic, which is key to the integrated process to achieving selective removal under high DOC coexistence. Overall, this study presents an advanced strategy for the in-situ treatment of trace EOCs in wastewater, potentially providing insights for rational design of other AOPs treatments to circumvent the adverse effects of DOM, selectively removing target micro-organic contaminants with high cost-efficiency.
