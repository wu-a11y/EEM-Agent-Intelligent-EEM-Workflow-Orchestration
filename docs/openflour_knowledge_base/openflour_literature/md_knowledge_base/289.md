Rainwater brings considerable amounts of dissolved organic matter (DOM) from atmosphere to freshwater and marine environments, but little is known about the chemical composition and bioavailability of rainwater DOM. The quantity, quality, and bioavailability of DOM were investigated for 21 rain events at a coastal site in southeast China, using dissolved organic carbon (DOC) measurements, absorption spectroscopy, and fluorescence excitation-emission matrices-parallel factor analysis (EEM-PARAFAC). The DOC concentration ranged from 35 to $4 5 7 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , which was affected by the prevailing monsoon, rainfall amount and terrestrial/anthropogenic inputs. The volume-weighted average DOC was $1 1 8 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , corresponding to a rainwater DOC flux of $\bar { 1 . 9 8 } { \bf g } { \bf m } ^ { - 2 } { \bf y } { \bf r } ^ { - 1 }$ . Four fluorescent components were identified with EEM-PARAFAC, including three humic-like components (C1-C3) and one tyrosine-like component C4. Absorption coefficient $( a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 ) )$ and fluorescence intensities of C2-C4 strongly correlated with DOC, indicating they can be used for DOC estimation. Rainwater DOM was characterized by low DOC-specific UV absorbance $\mathrm { ( S U V A _ { 2 5 4 } ) }$ and humification index (HIX), which indicated a low aromaticity and humification degree. Principal component analysis (PCA) based on DOM indices revealed two principal factors, which were related to the DOM concentration and humification degree respectively. PCA, together with air-mass trajectory analysis, successfully separated different rain events with variable inputs from living plants/local sources, soil organic matter/humified materials, and the marine source. Rainwater DOM generally showed a high bioavailability of $5 0 \% ~ \pm ~ 1 9 \%$ , with higher degradability of nonchromophoric constituents and C1-C2 than other components. This study demonstrated the applicability of absorption and EEM-PARAFAC combined with PCA and air-mass trajectory analysis in differentiating rain events and tracking organic matter sources, and revealed different effects of microbial degradation on individual PARAFAC components in rainwater.

# 1. Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) in rainwater plays important roles in global carbon cycle (Willey et al., 2000; Iavorivska et al., 2016; Li et al., 2017), spectral attenuation of solar radiation in atmosphere (Kieber et al., 2006), formation of cloud condensation nuclei (Facchini et al., 1999; Gysel et al., 2004), and biogeochemical cycles of other elements such as iron (Willey et al., 2015). For example, the global rainwater flux of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) is estimated to be 90 and $3 4 0 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ to the sea and land surface, respectively (Willey et al., 2000), which is an important source of DOC and microbial metabolism substrate for both terrestrial and marine environments. However, the rainwater DOC concentration spans a broad range of $2 { \mathrm { - } } 9 6 6 7 \mu \mathrm { M }$ globally, corresponding to a rainwater flux of DOC from $0 . 6 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ for a remote ocean site to $3 . 5 \pm 3 . 4 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { y } \mathrm { r } ^ { - 1 }$ for 28 continental sites (Iavorivska et al., 2016). Therefore, it is important to further determine the DOC concentration and flux in typical sites and in different rain events, for revealing the underlying mechanisms of changing rainwater

DOM quantity and for better understanding the role of rainwater DOM in global biogeochemical cycles. In particular, seasonal changes in the dominant monsoon system in typical areas such as East Asia probably have profound effects on the air quality and rainwater DOM quantity, which is largely unknown.

Rainwater DOM is a complex mixture of different organic compounds which have different chemical structures, functional groups, and reactivities (Kieber et al., 2007, 2012; Altieri et al., 2009; Mead et al., 2013; Li et al., 2018). It is important to characterize the chemical composition and biogeochemical reactivity of rainwater DOM, for better understanding its fate and ecological effects in the receiving environments. A series of optical indices for DOM quality can be measured by absorption spectroscopy and fluorescence excitationemission matrices-parallel factor analysis (EEM-PARAFAC), which have been widely used for characterizing DOM in rivers, lakes, and oceans (Coble, 2007; Fellman et al., 2010; Ishii and Boyer, 2012; Derrien et al., 2017). The absorption spectra and fluorescence EEM have also been determined for rainwater in the last decade (Kieber et al., 2006, 2007; 2012; Muller et al., 2008; Santos et al., 2009, 2012; 2013; Mitra et al., 2017). However, PARAFAC has rarely been applied for further analyzing rainwater EEM data, which is a powerful tool for identifying different fluorescent components from EEM of overlapping peaks (Zhou et al., 2017). It is critical to track the source of organic matter in rainwater, for understanding the mechanisms controlling the concentration, chemical composition and deposition flux of rainwater DOM. Principal component analysis (PCA) based on absorption and EEM-PARAFAC results has the potential of discriminating DOM from different sources and/or subject to different transformations in aquatic environments (Jaffé; et al., 2008; Inamdar et al., 2012; Chen and Jaffé, 2014; Hur et al., 2014). The capability of such technique for differentiating rain events and identifying the source of rainwater organic matter is worth testing. The labile constituents of DOM in aquatic environments provide metabolism substrates for microbial growth and would be decomposed to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ (Benner, 2003; Singer et al., 2012; Bodmer et al., 2016), thus it is also important to examine the bioavailability of rainwater DOM for understanding its fate and environmental effects. A high fraction of bulk DOM is susceptible to microbial degradation in the rainwater at southeast USA (Avery et al., 2003). However, few studies have reported the bioavailability of bulk DOM for rainwater. The bioavailability of chromophoric DOM (CDOM) and different fluorescent components has not yet been reported.

Therefore, this study aimed to: (1) examine the effects of seasonal changes in the prevailing monsoon on the quantity of rainwater DOM at a typical coastal site of southeast China; (2) characterize the quality of DOM with absorption spectroscopy and fluorescence EEM-PARAFAC, for assessing the applicability of PCA based on DOM indices in differentiating rain events and tracking organic matter sources; and (3) examine the bioavailability of bulk DOM and different DOM components (CDOM and different fluorescent components), for providing further insights into the fate and ecological importance of rainwater DOM.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study area, sample collection and microbial incubation

Rainwater samples were collected at $\sim 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ above ground on the roof of the College of Environment and Resources, Fuzhou University Qishan campus $( 2 6 ^ { \circ } 0 3 ^ { \prime } 3 9 . 9 2 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N } , 1 1 9 ^ { \circ } 1 1 ^ { \prime } 1 9 . 8 9 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E } )$ , which was a coastal site in southeast China with a distance of $\sim 4 5 \mathrm { k m }$ from the sea (Fig. 1). The southeast China is strongly affected by north terrestrial monsoon in winter and south marine monsoon in summer. The local climate is characterized by dry and cold winter but warm and humid summer. The long-term average temperature is $1 0 . 9 – 1 1 . 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in January–February and $2 8 . 4 \substack { - 2 8 . 9 } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in July–August, while the annual rainfall is about $1 3 9 3 . 4 \mathrm { m m }$ with $8 2 \%$ occurring in March–September in 2017 (www. weather.com.cn). The long-term mean $\mathtt { p H }$ in precipitation during

2007–2014 is 4.68 and the occurrence frequency of acid rain is $6 3 . 1 \%$ in Fuzhou, mainly owing to the dissolution of sulfate and nitrate (Chen et al., 2016, Table 1). The campus is located in the suburb which probably receives a medium anthropogenic impact. The building itself had little pollution on rainwater DOM samples, because the rooms in the building were mainly offices and only few labs occasionally used simple organic solvents such as ethanol, which did not contain CDOM or humic substances. Potential pollution was minimized since the sample containers were placed on the roof at the start of each event and moved to lab immediately after rain stopped. Potential pollution from organic solvents could not be excluded totally, but the effect was negligible due to the low volume-weighted DOC, very low DOC for several samples, and strong correlations between DOC and optical parameters, since CDOM and humic substances were mainly from soils and terrestrial plants but not likely the building itself.

Rainwaters were sampled for 21 rain events on an event basis from the start to the end of each event during March 22, 2017-Jannuary 31, 2018, using two stainless steel round pots with a similar diameter of ${ 3 9 } \mathrm { c m }$ . The stainless steel pots were rinsed with large amounts of tap water and were pre-cleaned carefully with Milli-Q water. Blank test showed negligible DOM signal from the stainless steel pots. They were placed on the roof at the start of each rain event and the samples were filtered immediately after the end of rain events. The event rainfall amount ranged from as low as $0 . 5 \mathrm { m m }$ to up to $9 2 . 7 \mathrm { m m }$ , as recorded at an adjacent metrological station (http://slt.fujian.gov.cn/). pH and conductivity were measured with a Thermo Scientific Orion Star A Meter. Rainwaters were filtered through GF/F filters (pore size of $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , pre-combusted at $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 \mathrm { h }$ ) in a glass filtration system precleaned with HCl and Milli-Q water. The filtrates were transferred into pre-combusted brown glass bottles, and stored in frozen and dark for DOC and optical measurements. Aquatic samples are usually stored in cold before optical measurements due to potential changes in frozen (e.g., Thieme et al., 2016). However, we stored the filtrates in frozen to avoid potential changes of labile rainwater DOM, similar to some previous studies for seawater (Coble, 1996; Murphy et al., 2008; Stedmon et al., 2011), river water (Spencer et al., 2010), and rainwater (Santos et al., 2009). The potential frozen effect was tested for a rainwater sample on October 17, 2018, which showed little or limited effect on optical parameters (mostly $< 5 \%$ , except for two indices within $1 0 \%$ ), similar to two previous studies (Rochelle-Newall et al., 2014; Hancke et al., 2014).

The bioavailability of rainwater DOM was determined by incubating unfiltered samples with the microbes in rainwater and without adding other microbial inoculum, following the procedure described in detail previously (Avery et al., 2003; Hood et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2013, 2015, 2017). Briefly, samples were transferred into a series of $1 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ pre-combusted brown glass bottles, which were loosely capped to allow free exchange of air. The incubations were performed for 14–28 days at the room temperature in the dark, to simulate the biodegradation at local temperature (from $\sim 1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in winter to $\sim 2 9 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in summer). The incubation time was 14 days for 5 events while it was extended to 28 days for 6 other events. There was no significant difference in the changes of DOC and optical properties between the two groups of incubations with different incubation time (independent samples t-test, $p > 0 . 0 5 )$ . Thereafter, samples were filtered with pre-combusted GF/F filters for measuring DOC and optical properties, and the results were compared with the initial DOC and optical values of filtered samples. The changes in DOC were used for assessing the bioavailability of bulk DOM, while the changes in optical properties were used for assessing the bioavailability of CDOM and different PARAFAC components (Avery et al., 2003; Hood et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2013, 2015, 2017).

# 2.2. DOC, absorption, and fluorescence measurements

The DOC concentration was measured using the high-temperature catalytic oxidation method after removing inorganic carbon by acidification with HCl and oxygen purging, using a Shimadzu TOC-L analyzer (Yang et al., 2012, 2017). Absorbance spectra of CDOM were scanned using $_ { 1 0 \mathrm { c m } }$ cuvettes on a Techcomp 2600 UV–Vis spectrometer over $2 4 0 { - } 8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with Milli-Q water as the blank, and were baseline-corrected by subtracting the mean absorbance at $7 0 0 { - } 8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Yang et al., 2012, 2013). Absorption coefficients at each wavelength $\left( \lambda \right)$ were calculated as:

where $A ( \lambda )$ is the corrected absorbance at wavelength $\gimel$ and l is the pathlength $\left( 0 . 1 \mathrm { m } \right)$ (Morel and Smith, 1982; Hu et al., 2002; Kowalczuk et al., 2009). The absorption coefficient at $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ $( a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 ) )$ was used as an index of the CDOM level in rainwater (Kieber et al., 2006). DOC-specific UV absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { ( } \mathbf { S } \mathbf { U } \mathbf { V } \mathbf { A _ { 2 5 4 } } )$ and the spectral slope over $2 7 5 { - } 2 9 5 \mathrm { n m } ( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ were calculated following procedures described previously (Weishaar et al., 2003; Helms et al., 2008). $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ correlates positively with the aromaticity of DOM (Weishaar et al., 2003), while a higher $\boldsymbol { S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } }$ indicates a lower average molecular weight of DOM (Helms et al., 2008).

Fluorescence EEMs were scanned using $^ { 1 \mathrm { c m } }$ cuvettes on a Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer at excitation/emission wavelengths of $2 4 0 { - } 4 5 0 / 2 8 0 { - } 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , following the procedure described in detail previously (Yang et al., 2012, 2015, 2017). The increments of excitation and emission wavelengths were 5 and $1 \mathrm { n m }$ , and the scanning rate was $4 8 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . Each EEM was corrected for inner-filter effects based on absorbance data (McKnight et al., 2001), and subtracted the fluorescence response of blank (Milli-Q water). A total of 53 EEMs were modeled with PARAFAC in MATLAB 7.0 using the DOMFluor toolbox 1.7 (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). The number of PARAFAC components was determined with split-half validation, and the fluorescence intensity $\left( F _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } \right)$ of each component was used to represent its level (in the Raman Unit, RU, Stedmon and Bro, 2008). The percent of each component in the total fluorescence (i.e., $\% { \bf C } 1 { - } \% { \bf C } 4 _ { . }$ ) was used as the index for chemical composition of DOM (Kowalczuk et al., 2009). Humification index (HIX) and biological index (BIX) were also calculated from fluorescence EEMs, as indices for the humification degree and source of DOM respectively (Zsolnay et al., 1999; Huguet et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2017). HIX was the ratio of the area under the emission spectra over $4 3 4 { \ - } 4 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that over $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 6 \mathrm { n m }$ , at excitation $2 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . BIX was the ratio of the fluorescence intensity at emission $3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that at emission $4 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , at excitation $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ .

# 2.3. Air-mass back-trajectory analysis and statistical analysis

To study the influence of air-mass source on the rainwater DOM, the air-mass source for each rain event was tracked with air-mass backtrajectory analysis. The analysis was performed using the Hybrid Single-Particle Lagrangian Integrated Trajectory (HYSPLIT) model of Air Resources Laboratory, National Oceanic Atmospheric Administration, based on $0 . 5 ^ { \circ }$ Global Data Assimilation System (GDAS) meteorological data (Stein et al., 2015; Rolph et al., 2017). Each trajectory was generated for $7 2 \mathrm { h }$ backward starting at $5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ above ground level, and a series of trajectories were calculated every 3-h from the beginning to the end for each rain event (Fig. S1; Kieber et al., 2006; Mead et al., 2013).

PCA was carried out using the Statistical Product and Service Solutions (SPSS) Statistics software, based on 14 DOM indices including DOC, $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ , $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , $ { S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } }$ , HIX, BIX, $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1-C4, and $\% { \bf C 1 }$ - $\% { \bf C 4 }$ . The data of each index for PCA were standardized by subtracting the mean value of all samples and dividing by the standard deviation. Correlation analyses and comparison of means were also performed using the SPSS Statistics software.

# 3. Results

# 3.1. DOC and absorption results

The DOC concentration changed greatly from 35 to $4 5 7 \mu \mathrm { M }$ in the rainwater of Fuzhou during March 2017–January 2018 (Fig. 2; Table 1). The volume-weighted average value of DOC was $1 1 8 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , which was calculated as the sum of the products of event DOC and event rainfall amount divided by the total rainfall of all events. DOC changed greatly $( 8 7 - 4 5 7 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ for Events 1–6 during March–April in early spring, while it was lower $( 3 5 \mathrm { - } 1 6 2 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ for Events 7–15 during April–October from later spring to early autumn. Then, it increased to higher values of $1 3 7 { - } 2 9 3 \mu \mathrm { M }$ for Events 16–18 during November–December, while it was lower $( 4 8 \mathrm { - } 1 2 1 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ in Events 19–21 in January 2018. The DOC concentration generally decreased with increasing event rainfall amount, while it increased with increasing conductivity $( r = 0 . 9 3 1$ , $p \ : < \ : 0 . 0 0 1$ , Fig. 3). There was no significant correlation between DOC and pH in this study $( r = - 0 . 2 3 0$ , $p = 0 . 3 1 5$ ).

The absorption coefficient of rainwater CDOM decreased exponentially with increasing wavelength in this study (Fig. S2). $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ changed greatly from 0.26 to $4 . 7 9 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , with an average value of $1 . 6 6 \pm 1 . 1 9 \mathrm { { m } ^ { - 1 } }$ , which correlated strongly with DOC $( r = 0 . 9 1 2$ , $p \ < \ 0 . 0 1$ , Fig. 4). $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and S275-295 were 049- $2 . 2 0 \mathrm { L m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 1 }$ and $0 . 0 0 6 4 - 0 . 0 2 3 0 \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ , with average values of $0 . 8 4 \ : \pm \ : 0 . 3 7 \mathrm { L m g } ^ { - 1 } \ : \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 1 }$ and $0 . 0 1 4 7 \pm \ : 0 . 0 0 4 \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively (Table 1; Fig. S3). $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ showed no notable temporal variation, while $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ was somewhat lower during later spring-summer (Events $8 – 1 4 \colon 0 . 1 2 0 – 0 . 1 4 1 \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ , mean: $0 . 1 2 7 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 0 1$ , Fig. S3).

# 3.2. Fluorescence results

Four fluorescent components were identified using EEM-PARAFAC in this study, which were validated by split-half analysis (C1-C4, Fig. 5). Component C1 showed two excitation $\left( \operatorname { E x } \right)$ maxima at $\leq 2 4 0$ and $3 4 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , and one emission $\left( \mathrm { E m } \right)$ maximum at $4 3 7 \mathrm { n m }$ . Component C2 had two Ex maxima at $\leq 2 4 0$ and $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and one Em maximum at $4 0 8 \mathrm { n m }$ . Component C3 displayed $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maxima at (250, 330)/ $4 5 6 \mathrm { n m }$ , while C4 had $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maxima at $( \leq 2 4 0 , 2 7 5 ) / 3 1 1 \mathrm { n m }$ . The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1-C4, indicative of component levels, were 0.009–0.323, 0.018–0.377, 0.011–0.215, and 0.019–0.160 RU in the rainwaters, with average values of $0 . 0 6 6 \pm \ : 0 . 0 9 2$ , $0 . 1 3 5 \ : \pm \ : 0 . 0 7 9$ , $0 . 0 5 0 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 4 8$ , and $0 . 0 6 2 \pm \ : 0 . 0 3 7$ RU, respectively (Fig. 6; Table S1). # 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Temporal changes and annual flux of rainwater DOC

The rainwater DOC changed notably $( 3 5 { - } 4 5 7 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ in this study, which is consistent with the large variation of organic carbon content in precipitation globally $( 2 \mathrm { - } 9 6 6 7 \mu \mathrm { M } _ { \mathrm { : } }$ , Willey et al., 2000; Iavorivska et al., 2016). The highest DOC was found for Event 6 which might be related to the lowest event rainfall amount (Table 1, Fig. 3). Rainwater DOC showed a decreasing trend with increasing rainfall amount of each event in this study (Fig. 3A), suggesting a dilution effect at higher rainfall amounts (Kieber et al., 2006; Santos et al., 2013; Zhou et al., 2017). In addition, the air mass of Event 6 originated from southwest and probably received pollutants during its passage through the Guangdong Province, a most developed region of China (Fig. 2, Fig. S1, Table 1). DOC was lowest for Events 7 and 15 $( 3 5 { - } 4 3 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ when the air mass was mainly from coastal seas and was less polluted. These results agreed with previous studies showing higher DOC for terrestrialdominated events than marine-dominated events (Willey et al., 2000;

However, DOC was elevated in Events 4 $( 2 9 9 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ and 14 $( 1 2 3 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ with marine air masses, which might be due to the low rainfall amount in Event 4 $4 \mathrm { m m }$ , Table 1, Fig. 3) and potential inputs of DOC from local terrestrial/anthropogenic sources from the Fuzhou City.

The temporal changes of rainwater DOC concentration corresponded to the changes of prevailing monsoon and air quality in different seasons. In the study area of southeast China which is dominated by subtropical marine monsoon climate, north terrestrial winds dominate in winter and early spring, and the rainwater may be affected notably by terrestrial or anthropogenic sources, thus DOC changed greatly $( 8 7 - 4 5 7 \mu \mathrm { M } _ { \mathrm { : } }$ , mean: $2 4 8 ~ \pm ~ 1 4 2 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , volume-weighted mean: $2 0 5 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ for Events 1–6 in the early spring (Fig. 2). In contrast, south and east marine winds prevail in summer and the rainwater is less polluted, resulting in low DOC for Events 7–15 from the late spring to early autumn (mean: $1 0 3 \pm 4 5 { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ , volume-weighted mean: $1 0 7 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ than those for Events 1–6 (independent samples t-test, $p = 0 . 0 5$ , Fig. 2). Thereafter, DOC increased to higher values (mean: $2 1 3 \pm 7 8 { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ , volume-weighted mean: $1 9 5 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ in Events 16–18 in the autumn and early winter than in Events 7–15 (independent samples t-test, $p \ : < \ : 0 . 0 5 )$ which probably resulted from the transition between marine and terrestrial monsoons. The temporal changes of DOC corresponded well with the evident changes in the air quality such as the monthly-average concentration of particulate matter $\leq 2 . 5 { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ (PM2.5) in 2017 (Fig. S5), which were obtained from the China air quality online monitoring and analysis system (http://www.aqistudy.cn/). In fact, part of rainwater DOC could be derived from leaching of particle matters or aerosols in the atmosphere and after depositing to the sample collector, although the samples were filtered immediately after rain stopped (Bao et al., 2017). The low DOC (mean: $8 6 ~ \pm ~ 3 7 \mu \mathrm { M } _ { \mathrm { : } }$ , volume-weighted mean: $6 4 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ for Events 19–21 in January 2018 was also probably caused by the improved air quality (Fig. 2), which was indicated by the lower monthly-average PM2.5 concentration in January 2018 $( 2 8 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } )$ and the lower average PM2.5 of antecedent 7 days (Fig. 2). The low DOC for Event 19 might be in part caused by the dilution effect at a high rainfall amount of $3 9 . 9 \mathrm { m m }$ (Table 1). In addition, there was a strong positive correlation between rainwater DOC and conductivity (Fig. 3B). On one hand, the elevated conductivity might be related to stronger pollution which could introduce both DOC and some ions such as ammonium, nitrate and sulfate (Lara et al., 2001; Hu et al., 2003). On the other hand, the dissolution of some organic constituents such as organic acids might contribute in part to higher conductivity (acetic acid, formic acid, humic acids, etc., Willey et al., 2000).

The volume-weighted DOC $( 1 1 8 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ in this study was lower than the median value for coastal/island sites globally $( 1 5 6 \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ , Iavorivska et al., 2016). It was higher than the value for North Carolina, USA $( 6 1 \mu \mathrm { M } _ { : }$ , Kieber et al., 2006) and a coastal town of western Portugal (Santos et al., 2012), but it was lower than that for the Gulf of Mexico, USA $( 5 9 1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , Mitra et al., 2017). Based on the volume-weighted DOC concentration and the annual precipitation in 2017 $( 1 3 9 3 . 4 \mathrm { m m } )$ , the rainwater flux of DOC was $1 . 9 8 \mathrm { g m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ in the coastal site of Fuzhou. This was a conservative estimation, since the long-term average annual precipitation was $2 1 \%$ higher than the annual precipitation in 2017 $\mathbf { 1 6 7 9 . 9 m m }$ during 1995–2016, http://slt.fujian. gov.cn/). In addition, the uncertainty of the volume-weighted DOC could be decreased by collecting more samples for more years in the future. This value was close to the median value of the deposition flux at coastal/island sites worldwide $( 2 . 1 \ \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 } )$ , while it was three folds higher than that for the remote ocean site $( 0 . 6 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { y } \mathrm { r } ^ { - 1 }$ , Iavorivska et al., 2016) and was higher than those for three remote continental stations $( 0 . 1 6 { - } 0 . 8 4 \ g \ m ^ { - 2 } \ y \mathbf r ^ { - 1 }$ , Li et al., 2017). The higher rainwater DOC flux in this study and at other coastal sites than those for remote sites was probably related to the contribution of terrestrial/ anthropogenic sources with higher organic matter content. Therefore, atmospheric wet deposition plays a critical role in supplying DOC to the coastal environments, which should be incorporated into the regional and global carbon cycle study.

# 4.2. Absorption and fluorescence of rainwater DOM: implications for separating different rain events

Four components were identified with fluorescence EEM-PARAFAC (C1-C4, Fig. 5) and were compared with those in the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al., 2014a). The Ex/Em maxima of C1 ( $( \leq 2 4 0$ , 345)/ $4 3 7 \mathrm { n m }$ ) was similar with a ubiquitous humic-like component mainly from terrestrial sources (C1 in Murphy et al., 2014b; C4 in Zhou et al., 2017). Component C2 (Ex/Em maxima: $( \leq 2 4 0 , 3 0 0 ) / 4 0 8 \mathrm { n m } )$ had a shorter Em wavelength than C1, and it resembled a microbial humiclike component (C2 in Søndergaard et al., 2003; C2 in Murphy et al., 2006; C1 in Zhou et al., 2017). It also covered the fluorescence region of peak R in the rainwater of Carolina, which was susceptible to photobleaching (Kieber et al., 2012). Component C3 displayed $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maxima ((250, 330)/456 nm) similar to a terrestrial humic-like component (C1 in Bianchi et al., 2014; C2 in Zhou et al., 2017; C2 in Retelletti Brogi et al., 2018), which was associated with photo-production or photodegradion (C2 in Chen et al., 2010 and Cawley et al., 2012). Component C4 (Ex/Em maxima: $( \leq 2 4 0 , \ 2 7 5 ) / 3 1 1 \mathrm { { n m } ) }$ resembled the tyrosine-like component (C3 in Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2016; C5 in Zhou et al., 2017).

Absorption spectroscopy and fluorescence EEM-PARAFAC provide a series of parameters for assessing the quantity and quality of DOM (Coble, 2007; Fellman et al., 2010; Ishii and Boyer, 2012; Derrien et al., 2017). On one hand, $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ and $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C2-C4 strongly correlated with DOC $( r ; 0 . 7 0 4 - 0 . 9 1 2 , p < 0 . 0 1$ , Fig. 4), suggesting their temporal changes were dependent on similar factors with DOC such as seasonal variations in the prevailing monsoon, inputs from terrestrial/anthropogenic sources, and dilutions at higher rainfall amounts as discussed above for DOC in section 4.1. For example, $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ and $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C3- C4 all decreased with increasing event rainfall amount, indicating the importance of dilution effect (Fig. S6A-C). The results also indicated that the CDOM absorption coefficient and fluorescence, which can be measured rapidly with no consumables and in-situ with optical sensors for improving the spatial and temporal resolutions, could provide valuable proxies for estimating the DOC concentration. The insignificant correlations between C1 and other DOM quantity parameters (DOC, $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ and C2-C4) suggested C1 changed independently due to a different source and/or reactivity. C1 was most abundant in Events 16 and 20 when north terrestrial air mass prevailed, indicating a terrestrial/anthropogenic source from north. On the other hand, the chemical composition and source of rainwater DOM can be inferred from $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , $ { S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } }$ , HIX and BIX. In particular, the rainwater $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and HIX values in this study $( 0 . 8 4 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 3 7 \mathrm { L } \mathfrak { m } \mathfrak { g } ^ { - 1 } \mathfrak { m } ^ { - 1 }$ and $2 . 4 ~ \pm ~ 1 . 4 _ { \cdot }$ , respectively) fell at the low end of the ranges for a variety of aquatic environments $( 0 . 6 { - } 5 . 3 \mathrm { L m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 1 }$ and $< 4 \mathrm { - } > 1 6$ , respectively, Fig. S4, Weishaar et al., 2003; Huguet et al., 2009; Derrien et al., 2017; Massicotte et al., 2017). This suggested that rainwater DOM had a low aromaticity and humification degree, which might be caused by strong photobleaching/photodegradation of volatile organic compounds in the form of gas or vapor in atmosphere (Li et al., 2017). The BIX values (0.31–0.91, mean: $0 . 6 0 \ \pm \ 0 . 1 4 )$ further indicated variable inputs of terrestrial and biological/aquatic sources to the rainwater DOM (Fig. S4; Huguet et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2012; Derrien et al., 2017). The optical properties of DOM could be in part affected by antecedent dry days, which correlated positively with $\% { \bf C 1 }$ and HIX but negatively with BIX (Fig. S6D-F).

PCA analysis based on DOM indices revealed that the first two principal factors (PC1 and PC2) explained $3 3 . 7 \%$ and $2 9 . 4 \%$ of the total variance, respectively (Fig. 7A). PC1 correlated positively and strongly with most DOM quantity parameters including DOC, $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ , $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C2-C4, suggesting this factor was related to one or more DOM sources of high concentration such as terrestrial and/or anthropogenic sources. PC2 correlated positively with HIX, $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1 and $\% { \bf C 1 }$ , while it correlated negatively with BIX and $\% { \mathrm { C 4 } }$ . This suggested that PC2 was probably related to microbial transformation processes that increased the humic content and decreased the protein-like content. For example, DOM from fresh terrestrial plants/anthropogenic wastes would have a high score of PC1 due to elevated concentration but a low score of PC2 due to limited microbial humification and/or high protein-like percentage. The protein-like C4 correlated strongly with DOC, $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ and humic-like C2 and C3 in this study $( r \colon 0 . 6 8 5  – 0 . 8 8 6$ , $p \ < \ 0 . 0 1 $ , suggesting that the protein-like component was mainly from fresh terrestrial plants, anthropogenic wastes, and microbes on aerosols and in rainwaters (Muller et al., 2008; Zhou et al., 2017; Mitra et al., 2017).

The scores of PC1 and PC2 for each rainwater DOM sample except for three events 1, 2, and 6 were retrieved from PCA analysis, which could be used for separating different rain events (Fig. 7B). Events 4, 7 and 20 were dominated by three different groups of DOM endmembers which could be denoted by Endmember A, B, and C respectively. Event 4 (Endmember A) had a highest score of PC1 and a low PC2, suggesting DOM in this event was strongly affected by DOM sources of high concentration and low humification degree. This event was associated with maritime and clean air masses from south, thus the high DOM content was possibly due to emissions from local terrestrial/anthropogenic sources besides of the low rain amount. The low humification degree was probably related to limited microbial transformation during the short-distance transport from local sources and/or the inputs from living plant materials (pollens, volatile organic matter, etc). The leaching of particles occurred both in the atmosphere and after depositing to the land and to the sample collector, although the samples were filtered immediately after the end of rain events. Event 20 (Endmember C) had a highest score of PC2 and a low PC1, indicating a highest humification degree of DOM and a low DOM concentration. Therefore, PCA based on DOM indices provides a valuable tool for differentiating rain events and has the potential of identifying the characteristics and source of rainwater DOM.

# 4.3. Bioavailability of bulk DOM and different DOM components

The bioavailability of rainwater DOM $( \% \mathbf { B D O C } )$ was $2 3 \% { - } 9 0 \%$ in this study, with an average value of $5 0 \% ~ \pm ~ 1 9 \%$ (Table S2). The bioavailability of DOM has rarely been tested for rainwater, although it is up to $6 3 \% \pm 1 4 \%$ in the coast of North Carolina, USA (Avery et al., 2003). The bioavailability of rainwater DOM is higher than those for river water $( < 2 5 \%$ , Wiegner et al., 2009; Mann et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2013), groundwater $( < 2 0 \%$ , Yang et al., 2015), and hydrothermal fluids $( 3 \% - 1 2 \%$ , Yang et al., 2017). It is comparable to the high bioavailability of glacial DOM (up to $9 4 \%$ , Hood et al., 2009; Singer et al., 2012; Spencer et al., 2014) and biomass leachate (Chen and Jaffe, 2016). Therefore, a significant fraction of rainwater DOM is readily available for microbial utilization. The rainwater BDOC concentration $( 2 1 - 2 6 4 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , volume-weighted average: $6 9 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ is higher than those for groundwater, hydrothermal fluids, and glaciers $( 0 { - } 2 3 \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ , Hood et al., 2009; Singer et al., 2012; Spencer et al., 2014; Yang et al., 2015, 2017), while it is comparable with those for different rivers $( 0 . 2 \mathrm { - } 2 2 4 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , Wiegner et al., 2009; Mann et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2013). Our estimation of rainwater BDOC was conservative due to potential leaching of particles in the unfiltered sample. However, DOC is the major form of organic matter in rainwater globally (Iavorivska et al., 2016), and it contributed to $9 4 . 4 \%$ of rainwater total organic carbon (TOC) on October 15, 2018 at our sampling site. The high bioavailability of rainwater DOM may be attributed to inputs of labile DOM from fresh terrestrial plants (Strauss and Lamberti, 2002; Chen and Jaffe, 2016), marine phytoplankton (del Giorgio and Davis, 2003), combustion sources (Spencer et al., 2014), and/or photochemical transformations of organic compounds in atmosphere (Moran and Zepp, 1997). For example, the inputs of labile constituents from fresh terrestrial plants and combustion sources were probably responsible for the high bioavailability of rainwater DOM in some terrestrial-dominated events (e.g., Events 1–6 and 18). However, the microbial utilization of DOM is also in part dependent on nutrient condition and bacterial growth. The lower bioavailability of DOM in some marine-dominated events (e.g., Events 12–13) might be explained by lower nutrients in surface seawater due to biological uptake (Chen and Tsunogai, 1998), which warrants testing in the future. In addition, these results might be related to the priming effect of sunlight which increased the bacterial degradation of terrestrial DOM with the opposite effect to algae-derived DOM (Moran and Covert, 2003; Chen and Jaffé, 2014). The low bioavailability of DOM in Event 16 might be related to either limited growth of bacteria or low percent of labile constituents due to potential removals during long-distance transport which resulted in high HIX and $\% { \bf C 1 }$ (4.38 and $4 3 . 5 \%$ ) but low BIX and $\% { \mathsf { C 4 } }$ (0.36 and $1 5 . 0 \%$ , Table S1). It is worth testing nutrients, bacteria and other matrices (e.g., particles) in the future for a full understanding of the mechanisms underlying the variable bioavailability of rainwater DOM. The deposition flux of BDOC could be roughly estimated to be $1 . 1 6 { \bf \delta g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } { \bf y } \mathrm { r } ^ { - 1 }$ by multiplying the BDOC concentration and annual precipitation in this study. Such a deposition of BDOC to coastal environments is important for regional carbon cycle and ecosystem, since it may stimulate microbial secondary production and may be rapidly degraded to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ . The deposition of rainwater BDOC to terrestrial and marine environments may pose an underestimated priming effect on the degradation of refractory DOM from other sources such as soil, river, groundwater, and upwelling of deep seawater, which is worth future testing for better understanding the carbon cycle at the land-ocean interface (Guenet et al., 2010; Bianchi, 2011; Zhuang and Yang, 2018).

Microbial transformation of rainwater DOM was further examined based on changes in the absorption and fluorescence indices in the microbial incubation experiment (Table S2). The absorption coefficient $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ decreased by $0 \% { - } 5 8 \%$ (average: $1 7 \% \pm 1 6 \% )$ , which was lower than the bioavailability of bulk DOM (mean of %BDOC: $5 0 \% \pm \ : 1 9 \%$ . This suggested that CDOM was less bioavailable than non-chromophoric components of DOM in rainwater. ${ \tt S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , as a ratio of UV absorbance to DOC, increased in all incubations, indicating an increase in the aromaticity of DOM as a result of microbial transformations (Weishaar et al., 2003; Derrien et al., 2017). The increase of $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ might be caused by consumption of aliphatic, non-aromatic constituents and/or the production of humic-like C3. The responses of C1-C4 to microbial transformations were more complicated. The humiclike C1 was removed after biodegradation for most events except for Event 14, and C2 was also removed for most events with little changes for Events 15 and 18. C3 increased after incubation for all events, suggesting a microbial production of this humic-like component at a longest emission maximum wavelength of $4 5 6 \mathrm { n m }$ . Overall, our results suggested that microbes might transform parts of humic-like components at shorter emission wavelengths (C1-C2) to that at a longer emission wavelength (C3), which might indicate an increase in the length of conjugated unsaturated structures of DOM. The tyrosine-like C4 was produced in 4 events while it was removed in other 3 events, suggesting this component could act as either a by-product of microbial activity or a metabolism substrate for microbial utilization. Therefore, the results demonstrated that the effects of microbial activity on rainwater fluorescent DOM were dependent on both different components and different events. This was similar to different effects of microbial transformation on different fluorescent components in other aquatic environments (Lønborg et al., 2010; Chen and Jaffé, 2014, 2016; Yang et al., 2017).

# 5. Conclusions

Rainwater DOC concentration showed a notable variation in southeast China, depending on seasonal changes in the dominant monsoon system, rainfall amount and terrestrial/anthropogenic inputs. The annual rainwater flux of DOC in this study was comparable to those for other coastal sites globally and was much higher than those for remote ocean/continental sites, indicating the importance of atmospheric wet deposition in coastal carbon cycle. The high bioavailability of rainwater DOM further indicated that rainwater was an important allochthonous source of microbial metabolism substrate and much of the rainwater DOC could be degraded to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ . CDOM was generally less labile than non-chromophoric DOM, and different fluorescent components showed different responses to microbial transformations in different rain events. The strong correlations between DOC and optical parameters $( a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ and $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C2-C4) suggested absorption and fluorescence techniques were valuable tools for estimating the bulk DOM quantity. PCA and air-mass trajectory analysis showed optical techniques could provide DOM indices for discriminating different rain events and had the potential of tracking organic matter sources. These results are important for better understanding the underlying mechanisms controlling the source, concentration, chemical composition, deposition flux and biogeochemical reactivity of rainwater DOM.
