Photodegradation and biodegradation of pyrogenic dissolved organic matter (pyDOM) play crucial roles in regulating greenhouse gas emissions and stabilizing organic mater.In this study,we conducted a 56-day microbial laboratory incubation to investigate the biodegradation paterns of pristine and photobleached pyDOM leached from a thermal series of chars (3oo,450,and $6 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ based on FT-ICR-MS,gas analysis,N speciation, and 16S rDNA sequencing.As the pyrolysis temperature increased,the among of biomineralized carbon of pyDOMdecreased, while the molecular diversity and aromaticity increased.Photobleaching increased the among of biomineralized carbon of pyDOM from $4 0 \% - 7 0 \%$ to $6 0 \% 8 0 \%$ ,but decreased the molecular diversity,molecular weight,and aromaticity,with a major removal of lignin-likecompounds.Microbial incubation lowered the molecular diversity but increased the molecular weight and aromaticity of both pristine and photobleached samples.Tannin-like structures,carbohydrates,and aliphatic/protein were preferentially biodegraded,while lipid,lignin-like structures,unsaturated hydrocarbons,and condensed aromatics were more likely to be preserved or newly produced.The photobio-resistant components were mainly comprised of lignin-like and tanninlike structures.Photobleached pyDOM samples released more $\mathrm { C O _ { 2 } }$ but less $\mathrm { N _ { 2 } O _ { ; } }$ ，as they may possess high denitrification ratedue to their high carbon reactivity.Thebacterial richness and diversity in pyDOMwere lower than the original inoculum.In addition,distinct differences were noted between the bacterial community structure of the original inoculum and that of pristine and photobleached pyDOM samples following Various incubation times. Specifically，proteobacteria was increased and remained the predominant phylum in all pyDOM solutions compared to the original inoculum,while Actinobacteriota,Bdellvibrionota,Firmicutes,or Nitrospirota phyla were increased in several samples either after7or 56 days of incubation.In particular, photobleached pyDOM pyrolyzed at $6 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ exhibited the strongest filtration effects on the bacterial microflora. These results help to elucidate the biogeochemical cycling and turnover of pyDOM in sunlit and dark aquatic environments such as fluvial and groundwater networks,lakes,and the ocean.

# 1．Introduction

Global forest fires and widespread pyrogenic organic matter (pyOM) application in the form of biochar contribute substantially to the import of pyrogenic dissolved organic matter (pyDOM) into aquatic environments through rainwater runoff (Bostick et al., 202O; Fu et al., 2016; Fu et al., 2018; Smith et al., 2013). Approximately 18-203 Tg year-1 pyrogenic dissolved organic carbon of condensed aromatic carbon is projected to enter the environment every year (Bostick et al.,2018).

Notably,pyDOM is highly heterogeneous and contains a variety of aliphatic molecules (Wozniak et al., 2020), which suggests that pyDOM may be of high biolability and an active component in the exchangeable carbon pools.Thus,current studies should underline the importance of pyDOM in accounting for greenhouse gas emissions (i.e., $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ ,and $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ ）and carbon stabilization so as to better understand the corresponding effects on climate change.

PyOM is widely known for its stability and carbon sequestration potential (Goldberg,1985; Han et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2018). As the more labile and mobile fraction in the pyOM continuum, pyDOM may be more bio-accessible than other particulate forms of carbon (Bostick et al.，2O2l).However,information about the biodegradability of pyDOM along the combustion continuum is far from robust.Due to the heterogeneous nature of the pyOM continuum (Wozniak et al., 2020), the bioavailability and reactivity of pyDOM may vary with its varying inherent qualities (Guillemette et al., 2016; Hur et al., 2011). As such, using pyDOM extracted from pyOM of various feedstock sources and pyrolysis temperatures can provide another avenue for studying the environmental behaviour of pyDOM.

The early research agenda on pyDOM is mainly about its photoactivity,as it is one of the more photolabile fractions in the DOM pool (Bostick et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2016; Yan et al., 2022). Upon entering the aquatic environment, pyDOM inevitably undergoes photoirradiation, which can substantially alter its molecular composition and biogeochemical behaviour (Bostick et al., 2021; Goranov et al., 202O; Ward et al., 2014; Yan et al., 2O22). Only until recently, research has begun to explore the biolability of pyDOM (Bostick et al., 202l; Goranov et al., 2022; Qi et al., 2020; Wagner et al., 2021). Bostick et al. (2021) evaluated the biodegradability of oak-derived pyDOM produced at rather high temperatures (400 and $6 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ and determined that higher pyrolysis temperature and photobleaching treatment increased the carbon biomineralization. Based on the work by Bostick et al. (2O21),Goranov et al.(2O22） further investigated the composition evolution of oakderived pyDOM after 1O days of laboratory incubation and revealed that microbial reworking may contribute to the complexity and diversity of DOM pool.However, the compositional evolution of other biomassderived pyDOM under different degrees of photobleaching treatments, as well as corresponding elemental cycling (e.g.,nitrogen) and microbial succession,is only now on the verge of the wider view.

Microbes play a key role in regulating DOM transformations and vice versa (Chen et al., 2022; Osterholz et al., 2016). Previous reports have underlined the potential role of natural DOM in the succession of bacterial communities (Moran and Zepp,1997; Zhou et al., 2021). It is noted that DOM aromaticity was significantly correlated with bacterial community succession (Zhou et al., 2021).PyDOMhas high aromaticity (Yan et al., 2O22) and may have strong influences on the structure of bacterial communities.In addition,as the aromatic fraction was more susceptible to photoalteration，the aromaticity of pyDOM can be significantly lowered by photobleaching (Yan et al.,2O22).Thus,the corresponding bacterial community succession in photobleached pyDOM samples may differ significantly from that in pristine pyDOM samples. Up until now,research on the succession of microbes in pyDOM and their possible role is lacking.We hypothesize that pristine and photobleached pyDOM present distinctly different opportunities for biodegradation,which may influence the structure of the microbial community and its succession over different incubation times.

In this study,pyDOM extracted from corn straw pyOM samples of wider pyrolysis temperature range (3oo,450,and $6 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ was subjected to photobleaching treatment and 56-day laboratory incubation. Fourier transform - ion cyclotron resonance- mass spectrometry (FT-ICR-MS), gas analysis,N speciation,and 16S rDNA sequencing were jointly applied to explore the biodegradation of pyDOM.The major objectives of this study were to (1） explore the biodegradation patterns and structural shifts of pyDOM in response to pyrolysis temperature,photobleaching treatment,and microbiological incubation time,(2) track the carbon and nitrogen dynamics during pyDOM degradation, and (3) investigate the temporal dynamics of bacterial community succession in response to various pyDOM sources and incubation times.

# 2.Materials and methods

# 2.1.Preparation of a thermal series of pyDOM

Corn straw was collected from Jiangsu Province (China) and airdried prior to use.The dried biomass was pulverized into a fine powder using a high-speed grinder.A thermal series of pyOM samples was prepared as per a previous study with minor modifications (Wang et al.,2Ol6).In brief, the corn straw powder was pyrolysed in a muffle furnace under oxygen-limited conditions. The pyrolysis temperatures were set at 300,450,and $6 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ witha heating rate of $1 5 ~ \mathrm { { ^ circ C / m i n } }$ and were maintained at the target temperatures for $^ \textrm { \scriptsize 1 h }$ ,respectively.

The specific processes of pyDOM extraction were reported in earlier studies and only briefly introduced here (Yan et al., 2022).Milli-Q water was added to the recovered pyOM samples at a water to solid ratio of 10:1.The mixture was shaken at $1 5 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ and then centrifuged at $4 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ for $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ .Subsequently,the supernatant was passed through $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ mixed cellulose ester membranes (MF-Millipore） to obtain filtrates (pyDOM). Next, the pyDOM solutions were treated with 1 M HCl $( 3 2 \%$ ,guaranteed reagent) to $\mathrm { p H } < 3$ to remove most carbonates and then neutralized using O.1 M NaOH.Here,the recovered pyDOM from pyOM samples produced at 300,450,and $6 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ were abbreviated as pyDOM300,pyDOM450,and pyDOM600,respectively. The recovered pyDOM concentrations,as well as the pH values of the original and HCl-NaOH treated pyDOM, were listed in Table S1.

# 2.2.Photoirradiation treatment

In order to quickly obtain the photobleached samples and minimize the effects of microbial degradation,the pyDOM solutions were diluted toarelatively high concentration $( 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ O C / L } )$ using Milli-Q water prior to photoirradiation treatment (Fu et al., 2Ol6). In a typical run, $2 5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of $1 0 0 \ : \mathrm { m g } \ : 0 \mathrm { C / L }$ pyDOM solution was added into $3 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ quartz tubes with an aluminium foil cover to prevent evaporation.Then, the quartz tubes were subjected to ultraviolet irradiation for $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ at a distance of $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ to quickly obtain the photobleached samples in a PLDY1600 photochemical reactor (Beijing Precise Technology Co.,Ltd., China). The output energy was $2 0 0 \mathrm { \ : W / m ^ { 2 } }$ in the range of $2 9 0 { \mathrm { - } } 4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ which is similar to the wavelength range of natural sunlight (Helms et al.,2008; Spencer et al.， 2009) and equivalent to 4.6 sun power (Hulstrom et al.,1985). Therefore,assuming 12-h dark-light cycles, the 24-h laboratory irradiation is equivalent to 9.2 days of natural exposure to sunlight.All tubes were magnetically stirred at $2 5 \mathrm { r p m }$ using a Teflon magnet at the bottom.In a typical run,the tubes were evenly distributed in the central rotating disc at $6 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ to receive uniform light illumination.The air-cooled instrument maintained the reaction temperature at $2 5 \pm 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ Approximately $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of pyDOM solution was withdrawn at 0,1,2,4,8,12,24,48,96,and $^ \textrm { \scriptsize 1 9 2 h }$ for characterization.Each treatment was performed in triplicate.According to the results of preliminary analyses and our previous study (Yan et al., 2O22), most fluorescent and aromatic fractions were removed in the initial $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ Thus, following $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ of irradiation,the photobleached and pristine samples were exposed to microbiological laboratory incubation.The pristine and photobleached pyDOM sampleswere referred to as pyDOM300, pyDOM450， pyDOM600andP-pyDOM300，P-pyDOM450，PpyDOM600,respectively．Here，the numbers (i.e.，300，450,600) represent the production temperatures.In addition, the capital letter P refers to light illumination treatment and represents photobleached samples.

# 2.3.Incubation experiment

The microbiological incubation was set as a two-factorial of pyDOM pyrolysis temperature and photobleaching treatment. The inoculum was prepared by concentrating bacterial suspensions from fresh topsoil (top $2 0 \mathrm { c m }$ )collected atthe Shangzhuang experimental station,Beijing.The topsoil is periodically amended with pyOMand its microbes are likely to interact with pyDOM (Mitchell et al.,2015).Under rainy weather, pyDOM along with soil microbes may be leached into water and transported into the aquatic environment.The media for the incubation experiment were prepared by diluting the original and photobleached pyDOM solutions to $\sim 2 0 \ : \mathrm { m g \ O C / L }$ using Milli-Q water,which is within the range of aquatic DOC concentrations $( 0 . 2 – 2 0 \ m g \ { \mathrm { C / L } } )$ (Driscoll et al., 1995).

The 56-day incubation experiment was started in July 2O21 and lasted for 56 days.There were six treatments (pyDOM300,pyDOM450, pyDOM6oo,and their photobleached samples) each with 24 replications.Each $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m L } }$ glass bottle received $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ pyDOM medium and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ inoculum,as well as a microbial nutrient supply of $8 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ $( \mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } ) _ { 2 } S 0 _ { 4 }$ and $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ ${ \mathrm { N a } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { H P O } } _ { 4 }$ according to a previous study (Zhou et al.,2021).Among the 24 replications,three subsamples were used for headspace gas monitoring.The other 21 subsamples were used for periodic destructive sampling to sequentially measure aqueous DOC,IC, $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ and $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ concentrations,as well as bacterial community structure. All glass bottles were sealed with stoppers (Cat.# 60304-50) equipped with disposable aluminium caps (PART# 9301-0721).Approximately $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ headspace gas samples were withdrawn periodically from three replicates after0,7,14,21,28,42,and 56 days for $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ and $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ detection.After gas sample collection, the botle caps were removed for fresh air replacement to guarantee enough oxygen supply.Subsequently, the bottles were resealed with disposable rubber aluminium caps,and the interior gas was collected as the initial sample in the following incubation stage.Meanwhile,destructive sampling was conducted at the same time of 0,7,14,21,28,42,and 56 days,and each sampling irreversibly consumed three subsamples.The controls of abiotic incubations were also prepared by adding $5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of saturated $\mathrm { H g C l } _ { 2 }$ solution, and the corresponding DOC concentrations were continuously checked.

# 2.4.Analytical methods

# 2.4.1. Measurements of cumulative greenhouse gas emissions

After gas collection,the concentrations of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ and $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ in all gas samples were measured immediately using a gas chromatograph (Agilent Technologies,789oB GC System） through manual injection. Specifically,approximately $3 \ \mathrm { m L }$ gas was subjected to $\mathrm { C O _ { 2 } }$ or $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ measurement separately bya flame ionization detector (FID, $2 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ， and the rest $3 \mathrm { m L }$ gas was subjected to $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ measurement by an electron capture detector (ECD) operating at $3 3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ .The detection limits were 2.02-60.6 ppmv for $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ and 356-4020 ppmv for $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 } ,$ and the analysis precisions were $\pm \nobreakspace 3 2 \nobreakspace$ ppbv for $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 } , \pm 1 . 2 9 $ ppmv for $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\pm \ 5 . 0$ ppbv for $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ (Wang and Wang, 2003; Wang et al., 201O). The gas concentrations were standardized by standard gas comprising of 345 ppbv of $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ ,2 ppmv of $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ ，380 ppmv of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ . The cumulative gas emissions were calculated as the difference between the final gas concentration and the initial gas concentration after a period of incubation. Almost no $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ emissions were detected during the entire process.

# 2.4.2. Chemical analyses

The DOC and IC concentrations of pyDOM solutions were measured using a TOC-L-ASI analyser (Sievers 9oO) with potassum hydrogen phthalate calibration.The concentration of aqueous $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ and $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ in pyDOM was detected using a continuous flow analyser (Seal AA3, SEAL Analytical, Germany) with a detection limit of $0 . 0 0 5 \mathrm { ~ m g } \mathrm { - N ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$

The two-pool exponential model was employed to interpret the carbon loss of the pyDOM solutions. The calculation equations were as follows:

$$
\begin{array} { l } { { C _ { m } = C _ { L } \left( 1 - e ^ { - k _ { 1 } t } \right) + \left( 1 0 0 - C _ { L } \right) \left( 1 - e ^ { - k _ { 2 } t } \right) } } \\ { { \ } } \\ { { H a l f - l i f e = l n ( 2 ) \times k ^ { - 1 } } } \end{array}
$$

where $C _ { m }$ is the DOC mineralization ratio of pyDOM $( \% )$ ， $C _ { L }$ and $( 1 0 0 – \mathrm { C } _ { L } )$ are the relative abundance of labile and stable DOM pool in total pyDOM using 100 as a whole, $t$ is the incubation time (d),and $k _ { 1 }$ and $k _ { 2 }$ are the biodegradation rate constants for the labile and stable carbon $( \mathsf { d } ^ { - 1 } )$ ,respectively.

# 2.4.3. Characterization of pyDOM

The original and photodegraded pyDOM,as well as their corresponding biodegraded products,were measured by UV-vis and fluorescence spectroscopy.The UV-vis spectra were obtained using a DR6000 spectrophotometer with a scanning range of $2 0 0 { - } 8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and a step size of $_ { 1 \mathrm { n m } }$ .The UVabsorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ of pyDOM was recorded to obtain the $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (the absorbance at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m }$ to DOC ratio) parameter, which served as an indicator of aromaticity (De Haan and De Boer，1987; Weishaar et al.， 2003). The slope ratio $( S _ { R } = S _ { 2 6 5 - 2 7 5 } /$ S350-4oo) was utilized as an indicator of molecular weight (Helms et al., 2008).Three-dimensional excitation-emission matrices (EEMs） were recorded using an F-46oo fluorometer.The excitation/emission wavelengths were set as 200-450/250-550 nm with a step size of $5 \mathrm { n m }$ .To remove the majority of Raman and Rayleigh scatterings,a blank scan was performed using Milli-Q water.Prior to EEM analysis,the DOM samples were diluted for highly absorbing samples to guarantee the UV absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ below 0.3 (Miller and McKnight, 201O). Also, the inner-filter effect correlation was conducted for proper data processing of EEMs (Kothawala et al., 2013; McKnight et al., 2001).

Electrospray ionization coupled with ultrahigh resolution Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (ESI-FT-ICR-MS) was employed to resolve the molecular composition shifts in pyDOM samples.Prior to ESI-FT-ICR-MS analysis,pre-treatments,including acidification,desalination,and enrichment of pyDOM samples,were processed by solid-phase extraction (SPE) using Varian Bond Elute-PPL cartridges ( $_ { 1 \ g }$ per $6 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ ,Part No: 12255225) (see Text S1 for detail information) (Dittmar et al., 2Oo8). These procedures helped to maximize the extraction efficiency of phenols and organic acids (Dittmar et al.,2Oo8).The recovery rate of DOC during SPE procedure was $7 5 . 0 { - } 8 5 . 0 \%$ in this study.Then,ESI-FT-ICR-MS analysis was performed to obtain ultrahigh-resolution mass spectra using a Bruker SolariX FTICR-MS equipped with a $1 5 . 0 \mathrm { ~ T ~ }$ superconducting magnet and a dualmode ESI/MALDI ion source.In brief, the ESI needle voltage was set as $- 3 . 8 \ \mathrm { k V }$ ，The treated samples were infused into the ESI source at a flow rate of $1 2 0 ~  { \mu \mathrm { L } } /  { \mathrm { h } }$ and analyzed in negative ionization mode with broadband detection.The spectra were externally calibrated with 10 mM sodium formate dissolved in $5 0 \%$ isopropyl alcohol solution using a linear calibration.The calibrant is complex ions bonded by sodium formate through hydrogen bonds,having a mass range of 18o-l200 Da. Then,internal recalibration was conducted using an in-house reference mas list.After calibration,the mass error was constrained to $< 5 0 0 \mathrm { p p b }$ Also,peaks observed in solvent blanks were removed.Each sample was processed and analyzed twice,and the shared molecular assignments in duplicates were averaged for further calculation.Formulas assignment of possible peaks was constrained as $\mathrm { C _ { 1 - 8 0 } H _ { 2 - 2 0 0 } O _ { 0 - 4 0 } N _ { 0 - 3 } S _ { 0 - 2 } }$ (Herzsprung et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2015; Lv et al., 2016; Shi et al., 2021; Vetere and Schrader, 2017). The elemental ratios of $0 . 3 3 3 < \mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C } <$ 2.25， $0 / \mathrm { C } < 1 . 2$ ， $0 \leq \mathbf { C } + 2$ were applied as further restrictions for formula calculation (Herzsprung et al., 2Ol2). Detail information about ESI-FT-ICR data processing was provided in Text S2.The weighted parameters, including $\mathrm { O } / \mathrm { C } _ { \mathrm { W } }$ $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C } _ { \mathrm { W } }$ ，weighted molecular weight $( \mathrm { M W _ { W } } )$ modified aromaticity index $\left( \mathrm { { A I } _ { \mathrm { { m o d } } } } \right)$ (Koch and Dittmar, 2006; Riedel et al., 20l2),and weighted double bond equivalent $\scriptstyle ( \mathrm { D B E } _ { \mathrm { W } } )$ (Koch and Dittmar, 2006; Riedel et al., 2012; Stenson et al., 2003), were calculated to reveal shifts in the molecular characteristics of pyDOM after photobleaching treatment and again after laboratory incubation (Text S3). The distribution of seven component groups based on van Krevelen diagrams,including aliphatic/protein (H/C:1.5-2.2; O/C:0.3-0.67), carbohydrate (H/C:1.5-2.2; O/C:0.67-1.2),unsaturated hydrocarbon (H/C:0.7-1.5;O/C:0-0.3),tannin-like (H/C:0-1.5;O/C: 0.67-1.2), lignin-like (H/C:0.7-1.5;O/C:0.3-0.67),lipid (H/C:1.5-2.2; O/C: 0-0.3),and condensed aromatics (H/C:0.2-0.7;O/C:O-0.67)，was drawn to visualize the structural shifts in pyDOM (Herzsprung et al., 2012; Hockaday et al., 2009; Kim et al., 2003).

# 2.5． Statistical analyses

Prior to data analysis,all variables were subjected to the Shapiro-Wilk test for normality and the Levene's test for homogeneity of variance. If not passed, the data were then subjected to logarithmic or square-root transformation.The effects of production temperature, photobleaching treatment,and incubation time on pyDOM structure, C and N dynamics,and microbial communities were tested by three-way analysis of variance (ANOVA).If there were significant effects,Tukey's HSD test was conducted to assess the differences at $p < 0 . 0 5$ .Principal coordinate analysis (PCoA) was conducted to illustrate the differences in pyDOM molecular composition and microbial community based on Bray Curtis distance.The removescatter toolbox was employed to remove extra scatterings in EMM spectra using MATLAB R2O17b.Then,parallel factor (PARAFAC) modeling was performed with non-negative constraints using MATLAB R2017b with DOMFlour toolbox.All the statistical analyses were processed and visualized using the software IBM SPSS statistics 26 (SPSS Inc.Chicago,IL,USA) and MATLAB R2017b.

# 3．Results and discussions

# 3.1.Structural shifts in pristine and photobleached pyDOM at diffrent pyrolysis temperatures after 56-day incubation

In the $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ photoirradiation,pyDOMof lower pyrolysis temperature degraded more rapidly under photoirradiation (Fig. S1) (Stubbins et al., 2012; Ward et al., 2014; Yan et al., 2022), with the photomineralization ratio of carbon in pyDOM ranging from $6 8 . 7 2 \substack { - 8 5 . 3 5 \% }$ ，The photobleaching intensity in this study was stronger than previous records (Bostick et al., 202l; Goranov et al., 2022) and thus resulted in greater structural changes of pyDOM.Alternatively,our pyDOM samples could have been of higher photo-reactivity due to the different pyOM feedstock (corn straw) or differences in pyOM pyrolysis conditions.

$S _ { R }$ and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ parameters by UV-vis analysis were applied to evaluate the basic structural shifts of pyDOM in response to pyrolysis temperatures,photoirradiation treatment,and 56-day microbiological incubation (Fig.la-b).As the pyrolysis temperature increased, $S _ { R }$ was barely altered,while $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ first increased and then decreased.This indicated the molecular weight of different pyDOM samples was similar, while the aromaticity of pyDOM followed a descending order of $\mathrm { p y D O M 4 5 0 } > \mathrm { p y D O M 6 0 0 } > \mathrm { p y D O M 3 0 0 }$ (Yan et al.,2022). Significant differences in $S _ { R }$ and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ were noted for pyDOM from pristine and photobleached samples $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ . The $S _ { R }$ values of pristine pyDOM samples were increased after photoirradiation treatment,while the $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were lowered, suggesting the preferential removal of high-molecular-weight and high-aromaticity fractions under photoirradiation (Bostick et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2016; Ward et al., 2014). As the laboratory incubation time was prolonged,the $S _ { R }$ value was generally lowered in both pristine and photobleached pyDOM samples (Fig.la). Specifically, the $S _ { R }$ value of pristine pyDOM samples was only slightly lowered after microbial incubation,owing to the recalcitrant nature of pristine samples.In comparison, the $S _ { R }$ value of photobleached pyDOM samples was significantly lowered after microbial incubation. This is understandable,as the photobleaching treatment may enhance the bioavailability of pyDOM by transforming high-molecular-weight fractions into low-molecular components.In general, the $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ of both pristine and photobleached pyDOM increased as the incubation proceeded (Fig.1b),suggesting the preferential mineralization of lowmolecular-weight and less aromatic fractions as observed by Bostick et al. (2021).

The EEM spectra of pristine and photobleached pyDOM at different incubation times were listed in Fig. S2. Obviously,after $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ of photoirradiation, the fluorescent components of pyDOM were substantially removed,as they constituted the more photolabile fraction in pyDOM (Fu et al., 2Ol6). To assess the biodegradation of different underlying fluorophoric groups,PARAFAC analysis was performed (Murphy et al., 2014).The PARAFAC analysis supported a three-component model (C1, C2,and C3)(Fig.lc-e),which have been observed in natural DOMin the lake,ocean,and soil in previous studies according to the OpenFluor online software (Murphy et al.,2O14). Component C1 featured two excitation/emission peaks at $2 4 0 / 3 9 0 \ \mathrm { \ n m }$ and $3 1 0 / 4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ，while component C2 centred at $2 6 0 / 4 6 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 3 0 / 4 6 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ .These two components could be humic acid-like substances with high hydrophobicity and large molecular size (Uchimiya et al., 2O13). Component C3 peaked at $2 2 5 / 3 2 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $2 7 5 / 3 2 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ，which could be substances with tyrosine,tryptophan，protein-like,or tyrosine-like structures located in the low excitation/emission region (Ghidotti et al., 2017). Overall,photoirradiation significantly decreased the FMax value of components C1 and C2 in pyDOM300,pyDOM450,and pyDOM600 (Fig.lf-h),suggesting the photodegradation of those high-molecularweight humic acid-like substances under illumination.During the 56 days of incubation,the FMax values of C1 in pristine pyDOM samples were generally decreased,while that of C3 in both pristine and photobleached pyDOM samples were decreased,suggesting the microbial utilization of such substances.The reason for the degradation of Cl only in pristine samples was potentially attributed to the very low proportion of C1 in the photobleached samples.In contrast,microbial incubation slightly increased the FMax value of C2,suggesting that proteinaceous exudates were released into solution by the microbes.This agrees with previous observations by Goranov et al. (2022).

ESI-FT-ICR-MS was employed to show the degradation patterns of pristine and photobleached pyDOM (Figs.2-4，Figs.S3-S4，and Table S2). One potential explanation is that the progression of depolymerization reactions produced more types of organic compounds,and more aromatic and high-molecular-weight fractions were formed at higher temperatures (Hao et al., 2018). This is consistent with the photodegradation of natural DOM,with the transformation of high molecular weight components into low molecular weight components and the conversion into less condensed compounds (Stubbins et al., 201O; Wagner and Jaffé, 2015). After 56 days of incubation，the molecular diversity decreased,except in P-pyDOM600 (Table S2).This is different from previous research which reported an increased molecular diversity after incubation (Goranov et al.,2022).

The peak intensity-weighed relative abundance of the seven component groups further revealed shifts in the molecular composition of different pyDOM samples (Fig.2).As summarized in Fig.2c,pyDOM produced at higher temperatures had a higher relative abundance of tannin-like structures and condensed aromatics but lower carbohydrate, aliphatic/protein,and lignin-like contents.This may be associated with dehydrogenation, demethylation,and polymerization reactions during the pyrolysis process,which may transform unsaturated aliphatic hydrocarbons into condensed aromatics (Sleighter and Hatcher, 2007; Zhang et al.,2O2O).Photobleaching treatment distinctly lowered the relative abundance of lignin-like structures but increased the proportion of the other six component groups (i.e.,tannin-like,carbohydrate, aliphatic/protein,lipid,unsaturated hydrocarbon,and condensed aromatics). As the laboratory incubation proceeded,a similar biodegradation pattern was seen for both pristine and photobleached pyDOM at different pyrolysis temperatures.The relative abundance of tannin-like structures,carbohydrates,and aliphatic/protein decreased,while lipids,lignin-like structures,unsaturated hydrocarbons,and condensed aromatics occupied increasing proportions after incubation.This indicated that these highly condensed components,such as condensed aromatics and lignin-like components,were extremely bio-recalcitrant (Burhenne et al.,2013; Kim et al.，2006),and those enriched compounds,such as aliphatic/protein and carbohydrates,were either less degraded or newly generated by microbes (Goranov et al., 2O22).The results showed that lignin-like structures were more photolabile,while tannin-like structures,carbohydrate,and aliphatic/protein were more biodegradable.The shifts in elemental composition of diferent pyDOM pre- and post-incubation were also displayed by PCoA based on Bray Curtis distance.(Fig.2d).The PC1 and PC2 principal coordinates together explained ${ \sim } 6 6 \%$ of molecular alteration of various pyDOM. # 3.3.Carbon and nitrogen dynamics of pristine and photobleached pyDOM

The carbon dynamics of pristine and photobleached pyDOM was checked by detecting the cumulative $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ emissions, as well as the DOC and IC concentration variation (Fig. 5a-c). Over the 56-day incubation, only a small proportion $( 3 - 5 \% )$ of organic carbon was lost through abiotic oxidation,similar to what Bostick et al.(2O2l) saw.As shown by the DOC concentration,the mineralization ratio had a sequence of P-pyDOM300 $>$ pyDOM300 $>$ P-pyDOM450～PpyDOM600 $>$ pyDOM450 $\sim$ pyDOM600 $( p \ < \ 0 . 0 5 )$ ，ranging from $1 4 . 6 { - } 7 9 . 9 \%$ (Fig.5a). Specifically，pyDOM30o had an ultrahigh mineralization ratio,which was supported by the high carbon loss $( 6 0 \% )$ from the mesquite char leachate with a rather low pyrolysis temperature of $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Norwood et al., 2013). Such results showed that pyDOM with a lower pyrolysis temperature was more biodegradable.This is understandable,as pyDOMat higher pyrolysis temperatures contained higher proportions of recalcitrant carbon sources.Moreover，the carbon mineralization ratios of both pristine and photobleached pyDOM were over $4 0 \%$ ,with those of the photobleached samples exceeding $6 0 \%$ .This showed that photobleaching treatment increased the mineralization ratio of pyDOM(Figs.2c and 5a). One possible explanation is that those recalcitrant fractions with high aromaticity and molecular weight were decomposed into smaller sized aromatic clusters with higher bioavailability.Moreover,the priming effects of the photogenerated small molecules may also serve as a potentially valid explanation (Zimmerman and Ouyang,2Ol9).Noteworthy is the fact that our study showed stronger effects of photobleaching treatment on carbon biomineralization than previous records (Bostick et al., 202l; Goranov et al., 2022), which was potentially attributed to the stronger photobleaching intensity,the use of different feedstock type and the selection of different pyrolysis conditions (pyrolysis temperature and baking time） in this study.The two-pool exponential model further illustrated the component distribution of labile and stable carbon,as well as the corresponding half-life (Table 1).The results showed that photobleached samples often had a higher proportion of labile carbon and shorter halflife of both labile and stable fractions except for P-pyDOM3oo.The significantly lowered half-life of the photobleached samples supplied a new perspective on carbon cycling and turnover of pyDOM in natural aquatic environments,where it experiences continuous sunlight illumination and microbial utilization.

# 3.4.Bacterial community succession after 7-day and 56-day incubation

As mentioned above,distinct structural differences in organic matter were observed for pyDOM of different pyrolysis temperatures pre- and post-irradiation.Thus,the corresponding available carbon constitution varies greatly and can somehow affect microbial succession and vice versa (Zhou et al.,2O21).In this study,we first conducted a microbial succession study on pristine and photobleached pyDOM samples. Compared with the original inoculums, the abundance and diversity of bacteria were greatly modified by different pyDOM sources with different incubation times (i.e.,7 days and 56 days) (Table 2).The operational taxonomic unit (OTU) abundance,ACE, Chao 1, Shannon, and Simpson indices for the bacterial 16S gene were all lowered in different pyDOM solutions.As such,the abundance and diversity of bacteria decreased during incubation with varied pyDOM sources.In particular,the photobleached pyDOM had lower bacterial abundance and diversity than the untreated samples.Additionally,the sharpest decreases in these indices were noted for P-pyDOM6oo among the pristine and photobleached pyDOM after 7 days of incubation.As the incubation time was prolonged from 7 days to 56 days,the indices of PpyDOM300,pyDOM450,and P-pyDOM450 further decreased,while those of pyDOM300，pyDOM600,and P-pyDOM600 increased，suggesting greater microbial selection pressures of the former environment but the support of microbial succession in the latter.Overall,PpyDOM3o0 and P-pyDOM450 had the lowest bacterial abundance and diversity after 56 days,while pyDOM6oo showed the highest bacterial abundance and diversity.Such results suggested that both pyDOM sources (regarding pyrolysis temperature and whether treated with photobleaching） and incubation time served as crucial factors that governed the metabolic viability influences on the bacterial community structure.

Distinct differences were also noted for the bacterial community compositions of the original inoculum,pristine pyDOM samples and photobleached samples at different incubation times (Fig.6a-b).At the phylum level, the original inoculum mainly comprised Proteobacteria and Bacteroidota,which expressed ${ \sim } 7 5 \%$ of its microbial composition (Fig.6).During the incubation,taxa with varied abundance patterns responded differently to different pyDOM sources.After 7-day and 56- day incubation,Proteobacteria,which comprised a huge variety of nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Delmont et al.,2Ol8),was increased and remained the predominant phylum in all pyDOM solutions,which supported the speculation by Goranov et al. (2O22).In contrast, the relative abundance of Bacteroidota was lowered in pyDOM300, pyDOM600，and P-pyDOM600 after 7 days and in pyDOM300, pyDOM450,P-pyDOM450,and P-pyDOM600 after 56 days.Selected phyla,including Actinobacteriota,Bdellovibrionota,and Firmicutes, were increased in certain samples after 7 days of incubation.In addition, the Actinobacteriota and Nitrospirota phyla were increased in several samples after 56 days of incubation.The Actinobacteria phylum was more tolerant to environmental variation (Dai et al.,2O2O),while the Nitrospirota phylum was associated with nitrifying bacteria (Svobodova et al.,202l).At the genus level, the top 14 genera in pyDOM solutions only occupied a small proportion $( { \sim } 1 2 \% )$ of the original inoculum, suggesting strong microbial succession in different pyDOM solutions (Fig.6). Some consistent genera were seen in pyDOM300,P-pyDOM300, pyDOM450,P-pyDOM450,and pyDOM600,while the generic composition of P-pyDOM6oo was substantially different.In addition，the generic compositions of the 7-day samples were significantly different from those of the 56-day samples.Typically,the observed Nitrospira in 56-day samples could contribute to nitrification as an aerobic chemolithoautotrophic nitrite-oxidizer (Daims and Wagner, 2018).

# 3.5.Environmental implications

The photochemistry and biogeochemistry of pyDOM,which served as an important endmember in DOM pool,are crucial to global carbon flux and regulating the global cycling of other important elements (i.e., N,P,and Si) (Lian et al., 2O21).This study demonstrated that pyDOMs in terms of different pyrolysis temperature and photobleaching treatment could distinctly alter the microbial community,and thus regulate their own carbon and nitrogen metabolism,as well as molecular composition. These results are crucial to our understanding of the compositional evolution,turnover,elemental cycling,and microbial succession of pyDOM.Also,our study provided a new perspective on the effects of pyDOM on greenhouse gas emission and associated climate change.

The application of soil microbes as inoculum in this study concerned the high diversity of soil microbes and the frequent transport of soil microbes to adjacent aquatic system,which may reveal the microbial succession from land to aquatic environment.Also,the soil microbes used was from the topsoil periodically amended with pyOM (Mitchell etal.,2O15)and its microbes are likely to already develop strategies for pyDOM metabolism and transformation.In future work,however, the use of different microbial inoculum involving riverine,estuarine,and marine environment would be crucial to fully understand the compositional evolution and turnover of pyDOM regarding different environment conditions.Moreover,further investigations should be carried out in terms of more aspects of pyDOM mineralization,such as the measurement of functional genes,which has not been performed in this study but may help a lot in understanding the biogeochemical process of pyDOM.

In the real environment, the DOC concentrations are rarely above 20 $\mathrm { m g \ O C / L }$ .However,in order to obtain enough samples in a short time $( 2 4 ~ \mathrm { h } )$ and minimize the effects of microbial degradation,a relatively higher pyDOM concentration of $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ O C / L }$ was selected to process the photodegradation experiment (Fu et al.,2Ol6).Also,the photodegradation and compositional evolution of pyDOM in the initial $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ varied from the subsequent stage $( 2 4 { - } 7 2 0 \ \mathrm { h } )$ (Yan et al., 2022). Thus, the photobiodegradation pattern of pyDOM considering a lower concentration and a longer photoirradiation time should be further investigated.

# 4. Conclusions

This study investigated the biodegradation pattern，component evolution,elemental cycling,and microbial community succession of pristine and photobleached pyDOM produced at three pyrolysis temperatures (300,450,and $6 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ . The results showed that pyDOM of lower pyrolysis temperature was more biodegradable and photobleaching treatment increased the cumulative biomineralization ratio from $4 0 \% - 7 0 \%$ to $6 0 \% 8 0 \%$ ，As the pyrolysis temperature increased, the molecular diversity,molecular weight,and aromaticity of pyDOM increased,with pyDOM of higher pyrolysis temperature comprising higher proportions of tannin-like structures and condensed aromatic. The photobleaching treatment decreased the molecular diversity, molecular weight,and aromaticity of pyDOM,with major removal of lignin-like compounds.As the laboratory incubation proceeded,the molecular diversity was lowered, but the aromaticity was improved in both pristine and photobleached samples. Tannin-like structures,carbohydrates,and aliphatic/protein were preferentially metabolized, while the relative abundance of lipids, lignin-like structures,unsaturated hydrocarbons,and condensed aromatics increased.The photobioresistant components mainly comprised lignin-like and tannin-like structures.During the incubation,the photobleached samples released more $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ but less $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ ,as they may possess high denitrification rate due to high carbon reactivity.The bacterial richness and diversity in pyDOM were lower than that in the original inoculum.Additionally,distinct differences were noted for the bacterial composition of the original inoculum,pristine pyDOM samples,and photobleached samples at different incubation time. Compared to the original inoculum,proteobacteria was increased and remained the predominant phylum in all pyDOM solutions,while Actinobacteriota,Bdellovibrionota,and Firmicutes phyla were increased in some samples after 7 days of incubation and Actinobacteriota and Nitrospirota phyla were increased in several samples after 56 days of incubation. The strongest microbiological filtration effects were seen in the bacterial community of P-pyDOM600. Our study provides a new perspective on the biogeochemical cycling and turnover of pyDOM. Particularly,in a natural aquatic environment, pyDOM undergoes continuous sunlight illumination and microbial degradation.Further investigation should underline the biodegradation pattern of pyDOM along with photodegradation in a natural environment.
