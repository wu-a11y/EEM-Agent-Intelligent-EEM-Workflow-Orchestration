# ABSTRACT

The application of fluorescence spectroscopy to monitor natural organic matter (NOM) reduction as a function of biofiltration performance was investigated. This study was conducted at pilot-scale where a conventional media filter was compared to six biofilters employing varying enhancement strategies. Overall reductions of NOM were identified by measuring dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , as well as characterization of organic sub-fractions by liquid chromatography eorganic carbon detection (LC-OCD) and parallel factors analysis (PARAFAC) of fluorescence excitationemission matrices (FEEM). The biofilter using granular activated carbon media, with exhausted absorptive capacity, was found to provide the highest removal of all identified PARAFAC components. A microbial or processed humic-like component was found to be most amenable to biodegradation by biofilters and removal by conventional treatment. One refractory humic-like component, detectable only by FEEM-PARAFAC, was not well removed by biofiltration or conventional treatment. All biofilters removed protein-like material to a high degree relative to conventional treatment. The formation potential of two halogenated furanones, 3-chloro-4(dichloromethyl)-2(5H)-furanone (MX) and mucochloric acid (MCA), as well as overall treated water genotoxicity are also reported. Using the organic characterization results possible halogenated furanone and genotoxicity precursors are identified. Comparison of FEEM-PARAFAC and LC-OCD results revealed polysaccharides as potential MX/MCA precursors.

# 1. Introduction

Interest in biologically active media filtration (biofiltration) for the reduction of organic matter in drinking water has grown in recent years. Biofiltration represents an important water treatment option because of its low operational complexity coupled with the ability to reduce biodegradable organic matter (LeChevallier et al., 1992) and disinfection by-product (DBP) precursors, while achieving acceptable filtration performance with respect to particle removal (Emelko et al., 2006). Several biofilter enhancement strategies have been investigated, such as addition of nitrogen and phosphorous or coagulant, in an effort to augment performance (Lauderdale et al., 2012; Azzeh et al., 2015).

It has proven difficult to evaluate the impacts of biodegradation of natural organic matter (NOM) or benchmark the overall performance of biofilters. Organic matter in natural source waters is comprised of a wide range of compounds such as humic and fulvic acids, proteins, and carbohydrates, with varying molecular structures and chemical characteristics (Her et al., 2003). Traditional organic surrogates such as dissolved organic carbon (DOC) are not capable of quantifying these distinct sub-fractions, therefore provide a more broad representation of water quality. As such, methods capable of identifying the removal of specific organic subfractions would provide a more informative representation of biofiltration performance. For example, Peldszus et al. (2011) reported reduced protein-like material due to biofiltration, which in turn was thought to decrease ultrafiltration membrane fouling rates.

When combined with chlorine used for disinfection, NOM at drinking water treatment plants forms a wide range of DBPs. These by-products include commonly regulated compounds such as trihalomethanes (THMs) and haloacetic acids (HAAs), as well as less common and unregulated compounds of health concern such as the halogenated furanones: 3-chloro-4(dichloromethyl)-2(5H)- furanone (MX) and mucochloric acid (MCA) (Krasner et al., 2006). When formed in drinking water, these compounds can significantly contribute to a genotoxic response (Richardson et al., 2007) and as part of a review providing recommendations for the Fourth Drinking Water Contaminant Candidate List (CCL4), MX showed high risk relative to other DBPs (Roberson et al., 2015). Differing sub-fractions of organic matter are also known to have unique reactivity to disinfectants for formation of disinfection by-products (DBPs) (Barrett et al., 2000). As such, it is important to understand treatment performance specific to the removal of reactive organic sub-fractions in order to minimize DBP formation.

Fluorescence spectroscopy has been demonstrated to be a sensitive, accurate, and rapid NOM characterization technique (Peiris et al., 2010). The potential for implementation of online fluorescence monitoring for real time organic matter characterization offers great applicability to process control in water treatment. Several fluorescence excitation-emission matrix (FEEM) analysis techniques are reported in the literature, commonly tracking intensity changes of key wavelengths (Murphy et al., 2011). However, molecular structure has a noted effect on peak position which may have a degree of commonality among individual organic molecules causing convolution of organic fluorescence peaks (Persson and Wedborg, 2001). To account for this overlapping nature of organic classes, dimensionality reduction and deconvolution using multiway parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis has been successful (Stedmon et al., 2003; Murphy et al., 2011).

This study focused on applying fluorescence spectroscopy as a monitoring method for biofilter performance. A comparison between conventional water treatment (coagulation, flocculation, settling, and dual media conventional filtration) to biofiltration at pilot-scale for the removal of NOM, DBP precursors of halogenated furanones, and genotoxicity was performed. Some biofilters were subject to continuous chemical enhancement strategies including the addition of nutrients, hydrogen peroxide, or in-line coagulant. The use of fluorescence-PARAFAC analysis is an increasingly popular method for characterization of NOM. This study presents novel data illustrating enhanced biofiltration performance in terms of fluorescence components as well as linking the components to formation potentials of emerging DBPs of concern.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Source waters

Pilot-scale biofiltration studies were conducted with water from

the Otonabee River, Peterborough, Ontario. Influent water quality parameters over the study period included temperature: $0 { - } 2 8 \ ^ { \circ } C ,$ pH: 7.3e8.6, turbidity: $0 . 3 { - } 2 . 4 ~ \mathrm { N T U }$ , DOC $4 { \mathrm { - } } 6 ~ { \mathrm { m g / L } }$ .

# 2.2. Pilot plant configurations

The pilot system was comprised of six parallel biofiltration trains, as well as a conventional treatment configuration. Biofilters received raw water, while the conventional media filter was supplied with coagulated and settled water. To match full-scale operations, the conventional filter was operated with an empty bed contact time (EBCT) of $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ and an aluminum sulphate (General Chemical, Parsipanny, NJ) dose of ${ \sim } 4 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ . The biofilters had an EBCT of $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The conventional filter and five biofilters contained $5 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ of anthracite (effective size $\mathrm { d } _ { 1 0 } = 0 . 8 5 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , uniformity coefficient ${ \mathrm { U C } } = 1 . 8$ ) over $5 0 \mathrm { c m }$ of sand $\mathrm { . } \mathrm { d } _ { 1 0 } = 0 . 5 \ : \mathrm { m m }$ , ${ \mathrm { U C } } = 1 . 8$ ). One biofilter was comprised of Filtrasorb 300 GAC (Calgon Carbon, Moon Township, PA) previously exhausted in adsorptive capacity following over 11 years of operation, over $5 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ of sand.

Several enhancement strategies were applied to investigate whether biofilter performance could be optimized, including the addition of phosphorous and nitrogen, hydrogen peroxide, and low coagulant dosing immediately prior to filtration. Based on previous work by Azzeh et al. (2015), phosphorus (as phosphoric acid), and nitrogen (as ammonium chloride) were each added at a concentration of $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ to examine the biological impact on DBP precursor biodegradation, even though these nutrients were not limited. Hydrogen peroxide was added at $0 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ to observe improvements on reduction of head loss (Lauderdale et al., 2012) and whether this treatment would impact DBP precursor removal. Inline aluminum sulphate was added at $0 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ as $A | ^ { 3 + }$ to maximize organic removal (Azzeh et al., 2015), in particular the biopolymer sub-fraction.

All biofilters were backwashed with their own unchlorinated effluent three times per week, whereas the conventional filter was backwashed using chlorinated water from the full-scale plant to limit biological growth in the filter.

# 2.3. General organic measures and biofilter activity

Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) was measured using a persulfate wet oxidation method as described in Standard Method 5310 D (APHA, 2012) with O-I Corporation Model 1010 TOC Analyzer (College Station, Texas, USA). Ultraviolet absorbance (UVA) at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ was determined using a CE 3055 model spectrophotometer (Cecil Instruments, Cambridge, England) with a quartz cuvette following Standard Method 5910 B (APHA, 2012).

Biological activity on the filter media was characterized by adenosine triphosphate (ATP) from a media sample taken from the top $5 \mathrm { \ c m }$ of filter. ATP concentrations were assayed with a LuminUltra Deposit Surface Analysis kit (DSA-100C, Fredricton, NB) following the manufacturer's instructions.

# 2.4. Fluorescence

Fluorescence spectra of the treated water samples were collected with an Agilent Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer. Optimal instrument settings were determined based on previous studies (Peiris et al., 2009, 2010) and in-house testing. Excitation and emission wavelength ranges were $2 3 0 { - } 3 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ( $1 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ increments) and $2 5 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ increments), respectively. All fluorescence spectra were collected post $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ membrane filtration to mirror the approach taken for LC-OCD and DOC analysis. A spectrum of Milli- $\mathbf { \nabla } \cdot \mathbf { Q }$ water was collected on each sampling day as a reference for each set of samples and subtracted from the sample spectra to account for the solvent background. Raman corrections were applied using an excitation wavelength of $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and a bandwidth of $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ to report fluorescence intensities in Raman Units (RU) (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009). Absorbance spectra between 230 and $6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ increments), recorded using an Agilent 8453 UVeVis spectrophotometer (Mississauga, Canada), were used to correct for possible inner filter effects (Kothawala et al., 2013). This approach utilizes a measured absorbance at each pair of excitation $\left( \mathsf { A } _ { \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } } \right)$ and emission $\left( \mathsf { A } _ { \gtrsim _ { \mathrm { m } } } \right)$ wavelengths. The fluorescence intensity observed $( \mathsf { F } ^ { 0 } )$ can then be corrected $( \mathsf { F } ^ { \mathsf { c } } )$ using eq. (1). The method was implemented using inhouse scripts written in the R language.

$$
F _ { \lambda _ { e x } \lambda _ { e m } } ^ { c } = F _ { \lambda _ { e x } \lambda _ { e m } } ^ { o } \times 1 0 ^ { 0 . 5 \times \left( A _ { \lambda _ { e x } } + A _ { \lambda _ { e m } } \right) }
$$

Analysis of the spectra was carried out using the drEEM toolbox for MATLAB (Murphy et al., 2013) for implementing parallel factors analysis (PARAFAC). The methodology presented by Murphy et al. (2013) was followed to determine an adequate PARAFAC model. The pre-processed data (blank subtraction, Raman normalization, and IFE corrections) was imported into MATLAB. Rayleigh and Raman scatter regions were then removed since they do not conform to the linear assumptions required for PARAFAC modelling and the regions were smoothed using the drEEM interpolation functions. The fluorescence signals were then normalized to reduce the leverage of extreme values for the purposes of developing a PARAFAC model and the normalization processes was reversed after validation. To establish a robust PARAFAC model, several samples were identified as outliers through observation of sample leverages on the model. The validity of the PARAFAC model was established through several ways. Spectral loadings of the components were observed to conform to general guidelines of how organic fluorophores signals appear (e.g. only one emission peak, no abrupt changes in loadings with wavelength). Residuals calculated as the difference between the modelled and measured spectra were generally random with few minor peaks. Split-half validation was also carried out based on a randomized split of the dataset into 6 equal parts to form 3 unique comparisons of dataset halves. For each unique half (combination of two splits) an independent PARAFAC model was developed and the components were found to be identical for all combinations as well as to the overall model. Finally comparison of the resulting PARAFAC components with the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al., 2014b) also provides some indication of model validity. After reversal of the fluorescence normalization, results from the model were reported as $\operatorname { F } _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ values in RU.

# 2.5. Liquid chromatography e organic carbon detection

LC-OCD analyses were conducted using the method described by Huber et al. (2011). LC-OCD allows for simultaneous quantification of several organic matter fractions. The method fractionated DOC into five molecular weight ranges labelled bio-polymers, humics, building blocks, low molecular weight (LMW) acids, and LMW neutrals. Further description of these classes can be found in Huber et al. (2011). Samples were filtered through a $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ polyethersulfone filter to remove any particulates prior to analyses. A weak cation exchange column $2 5 0 \ \mathrm { m m } \times 2 0 \ \mathrm { m m }$ , Toso, Japan) provided chromatographic separation of the NOM fractions prior to organic carbon quantification. The mobile Phase used was a phosphate buffer (MLE, Dresden, Germany) exposed to UV at a flow rate of $1 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . Calibration was performed on-site using potassium hydrogen phthalate. Data acquisition and processing was conducted using a customized software program (ChromCALC, DOCLABOR, Karlsruhe, Germany).

# 2.6. Genotoxicity

The SOS Chromotest™ assay was used to quantify genotoxicity by following the manufacturer's instructions (EBPI, Environmental Bio-Detection Products Inc., Mississauga, ON). The test colorimetrically determines the a response genetic material being damaged in genetically modified Escherichia coli (E. coli) cells where a $\beta .$ - galactosidase gene is fused to the bacterial SOS operon. The ratio of $\beta$ -galactosidase to general protein synthesis (quantified by alkaline phosphatase) is referred to as the induction factor (IF). Postchlorination and quenched water samples (2 L) were acidified to $\mathsf { p H } 2$ and by-products were extracted using an Oasis HLB cartridge (12 cc, 500 mg, Waters Corporation). After elution with acetone and then evaporated to dryness using nitrogen, $3 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of dimethyl sulfoxide was used for reconstitution prior to use in the assay. Serial dilutions of 66 equivalent mL of each extracted sample were used in a well plate alongside a positive control of 4-nitroquinoline 1-oxide (4-NQO). $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of bacterial suspension were then added to each well, which was then incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . An additional hour of incubation at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ was allowed after addition of $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of chromogen $\beta$ -galactosidase and alkaline phosphatase). Activity of $\beta$ -galactosidase was read as the optical density at $6 0 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } } \left( \mathrm { { O D } } _ { 6 0 5 } \right)$ and activity of alkaline phosphatase at $4 2 0 \ \mathrm { n m } \left( \mathrm { O D } _ { 4 2 0 } \right)$ . Genotoxic responses were corrected to the relative toxic equivalent concentration (TEQ) 4-NQO by comparing slopes of IF to relative enrichment factors (serial dilutions of each extracted sample). The enrichment factor is the concentration achieved during extraction divided by the dilutions applied in the assay. Additional details on the genotoxicity test used and TEQ measure is given by Zheng et al. (2015).

# 2.7. 3-Chloro-4(dichloromethyl)-2(5H)-furanone (MX) and mucochloric acid (MCA)

Prior to DBP analyses, samples were chlorinated to achieve a residual of $1 . 0 \pm 0 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ after $2 4 \pm 2$ h at $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ without pH correction (APHA, 2012). Free chlorine residuals were measured as per Standard Method 4500-Cl G (APHA, 2012) and quenched with Lascorbic acid. Fluorescence spectra were recorded prior to DBP formation tests and addition of $\mathbf { L }$ -ascorbic acid. MX/MCA samples were extracted using tandem solid phase extraction cartridges with a trifunctional C18 (tC18) cartridge (Sep Pak 6 cc, 1 g, Waters Corporation, Mississauga, ON) for humics removal in-line with an Oasis HLB cartridge (12 cc, 500 mg, Waters Corporation). Analyses were conducted using a Varian 3800 GC, 4000 MS with a CombiPAL autosampler (Mississauga, ON) equipped with a J&W Scientific DB1701 column $( 3 0 ~ \mathrm { m } , 0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ I.D., $0 . 2 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ film thickness). Calibration and quality control standards were produced using a standard of MX from Toronto Research Chemicals Inc. (North York, ON) and an MCA standard was obtained from Signma-Aldrich Inc. (Oakville, ON). A six point calibration was completed over $0 { - } 2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n g / L }$ of both MX and MCA $( \mathsf { R } ^ { 2 } > 0 . 9 8 )$ along with spike and recovery samples (1 every 6) of MX and MCA at $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n g / L } }$ (recovery $8 0 - 1 2 0 \% )$ . The GC/MS method was adapted from one described by Zweiner and Kronberg (2001) and by Zheng et al. (2015).

# 3. Results and discussion

The focus of this study was to compare the performance of conventional and biological filters with varying nutrient or chemical enhancements within the context of organic matter and DBP precursor removal. All biofilters received untreated source water (Otonabee River) such that a comparison could be made to a parallel conventional treatment train (coagulation/flocculation/sedimentation/filtration). Performance was evaluated in terms of dissolved organic carbon (DOC), ultraviolet absorption (UVA), liquid chromatography with organic carbon detection (LC-OCD), genotoxicity potential and PARAFAC analysis fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (FEEM).

# 3.1. Fluorescence analysis

For fluorescence analysis, PARAFAC was applied to a data set of 45 samples encompassing 5 sampling dates. A five component model was established based on several diagnostic criteria and split validation. The loading values for the 5 components are shown as Fig. 1. Derived fluorescing components were cross-referenced with the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al., 2014b), which provided context for the similarities between these organic fluorophores to those reported globally from other studies. A high similarity score $\mathrm { ( { > } 0 . 9 7 ) }$ was found for each of the 5 derived component, as shown in Table 1, which lists identities and references found through OpenFluor.

Correlations between components and to other organic measures were calculated (Table 2) in the treated water samples. Raw water sample size $\mathrm { \Delta } \cdot \mathrm { \Delta } \mathrm { n } = 5 \mathrm { \Delta } ,$ ) was not thought to be high enough to allow for adequate confidence in the results. As such, the correlations represent similarities in changes due to treatment rather than occurrence in the source water. Highest correlations were observed among the three terrestrial humic-like components, C1, C2 and C4 $( \mathrm { R } ~ = ~ 0 . 7 7 - 0 . 9 8 )$ . Weaker relationships between C3, processed humic-like, and the terrestrial humic-like components were also noted $( \mathsf { R } = - 0 . 0 2 - 0 . 5 2 )$ ). Although the strength is poor, the highest correlation with tryptophan (C5) was found with C3 $\mathrm { R } = 0 . 5 1$ ).

Fluorescent components did not relate well with overall DOC demonstrating the selectivity of the FEEM-PARAFAC method. When compared to another light absorbance based measures (UVA), relationships with humic-like components were stronger (R 0.67e0.87), however UVA at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ was unable to readily capture changes in protein-like material (C5) or the humic-like C4 $( \mathsf { R } = 0 . 2 8 { - } 0 . 2 9 )$ .

With respect to LC-OCD fractions, some associations were apparent. As would be expected, the humic fraction identified by LC-OCD was most correlated with humic-like C1, C2, and C3 $\left( \mathrm { R } = 0 . 8 0 { - } 0 . 9 1 \right)$ ). A moderately low correlation between tryptophan (C5) and LC-OCD biopolymers (R 0.53) was not expected since both are thought to provide representation of protein-like material. A lack of relationship between C4 and LC-OCD fractions, as well as

DOC and UVA, suggests it was unable to be captured by the other organic measures. This was unexpected given the strength of the correlation with humic-like fluorophores reported in other studies (Table 1) and to the other terrestrial humic-like components.

# 3.2. Biofilter performance

To better illustrate the effect of various biofilter treatments, post-filter $\operatorname { F } _ { \operatorname* { m a x } }$ values were subtracted from raw water on the given sampling day and then divided by the raw water $\operatorname { F } _ { \operatorname* { m a x } }$ to report $\%$ removal. Relative consistencies of the raw water $\operatorname { F } _ { \operatorname* { m a x } }$ values for the study period are shown in Fig. 2. Percent removals for individual PARAFAC components are reported in Fig. 3.

Biofilters with enhancements performed similarly for identified fluorescence components with a few notable differences (Fig. 3). The GAC biofilter (exhausted in terms of absorptive capacity) outperformed all enhancement strategies, except for the processed humic-like C3. A pronounced spike in GAC biofilter performance was measured on June 23rd. Fluorescence results were mirrored by DOC where GAC biofilter removal was $2 7 \%$ greater than average over the other sampling days and this coincided with a doubling of the ATP concentrations for the GAC biofilter, implicating a temporary increase of bioactivity (data not shown). Removal of the likely processed or degraded humic-like component (C3) did not increase on June 23rd showing a unique response and was unaffected by increased bioactivity. No corresponding increase in raw water component $\mathtt { F } _ { \mathtt { m a x } }$ was noted during this time period (Fig. 2). ATP concentrations on the media returned to average following the spike, however removals by the GAC biofilter remained relatively elevated. It appears that the spike in bioactivity may have led to a community which more efficient removed these components. Increased biofilter performance using GAC media has been reported by other authors, attributing high surface area and the porous nature allowing greater attachment and growth of microorganisms (Klymenko et al., 2010; Zhu et al., 2010).

For terrestrial humic-like components C1 and C2, minimal removal (average 3.7 and $1 . 9 \%$ , respectively) was seen for all biofilters, except GAC. More substantial reductions were observed for the degraded or processed humic-like C3 by biofiltration (average $1 4 . 8 \% )$ implying degraded humic matter is more amenable to removal via biofiltration. Removals were substantially higher for conventional treatment $3 7 \%$ , $54 \%$ , $89 \%$ average removal of C1, C2, C3). The component most readily removed by biofiltration, C3, was also most easily removed by conventional treatment. Consistently high removals of tryptophan (C5) (average $2 7 . 8 \%$ ) via biofiltration was observed.

# 3.3. Reduction of disinfection by-product precursors

Disinfection by-product formation potentials were monitored in the raw and settled water, as well as across the conventional filter (non-biological), GAC biofilter, and alum enhanced biofilter effluents. The biofilters showed low overall organic removal levels $( 6 { - } 1 1 \% \pm 3 { - } 1 0 \%$ DOC averages for individual biofilters over all sampling days; see supplementary information for DOC removal). In contrast, reduction of total THM and total HAA formation $( 5 - 1 7 \% ~ \pm ~ 3 - 1 1 \%$ average reduction over all sampling days; $5 0 { - } 2 7 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ range), MX formation $( 2 0 - 2 7 \%$ average reduction; $2 . 5 { - } 2 4 . 5 ~ \mathrm { n g / L }$ range), and genotoxicity $( 1 1 - 2 8 \% \pm 6 - 1 4 \%$ average reduction over all sampling days; $0 . 1 { - } 0 . 9$ TEQ-4NQO pmol range) were greater than the bulk organic removals. Only MCA precursors were not biodegraded, however removal $( 5 9 \% )$ was observed with conventional treatment (coagulation/flocculation/sedimentation/ dual media filtration).

A possible online performance measure, such as fluorescence, is preferable over routine DBP formation analyses for real time monitoring and control. Previous studies have shown strong correlations between fluorescence components and THM and/or DBP formation potential (Hua et al., 2010; Pifer and Fairey, 2012). In this study, the focus was to better understand precursor characteristics for formation of MX, MCA, and genotoxicity in the context of biofiltration performance. To that end, linear correlations between organic matter measures and the formation of MX, MCA, and genotoxicity were calculated (Table 3). MX was most explained through moderate to high correlations with terrestrial and processed humic-like components $\mathsf C 1 - \mathsf C 3$ $( \mathsf { R } ^ { 2 } 0 . 6 7 - 0 . 6 9 )$ , as well as LC-OCD biopolymers and humics $( \mathsf { R } ^ { 2 } \ 0 . 7 8$ and 0.54, respectively). C5 was considered a significant predictor of MX based on a linear model at $9 5 \%$ confidence, despite low correlational strength. Genotoxicity was not well correlated with any organic measure $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } \le 0 . { \dot { 4 } } 0 )$ . However, statistical significance $\left( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ ) was found between the C4 PARAFAC component and genotoxicity as well as most LC-OCD molecular weight fractions and DOC/UVA. Given that C4 was the component least impacted by biofiltration and conventional treatment, additional studies are required to understand the significance of this relationship.

General organic measures of DOC and UVA showed significant relationships with formation of MX, MCA, and genotoxicity. Comparability between UVA and fluorescence results would be expected, since all PARAFAC components show excitation loadings (or absorbance) at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ . The lack of significant relationships with select PARAFAC components demonstrates the increased specificity of fluorescence measures compared to UVA and DOC. Therefore fluorescence monitoring may better inform treatment optimization and result in improved universal models.

MX and MCA formations were most correlated with the LC-OCD biopolymer fraction. The biopolymer fraction is believed to be the sum of protein and polysaccharide concentrations (Huber et al., 2011). Due to the relatively low corresponding correlations between protein-like material (C5) and MX/MCA, polysaccharides are implicated as possible halogenated furanone precursors. LC-OCD results conform to previous work by Zheng et al. (2015), where the biopolymer fraction was also highly correlated with MX and MCA formation. In contrast, others have indicated that MX precursors are generally humic-like in nature (Xu et al., 1997) or were shown to form after oxidation of humic and fulvic acids (Huixian et al., 2000); however, the latter authors also reported tryptophan and tyrosine as possible MX precursors. Similarly, in our analysis a significant linear relationship $\left( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ between tyrosine fluorescence (C5) and MX is seen. Additional studies using polysaccharides are required to better understand MX/MCA formation potentials and precursor characteristics.

# 4. Conclusions

The objective of this study was to assess the applicability of fluorescence spectroscopy to monitor biofilter performance. Nutrient or chemical enhancement strategies had little impact on biofilter performance related to NOM and DBP precursor reduction, and this was reflected in the FEEM analysis. Use of GAC media did however increase performance for the majority of fluorescence components identified by PARAFAC.

A microbial humic-like or processed humic-like component, identified as C3 from FEEM-PARAFAC analysis, was most amenable to removal by biofiltration and conventional treatment. A terrestrial humic-like component, C4, showed weak relationships with all of the LC-OCD fractions, as well as DOC and UVA at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . This component, uniquely identified by FEEM-PARAFAC, was not removed by biofiltration (except GAC). A possible relationship to chlorinated LMW acids, as identified by LC-OCD, is based on the highest relative individual correlations to water genotoxicity $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 }$ 0.39e0.40).

Conventional treatment showed significantly more humic-like removal potential when compared to direct biofiltration under both enhanced and passive operating conditions. However, direct biofiltration resulted in relatively high removal levels of tryptophan (C5) or protein-like reduction relative to conventional treatment $2 7 . 8 \%$ vs. $5 0 \%$ average reduction). Based on the simplicity of passive biofiltration of raw water compared to conventional treatment, this method may be attractive for targeted protein-like removal; for example, in the removal of protein-like ultrafiltration membrane foulants (Peiris et al., 2010).

Preferential removal of DBP precursors in relation to overall bulk NOM surrogate parameters was observed from biofiltration. The majority of humic-like fluorescing compounds and LC-OCD biopolymers correlated with MX formation $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 6 7 – 0 . 7 8 )$ ). However, strong relationships between MX and MCA formation with LCOCD biopolymers (proteins and polysaccharides) were not mirrored by C5 (tryptophan/protein-like fluorescence). Therefore, it is proposed that polysaccharides may play a role as a precursor for these halogenated furanones during chlorination.
