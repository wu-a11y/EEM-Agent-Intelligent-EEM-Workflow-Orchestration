Despite that fluorescence spectroscopy coupled with Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC) has been widely used in the investigation of Fluorescent Dissolved Organic Matter (FDOM) in aquatic systems, the proper performance of PARAFAC analysis on datasets originating from various sources is not to be taken for granted. In this study, we examine the impact of the co-analysis of datasets from various natural water systems located in the same geographical region in the Eastern Mediterranean Sea. For this purpose three datasets were formed representative of open sea waters (SW), rivers and streams (RV) and lagoons (LG). The Excitation Emission Matrices (EEMs) derived from fluorescence analysis were subjected to individual PARAFAC analysis per dataset as well as combined analyses i.e.: SWRV, SWLG, RVLG, ALL (SW-RV-LG). We evaluated the reliability of the components that were validated in the combined models through the investigation of model’s residuals and components correlation. We also assessed the similarity of the common identified components among models in regards of: (a) spectral position, by calculating the Tucker congruence coefficient (TCC) of the excitation and emission loadings of the PARAFAC components, and (b) fluorescence intensity, through regression analysis of Fmax, among models. Our analysis showed that for natural waters of various sources within the same geographical region, combined PARAFAC modeling can have both negative as well as positive impact. In the case of the combined SWLG and RVLG models, the PARAFAC analysis was able to resolve the fulvic component that was initially observed only in the LG dataset and thus a new component for SW and RV datasets was resolved. The fulvic-like component was actually identified for the first time in the open sea using the combined datasets. Moreover in the combined SWRV analysis tyrosine-like component was resolved which was found initially only in the RV dataset. Contrary, tyrosine-like component was lost in the combined RVLG dataset. We also show that the resolution of extra components in a combined analysis is not always a good fit for the dataset and the model should be assessed in terms of residuals prior acceptance. Finally, our study proposes that the similarity of the common components between combined and individual models is largely dependent on the similarity between the components of the individual models and that the estimation of the Fmax of a component is probably less affected by data diversity compared to the estimation of its spectral position. $^ { © }$ 2021 Elsevier B.V. # 1. Introduction

Spectroscopic techniques such as UV–visible fluorescence, NMR, FTIR, FT-ICR-MS, have been incorporated during the last decades in the research of dissolved organic matter (DOM) [1]. Despite the fact that fluorescence spectroscopy provides fewer structural insights regarding DOM, compared to the other techniques, the simplicity and rapidity of the method as well as the lack of necessity for DOM isolation have established fluorescence spectroscopy a widely used tool in DOM investigation [2,3]. Three dimensional fluorescence spectroscopy examines fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM), which is the fraction of DOM that has the ability to fluoresce upon absorption of radiation in the ultra-violet and visible region. DOM holds a significant role in the carbon cycle while its presence in natural aquatic ecosystems is attributed to primary production and terrestrial inputs. Biotic and abiotic transformation mechanisms act on DOM resulting in a complex mixture of substances which is still largely unidentified and presents a range of physicochemical characteristics [4,5]. It is well known that DOM shows significant quantitative and qualitative differences among the various aquatic ecosystems [6]. However, more recently the presence of a universal DOM background regardless of origin and history of the organic matter was proposed which is believed to be formed by a cascade of degradation processes or common synthetic pathways [7]. The optical signal in the 3-D excitation emission matrices (EEMs), can provide information regarding the chemical characteristics, sources and transformation processes of the fluorescent fractions of the DOM. Due to the complex character of DOM, overlapping among the different fluorescent components is a very common and challenging feature in the fluorescence spectra. Multivariate modelling techniques such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA), and Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC), have been introduced in the analysis of the EEMs in order to tackle components overlapping and retrieve additional information. PARAFAC is considered to be more appropriate for EEMs analysis as it provides both a quantitative and a qualitative model of the data [8].

The reliability though of PARAFAC analysis on datasets that consist of samples from various sources e.g. natural waters, soil, wastewater, water treatment plants has been questioned [9–12]. Murphy et al. [9], reported six very similar individual PARAFAC models for samples from six different water recycling treatment plants while a unique PARAFAC model on a combined dataset demonstrated shifts in the positions of some components and modification of the spectra. These observations were interpreted as limitation of the sensitivity of fluorescence for distinguishing individual organic matter sources in water samples from different sources. Yu et al. [10] assessing the effect of sample set diversity on the accuracy and sensitivity of PARAFAC model using samples from rivers, effluents of wastewater treatment plants and algae excretion reported that components from unrelated sources can be discriminated while the similar components from different sources were found to be treated as a single component. However, peak shift that occurred between similar components from different sources, was suggested to undermine the sensitivity and representativeness of the model. Murphy et al., [11] in an attempt to reduce complexity and eliminate environmental gradients other than photodegradation, exposed DOM extracts to simulated solar radiation under controlled pH and analyzed each extract independently in order to create multiway datasets from individual DOM samples. The DOM samples covered a large spectrum of natural waters originating from rivers, lakes and ocean environments. The conclusion of this study was that PARAFAC components in DOM appear to be highly conserved across diverse samples and some of these components respond to environmental stresses in predictable ways. They also suggest that in attempts to represent diverse groups of samples with a single PARAFAC model the systematic differences that appear are very likely to be due to the fact that the model does not represent all samples equally well rather than to differences in DOM source. Finally, a recent study of Garcia et al, [12] showed that the co-analysis of samples from different streams at different sampling times produced the same results compared to the individual analyses performed separately on each sampling dataset. The same was observed for lake samples. These authors proposed that when different datasets present low internal variability due to similar characteristics of the ecosystems and spatial proximity, a combined PARAFAC analysis can produce the same results as discrete analyses.

Since PARAFAC analysis of EEMs derived from samples with a relatively high diversity of sources appears to be questionable, the purpose of the present work is to assess PARAFAC modeling on a narrow range of samples’ origin from natural water sources only, such us open sea, rivers and streams, lagoons, in geographical proximity, i.e. a Mediterranean regime. Furthermore, we aim to investigate the impact of the presence of few EEMs from samples with distinct fluorescent characteristics, that initially could be considered as outliers, on the performance of combined PARAFAC analysis.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Sampling

Seawater dataset (SW): Seawater samples $ { { \mathrm { ( n = 7 3 } } }$ ), were collected from the surface to $3 9 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ depth in the region south of Crete Island, Eastern Mediterranean Sea. This dataset consists of 20 surface water samples $( 1 0 - 1 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ , 25 intermediate water samples ( $1 2 0 { - } 1 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { m } )$ and 28 deep water samples $1 2 0 0 { - } 3 9 0 0 \ \mathrm { m } ,$ ). Sampling was conducted with the $\mathsf { R } / \mathsf { V }$ Aegaeo in April 2016.

Riverine dataset (RV): Riverine samples $\mathbf { \chi } _ { \mathrm { { n } } } = 6 9 \mathbf { \chi } _ { \cdot }$ ), were all surface samples and were collected from small rivers and streams at the southwestern and the central parts of the Greek Peninsula, during 2016–2019.

Lagoon dataset (LG): Lagoon samples $\left( \mathrm { n } = 8 \right)$ , were also surface samples collected from one lagoon at the southwestern part of the Greek Peninsula, in December 2018 and March 2019. The maximum depth of the lagoon is approximately $1 \textrm { m }$ and covers an area of approximately $6 ~ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ .

More details regarding the sampling areas are given in the Appendix A (Figure A1 and Table A1). All samples were filtered through $0 . 2 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ polycarbonate filter upon sampling. The filters were flushed with approximately $2 0 0 \ \mathrm { m l }$ MilliQ water prior to the filtration to avoid any contamination. The filtered samples were collected into acid (HCl $1 0 \%$ , 12 h) cleaned amber glass bottles and kept in the dark at $+ 4 ^ { \circ } C$ until analysis.

# 2.2. Fluorescence analysis and PARAFAC modelling

DOM fluorescence was measured using a Horiba Aqualog-UV800 spectrofluorometer equipped with a 150 W ozone-free xenon arc-lamp and a $1 \mathrm { \ c m }$ path length quartz cuvette. Excitation Emission Matrices (EEMs) were obtained over a range of wavelengths between 240 and $4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ in $5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ increments for excitation and $2 5 0 { - } 8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ in ${ \sim } 0 . 6 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ increments for emission. The integration time of the measurements was adjusted according to the fluorescence intensity of the different samples. A blank EEM (MilliQ water) was measured daily and subtracted from each sample in order to correct for Raman and Rayleigh scattering. Absorbance of each sample was recorded prior EEM accumulation using the Horiba Aqualog spectrofluorometer and all EEMs were corrected for inner-filter effect following the Aqualog Operational Manual. A Raman scatter emission peak was also obtained using MilliQ water after each sample analysis and it was used for the normalization of fluorescence intensity to Raman Units (R.U.) [13].

The EEMs were further analysed using Parallel Factor Analysis, PARAFAC, as described by Murphy et al., [14]. The analysis was performed in MATLAB R2015a using the ‘‘drEEM toolbox” for MATLAB [14]. Firstly, since no significant CDOM fluorescence signal is expected for emission wavelengths over $5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , the emission region between 550 and $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ was discarded before proceeding with the analysis. Excitation wavelengths lower than $2 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $2 4 0 { - } 2 4 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ) were also removed from all the datasets as significant noise was observed in some EEMs. Moreover, since subtraction of Milli-Q spectra did not remove completely the Rayleigh and Raman scatter peaks in the EEMs, both first and second order Rayleigh and Raman scatter bands were interpolated using the ‘‘smootheem” function of the drEEM package. The settings of the ‘‘smootheem” function were adjusted during the analysis of the different datasets according to the intensity of the signal and the extent of the remaining scatter. All EEMs were then normalized to their total signal to tackle the large concentration gradients that are expected in the combined datasets. The model was run five times with random initial values and model validation was performed through split half analysis using four splits that yielded six different combinations and it was repeated five times $( S _ { 4 } C _ { 6 } \mathrm { T } _ { 5 } )$ . The convergence criterion was $1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ . Along with split half analysis visual inspection of the residuals was performed to assure randomness. Samples that produced residuals that deviated from randomness were excluded as outliers. The spectral loadings were assessed as well according to Murphy et al., [14]. Finally OpenFluor database [15] was used to compare the components produced in our study with those reported in the literature and check their similarity (Tucker congruence coefficient ${ \tt > } 0 . 9 5$ for both excitation and emission loadings).

# 2.3. Statistical analysis

For the estimation of the average residuals of the samples involved in a dataset (Section 3.2), we extracted the numeric values from the residuals plot of each sample that are provided from the ‘‘eemview” function of the DrEEM package. Then in house MATLAB functions were used for the construction of 291 (number of emission wavelengths) $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { n } } \times 4 \boldsymbol { 1 }$ matrices (where n is the number of the samples in each dataset and 41 is the number of excitation wavelengths) using the extracted numeric values of the residuals plots. Next we calculated the average of each column of the 291 $( \mathsf { n } \times 4 1 )$ matrices resulting into 291 $1 ~ \times ~ 4 1$ vectors. In house MATLAB functions were also used for the 3D illustration of the 291 $1 \times 4 1$ vectors i.e. the average residuals. Tucker congruence coefficient (TCC) was used for the spectral comparison of the components among the different models (Section 3.3). TCC were estimated using R software and the ‘‘rrcor3way” package. Finally, linear regression was used for the examination of the variation in the estimated fluorescence intensities, Fmax, of similar components among PARAFAC models (Section 3.4). Linear regression was performed using IBM SPSS Statistics and all the correlation coefficients $( \mathbb { R } ^ { 2 } )$ that are reported are statistically significant $\mathrm { ~ P ~ } { < } 0 . 0 5 $ .

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Fluorescence results and PARAFAC models

Examples of EEMs from fluorescence analysis on the SW, RV and LG datasets are given in Fig. 1. The intensity is expressed in Raman Units (R.U.) and the Rayleigh and Raman scatter bands have been interpolated.

PARAFAC analysis was initially performed on the individual datasets SW $\langle \boldsymbol { \mathrm { n } } = 7 3 \cdot$ ), RV ${ \mathrm { ( n = 6 9 ) } }$ and LG $\left( \mathrm { n } = 8 \right)$ . The components derived from the individual PARAFAC analyses are illustrated in Fig. 2a and their spectral characteristics are given in Table 1. Then PARAFAC was run again on all possible combinations of the three individual datasets i.e. SWRV (seawater and riverine, $\mathrm { ~ n ~ } = \ 1 4 2 ^ { \cdot }$ ), SWLG (seawater and lagoon, $\mathbf { n } = 8 1$ ), RVLG (riverine and lagoon, $\mathrm { n } = 7 7 \mathrm { \cdot }$ ) and ALL (seawater, riverine and lagoon, $\mathrm { n } = 1 5 0 \mathrm { . }$ ). The components of the combined PARAFAC models are given in Fig. 2b and their Excitation/Emission maxima are given in Table 1. All the components that were found in the individual and combined models are given in Figs. 2a and 2b in the order of the components that were found in the SW dataset, for comparison reasons. The same is valid for the Excitation/Emission maxima of the components presented in Table 1. The results and components derived in each case are described in the following paragraphs while the excitation and emission loadings of all the components are given in Figure A2 in Appendix A.

It is apparent in Fig. 1 that a lot of noise is present in the seawater samples while the riverine and lagoon samples are much smoother. Noise is a negative feature that is encountered in the analysis of low signal samples such as open seawater samples. In the combined analysis of SWRV and SWLG it is expected that the noise of the SW samples will have negative impact on the analysis. This is apparent in Fig. 2b as the components of the combined analysis with the SW dataset, i.e. SWRV and SWLG present some noise while the components of the individual RV and LG analysis are smooth (Fig. 2a). However, since noise is a feature that cannot be avoided in the analysis of open seawater samples we proceeded with the investigation taking into account that the noise impacts negatively the combined analysis in which seawater samples participate.

All the components resolved in our models were cross checked with others previously reported in the literature using the OpenFluor database [15]. We found numerous $_ { ( > 1 0 ) }$ matches $\mathrm { T C C } > 0 . 9 5 ,$ for all the identified components of the individual and combined models with the exception of the fulvic component (LG1, SWLG4, RVLG4, ALL4) where only one match was found and peak B (RV4, SWRV4) where four matches were found. In Table 2 we present some of the components that were found identical $\mathrm { ( T C C > 9 5 ) }$ to the ALL model components as well as to component RV4 (peak B) since this component was the only component found in the individual models that was not resolved in the ALL model. For the fulvic component, ALL4, we also report in Table 2 similar components from previous studies that were not matched in the OpenFluor database.

Seawater (SW) PARAFAC model: PARAFAC performed on the seawater dataset revealed 3 components (Fig. 2a, Table 1). Component SW1 $( \mathrm { e x } / \mathrm { e m } 2 5 0 , 3 7 5 / 4 6 8 \mathrm { n m } )$ exhibits two excitation peaks and a single emission peak. The primary peak resembles Coble’s peak A while the secondary peak corresponds to peak C [2]. Peak A was attributed to high molecular weight substances of humic character while the longer emission wavelength of peak C indicates terrestrial humics of higher molecular weight and a more aromatic character compared to peak A [45]. Component SW2 $\mathrm { ( e x / e m ~ \ 2 5 0 ~ }$ $3 2 0 / 4 0 7 ~ \mathrm { n m } )$ as well, presents two excitation peaks representing peaks A and peak M [2,16]. Peak M is considered to be related to marine humic substances possibly of bacterial origin [13]. Component SW3 $( 2 7 5 / 3 2 4 \ : \mathrm { n m } )$ ) presents spectral characteristics that indicate proteinaceous material. The emission maxima of SW3 is closer to the emission maxima of tryptophan-like (peak T) component than to the tyrosine-like (peak B) [16,17] and thus component SW3 will be considered as representative of peak T.

Riverine (RV) PARAFAC model: Four components were identified through PARAFAC analysis in the riverine dataset (Fig. 2a, Table 1).

Finally component RV4 $( 2 7 0 / 3 0 4 \ : \mathrm { n m }$ ) presenting lower excitation and emission maxima resembles to tyrosin-like material (peak B).

This is more apparent in five of the eight samples (lagoon4-lagoon8, Figure A3). # 3.2. Evaluation of the components that were resolved only in the combined datasets (peak B-SWRV4 and fulvic-SWLG4, -RVLG4, -ALL4) and of their impact on the identification of the other components

The resolution of one or more, poorly estimated components in a model is believed to significantly affect the spectra and scores of the other components due to the interdependence of the simultaneously estimated components [9]. In our findings, concerns rise regarding the presence of peak B (component SWRV4) in the SWRV model as well as the fulvic component (components SWLG4, RVLG4, ALL4) in the SWLG, RVLG and ALL models since none of the two components was resolved in all of the individual models. In both cases, in order to confirm that the two components (peak B and fulvic) are indeed present in all the samples of the combined datasets and that are accurately estimated through the combined PARAFAC models we proceeded in the investigation of model’s residuals. Residuals of well fitted models should contain only random error and no defined peaks should be discerned [14]. Since the residuals of each sample that participate in the dataset to be modeled were examined carefully prior model validation and any samples with residuals that deviated from randomness were excluded from the analysis, we present here the average residuals of all the samples that were retained in each dataset. The average residuals were calculated and plotted as described in Section 2.3.

In the case of the combined SWRV model, because peak B was originally resolved in the RV individual model but not in the SW individual model, our investigation was focused only on the residuals of the SW sub-dataset in the combined SWRV dataset. Therefore, we have examined and compared the residuals, R4, of SW spectra of the 4 components SWRV model that includes peak B and the residuals, R3, of the SW spectra in the 3 components SWRV model which was also validated during PARAFAC analysis. In the 3 components model the components that were identified were similar to the SW1/RV2, SW2/RV1 and SW3/RV3 components while peak B was not resolved. The validated components of the 3 components model are presented in Appendix A, Figure A4. By investigating the modification of the residuals in the progression from the 3 components to the 4 components model we aim to clarify which model describes best the SW samples in the combined SWRV dataset. The average residuals, R3 and R4, of the SW spectra in the two models are presented in Fig. 3.

In the 3 components model residuals, R3 (Fig. 3a), some signal with shape that resembles a fluorescent peak is observed just above the diagonal of the first order Rayleigh and Raman scattering at excitation wavelengths between 275 and $3 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and emission wavelengths between 300 and $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , i.e. in the region where peaks T and B are observed. In the fluorescence EEMs small peaks are common along the diagonal and are due to incompletely removed scatter. These peaks can be ignored since they are not trilinear and should not be featured in the model [14]. However, the peak that appears in the 3 components model residuals is quite wide while in the 4 components model residuals, R4 (Fig. 3b), the corresponding peak appears to be significantly narrower despite the fact that nothing changed regarding scatter removal. In addition, in R3 troughs (a region of negative values) appear adjacent to the detected peak indicating that any identified peak in this region of the spectra might be modeled using two or more poorlyfitting components [14]. Contrary in R4 there are no troughs at the certain wavelengths. These observations indicate that the 4 components model and the resolution of peak B is actually a better fit for the seawater samples in the combined dataset. The difference between the residuals in 3 components and 4 components models, DR, was also calculated by subtracting the R4 spectra from the R3 spectra (R3- R4) and it is presented in Fig. 4.

Positive values in DR, are indicative of fluorescence that was removed from the residuals by the evolution to models with additional components while values near zero indicate negligible impact [46]. In accordance to our observation regarding the individual R3 and R4 (Fig. 3 a, b), the DR plot (Fig. 4) shows an area of positive values where peaks B and T are usually observed. This enhances the assumption made right above that there is fluorescence in SW samples which is not adequately described in the 3 components model. Thus the identification of peak B in SW samples in the 4 components model can be considered valid and do not seem to impact negatively the resolution of the rest three components since no significant deviation was observed in the residuals in R3 and R4 in the regions of the spectra that these three components are observed.

In the combined SWLG dataset the fulvic component was identified which was initially identified only in the LG individual dataset. Therefore, the residuals of the SW sub-dataset in the validated models of the combined SWLG dataset were assessed. Once again we have examined and compared the residuals of the 4 components model that has been described in Fig. 2b and Table 1, and of the 3 components model that was also validated. In the 3 components model the fulvic component was resolved, along with two other components that resemble components SW1-LG2 and SW3- LG4. The 3 components model is presented in Appendix A, Figure A5. The double peak A-M that was resolved in both the individual SW (component SW2) and LG (component LG3) datasets, was not resolved in the 3 components model of the combined SWLG analysis. The average residuals R3 and R4 of the SW spectra for the 3 components and the 4 components models respectively in the combined SWLG analysis are given in Fig. 5.

It is clear from Fig. 5a that the 3 components model is not a good fit for the spectra. First of all, a region with negative values is apparent in the area where the fulvic component was identified indicating that the fluorescence of this component was overestimated in the modeled SW spectra. Moreover, a peak with relatively high positive values can be identified at $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ wavelengths approximately 315/385 nm, designating significant remaining fluorescence signal in the residuals. Actually this signal is representative of the double peak A-M and the components SW2 and LG3 that were identified in the individual models but not in the 3 components model of the combined SWLG dataset. Moreover, a less intense but well defined peak is also observed in longer wavelengths, approximately $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m } 3 7 0 / 4 5 3 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , indicating additional remaining fluorescence signal. This signal corresponds to the identified component SWLG3 (double peak A-C) in the 3 components model indicating that despite the fact that this component was identified in the 3 components model, the fluorescence signal in this model is not estimated correctly and is actually underestimated. It seems that the resolution of the fulvic component in the 3 components model, causes the attribution of a lot of the fluorescence that is present in long excitation wavelengths and is probably a result of both humic components (C and M) as well as the fulvic component, mainly to the fulvic component in the modeled spectra. The residuals of the 4 components model, R4, on the other hand (Fig. 5b) are characterized by randomness with no specific peak formation. The negative values that were observed in R3 in the area of the fulvic peak are replaced with values very close to zero indicating a very good estimation of the fluorescence in the certain region. In addition, the well defined and intense peaks that were apparent in R3 are no longer evident. Overall, the residuals of the 4 components model denote an appropriate fit for the SW spectra in the combined SWLG analysis and a correct estimation of the fulvic component in the seawater samples. Moreover, the identification of the fulvic peak in the SW spectra and the 4 components model, does not seem to negatively impact the estimation of the rest identified components.

However careful examination of the model’s residuals and components correlation should follow model’s validation since this kind of combined analysis could lead to an overestimation of some components with concurrent underestimation of other components, as we observed in the case of the 3 components model in the SWLG and RVLG analysis. Therefore we can propose combined PARAFAC analysis of samples with apparent fulvic signal and natural water datasets, followed by model’s residuals evaluation as a way to uncover the fulvic component.

# 3.3. Spectral variation of components among PARAFAC models

In order to evaluate the spectral similarity of the components derived by the different models that were described in section 3.1. we used the Tucker congruence coefficient (TCC). The TCC was calculated for both Excitation and Emission loadings. TCC is used to assess the similarity of factor interpretations and it is evaluated as $\begin{array} { r } { \varphi ( \mathbf { x } , \mathbf { y } ) = \sum \mathbf { x _ { i } } . \mathbf { y _ { i } } / \sqrt { ( \sum \mathbf { x _ { i } ^ { 2 } } \Sigma \mathbf { y _ { i } ^ { 2 } } ) } } \end{array}$ where $\boldsymbol { \Phi } ( \mathbf { x } , \mathbf { y } )$ is the congruence coefficient and $\mathbf { X _ { i } }$ and $\mathrm { y _ { i } }$ are the excitation or emission loadings of the two PARAFAC components to be compared. A value higher than 0.95 states that the two factors compared can be considered equal while a value in the range $0 . 8 5 \substack { - 0 . 9 4 }$ means that the two factors compared display fair similarity [47]. All the Tucker coefficients are given in Table 4. Green color represents the pairs of components in which TCC values were higher than 0.95 for both the excitation and emission loadings and thus these components are considered spectrally indistinguishable. Yellow color denotes the pairs of components that showed fair similarity, TCC values between 0.85 and 0.94, in either of the excitation or emission loadings and strong similarity in the other. Finally red color stands for the pairs of components that showed no similarity, TCC values below 0.85, for at least one of the excitation or emission loadings.

Contrary, Yu et al., [10] reported low $\mathsf { R } ^ { 2 }$ values $( < 0 . 8 5 )$ for similar components from different sources in the comparison of Fmax between global and individual models. Based on this observation they suggested that the similar components involved in the global model impacted the sensitivity and accuracy of the model, a fact that is not apparent in our results. In our study, the high $\mathsf { R } ^ { 2 }$ values indicate that the co-analysis of datasets that presented similar components in individual models (e.g. SW and RV) and even the inclusion in the PARAFAC analysis of the lagoon samples that present significantly higher fulvic signal, do not impact the estimation of the fluorescence intensity of the rest of the components. The results of the regression analysis between the Fmax were actually in very good agreement with the results of the TCC analysis on the excitation and emission loadings since the only cases that presented $\mathsf { R } ^ { 2 }$ lower than 0.95 were actually the cases where the TCC analysis showed no significant similarity between either the excitation or emission loadings. The fact that in the cases were TCC showed fair similarity between the excitation or emission loadings, the correlation coefficient between the Fmax was higher than 0.95 implies that the combination of samples from natural sources of the same geographical region could have a more profound effect on the estimation of the spectral position (Ex, Em) of a component rather than on the estimation of its fluorescence intensity. Yu et al., [10] proposed that a poor estimation of $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ loading in a PARAFAC model does not necessary result in a poorly estimated Fmax. In our case contrary, all components with poor estimation of the Ex/Em loadings $( \mathrm { T C C } < 0 . 8 5 )$ presented lower correlation coefficients $\mathrm { T R } ^ { 2 } < 0 . 9$ i n most cases) compared to the components that showed at least fair similarity between excitation and emission loadings.

# 4. Conclusions

The findings of our study indicate that data diversity in a quite narrow range of sources, i.e. natural waters of the same geographical region, could have both negative and positive impacts on PARAFAC modeling that largely depend on the composition of the datasets. Yet, both these impacts are considered useful for the interpretations to be made on DOM nature. Depending on the aim of the investigation, the combined PARAFAC modeling may either provide information on the common/identical fluorophores found in the diverse aquatic sources or, help reveal the presence of fluorophores not resolved by PARAFAC modeling of EEMs from a single aquatic source. In the first case a combined PARAFAC model cannot always substitute the individual models but can be used supplementary to reveal the presence of common identical compounds in two aquatic environments. This is apparent in the combined analysis of riverine and lagoon samples as some information that was revealed during the individual riverine PARAFAC model i.e. the resolution of peak B, was lost in the combined one. In the second case, our study shows that the increase in data diversity within the narrow range of natural waters is possible to have positive effects on PARAFAC analysis. The combined analysis of seawater and riverine datasets allowed the identification of peak B in the seawater samples. Moreover, the addition of the lagoon samples in the individual seawater and riverine datasets but also in the seawater-riverine pair allowed the identification of the fulvic component for the first time in the open sea. However the resolution of extra components in combined analysis is not always an appropriate fit for the spectra, as in the case of the resolution of the fulvic component in the 3 components model for the combined SWLG and RVLG datasets. PARAFAC results should be checked carefully in terms of model’s residuals and components correlation prior acceptance. Based on these observations we propose that a supplement of few high fulvic signal EEMs from natural water samples into seawater and riverine datasets of connected regions, is possible to assist in the identification of the hardly resolved fulvic component. Our study also showed that when dealing with EEMs of diverse natural water samples from the same geographical region, the agreement of the spectral position between the components that derive from the individual and combined datasets is largely dependent on the agreement of the spectral position between the components of the individual datasets. Overall, data diversity seems to have a more profound effect on the estimation of the spectral position of the components rather than the estimation of the Fmax, but if significant deviation in the spectral characteristics of the components are observed then it is most likely that the Fmax between the components will also differ noticeably. In conclusion, our work suggests that PARAFAC analysis is not to be applied blind folded to combined datasets of EEMs and a thorough inspection of the datasets and their fit into the models is needed for choosing the most appropriate approach depending on the aim of the investigation.
