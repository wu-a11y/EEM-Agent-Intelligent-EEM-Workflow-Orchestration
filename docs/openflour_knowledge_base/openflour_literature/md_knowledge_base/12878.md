# 1．Introduction

Wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) play a crucial role in protecting public health and the water environment by removing pollutants from wastewater before it is discharged into the environment. However, the performance of WWTPs can be significantly affected if the concentration of biodegradable organic matter is too low. Under this scenario, the limited availability of organic matter can negatively afect the microbial population and its ability to effectively degrade pollutants, consequentlyleadingto decreased treatment efficiencyand effectiveness.For example,there are more than 40oo WWTPs across China with a combined treatment capacity of approximately O.2 billion $\mathrm { m ^ { 3 } / d }$ ，However,for more than 1000,or $2 5 \%$ of these WWTPs,the chemical oxygen demand (COD) of the influent is less than $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ which is only $4 3 \%$ of the design concentration level of $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ (Xu et al.,2O19).The low influent concentration of these WWTPs is due to issues of the urban sewer networks such as pipe defects and associated groundwater infiltration and inflow from other sources.Due to the sheer size of the underground sewer infrastructure,efficient and low-cost techniques for identifying the causes of low organic concentration in sewage are highly valued in finding a solution for the improvement of sewage collection and wastewater treatment efficiency.

Currently,sewer performance assessment is predominantly conducted using visual inspection methods,and the most commonly used method is closed-circuit television (CCTV) examination (Ellis and Butler, 2015; Liu and Kleiner, 2013; Tscheikner-Gratl etal., 2020; Nguyen et al., 2021).However, CCTV examination is expensive and disruptive (EPA, 2009) because of the need for sequential sewer blocking,draining,and sediment dredging before each CCTV inspection.An alternative approach based on the in-sewer measurement of chemical markers (e.g., total nitrogen,total phosphorus,artificial sweetener,water isotope tracers,and pharmaceutical products) has been used (Kracht et al., 2007, 2008; Shelton et al., 2011; Xu et al., 2016; Yin et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2020; Kobayashi et al., 2021; Hachad etal., 2022; Heiderscheidt etal.，2O22)．However，the chemical or biological marker-based approach is still not cost-optimal because numerous measurements are required for large-scale catchment assessment.

Compared with chemically and biologically based methods described above,fluorescence spectroscopy has the advantages of high sensitivity，simple measurement,fast analysis,low samplesize requirement,and free of secondary pollution (Henderson et al., 2009; McDonald et al., 2004; Murphy et al., 2013; Sgroi et al., 2017; Su et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2O23). Currently spectroscopic surrogates have been proposed to monitor a wide range of water quality parameters including COD, five-day biochemical oxygen demand $\mathbf { ( B O D _ { 5 } ) }$ ，dissolved organic carbon (DOC),pathogens,disinfection by-products and trace organic contaminants (Hudson et al., 2008; Sgroi et al., 2018; Cohen et al., 2014; Li et al., 2017;Gunter et al.,2023). Furthermore, it provides rich information about the dissolved organic matter (DOM) in engineered water systems that could be used to fingerprint the sources of the DOM. For example,fluorescence excitation-emission matrix (EEM) can reveal the water discharge into rivers from various sources,including WWTP effluent, industrial wastewater,and landfill leakage (Baker, 2Ool, 2002; Baker and Inverarity, 2OO4; Borisover etal., 2011). Additionally, it has been applied for water treatment process optimization in WWTPs (Carstea et al., 2016; Murphy et al., 2011; Gofin et al., 2018) and water quality management in drinking water supply systems (Li et al., 2020a; Wang et al., 2021).

Considering samples with high absorbance are common for wastewaters or wastewater-impacted surface waters,the impact of inner filter effect (IFE),suspended solids and nitrite or nitrate interferences on accurate fluorescence measurement of wastewater organic matter is also an interesting topic (Sgroi et al., 2020a, 2020b, 2020c). It was revealed that IFE was negligible in treated wastewater at excitation ${ > } 3 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and on-line monitoring by spectroscopic measurements seems particularly suitable during advanced wastewater treatments,where suspended solids have been removed.Also,fluorescence intensities measured at excitation wavelength ${ < } 2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ may not be useful for on-line monitoring in wastewater with high nitrate/nitrite concentrations $_ { ( > 3 - 5 }$ $\mathrm { { \ m g / L } }$ as N) due to severe quenching effect.In addition,the issue of validation is another one of the biggest issues facing fluorescence as a fast analysis and high sensitivity method moving forward.For example, after the validation to mitigate the inconsistencies between fluorescence and lab measurement techniques,the on-line online optical sensors had the potential as robust surrogates of ${ \tt B O D } _ { 5 }$ or other indicators like microbial contamination (Gunter et al., 2023; Khamis et al., 2021; Mao et al., 2021).

In the field of urban water infrastructure,EEM has been used to assess the anthropogenic influences of paved runoff and sanitary sewage on the water quality of wet-weather overflow (WWF),confirming the potential ability of EEM to monitor the dynamic changes in the primary pollutant sources (Chen et al., 2O17). Combining the degradation potential index and end-member mixing model, EEM data have also been used to apportion the contributions various sources of DOM to WWF of storm drainage systems (Liao et al., 2O2l).For the assessment of urban drainage system performance,fluorescent DOM and associated EEM data have been introduced to identify dry-weather flow misconnections into municipal storm pipes,especially for river water backflow into storm drains and the resulting storm pumping discharge during dry-weather periods (Yin et al.,202O).Recently, EEM spectroscopy was also used to track hotspots of raw wastewater in low-and medium-order urban streams that were impacted by sanitary sewer overflows,septic systems,and sewer exfiltration.The study findings confirmed the potential for EEM-based wastewater indicatorsasa quick，easy, cost-effective,and scalable technique to screen failing sewer infrastructure in streams (Batista-Andrade et al., 2O23).However, few studies have been conducted on the use of EEM data to identify the causes of low performance of urban sewer networks,a difficult task that involves the assessment of dominant factors among a mixture of potential source flows，including domestic wastewater，industrial wastewater,and extraneouswater.

In light of the above considerations,this study was designed to establish a cost-effective approach to identifying the key factors that result in the low performance of urban sewer networks based on the interpretation of fluorescent EEM data for in-sewer flow samples.The method developed in this study was then applied to a typical urban sewer network,and accordingly,assessment criteria were proposed to determine the dominant source flows contributing to the low biodegradability of the WWTP influent.

# 2.Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study area

The study site is the urban sewer network served by a WWTP in Wuhan City,Hubei Province, China. Covering $4 3 . 2 ~ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ ,itis a mixed residential and industrial area.The industrial sewage outputs are from electronics manufacturing industry，with daily sewage discharges ranging from 5000 to $2 0 , \dot { 0 } 0 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } / \mathrm { d }$

The design treatment capacity of this WWTP is $1 0 , 0 0 0 { \mathrm { m } } ^ { 3 } / { \mathrm { d } }$ ,and the design influent COD, $\mathrm { B O D } _ { 5 } ,$ ，ammonia nitrogen $\mathrm { ( N H } _ { 3 } – N )$ ,and total nitrogen (TN) concentrations are $4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ $1 8 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ $3 2 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ ,and 40 $\scriptstyle { \mathrm { m g / L } }$ ,respectively.However, the actual average influent COD, $\mathrm { B O D } _ { 5 } ,$ $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 - \mathrm { N } }$ ,and TN concentrations on dry-weather days are only $9 9 . 9 \mathrm { m g / L }$ $3 4 . 1 \ \mathrm { m g / L }$ $1 2 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ ,and $2 2 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ ，respectively,which are only $1 8 . 9 \mathrm { - } 5 6 . 3 \%$ of the design values,causing a range of issues in wastewater treatment performance and efficiency.Therefore,there is an unmet need to track the sources of sewer flows to probe the main reasons for the low influent concentrations and to propose targeted actions to resolve this problem.

# 2.2.Sampling sites and analytical methods

In response to this need,a sampling campaign was conducted within the sewer network area.As shown in Fig.1,a total of 17 sampling sites were chosen,in which 7 sites (Gl-G7) were located along the trunk sewer to monitor the fluorescent EEM fingerprint of the sewer flows entering the WWTP.As the trunk sewer flow is a mixture of sewage from residential communities and treated wastewater from industrial facilities,an additional seven sites (Zl-Z7) were selected at sewage manholes connecting to the municipal sewers to analyze the fingerprints of the domestic sewage (sites Z1, Z3, Z5,and Z6) and treated wastewater from electronics manufacturing facilities (sites Z2,Z4,and Z7).In addition,considering that the rainfall in this area is abundant and the groundwater table is generally above the sewer invert, three groundwater monitoring sites (D1-D3) were selected to analyze the potential groundwater infiltration into the sewers.

The sampling period was from June7 to 8,2021,before which a 48-h antecedent dry period was observed to minimize the possibility of rainfall impact on sewage flows. The sampling campaign continued for $4 8 \mathrm { { h } }$ ,with a fixed sampling interval of $^ { 4 \mathrm { h } }$ .A total of 204 samples were collected from all 17 monitoring sites.These samples were filtered through $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ membranes (Whatman,Maidstone,UK),stored at approximately $4 \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ and then transported to the laboratory for immediate processing.

The fingerprints of all samples were interpreted based on the fluorescence EEM spectra,which were analyzed using a fluorescence spectrofluorometer (Hitachi，F-71oo,Tokyo,Japan）with $^ { 1 } \mathrm { \ c m }$ quartz cuvettes.The EEM spectrum of each sample was recorded with excitation $\left( \operatorname { E x } \right)$ and emission (Em) wavelengths of $2 0 0 { - } 5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals and $5 \mathrm { { n m } }$ slit widths.Th scanning rate was $1 2 , 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m / m i n }$ and the voltage of photomultipliers tube was $7 0 0 \ \mathrm { V }$ .It was reported that absorbing species in raw wastewater could induce strong IFE (Ohno, 2002; Goffin et al., 2018; Sgroi et al., 2020c). However, the IFE could be minimized when the UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ decreased toa level below 0.05 (Goffin et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2017). Therefore,all the collected samples were diluted 1O times to ensure an anticipated UV absorbance level mentioned above.

The EEM spectrum of Milli-Q water was collected for every 10 diluted wastewater samples to ensure the cuvette was not contaminated. Subsequently,these Milli-Q water spectra were subtracted from the spectra of the diluted wastewater samples to minimize background response,and any negative intensity values produced by this subtraction were replaced by zero values.Meanwhile,the first-and second-order Rayleigh and Raman scattering were decreased by the subtraction. The Delaunay triangulation interpolation algorithm was further used for correcting the Rayleigh and Raman scattering regions before data interpretation.Finally,the Raman peak of Milli-Q waterat excitation wavelength of $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ was used to convert Raman Units (R.U.).

# 2.3.EEM data interpretation

# 2.3.1.Parallel factor analysis

Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC),also known as parallel factor decomposition,is a data interpretation approach used in fluorescence spectroscopy to extract information from complex fluorescence datasets. It is a powerful tool for decomposing the DOM signature into different pools,especially for samples with overlapping fluorophores in fluorescence spectroscopy.In this way，the underlying independent fluorophores at specific wavelengths can be revealed.

Mathematically,PARAFAC decomposes the data array into a set of

orthogonal factors as follows:

$$
x _ { i j k } = \sum _ { f = 1 } ^ { F } a _ { i f } b _ { j f } c _ { k f } + \varepsilon _ { i j k } , i = 1 , . . . , I ; j = 1 , . . . , J ; k = 1 , . . . , K ,
$$

where $x _ { i j k }$ represents one element of the EEMdata array with dimensions $I \times J \times K ,$ i.e.,the fluorescence intensity of sample i measured at an Em of $j \ \mathrm { n m }$ and an Ex of $k \mathrm { \ n m }$ ： $\varepsilon _ { i j k }$ represents the unexplained signal, including the measurement noise and other un-modeled variations. $a _ { i f } .$ $b _ { j f }$ ，and $c _ { k f }$ are the model outputs that indicate the concentration, Em spectra,and Ex spectra of the interpreted fluorescent components, respectively. $F$ is the optimal number of fluorescent components for fitting the PARAFAC model,which is usually determined through residual examination,core consistency diagnostics,and split-half validation (Stedmon and Bro, 2008; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Murphy et al.,2013).PARAFAC here was conducted using the drEEM (Murphy et al.,2013) toolbox in MATLAB R2022a.

# 2.3.2.Fluorescence regional integration

Fluorescence regional integration (FRI) is a method developed to integrate the area beneath the EEM spectrum (Chen et al., 2OO3; Li et al., 2020b). Using this approach, it is postulated that the integration beneath the EEM spectra within selected regions will represent the cumulative fluorescence response of the DOM with similar properties.The volume $( \Phi _ { i , n } )$ beneath region ican be calculated as follows:

$$
\Phi _ { i , n } = M F _ { i } \iint _ { \it e x e m } I _ { ( \lambda _ { e x } \lambda _ { e m } ) d \lambda _ { e x } d \lambda _ { e m } } ,
$$

where $\Phi _ { i , n }$ is the normalized excitation-emission area volume of region $i ,$ and $M F _ { i }$ is the multiplying factor of region $i ,$ which is equal to the reciprocal of the proportion of the fractional projected excitationemission area of region $i$ to the total integrated area of five regions.

Two other parameters for FRI are the total normalized excitationemission area volume of different integration regions $( \Phi _ { T , n } )$ and the percentage fluorescence response in a specific region iof the EEM $( P _ { i , n } )$ which can be calculated as follows:

$$
\Phi _ { T , n } = \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { 5 } \Phi _ { i , n } ,
$$

$$
P _ { i , n } = \Phi _ { i , n } \big / \Phi _ { T , n } \times 1 0 0 \% .
$$

Usually,FRI decomposes the EEM into five specific regions in which the organic matter exhibits similar properties (Carstea et al., 2Ol6; Chen et al.,2003; Islam et al.,2021). Table 1 lists the fluorescence regions in FRI and the associated types of organic matter. However， the excitation-emission integration areas can be adjusted according to the actual measured Ex and Em wavelengths.

# 2.3.3.Principal component analysis

Principal component analysis (PCA) was applied to find out how fluorescence components and FRI results were correlated among different sampling sites in the investigated sewer,so as to assess correlations or heterogeneities of fluorescence characteristics between the sources and in-sewer flow.Specifically,the operation steps of PCA are as follows:

Step 1: Arrange a sample vector x of m variables, that is, $x = \{ { \mathbf { x } } _ { 1 } , { \mathbf { x } } _ { 2 } ,$ $\ldots , \mathbf { X } _ { \mathrm { m } } \}$ .Each sample vector comprises four variables corresponding to the PARAFAC-derived fluorescence intensities,or five variables corresponding to FRI percentages.Assuming there are $n$ monitoring samples, a $n \times m$ data matrix $X$ with each row representing a sample can be used to build a PCA model.

Step 2: For each sample vector $\mathbf { X } _ { i }$ ,centralize all variables by:

$$
\stackrel { \prime } { x _ { i } } = x _ { i } - 1 \stackrel { \prime } { \quad } m \times \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { m } x _ { i } .
$$

Step 3:Form a new centralized data matrix $X$ and calculate the covariance matrix $X X ^ { T }$ .Then perform $m$ -dimensional eigenvalues and $m \times m$ eigenvector matrix $E$ decomposition on the covariance matrix.

Step 4: Select the low space dimension $k$ from the $m$ -dimensional eigenvalues,and then take the reduced $m \times k$ eigenvector matrix $E ^ { ' }$ corresponding to the selected eigenvalues $\mathbf { w } _ { 1 } , \mathbf { w } _ { 2 } , . . . , \mathbf { w } _ { \mathrm { k } }$

Step 5:Output $n \times k$ projection matrix for correlation analysis by multiplying centralized data matrix $X$ and eigenvector matrix $E ^ { ' }$ together.

PCA analysis for fluorescence here were performed by Origin Pro (2021).

# 3.2.2.In-sewer flow samples

As shown in Fig.3,for the in-sewer flow samples collected from the trunk sewer pipe (sites G1-G7),tyrosine-like B2 exhibited the highest $F _ { m a x }$ among the four identified components,and the relative amount $\%$ $F _ { m a x }$ ranged from $3 8 \%$ to $5 1 \%$ at these seven monitoring sites.The tryptophan-like $\mathrm { ~ T ~ } \% F _ { m a x }$ values were the second largest,ranging from $1 6 \%$ to $2 4 \%$ . Such an occurrence was unlike the collected domestic sewage samples,for which the $\% F _ { m a x }$ values of tryptophan-like T were the highest.Instead,it was similar to the industrial wastewater and groundwater samples,in which tyrosine-like B2 was dominant.As the average $F _ { m a x }$ values of B1,B2,T,and An at all the seven sites were greater than the values for groundwater, the $F _ { m a x }$ values of the in-sewer flow samples were more similar to those of the industrial wastewater samples than those of the groundwater at this site.

Using the composite indicators demonstrated in Figs. 4 and 5 shows the T/B2 and T/A at the seven monitoring sites. Specifically,the T/B2 of the in-sewer flows ranged from 0.34 to 0.72,with an average of 0.5l and variation coefficient of 0.40; the T/A of the in-sewer flows ranged from 1.49 to 1.98,with an average of 1.76 and variation coefficient of 0.26. The spatial variation of T/B2 was more obvious than that of T/A,which indicated that T/B2 is more robust in signifying the sewer flow chemistry associated with low biodegradability of WWTP influents.

Based on the T/B2, the calculated data of in-sewer flow were almost the same as those for the industrial wastewater (i.e.,average $\mathrm { T } / \mathrm { B } 2 =$ 0.49) and were $4 0 \%$ of those of the domestic sewage.This further supported the conclusion that the in-sewer flow was dominated by industrial wastewater.Fig.5aalso indicatesa sudden increase in the $\mathrm { T } / \mathrm { B } 2$ at monitoring site G4,which is associated with the direct input of industrial wastewater at this site (Fig.1).Generally,a gradual decrease in the T/B2 may indicate the degradation of tryptophan-like Twithin the trunk sewer,as the T/B2 was lower than those of industrial wastewater and groundwater down to monitoring site 4.

It was stated that peak T was usually associated with high microbial abundance (Carstea et al., 2016). Bai et al. (2023) unraveled the longitudinal patterns of DOM quality and bacterial community degradation function along the mainstream of Three Gorges Reservoir during impoundment period.It was found that in-situ microbial degradation was crucial in controlling DOM quantity and quality. Bacteria utilized the biodegradable substances,leading to the decreased tryptophan-like fluorescence and the accumulation of utilization-associated products such as tyrosine-like substance.Other previous study showed that the hydrophilic,less aromatic,and fresh DOM was more ready for biodegradation than the hydrophobic compounds containing condensed aromatic structures (Bai et al.,2Ol7).As aforementioned, compared with peak Tof higher freshness,fluorescence peak B is usually derived from more degraded microbial products or peptides with less freshness.This also strengthens that the in-situ biotransformation would preferentially utilize tryptophan-like substances followed by tyrosine-like substances. From another perspective， the decrease of T/B2 indicated the non-conservative behavior of DOM degradation along the trunk sewer, which also contributed to the low biodegradability of WWTP influent to some extent.

# 4. Implications

Based on above discussions,the telltale fluorescence data can interpret the fingerprint information of sewer flows in an effective way. Especially,unlike the EEM-PARAFAC ratio of tryptophan-to humic-like component (T/A) and FRI fraction of microbial product-like region IV to that of humic acid-like region V (IV/V) to track hotspots of raw wastewater in urban streams,our study presented novel indicators of peak T to peak B(T/B2) and FRI fraction of tyrosine-like region I to that of tryptophan-like region II (I/II) to discriminate a mixture of source flows within urban sewers,including domestic wastewater,industrial wastewater,and extraneous water.Accordingly,the low biodegradability of WWTP influents was unveiled.

From a wider perspective,the followings are the practical implications of such application for a real full-scale system including sample collection,sample analysis,and response of the WWTP to detected variation of influent quality.

# 4.1．Sample collection

Practically it is difficult to identify the travel time of each source input and perform an accurately transient sample collection according to the time lag between upstream and downstream manholes.This difficulty can be mediated by performing continuous sample collection within a longer time interval.In our case,it was suggested that the sampling activity continues for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ witha fixed time interval of $4 \mathrm { h } _ { \cdot }$ and accordingly generates12 samples for each sampling site.In this way,the averaged fluorescent characteristics of the multiple samples can represent in-sewer flow chemistries between upstream and downstream manholes effectively.Also,such sample collection approach can provide support for the establishment of fluorescent fingerprint database of different sources.

# 4.2. Sample analysis

Comparing to the measurement of chemical and biological indicators, fluorescence measurement has the advantages of simple measurement,and lack of secondarypollution.However,the sample EEMs should be corrected for IFE,blanks,Raleigh and Raman scattering removal,and Raman normalization prior to further analysis.Dilution of collected samples can minimize the IFE from suspended solids and nitrate or nitrite.Then,the EEM-PARAFAC,FRI,and PCA can be combined together to interpret the fluorescent fingerprint information for sewer condition assessment,e.g.,the low biodegradability of WWTP influents in this case.

# 4.3. Threshold for source identification

The domestic sewage samples had the highest freshness peak T, whiletheinorganicindustrialwastewater(semiconductor manufacturing wastewater in this site) and groundwater samples had the highest peak B2 with less freshness.The T/B2 can be used to discriminate domestic sewage from inorganic industrial wastewater and groundwater.Industrial wastewater and groundwater can be further distinguished using the FRI fraction of I/II.In our case, $\mathrm { T } / \mathrm { B } 2 < 1 . 3$ indicates the entry of industrial wastewater or groundwater into urban sewers.Furthermore, $\mathrm { I } / \mathrm { I I } > 0 . 5$ indicates industrial wastewater,and I/I $< 0 . 5$ signifies infiltrated groundwater. Using these thresholds,we can determine the inflow source type that results in the low biodegradability of WWTP in an effective way.

# 4.4.WWTP response to influent quality

Based on T/B2 and I/II,if the WWTP influent is characterized as large amounts of groundwater infiltration,actions on sewer inspections and repairs should be performed.If the low biodegradabilityof WWTP influent is characterized as industrial wastewater entry,actions should be focused on the surveillance of industrial wastewater,while sewer detections and repairs are not mandatory.Therefore,this methodology is cost-effective in the system-wide assessment of urban sewer performance and also aids in the selection of targeted countermeasures to improve the concentration or biodegradability of the WWTP influents.

However,there is a limitation that the developed methodology was tested with one study area.It is anticipated that this method will be applied to more urban sewer networks to further test the potential of the telltale fluorescence fingerprints for sewer performance assessment.

# 5.Future perspectives

Our study indicated that with the continuous development and improvement of various EEM indicators such as peak intensity,typical wavelength regions,fluorescence indices and principal components interpretation, fluorescence measurements can be effectively used to discriminate the entries leading to the low performance of urban sewer network.In the future,fluorescence measurement is expected to provide strong support for real-time monitoring and timely regulation of urban sewers.

Currently the portable fluorometers to monitor single fluorescence peaks in wastewater,e.g.,peak Tand peak C,have been reported. Our study presented that T-to-B2 ratio can be used to discriminate domestic sewage from industrial wastewater and groundwater.From this perspective,fluorescence devices to monitor these special fluorescence peaks for practical online monitoring purposes,might be applicable to intelligent management of urban sewers.However,real on-line monitoring of EEMis scarcely reported.Therefore,there needs to be more incentives to explore the potential new technologies for online EEM spectroscopy,which will further highlight the wider benefits of fielddeployable senor technologies for smart detection and regulation of urban sewer network.
