# Abstract

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) absorption and fluorescence properties were investigated for seawater samples collected from the polar mixed layer (PML; $0 { - } 3 0 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ of the Canada Basin in 2010-2012. Sea ice concentration as well as fractions of meteoric and sea ice melt water (fMW and fSIM) calculated from oxygen isotope ratio $( \delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O } )$ were applied to assess the importance of discrete freshwater inputs to the distribution of DOM. Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) identified four humic-like (C1-2 and C4-C5) and one protein-like (C3) fluorescent components in 380 excitation-emission matrix spectra (EEMs). Surprisingly, despite different sea ice cover and biological regimes, DOM composition was largely homogeneous spanning these annual surveys. A strong and reoccurring coastal influence on DOM absorption and humic-like DOM was observed each year and was particularly pronounced during the summer 2011 survey. Enrichment of DOM humic signal (C1, C2, and $\yen 4)$ in brine-rich $\mathrm { ( f s m } < 0 \mathrm { ) }$ waters relative to sea ice melt dominated waters $\mathrm { ( f _ { S I M } > 0 }$ ; $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ ） was found during 2011 and 2012 in the offshore region $( > 7 6 \mathrm { ^ \circ N } )$ where coastal influences were minimal $\mathrm { \ f { _ { M W } } } { < } 0 . 1 $ ). Similar $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } } < 0$ were found for 2011 and 2012, either when considering the Canada Basin as a whole or the offshore region $( > 7 6 \mathrm { ^ \circ N } )$ exclusively, which could imply that brine formation influenced humic signals in PML seawater. Findings herein highlight that future projected changes in freshwater sources and brine production in the Canada Basin will likely implicate the distribution and composition of DOM.

# Plain Language Summary

In this paper we showed that despite different sea ice cover in summer-early fall, the average levels and composition of colored dissolved organic matter remained relatively unchanged in surface waters in the Canada Basin in 2010-2012. Only the northwest region experiencing strong brine rejection showed elevated humic signals. We also suggest to use the $1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ line as reference for DOM monitoring in the Canada Basin in future studies..

# 1 Introduction

Reduction in September sea ice extent minima in Arctic seawater has been reported since prior to 1953, a trend that has become apparent over the past two decades (Stroeve et al., 2007; 2012). A record minimum was reached in September 2012, $4 9 \%$ below the 1979-2000 average sea ice extent (Perovich et al., 2012). Projections have shown that summer sea ice extent and sea ice thickness will continue to decline in the next decades (AMAP 2012), which may enhance primary production due to greater light penetration (Arrigo et al., 2008; 2012; Ardyna et al., 2014) and/or enhance the rate of remineralization of organic carbon by photolysis (Bélanger et al., 2006). A recent study on summertime biological productivity suggested that whereas the rapid reduction in summer sea ice extent may not change the carbon balance in the Beaufort Gyre, it may improve microbial efficiency at recycling carbon (Stanley et al., 2015). This disparity in projected implications makes it important to assess how changes in sea ice production/melt regimes affect carbon cycling as part of the particularly dynamic dissolved organic matter (DOM) pool in Arctic surface water.

DOM is a complex and heterogeneous mixture of chemical constituents, including incompletely characterized humic substances, lignin, proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, and other biomacromolecules (Nebbioso and Piccolo, 2012; Repeta, 2015). Chromophoric DOM (CDOM) strongly absorbs solar radiation and can thus shield biota from harmful UV radiation and stimulate bacterial growth (Walsh et al., 2003; Mopper et al., 2015). CDOM is also sensitive to photodegradation (Benner and Kaiser, 2011; Guéguen et al., 2016) which can produce lower molecular weight organic compounds that have been proposed to be more readily utilized by bacteria (Mopper et al., 2015). A fraction of CDOM that emits a fluorescence signal (Fluorescent DOM, FDOM) can be used to trace sources, mixing, and removal of FDOM components in marine waters (Guéguen et al., 2005; Murphy et al., 2008; Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; Yamashita et al., 2013; Logvinova et al., 2015; Goncalves-Araujo et al., 2016). The distribution and dynamics of CDOM and FDOM in Arctic surface waters are particularly important given its disproportionate share of terrestrially-derived organic matter from northern circumpolar freshwater systems (Opsahl et al., 1999). While there have been advances in tracing and characterizing CDOM/FDOM in Arctic surface waters (Walker et al., 2009; Dainard and Guéguen 2013; Fichot et al., 2013; Guéguen et al., 2014), the role of sea ice has only garnered attention more recently (Jørgensen et al., 2015; Logvinova et al., 2015; Mendoza et al., 2017).

Distinctions in DOM absorption and fluorescence in seawater, brines, and sea ice have been previously observed in Beaufort and Chukchi Seas (Guéguen et al., 2007; Logvinova et al., 2016; Shen et al., 2016), Baltic Sea (Stedmon et al., 2007; Müller et al., 2011), East Greenland Sea (Gonlçalves-Araujo et al., 2016), Sea of Okhotsk (Granskog et al. 2015) and Southern Ocean (Norman et al., 2011; Stedmon et al., 2011). Sea ice formation has been shown to result in partial rejection of CDOM (Stedmon et al., 2007; Müller et al., 2011) and consequent enrichment in brine (Stedmon et al., 2011). It has also been found that newly formed sea ice was composed of smaller DOM molecules (Müller et al., 2013) with an enriched protein-like signature (Stedmon et al., 2007; Granskog et al. 2015) compared to initial seawater. Improved understanding of the role of sea ice in the storage/release of CDOM/FDOM has supported interpretations of how future changes in sea ice regimes could affect biogeochemical processes in Arctic surface waters (Logvinova et al. 2015; 2016; Mendoza et al. 2017). A keen interest was therefore to assess how highly variable sea ice conditions for an interannual dataset, including the record low sea ice extent during the polar summer of 2012, influenced DOM absorption and fluorescence properties.

In this study, absorption and fluorescence measurements, including PARAFAC modeling of excitationemission matrix spectra (EEMs) data, were conducted on $3 8 0 \mathrm { D O M }$ samples collected from the surface layer (0- $3 0 \mathrm { m } )$ of the Beaufort Gyre Region of the Canada Basin during cruises in September-October 2010, July-August 2011 and August-September 2012. Absorbing and fluorescing characteristics of DOM were related to meteoric and sea ice melt fractions (i.e. fMW and $\mathrm { f _ { S I M } } .$ , respectively) calculated from $\delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O }$ (Östlund and Hut, 1984; Cooper et al., 2005; Guéguen et al., 2007; Yamamoto-Kawai et al., 2009) to assess how freshwater sources relate to (1) the characteristics of DOM in the Canada Basin in summer/early fall and (2) the interannual variability in CDOM/FDOM distribution and composition in the Canada Basin, including a repeated northwest transect at $1 4 0 \mathrm { ^ \circ W }$ .

# 2 Materials and Methods

# 2.1 Sample collection

Sampling station locations and approximate sea ice concentration percent in 2010-2012 are illustrated in Figure 1. Sea ice concentration estimates were made hourly during a bridge-based ice watch (http:// www.iarc.uaf.edu/icewatch) and confirmed using the sea ice analysis maps made available by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration. It should be noted that all sea ice observed was rotten. A total of 380 DOM samples were collected during cruises of the Beaufort Sea from the polar mixed layer (0-30 m) during fall 2010 (September 16 – October 14; $\mathtt { n } = 9 8$ ), summer 2011 (July 23 – August 15; $\mathbf { n } = 1 0 0 ^ { \cdot }$ ) and summer 2012 (August 5 – September 7; $\mathrm { n } = 1 8 2$ ) as part of the Canada/US Joint Ocean Ice Study (JOIS)/Arctic Observing Network – Beaufort Gyre Observing System (AON-BGOS) program. Seawater samples $( \mathtt { n } = 1 9 5$ ) were collected in Niskin bottles mounted on a rosette equipped with a conductivity – temperature – depth (Seabird $\mathrm { S B E 9 + }$ CTD) profiler and a Seapoint chlorophyll fluorometer. 1L of seawater was filtered onto $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { M }$ glass-fiber filters (GF/F, Whatman) immediately after collection and stored frozen in the dark prior to spectrophotometric analysis for chlorophyll at the Institute of Ocean Sciences, BC. Significant linear correlation was observed between the Seapoint chlorophyll sensor and discrete spectrophotometric measurements $( \mathbf { r } ^ { 2 } =$ $0 . 7 5 \substack { - 0 . 8 2 }$ for 2010-2012). DOM samples were drawn from Niskin bottles (typically at depths of 5, 15, and 25- $3 0 \mathrm { - m }$ at each station) using a glass syringe and filtered using pre-combusted $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4h) $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ GF/Fs (Dainard and Guéguen, 2013). DOM samples were stored in pre-combusted $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ amber glass vials in the dark at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ In addition to depth samples, DOM samples were also taken from the ship underway system $( \sim 1 0 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ while the ship was steaming $( \mathtt { n } = 1 8 5$ ) (Guéguen et al., 2015). Unfiltered $\delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O }$ samples $\mathrm { ( n = 1 } 3 8$ ) were collected in $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ glass bottles and stored at room temperature.

# 2.2 DOM UV/Visible absorption and fluorescence

Absorption measurements were made following experimental protocol outlined in Dainard and Guéguen (2013). Briefly, CDOM absorption spectra were acquired against a Milli-Q water blank $( \geq 1 8 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m } \Omega ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 } )$ at 1 nm intervals spanning 250 to $7 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ using a Shimadzu UV 2550 spectrophotometer and $1 0 \mathrm { { c m } }$ quartz cuvettes. Absorption coefficient was calculated at $3 5 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $( a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 ) )$ as a means to quantify terrestrially-derived CDOM (Blough et al., 1993). CDOM absorption $( a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } )$ data was fit with a non-linear regression (Stedmon et al. 2000; Guéguen and Kowalczuk, 2013) to determine the spectral slope over wavelengths spanning 275-295 $\bf { n m } ( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ as a proxy for CDOM relative molecular weight and/or aromaticity (Helms et al., 2008).

Fluorescence scans were performed using a Fluromax-4 Jobin Yvon spectrofluorometer. Highly fluorescent DOM samples were diluted using Milli-Q $( \geq 1 8 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m } \Omega . \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) water to reduce inner-filter effects (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Excitation-emission matrix spectra (EEMs) were generated in signal to noise ratio using a 1-cm quartz cuvette with excitation and emission slit widths set at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . EEMs scans were obtained by collecting fluorescence intensity measurements at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals for excitation wavelengths ranging from 250 to $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and emission wavelengths ranging from 300 to $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . Fluorescence spectra were normalized to Raman units by integrating the area under the Raman scatter peak for deionized water (Milli-Q) blanks acquired daily (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009). Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was used to decompose 380 EEMs into independent fluorescent components. Nineteen samples $5 \%$ of the original EEMs) were flagged as outliers and omitted. EEMs data was parsed as part of data exploration procedures to include only excitation wavelengths of 280 to $4 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and emission wavelengths of 300 to $4 6 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . This EEMs pre-treatment was similar to that employed by Mendoza et al. (2017) to minimize interference of residual fluorescence produced by scatter, spectral noise, and second order peaks to PARAFAC modeling. PARAFAC analysis was carried out in Matlab R2013a (MathWorks) using the drEEM (Murphy et al., 2013) and DOMFluor (Stedmon and Bro, 2008) toolboxes. The model was constrained to non-negative values and run for three to ten components. The appropriate number of components was determined by split-half and Tucker congruence coefficient analyses (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Final model validation was conducted using random split half analysis and random initialization (Figure S1-S2). Proportions of fluorescent components were determined by dividing each component by total fluorescence (e.g. $\% { \mathrm { C l } } = { \mathrm { C l } }$ $/ \left( \mathbf { C } 1 + \mathbf { C } 2 + \mathbf { C } 3 + \mathbf { C } 4 + \mathbf { C } 5 \right) ^ { * } 1 0 0 )$ . For statistical tests of variance and principal component analyses (PCA) the distribution of proportion data was normalized using the arcsine method (Pawlowsky-Glahn and Egozcue, 2006). Spearman’s rank correlation coefficients were calculated using the software package PAST $\mathbf { V } 2 . 1 7$ (Hammer et al., 2001).

# 2.3 Sea ice melt (fSIM) and meteoric water (fMW) fractions

The oxygen isotope ratio $( \delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O } )$ was measured with a mass spectrometer connected to a $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 } { \cdot } \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ equilibration unit. Samples were measured with two working standards calibrated against Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water (VSMOW). From duplicate samples, the uncertainty of $\delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O }$ values for each water sample was $\sim 0 . 0 3 \text{‰}$ .

Values of $\delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O }$ were used to estimate the relative contribution of meteoric water (runoff $^ +$ precipitation) and sea ice melt water (e.g. Östlund and Hut, 1984; Macdonald et al., 1999; 2002; Yamamoto-Kawai et al., 2005). $$

$$

$$

$$

where f, S and δ are the fraction, salinity, $\delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O }$ values, respectively. The subscripts SW, MW and SIM designated Pacific-originated seawater, meteoric water, and sea-ice melt water, respectively. The end-member oxygen isotope compositions (Yamamoto-Kawai et al., 2005) used for the calculation are listed in Table 1. The $\delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { \overline { { O } } }$ of SIM was set at $- 2 \text{‰}$ to be representative of the $\delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O }$ signature of sea ice in the Canada Basin (Eicken et al. 2002; Pfirman et al. 2004). This is supported by a recent study in which an ice core sampled from the Canada Basin during August 2013 was reported to have $\bar { \delta } ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O }$ ranging from $- 2 . 6 \text{‰}$ (0 to $2 4 \ \mathrm { c m }$ ) to -1.2 to $- 0 . 8 \text{‰}$ (24 to 103 cm; Evans and Nishioka, 2019). The error in the calculated fractions due to uncertainty in end-member values was ${ \sim } 0 . 0 3$ (Yamamoto-Kawai et al., 2005). Continental runoff (i.e. rivers) and precipitation have similarly depleted $\delta ^ { 1 8 } \mathrm { O }$ ratio based on preferential evaporation of lighter $\mathrm { ^ { 1 6 } O }$ . Sea ice melt water has distinct salinity and $\mathbf { \hat { \boldsymbol { \delta } } } ^ { 1 8 } \mathbf { \boldsymbol { 0 } }$ ratio from $\mathrm { f _ { M W } }$ , allowing delineation of these freshwater end-members. Sea ice formation and brine rejection into underlying waters was characterized by negative $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ (Östlund and Hut, 1984).

# 3 Results

# 3.1 Surface layer characteristics

Pronounced variabilities in temperature and salinity were observed within the study area (Figure 2; Figure S3). Warmer $( { > } 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ and fresher waters $( \mathrm { S } < 2 7 )$ were found near the shelves and in the Amundsen Gulf due to combined influences of solar exposure and persistent upwelling-favourable wind along the Beaufort Shelf pushing the Mackenzie River plume offshore in the surface Ekman layer (Mustapha et al., 2016). Based on the temperature and salinity diagram (Figure S3), the polar mixed layer (PML) can be separated into the upper and lower PML (uPML and lPML, respectively; Matsuoka et al., 2012). uPML, mainly influenced by sunlight, river runoff and sea ice melt waters, was characterized by lower salinity $( 2 5 . 6 8 \pm 2 . 4 1$ , $\mathbf { n } = 2 7 7$ ) and warmer temperatures $( 1 . 7 2 \pm 3 . 7 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ , $\mathrm { n } \mathrm { = } 2 7 7$ ) whereas the lPML waters $( { > } 1 7 \mathrm { m } )$ were colder and saltier ( $- 0 . 5 7 \pm 0 . 7 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 8 . 6 0 \pm 1 . 3 9$ ; $\mathtt { n } = 6 3$ ) $\left( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 1 \right)$ . No lPML waters were sampled in 2010 (Figure S3). The ice cover was also remarkably variable between our three surveys (Figure 2). Survey results for 2012 showed above freezing temperatures as far north as $7 6 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } N }$ and considerably lower ice concentrations compared to 2010 and 2011 (Figure 2). The ice concentration was $< 1 5 \%$ (i.e. ice free) for the majority of our sampling area in 2012 except in the $>$

$8 0 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } N }$ region where more extensive ice cover was found $( > 8 0 \% )$ . An arctic sea ice concentration record minimum was reached in September 2012 during the first year of extreme melt-back in the Beaufort Gyre region (Parkinson and Comiso, 2013; Krishfield et al., 2014). The fluorescence of chlorophyll ranged from $< 0 . 0 5$ to 0.2 $\mathrm { { \dot { m } g } } \mathrm { { \dot { m } } } ^ { - 3 }$ with greater variability closer to the Beaufort shelves $( < 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ . The three annual surveys were conducted under different biological regimes spanning from early summer bloom (i.e. 2011 survey) to late summer/early fall bloom (i.e. 2010 survey). Higher chlorophyll concentrations in the Canada Basin were found in 2010 relative to the other years (Figure 2). Elevated chlorophyll concentrations were particularly found in the $7 3 - 7 5 \mathrm { ^ \circ N }$ region in 2010 (0.081 vs $0 . { \bar { 0 } } 4 5 { \cdot } 0 . 0 6 1 \mathrm { m g } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 } )$ , congruent with the fall bloom (Arrigo and van Dijken, 2004; Brugel et al., 2009; Else et al., 2012; Ardyna et al., 2014). In contrast, no marked difference in chlorophyll concentrations $( \mathsf { p } { > } 0 . 0 5 )$ was found in near shore waters between surveys.

fMW and $\mathbf { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ ranged from 0.11 to 0.31, and from -0.10 to 0.16, respectively (Figure 3). Similar ranges have been reported for surface mixed layer $( 0 - 3 0 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ seawater of the Canada Basin $( \mathrm { f _ { M W } } \sim 0 . 1 2 \textrm { - } 0 . 2 2$ and $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } } \sim - 0 . 0 6$ - 0.16; Brown et al, 2014; McLaughlin et al., 2011). A $7 \%$ increase in $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { M W } }$ was found in uPML relative to lPML, congruent with the influence of river runoff in surface waters (depths ${ < } 1 7 \mathrm { m } )$ ). In the northern Canada Basin $( >$ $7 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ , fMW and $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ values ranged from 0.12 to 0.15 and $- 0 . 0 4$ to 0.10, respectively. Negative $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ , revealing the dominance of brine waters, were observed across the study area, particularly near the shelves in 2010-2012 and in the $7 6 - 7 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ region in 2011-2012. Despite significant changes in sea ice concentration (Figure 2), no significant difference was found in $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ between 2012 and 2010 or 2011 in the Canada Basin and at higher latitudes $( > 7 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ $( \mathtt { p } > 0 . 0 5$ ; Figure 3), suggesting a decoupling between ice concentration and ice melt waters. On the other hand, higher $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ values were found in the late summer/early fall survey in 2010 relative to the summer 2012 survey in the Canada Basin and ${ } > 7 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ , confirming that sea ice melting continued until late September/ early October 2010. fSW (not shown) increased from 0.84 in uPML to 0.90 in the lPML waters, supported by greater salinity in deeper waters.

# 3.2 DOM characteristics in the Canada Basin

The $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ values spanned from 0.08 to $0 . 8 5 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ in the Canada Basin $( > 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ , with a mean of $0 . 2 6 \pm 0 . 1 3$ $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Figure 3), which encompass the range of $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ previously reported for surface water samples $( < 1 5 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ in the Canadian Arctic Archipelago $\cdot 0 . 1 8 \cdot 0 . 4 5 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Guéguen et al., 2014). The highest $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ values $( > 0 . 4$ $\mathbf { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Figure 3) were restricted to the uPML near the Beaufort Sea shelves and, most prominently, near the Mackenzie River delta at $\sim 1 3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ , a major source of terrestrially-derived CDOM (Guéguen et al., 2005; Walker et al., 2009). This result is congruent with the positive correlation of $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ with $\mathrm { f _ { M W } }$ $\mathrm { \Phi } _ { \mathrm { { r } _ { \mathrm { { s } } } } } = 0 . 4 4$ , p < 0.01; Figure S4). The $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ values were negatively and weakly correlated with $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ $\mathrm { ( r _ { s } } = - 0 . 3 7$ , $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 1$ ; Figure S4) with the brine-rich waters $\mathrm { ( f _ { S I M } < 0 ) }$ ) enriched in $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ $( 0 . 3 1 \pm 0 . 0 7 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) relative to sea ice melt waters $\mathrm { ( f _ { S I M } ) } 0$ ; $0 . 2 4 \pm 0 . 1 3 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ; $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 5$ ).

$\mathrm { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ ranged from 0.022 to $\bar { 0 } . 0 3 5 \ \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ at ${ } > 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ (Figure 3), which was also very similar to that previously reported in Beaufort Sea surface water (Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; Fichot et al., 2013; Logvinova et al., 2015; 2016; Shen et al., 2016). At any given site, $\mathrm { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ decreased from uPML to lPML, likely the results of enhanced photoalteration (Dainard and Guéguen, 2015) in uPML. The elevated $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ signal associated with the lowest $\mathbf { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ values $( < 0 . 0 2 5 ~ \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Figure 3) indicated higher molecular weight CDOM of greater aromaticity (Guéguen et al., 2013; Helms et al., 2008).

A 5-component PARAFAC model (Figure S1) was found with residual fluorescence intensities that were typically low and featureless for all samples $( < 1 0 \% )$ . The four humic-like (C1-C2, C4-C5) and one protein-like (C3) components have been previously identified in marine studies. The first three components matched aquatic fluorescent components found in the online OpenFluor database depository $9 5 \%$ minimum similarity score; Murphy et al., 2014) and the last two components were similar to those found in marine studies (Logvinova et al., 2015 and 2016; Mendoza et al., 2017). For an in-depth description of the PARAFAC components identified in this study please see Supporting Information.

Humic-like fluorescence dominated the FDOM pool in the Canada Basin where C1 and C2 were the most dominant components $( < 0 . 0 1 { - } 0 . 0 6 ~ \mathrm { r . u . ) }$ followed by C4 $( < 0 . 0 1 { - } 0 . 0 3 \ \mathrm { r . u . } )$ $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5$ ; Figure 4). Overall, the intensities of humic-like C1-C2 and C4 were $0 . 0 1 2 \pm 0 . 0 0 3$ r.u. $\mathrm { ( n = 1 6 2 ) }$ ), $0 . 0 1 3 \pm 0 . 0 0 3$ r.u. $\mathrm { \Delta n } = 1 6 2 \mathrm { \Delta }$ ) and 0.006 $\pm \ 0 . 0 0 2$ r.u. $\mathrm { ( n = 1 6 2 ) }$ ), respectively, in the Canada Basin (excluding the coastal regions). The humic-like fluorescence intensities of C1-C2 and C4 were generally higher at lower latitudes near the Mackenzie shelves, whereas patches of high protein-like C3 and humic-like C5 intensities were locally found in the Canada Basin (Figure 4). The latitudinal distribution of humic-like C5 was relatively uniform each year. Total humic-like intensity $( \mathrm { C l } \mathrm { + } \mathrm { C } 2 \mathrm { + } \mathrm { C } 4 )$ was 2.72-fold higher near the shelves $( < 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ when compared to offshore waters $( { \mathfrak { p } } <$ 0.05), likely the result of the influence of river runoff and coastal processes. In fact, each humic-like component (except C5) was significantly higher $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ in the nearshore region. No significant differences were found in PARAFAC component intensities between uPML and lPML except for humic-like C2 $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 1 )$ ). The uPML waters were $30 \%$ enriched in C2 relative to the lPML waters. Like humic-like C1-C2 and C4 $( \mathbf { r } _ { \mathrm { s } } = 0 . 3 3$ to 0.46, $\mathrm { p } { < } 0 . 0 1$ ; Figure S4), protein-like C3 showed a weak positive correlation with fMW $( \mathrm { r _ { s } } = 0 . 3 3$ , $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 1$ ; Figure S4), indicative of terrestrial sources.

In terms of compositional distribution, the abundance of C1-C2 and C5 were relatively uniform latitudinally whereas protein-like C3 and humic-like C4 showed significant differences nearshore and at higher latitudes (Figure S5). The contribution of terrestrially derived C4 decreased as the influence of river runoff (fMW; Figure 3) diminished with increasing distance from the humic-rich Arctic River mouths (Guéguen et al., 2007; Osburn et al., 2009; Guéguen et al., 2016), plateaued at mid-latitudes $( 7 3 - 7 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ , and increased again at higher latitudes. This C-curve was particularly pronounced in 2011 and corroborated with the in-situ surface fluorescence signal (Figure 3). Humic-like C1, C2 and C4 were negatively correlated to $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ $( \mathrm { r _ { s } } ~ = ~ - 0 . 4 9$ to -0.61; $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 1$ ) suggesting that brine could be a potential source of humic-like FDOM at higher latitudes. The higher latitude waters were generally ice covered $( > 8 0 \%$ ; Figure 2), limiting light penetration and photodegradation processes of FDOM. C4 would be expected to be particularly susceptible to photodegradation based on its comparatively red shifted fluorescence (C4; Figure S1) which could in part explain the higher terrestrial humic-like abundance in brine-rich $( \mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } } < 1 )$ ice-covered ${ < } 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ waters. The opposite latitudinal distribution was found for protein-like $\mathbf { \nabla } \mathrm { C } 3$ and chlorophyll fluorescence (Figure 3) which both peaked at mid-latitudes. However, no significant correlation was found between protein-like C3 and Chla $( \mathtt { p } > 0 . 0 1 )$ , suggesting that the protein-like signal was not primarily due to primary production. This contrasts with Yamashita et al (2017) and Makarewicz et al. (2018) who found a positive correlation between tryptophan-like and the extracted Chla concentration. Although protein-like FDOM is mainly derived from aromatic amino acids, such as tryptophan and tyrosine molecules in marine environments (Yamashita and Tanoue 2003; Yamashita et al. 2015, 2017), it may also contain low molecular weight aromatics (Timko et al., 2015) such as tannins and lignins (Maie et al., 2007; Hernes et al., 2009; Yamashita et al., 2017).

# 4 Discussion

# 4.1 Interannual variation in DOM composition

The repeated $1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ transect was used to assess the interannual variation in DOM characteristics (Figure 5, Figure S6). The proximity of the Mackenzie River was evidenced in the $1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ line at lower latitudes by the warm and fresh waters of higher CDOM and humic fluorescence signals. Ice free waters were restricted to the Mackenzie shelf and slope in fall 2010 $( < 7 1 . 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ and summer 2011 $( < 7 1 . 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ , whereas ice free waters were found up to $7 7 \mathrm { { ^ \circ N } }$ in summer 2012. The average ice cover was $44 \%$ in 2010-2011 but only $14 \%$ in 2012. The Mackenzie shelf and slope waters were enriched in brine $\left( \mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } } < 0 \right)$ ), whereas north of the slope the surface waters were dominated by sea ice melt waters $\mathrm { ( f _ { S I M } > 0 ) }$ ). Maximum contribution of sea ice melt $( \mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } } \sim 0 . 1 6 )$ in ice free waters was found in 2012 during the record minimum summer sea ice concentration (Perovich et al., 2012). Similar results were found in late summer 2012 in Chukchi Sea (Cooper et al., 2016). The meteoric water contribution $( \mathrm { f _ { M W } }$ ; Figure 5) was comparable among the surveys $( \mathtt { p } > 0 . 0 5 )$ but decreased with increasing latitudes and distances from coastal margins as expected. No annual differences in $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ and $\mathrm { f _ { M W } }$ were observed in the $1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ ice covered waters $( \mathtt { p } > 0 . 0 5 )$ . No significant CDOM differences were found in 2010-2012 $( { \mathfrak { p } } >$ 0.05), except in the coastal portion of the $1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ transect $( < 7 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ . A stronger terrestrially derived (high $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ and low $\mathrm { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ , humic like DOM signal was found near the Mackenzie shelves in 2011.

No significant differences in FDOM intensity and composition were found between surveys or between the whole study area and the $1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ transect (excluding the shelf region) $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ , suggesting homogeneous FDOM composition in the mixed layer of the Canada Basin in 2010-2012. The only exception was the brinerich waters (open dots, Figure 4) in the northwest in 2011 where humic FDOM increased by $3 6 - 5 9 \%$ compared to sea ice melt dominated waters. These brine-dominated waters showed greater $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 5 )$ and lower $\mathrm { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ values relative to sea ice melt waters $\mathrm { \Delta f _ { S I M } } > 0 )$ ) $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ (Figure 3). The presence of brine waters was congruent with higher salinity (25.61-29.34; Figure 2) and ice cover $( > 9 5 \%$ ; Figure 2) in July-August 2011. Brown et al. (2014) found negative $\mathrm { f _ { S I M } }$ and high salinity in the northwest mixed layer Canada Basin in JulyAugust 2008, indicating convective mixing due to sea ice formation. The higher sea ice cover in 2011 (Figure 2) may have also resulted in a lower exposure of DOM to solar radiation in surface waters and limited photobleaching of humic-like (C1-C2 and C4) previously identified as photolabile FDOM components (Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Stedmon et al., 2007; Dainard et al, 2015; Logvinova et al., 2015). Although negative fSIM was found in the coastal (2010 and 2012) and ice-covered regions (2012 only) (Figure 3), no elevated humic DOM signal was observed.

# 4.2 DOM composition in brine-rich waters

The principal component analysis of FDOM composition, ice cover and oxygen isotope composition (fSIM and fMW) revealed significant differences in DOM composition in brine-rich waters relative to sea ice melt waters in 2011. The first two principal components (PC1-PC2) accounted for $5 9 \%$ of the total explained variance (Figure 6). Despite similar maximum excitation and emission maxima (Figure S1), humic-like C1 and C5 were found in diametrically opposite quadrants. Unlike C5, C1 was significantly correlated with $\mathrm { f } _ { \mathrm { S I M } }$ and fMW. No significant difference in C5 levels were found between brine- and sea ice melt-rich waters $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ . On the other hand, higher C1 intensities in brine dominated waters (0.018 vs 0.013 r.u.; $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 1$ ) associated with strongly correlated terrestrial humic-like component C2 and C4 $\mathrm { \Delta r _ { s } } = 0 . 8 4$ and 0.94, respectively; $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 1$ ; Figure S7) and fMW $( \mathrm { r _ { s } ~ = ~ 0 . 6 4 , ~ p ~ < ~ 0 . 0 1 }$ ; Figure S7) indicated that C1 was influenced by terrestrial processes/sources and sea ice formation. The dichotomy between behavior of terrestrially derived humic-like C1 and microbially derived humic-like C5 showed that they have different sources and/or are affected by different processes in the Canada Basin. The brine-rich waters were clustered together with greater PC1 scores than the sea ice melt waters, confirming the role of brine in C1-C2 and C4 levels in the Canada Basin mixed waters.

# 5 Conclusions

This study examines the interannual variability in DOM absorption and fluorescence properties in the Beaufort Gyre surface layer during 2010-2012. Elevated humic signal was associated with the coastal region, consistent with the proximity of river runoff, shelf processes and intense biological activities. Although the average levels and composition of CDOM and FDOM were remarkably constant in the basin over the three-year period, significant differences were found in the northwest region. Deep convective mixing due to brine rejection during sea ice formation $\mathrm { ( f _ { S I M } < 0 ) }$ ) in the early summer survey of 2011 was expected to have dictated the elevated humic signal observed in northwest. This was supported by principal component analysis results that confirmed the enrichment in humic-like (C1-C2 and C4) in brine dominated waters. CDOM and FDOM averages were comparable along the repeated $1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ transect and the whole study area, suggesting that the $1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W }$ line can be used as reference for monitoring CDOM/FDOM in the Canada Basin in future studies.
