# Abstract:

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) in aquatic environments forms a vast reservoir of carbon present as a complex super-mixture of compounds. An efficient approach to tracking the production and removal of specific DOM fractions is needed across disciplines, for purposes that range from improving global carbon budgets to optimising water treatment in engineered systems. Although widely used to study DOM, fluorescence spectroscopy has yet to deliver specific fractions with known spectral properties and predictable distributions. Here, we mathematically isolate four visible-wavelength fluorescent fractions in samples from contrasting lake, river, and ocean environments. Using parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC), we show that most measured fluorescence in environmental samples can be explained by ubiquitous spectra with nearly stable optical properties and semi-labile photodegradation rate constants over environmental pH gradients. Sample extraction changed bulk fluorescence spectra but not the number or shape of underlying PARAFAC components, while photobleaching preferentially removed the two longest-wavelength components. New approaches to analysing fluorescence datasets incorporating these findings should improve the interpretation of DOM fluorescence and increase its utility for tracing organic matter biogeochemistry in aquatic systems.

# Introduction

DOM composition and concentration varies within and across aquatic systems depending on source and exposure to degradation processes 1-3 . Although only a fraction of DOM contains conjugated $\pi$ -electron systems that fluoresce 4, fluorescent DOM (FDOM) frequently tracks the wider DOM pool in the environment 5. For this reason and due to the relative ease of obtaining sensitive, cost-effective measurements, FDOM is frequently used as a proxy for DOM as a whole 5-7 . Fluorescence excitation emission matrices (EEMs) obtained by scanning across multiple wavelengths (Fig. 1) can be assembled into multiway datasets that can be mathematically decomposed using parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis 8. PARAFAC assumes that the fluorescence signal is the sum of emission by a limited number of unique, non-interacting components with independent spectral characteristics, and identifies these using an iterative fitting algorithm9. When Beers Law is upheld, fluorophores are noninteracting, fluorescence spectra are corrected for optical artefacts, and the correct number of components is assumed, then PARAFAC is capable of revealing the pure chemical spectra of the major underlying fluorescent moieties in known mixtures, and their concentrations in each sample 8. In complex mixtures like DOM, the purity of PARAFAC spectra, and relationships between fluorescence intensity and concentration of the responsible chemical moieties, are both unknown.

Over the past decade, PARAFAC has evolved to become the most widely-used statistical tool for characterising FDOM across a broad range of applications 9-11 . Numerous studies show broadly similar PARAFAC spectra occurring across diverse aquatic environments 10, 12 ; however, the specific fluorescent components that unambiguously signify discrete DOM fractions have remained elusive. Meanwhile, criticism of the PARAFAC approach has mounted. Spectrally-similar components are frequently reported to exhibit contrasting responses to similar physical and chemical gradients 10. 82 severely hampered further developments in this field. A potential resolution to this paradox is   
83 to create multiway datasets from individual DOM samples 19 . We implemented this approach   
84 by exposing DOM extracts to simulated solar irradiation under controlled pH in a   
85 recirculating flow cell 20 . The induced fluorescence changes allowed us to mathematically   
86 isolate independently-varying components. Analysing each extract independently reduced   
87 complexity and bias and ensured that environmental gradients other than photodegradation   
88 were reduced or eliminated.

# Methods

Twenty-eight datasets were created and analysed independently in this study. Each dataset was derived from one of thirteen samples of aquatic DOM obtained from diverse natural waters, spanning dark-water samples dominated by decomposing terrestrial plant matter, clear-water lakes, and samples originating far from terrestrial sources and having exclusively aquatic DOM (SI: Table S1). Three samples represented filtered $( 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ whole-water samples containing bulk DOM, the remainder were isolates extracted using reverse osmosis (RO), solid-phase extraction (PPL 21 ), or XAD resin.

The Stokes shift, representing the energy difference between these positions, varied between 0.55 and $1 . 3 5 \mathrm { e V }$ , thus falling within the same range that is observed for pure organic compounds 29 . Reoccurring PARAFAC components appeared in all models and were named according to the average position of the emission peak maximum across all samples. Peaks observed at $3 9 9 \pm 8 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , $4 2 5 \pm 9 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , $4 4 8 \pm 4$ nm and $5 2 0 \pm 4$ nm were named $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 0 0 }$ , $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 2 0 }$ , F450 and $\mathrm { F } _ { 5 2 0 }$ , respectively.

Between datasets, the derived components were highly spectrally similar whether comparing (i) PPL extracts with their matching bulk samples (Fig. 3A; SI: S2); (ii) RO extracts degraded at different pH (Fig. 3B); (iii) various rivers and lakes (Fig. 3C); or (iv) terrestrial river/lake versus autochthonous deep-sea/Antarctic samples (Fig. 4A). The ubiquitous spectra were also highly congruent with one-sample HPSEC-EEM-PARAFAC spectra 19 (Fig. 4C) although HPSEC separates components by size instead of photochemical reactivity. Whereas DOM extraction preserved fluorescence spectral shapes, extraction efficiencies varied between components, with PPL samples containing relatively less $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 2 0 }$ and relatively more $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 5 0 }$ than their corresponding bulk samples (SI: S2).

The double-exponential decay kinetics of PARAFAC components suggest that photodecomposition occurred by more than one pathway. In addition to causing direct photobleaching of chromophores, absorption of light by organic chemicals produces a range of photochemically-produced reactive species involved in indirect photodegradation of DOM, including triplet excited states $( ^ { 3 } \mathrm { { D O M } ^ { * } ) }$ which both oxidize organic compounds and react with other species to produce further oxidants32 . Since the labile rate constants appeared to reflect a property of samples that was shared between PARAFAC components, this implies an important role of the sample matrix in determining $k _ { L } ,$ potentially via different relative concentrations of ${ } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { D O M } ^ { * }$ precursors36, and/or different $\mathrm { ^ 3 D O M } ^ { \ast }$ compositions and reactivities32, 37. For both $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 5 0 }$ and $\mathrm { F } _ { 5 2 0 } .$ , a weak positive correlation $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 3 2$ , $\mathrm { F } { = } 4 . 7 1$ , df $= 1 0$ , $\mathrm { p { = } 0 . 0 5 5 }$ between DOC and $k _ { L }$ across the dataset provides potential support for a link between $k _ { L }$ and DOM concentration. In contrast, the semi-labile rate constants were independent of sample matrices and may therefore represent direct photodegradation rates for the respective components under simulated solar irradiation. Further experiments are however warranted to establish the interpretation of the decay rates beyond reasonable doubt.

The observed high statistical congruence between fluorescence spectra and decay behaviours in different samples despite variable DOM composition, and varying chemical and analytical interferences, provides strong evidence that the data conformed to a trilinear structure for which the PARAFAC decomposition model was appropriate. The structural origin of the ubiquitous spectra is unknown, but it is likely that there is a pool of organic compounds or

269 We suspect this is because $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 5 0 }$ and $\mathrm { F } _ { 5 2 0 }$ have rather similar photodegradation rates (Fig. 5),

273 routinely produce models that are under-specified. Furthermore, in most statistical   
274 comparisons between pairs of samples, congruence between emission spectra was   
275 systematically higher than between excitation spectra (SI: S5). This is also evident from   
276 emission peak positions, which cluster tightly across the dataset in contrast to excitation peak   
277 positions (Fig. 2). While the diversity in excitation spectra may reflect a range of factors   
278 including greater susceptibility to matrix effects, as suggested by experiments at varying pH   
279 (SI: S4), it may also result from other artefacts as discussed below.   
280 While samples in this study were dominated by humic-like signals and four components   
281 explained nearly all measured fluorescence variation, additional components certainly exist   
282 and for specific samples or in specific environments, could explain a substantial proportion of   
283 measured signals. Although careful wavelength selection enabled the PARAFAC   
284 decomposition of most datasets we tested, surface ocean samples typically failed to produce   
285 stable models, presumably due to a fatal combination of previous exposure to sunlight, low   
286 initial fluorescence intensities and dominant UV-A fluorescence. UV-A fluorescence signals   
287 $\mathrm { E m } _ { \mathrm { m a x } } { < } 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , ‘protein-like’) are common in biologically productive environments but in   
288 our lake and river datasets usually appeared only as a shoulder on the $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 0 0 }$ component.   
289 Abundant UV-A signals could present a significant challenge for PARAFAC by increasing   
290 mathematical complexity at wavelengths where spectra are short and steep but data are   
291 relatively few (Fig. 6) and affected by Rayleigh and Raman scatter 8. Additionally, short   
292 excitation wavelengths have a disproportionate influence on models due to high absorbances   
293 combined with low measurement precision, producing high statistical leverages. Should it be   
294 possible to presume the spectral properties of some DOM components under a ubiquity   
295 hypothesis, this would create new opportunities for interpreting challenging samples   
296 containing many overlapping signals. For example, the Pantanal Lake 12 bulk sample had a   
297 prominent peak at $3 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and produced no distinct PARAFAC solution if varying the   
298 excitation wavelength range (SI: S7). However, the Pantanal Lake 12 dataset could be   
299 decomposed using the model from Pantanal Lake 3, which is located approx. $8 0 \mathrm { k m }$ away.   
300 When the spectra for three ‘ubiquitous’ Lake 3 components were presumed to be present in   
301 Lake 12 and PARAFAC was instructed to calculated two further components, this recovered   
302 the spectra for the missing ‘ubiquitous’ Lake 3 component plus a feasible UV-A component.   
303 These results have important implications for interpreting DOM fluorescence in aquatic   
304 samples. Although visible wavelength (‘humic-like’) DOM fluorescence peaks are still   
305 routinely assigned to ‘terrestrial’ sources, a significant body of research indicates that visible   
306 wavelength fluorescence is also produced in the absence of terrestrial sources or humification   
307 processes45, 46 . Our results extend this with a demonstration that terrestrially- and   
308 autochthonously-derived visible fluorescence spectra can be statistically indistinguishable   
309 with nearly identical semi-labile photodegradation rate constants. In all samples regardless of   
310 origin or preparation method, components emitting at long emission wavelengths were   
311 photodegraded fastest, explaining why DOM fluorescence invariably shifts toward   
312 increasingly shorter emission wavelengths upon photo-irradiation 15, 47 . Over time, this erodes   
313 compositional differences between samples that might once have been attributable to source   
314 or biogeochemical processing. In open systems containing spatially-variable DOM inputs, the   
315 faster photodegradation of $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 5 0 }$ and $\mathrm { F } _ { 5 2 0 }$ might easily be misconstrued as biological production   
316 of $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 0 0 }$ and $\mathrm { F } _ { 4 2 0 }$ . For diverse samples in the current dataset, exposure to $2 0 \mathrm { h }$ artificial solar   
317 irradiation caused contradictory changes in commonly-reported fluorescence indices (SI: S8).   
318 The fluorescence index, FI, decreased by $4 \mathrm { - } 1 0 \%$ indicating that irradiated samples appeared   
319 increasingly like ‘terrestrial’ DOM, while the freshness index, BIX, increased by $7 . 3 7 \%$   
320 indicating that samples became more ‘autochthonous’ in character26 .   
321 Fluorescence spectroscopy is very widely used in studies of DOM biochemistry, and   
322 PARAFAC is often used during data interpretation28 . Many previous studies reported that for   
323 various data subsets, PARAFAC components in different samples responded differently to   
324 various environmental gradients10 , often explaining this by differences in DOM source. While   
325 this may occur, it is often overlooked that due to PARAFAC trilinearity, systematic biases in   
326 model fits are accompanied by systematic biases in model scores. The corollary to this is that   
327 if a model does not represent all samples equally well, scores will be affected creating the   
328 appearance of systematic differences even when none are present. This is especially likely   
329 when representing diverse groups of samples with a single PARAFAC model. However,   
330 despite limitations with traditional multi-sample PARAFAC approaches, it should be   
331 remembered that all data interpretation approaches that fail to reliably separate overlapping   
332 fluorescence signals will suffer from similar shortcomings.   
333 This study shows that PARAFAC components in DOM EEMs appear to be highly conserved   
334 across diverse samples and some of these components respond to environmental stresses (e.g.,   
335 photobleaching) in predictable ways. Further studies of individual samples subject to   
336 controlled environmental gradients are needed both to validate and refine these results,   
337 leading to improved interpretations of DOM fate and transformation as assessed by   
338 fluorescence spectroscopy. Carefully-controlled experiments are also needed to delineate   
339 relevant chemical variation attributable to e.g. precursor material and biogeochemical   
340 processing, from methodological decisions such as sample pre-treatment, or experimental   
341 (measurement and modelling) error. Finally, future efforts should determine how best to   
342 integrate accumulated knowledge of ubiquitous spectra into data analyses. For example,   
343 should certain components be truly ubiquitous, then their presence might be assumed allowing   
344 their signals to be subtracted. This could improve the mathematical decomposition of complex   
345 samples and may reveal low-abundance fluorescence tracers that are presently impossible to   
346 isolate using traditional approaches, including photochemically- and microbially-labile   
347 components not captured in this study.
