# Abstract

Surface water samples were collected from 43 streams distributed throughout watersheds of mixed land use in southern Ontario, Canada. Absorbance and fluorescence spectroscopy with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was used to characterize dissolved organic matter (DOM). DOM characteristics were related to environmental variables, microbial activity indicators (bacterial production and extracellular leucine aminopeptidase activity), and riparian land use to understand better how these factors influence DOM in streams. PARAFAC produced a six-component model (C1 to C6). Temperature correlated with each PARAFAC component, suggesting that water source, drainage area, and light penetration broadly affected DOM characteristics. C1 and C2 represented terrestrial, humic-like DOM fluorophore groups and comprised $41 \%$ of stream DOM fluorescence. C5, a tryptophan-like component, related negatively to a humification index but positively to leucine-aminopeptidase activity and recently produced DOM, suggesting that C5 consisted of autochthonous, microbially produced DOM. C3, C4, and C6 showed signs of quinone-like, humic-like, and microbial transformable fluorophores. The distribution of these potentially redox-active PARAFAC components indicated that DOM was in a more reduced state in streams with higher bacterial production and agricultural land use than in streams with increased wetlands area, which had greater relative abundance of the oxidized quinone-like component. Anthropogenic land use and microbial activity altered the quantity and quality of DOM exported from human-affected streams from that observed in forest- and wetland-dominated streams. DOM in agriculturally affected streams was likely more labile and accessible to the microbial community than DOM in wetland streams, which supported low rates of microbial activity.

Carbon cycling in stream ecosystems is driven by the interplay of watershed land use, morphology, and microbial activity. These processes in turn shape the quantity and quality of dissolved organic matter (DOM) present in streams. Land use influences the quantity of terrestrial organic and inorganic matter subsidies introduced to stream ecosystems as well as autochthonous DOM production and cycling (Baker and Spencer 2004; Wiegner and Seitzinger 2004; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). Agricultural and anthropogenic land uses tend to increase nitrogen and phosphorus loading and concentration in streams above levels observed in streams with more natural forest and wetland covers (Boyer et al. 2002; Mattsson et al. 2009). In aquatic ecosystems, elevated inorganic nutrient levels are associated often with enhanced bacterial production (BP), which in turn can increase DOM decomposition (Reche et al. 1998). Thus, land use and the associated physical habitat of a stream reach can shape DOM characteristics and concentration in streams both directly by influencing terrestrial loading and indirectly by influencing stream autochthonous production and organic matter processing rates. Likewise, DOM source, quantity, and quality in their own rights can directly regulate aquatic BP and organic matter cycling (Findlay et al. 2001; Lennon and Cottingham 2008). However, a comprehensive understanding of how land use, BP, and organic matter modification interact to influence DOM characteristics is lacking in stream ecosystems.

In streams, the majority of DOM originates in the terrestrial landscape, but DOM is also produced in situ by micro- and macroheterotrophs, algae, and aquatic macrophytes. Determination of the optical properties of DOM enables inferences to be made regarding its source, quantity, and character (Blough and Del Vecchio 2002; Jaffe´ et al. 2008). As examples, DOM ultraviolet (UV)- visible absorbance and fluorescent properties have been used to identify humic-, amino acid–, quinone-, and microbial-like compounds (Cory and McKnight 2005), lignin content (McKnight et al. 2001), new production (Coble et al. 1998; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009), and aromaticity (Wieshaar et al. 2003) of organic matter in multiple aquatic environments.

UV-visible optical properties of DOM have been used successfully to identify some changes in terrestrial, microbial, and anthropogenic characteristics of DOM with land use (Baker and Spencer 2004; Stedmon and Markager

2005; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). Excitation–emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence coupled with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) is a new tool that can be used to resolve and quantify distinct DOM fluorophore groups (Stedmon and Markager 2005). For example, in a Danish estuary terrestrial humic-like components were observed in forest and agricultural areas but UVA-absorbing humiclike components were more abundant in agricultural and waste water treatment areas, suggesting terrestrial and anthropogenic influences on DOM (Stedmon and Markager 2005). In southern Ontario, Canada, streams, Wilson and Xenopoulos (2009) observed significant positive relationships between microbial humic-like DOM, total dissolved nitrogen, and agricultural land use, supporting a hypothesized nutrient-driven microbial transformation and/or production of DOM. However, it remains unclear if changes in DOM characteristics associated with land use are due to modifications of terrestrial sources of DOM, autochthonous modification and production of DOM, or both.

In the current study, we investigated ecosystem processes that potentially regulate DOM characteristics (UV-visible optical properties) in southern Ontario streams along a gradient of agriculture land use. We explored how land use and in situ microbial activity (leucine aminopeptidase activity and BP) influence the formation and transformation of DOM. We tested the hypothesis that the optical characteristics of DOM are influenced by microbial activity and land use, where increased agricultural and human land use leads to an increased microbial signature (i.e., DOM that was produced directly or modified by the stream microbial community) on the stream DOM pool.

# Methods

Land use in southern Ontario—The study streams range in catchment size from 10.4 to $9 9 2 . 6 \ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ and are located south of the Canadian Shield in mixed land use areas of southern Ontario. The surficial geology of each catchment comprises a mixture of sand, gravel, glacial till, clay, and organic deposited soils and sediments. In southern Ontario streams, catchment land use is highly correlated with that in the riparian zone $( r ~ = ~ 0 . 8 7 – 0 . 9 6 )$ and, as such, only riparian land uses (as a percentage of total land use and at a $1 0 0 – \mathrm { m }$ stream buffer; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2008) are presented here. The main land use types in southern Ontario as classified by the Southern Ontario Interim Landcover geospatial data set are monoculture cropland (continuously farmed annual crop; riparian coverage range, $1 . 2 \mathrm { - } 5 0 . 2 \% )$ , mixed cropland (rotationally farmed annual crop; $0 . 2 { - } 3 5 . 9 \% )$ , forage crop and idle land $( 2 . 1 \mathrm { - } 4 1 . 2 \% )$ , woodland $( 1 . 8 \mathrm { - } 5 0 . 3 \% )$ , and wetland $( 0 . 0 4 { - } 6 8 . 4 \% )$ .

In this study, wetland area was correlated negatively with monoculture cropland $( r = - 0 . 7 6 )$ and forage crop and idle land $( r ~ = ~ - 0 . 5 8 )$ . Monoculture cropland was correlated positively with forage crop and idle land $( r =$ 0.52). Mixed cropland was correlated negatively with woodland area $( r = - 0 . 4 5 )$ . Otherwise, land use categories were unrelated statistically. Monoculture cropland was spatially autocorrelated (i.e., distributed patchily) but simple and partial Mantel test results indicated that this spatial autocorrelation did not influence regression and correlation analysis results. Thus, when used individually, land use type can be related meaningfully to environmental, microbial, and DOM optical data.

Water collection and storage—During September 2008, water samples were collected from $\sim 1 0$ cm below the water’s surface at 52 streams located throughout southern Ontario. Each stream sampling point comprised an independent watershed and drainage basin. Each stream was sampled once in order to construct a snapshot of DOM characteristics for the stream network. Of the 52 streams in our sampling network, data from 43 streams are presented here (Fig. 1). Nine streams are omitted from this report because these streams were located in a rapidly expanding urban area or were influenced by the Canadian Shield formation and, thus, had quite different landscape and environmental characteristics than the remaining 43 streams.

In the laboratory, triplicate whole water samples were prepared for microbial activity incubations. In addition, whole water was filtered serially through prerinsed Whatman $0 . 7 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ GF/F and $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate membrane filters. Particle-free filtrates were then stored in acidwashed sample prerinsed $1 0 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ amber glass bottles at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and in the dark until optical chemistry and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) were measured within 1–4 weeks of sampling.

Environmental and microbial variables—Water temperature ${ } ^ { ( \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C ) } }$ was measured in situ with a YSI 30/10 FT handheld probe. DOC $( \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ was measured using an O. I. Analytical Aurora Model 1030 total organic carbon analyzer in combustion mode. Extracellular leucine aminopeptidase activity (AMA) is a biologically relevant microbial enzyme that can transform the DOM pool by cleaving N-terminus amino acids from peptide chains for microbial carbon and nitrogen uptake. AMA was determined following the fluorogenic model substrate kinetic method using L-leucine-7-amido-4-methylcoumarin (Chro´ st and Rai 1993). Triplicate whole water samples were incubated for $^ \textrm { \scriptsize 4 b }$ at 5, 25, 75, and $2 0 0 \phantom { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } \mathrm { \bar { L } } ^ { - 1 }$ final substrate concentrations and the resulting extracellular hydrolysis rates were fit to the Michaelis-Menten model to estimate potential maximum aminopeptidase hydrolysis rates (nmol $\mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { ~ h } ^ { - 1 }$ ). BP $( \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { ~ C ~ L ^ { - 1 } ~ d ^ { - 1 } ) }$ was measured following the standard $^ 3 \mathrm { H }$ -leucine incorporation centrifuge method (Kirchman 2001). AMA and BP incubations were conducted in the laboratory within $^ { \textrm { \scriptsize 8 h } }$ of sample collection and incubated at room temperature $( 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ and in the dark. These activity estimates are assumed to reflect in situ rates of bacterial biomass production and peptide enzyme hydrolysis, though sample handling and incubation conditions might have caused a slight overestimation of bacterial activities.

Optical measurements and analysis—UV-visible absorbance spectra were measured between 200 and $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (Perkin Elmer Lambda 25 spectrophotometer). The specific UV absorbance $\left( \mathbf { S } \mathbf { U } \mathbf { V } \mathbf { A } _ { 2 5 4 } \right)$ was determined as the absorbance at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m \ ( m ^ { - 1 } ) }$ divided by the DOC concentration $( \mathrm { m g ~ C ~ L ^ { - 1 } } )$ and was used as a surrogate for DOM aromaticity (Weishaar et al. 2003). Iron absorbs light at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and as such, iron concentrations $> 0 . 5 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ can cause erroneous interpretations of $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values (Weishaar et al. 2003). However, previously determined dissolved iron concentrations across southern Ontario streams were $\leq 0 . 3 3 \mathrm { \ m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ (Kelton et al. 2007), levels that should not bias $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ results.

EEM measurements were made using fluorescence spectroscopy (Varian Cary Eclipse Fluorometer) set to 5- nm bandwidths for excitation and emission at an integration time of $0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { s }$ . Emission scans were made over a range of emission $( 2 7 0 - 6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { ~ n m }$ at $2 { \cdot } \mathrm { n m }$ increments) and excitation $2 3 0 { - } 5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 { \mathrm { - } } \mathrm { n m }$ increments) wavelengths. EEMs were corrected for inner filter effects (Mobed et al. 1996; McKnight et al. 2001) and, using the manufacturerrecommended instrument settings, second-order Raman and Rayleigh scatter effects. Milli-Q water blank EEM fluorescence was subtracted from that of sample EEM, and EEMs were converted to Raman units (RU) using the area under the Milli-Q Raman scatter peak at excitation $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ .

To explore environmentally relevant fluorescence parameters, EEM data were used selectively to calculate humification index (HIX), fluorescence index (FI), and $\beta : \alpha$ ratio. All indices were calculated from corrected fluorescence data. HIX was calculated from excitation $2 5 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ as the ratio of the peak area under each curve at emissions $4 3 4 - 4 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 6 \ \mathrm { ~ n m }$ (Zsolnay et al. 1999). HIX is directly proportional to the humic content of DOM, where HIX values around 1–2 are associated with non-humified plant material and values $> 1 0$ are commonly reported for fulvic acid extracts (Zsolnay et al. 1999; Ohno 2002). FI was calculated from excitation $3 7 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ as the ratio of emission intensities at $4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (McKnight et al. 2001). FI is inversely related to the lignin content of DOM, where values around 1.3 suggest a dominant terrestrial, higher-plant DOM source and values around 1.8 suggest a predominant microbial DOM source (McKnight et al. 2001). The $\beta : \alpha$ ratio was calculated at excitation $3 1 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ from emission intensity at $3 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ( $\beta$ region) divided by the emission intensity maximum observed between 420 and $4 3 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ( $\mathscr { X }$ region; Parlanti et al. 2000; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). The $\beta : \alpha$ ratio is used to access the relative contribution of recently produced DOM to highly degraded DOM (Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). Higher fluorescence intensities in the $\beta$ region have been associated with the rapid degradation or transformation of microbially derived, protein-like DOM, whereas fluorescence intensity in the $\mathscr { X }$ region is controlled by loading of more highly degraded humic-like compounds (Huguet et al. 2009; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). In incubation experiments containing labile DOM, the $\beta : \alpha$ ratio tends to decrease over longer time periods and as labile DOM is reduced (Parlanti et al. 2000; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). $\beta : \alpha$ values $> 1$ indicate that DOM is primarily of autochthonous origin and values $< ~ 0 . 6$ indicate primarily allochthonous origin (Huguet et al. 2009).

To examine the complete EEM data, PARAFAC was used to reduce matrix data into discrete components. PARAFAC was conducted following the recommendation of Stedmon and Bro (2008) using the DOMFluor toolbox in MATLAB 7.0.4 (Mathworks). Prior to PARAFAC, EEM wavelength ranges were reduced to excitation $2 5 0 -$ $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and emission $3 0 0 { \ - } 6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and Rayleigh scatter was deleted from EEMs. PARAFAC generated a six-component model using EEMs from our complete stream network $( n = 5 2 )$ , where we present the PARAFAC model results for the 43 focal streams. The six-component model (C1–C6) was validated using the split-half and random initialization methods (Fig. 2; Stedmon and Bro 2008). The model produced a fluorescence maximum score $\mathrm { \Delta F _ { m a x } }$ ; RU) for each component at each station. To reduce the influence of DOC concentration on model scores, we present the relative distribution of $\mathrm { F } _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ as the percentage of $\mathrm { F } _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ $( \% \mathrm { F _ { m a x } } )$ for each component at each station (i.e., $[ \mathrm { F _ { \mathrm { m a x } } C _ { \# } } / \sum { F _ { \mathrm { m a x } } } \mathrm { C _ { 1 - 6 } } ] \times 1 \bar { 0 } 0 \% )$ .

Statistical analyses—Prior to statistical analysis, most data were ln, square-root, or fourth-root transformed to meet the assumptions of normality and homoscedasticity. Pearson’s bivariate correlation $( r )$ and linear regression analyses $( R ^ { 2 } )$ were used to distinguish significant $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ relationships between DOM optical properties, land use, and microbial activity. For PARAFAC components, correlation and regression analyses were conducted on relative abundance scores $( \% \mathrm { F _ { m a x } } )$ and univariate linear regression residual scores for the relationships between each component and temperature (relationships and equations not shown). Temperature was found to be a good predictor of the relative abundance of most PARAFAC components $R ^ { 2 }$ ranged between 0.27 and 0.58). The residual scores of these relationships were used to account for the variability explained by temperature, which allowed land use and microbial activity influences on PARAFAC components to be interpreted independently of ecosystem controls on temperature.

# Results

Environmental and microbial variables—During September 2008, temperature and DOC ranged between $1 1 . 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 0 . 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and between 4.1 and $2 6 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { ~ C ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ , respectively (Fig. 3a,b). AMA and BP ranged between 16 and 567 nmol $\mathrm { h } ^ { - 1 } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ and 0.8 and $4 2 9 . 3 \ \mu \mathrm { g } \ \mathrm { ~ C ~ } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \ \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively (Fig. 3c,d). Temperature was unrelated to microbial activity. DOC correlated negatively with AMA $( r = - 0 . 4 6 )$ but was unrelated to BP. Microbial activity tended to be higher in streams with increased anthropogenic land use relative to activities measured in streams with more pristine land cover.

UV-visible absorbance and fluorescence indices—HIX values ranged between 8.7 and 32.2, which was in the range of values previously reported for DOM with a principal humic nature (Fig. 3e). $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ranged between 1.82 and 3.77, suggesting that the stream DOM pool was a mixture of aromatic humic-like substances and other compounds (Weishaar et al. 2003; Maie et al. 2006). HIX values increased with DOC concentration $( r = 0 . 7 4 )$ ), DOM aromaticity $\mathrm { ( S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , $r \ = \ 0 . 7 3$ , and the proportion of riparian wetland area $( r ~ = ~ 0 . 4 5$ ; Table 1). HIX and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ did not correlate with agricultural and human land uses. BP was not related to HIX or $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ suggesting that microbial production did not have a detectible influence on stream humic content or aromaticity. Still, AMA correlated negatively with HIX, which might indicate that streams with higher humic content did not provide suitable substrate for this enzyme system and/ or that microbial demand for carbon and nitrogen was lower in streams with high HIX values.

FI ranged between 1.19 and 1.47, but most streams had relatively low FI values indicative of a predominantly terrestrially derived, lignin-rich DOM (Fig. 3g). A few streams, which flowed through areas with increased anthropogenic land use, had higher FI values, reflecting an autochthonous source of DOM. $\beta : \mathfrak { a }$ ratios correlated positively with FI $\mathit { \Delta } _ { r } = 0 . 5 4$ ; Table 1; Fig. 3h). FI and $\beta : \alpha$ ratio were lower in streams with a greater fraction of riparian wetland area and higher in streams with increased riparian monoculture cropland and BP (Table 1). Streams with a high proportion of wetland area were associated with humic-rich DOM and low levels of water column microbial activity and microbial-like DOM. Streams with anthropogenically modified riparian areas had increased rates of microbial activity and an increased recently produced microbial signature to the DOM pool.

C1 and C2 were the most abundant and least variable features of stream DOM, whereas C6 had the lowest relative abundance (Table 2). The relative abundances of C3, C4, and C5 varied greatly across streams. All components, except C5, formed UV and a visible excitation peaks, suggesting the presence of humic- and/or fulvic-like fluorophores within these components (Coble 1996). When compared to EEM fluorophore peaks assigned by Coble et al. (1990, 1998), peaks A and C were present in C1, C3, and C4 and peaks A and M were visible in C2 (Table 2). Peak E, representing soil–fulvic acid, was observed in C6. Tryptophan-like and recently produced fluorophores (peaks T and N) were observed in C5.

The relative abundance of all PARAFAC components $( \% \mathrm { F _ { m a x } } )$ correlated significantly with temperature (Table 3). Water temperature is influenced by water source and rainfall within the stream network but is also influenced by light penetration, land use, water residence time, and catchment size, among others. Determining the direct controls on stream temperature was outside the scope of this study, but the conditions that drove temperature also affected DOM characteristics in southern Ontario streams. To account for these stochastic influences, the residuals of the relationships between temperature and PARAFAC components were used to examine the variability in each PARAFAC component not explained by temperature. Other than HIX, microbial activities, optical indices, and land uses did not covary with temperature in PARAFAC component regression models, and as such, this strategy allowed the independent influences of the above variables on PARAFAC components to be addressed.

C3, C4, and C6 had optical characteristics similar to quinone-like components identified by Cory and McKnight (2005) and observed in many aquatic ecosystems (Jaffe´ et al. 2008). With a large excitation peak well within the UVhumic range, C3 was most similar to oxidized quinone-like fluorophores (Q), whereas C4 and C6 showed characteristics of semiquinone-like fluorophores (SQ; Table 2). C4 had a sharp UV excitation peak that seemed characteristic of reduced quinone-like fluorophores (Miller et al. 2006). Cory and McKnight (2005) found a strong negative relationship between FI and the ratio of terrestrial-derived SQ to terrestrial- and microbial-derived SQ (SQ1 : [SQ1 and SQ2], $R ^ { 2 } ~ = ~ 0 . 8 4 )$ . In the current study, this ratio was approximated as C6 (terrestrial; SQ1) to C6 and C4 (microbial; SQ3), which related negatively to FI $R ^ { 2 } \ =$ 0.32, $p \ < \ 0 . 0 0 1 $ ). The strength of our relationship was weaker because of nonquinone-like fluorophores present in C4 and C6. For example, the emission peak $\sim 5 2 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ of C6 overlapped with that of soil fulvic acid (Coble et al. 1990) and SQ1 (Cory and McKnight 2005). The dominant excitation split peak (280, $2 5 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ) of C6 overlapped with that of SQ1, whereas the third excitation peak $( 4 4 5 \ \mathrm { n m } )$ of C6 blanketed that of soil fulvic acid (Table 2; Fig. 2). Thus, C6 seemed to include fluorophore characteristics of both soil-derived fulvic acid and terrestrial, semiquinone-like components.

In southern Ontario streams, the relative distribution of C3 was inversely related to that of C4 $( r ~ = ~ - 0 . 9 9 )$ , suggesting linked input to removal and/or transformation mechanisms for these aspects of DOM. C3 was higher in streams with increased wetland area and lower BP rates (Table 3). Residuals with temperature of C3, C4, and C6 were related strongly to wetland area, monoculture cropland, forage and idle lands, and BP (Table 3; Fig. 5). In addition, C6 correlated positively with HIX and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , echoing its terrestrial-like nature (Table 3). Taken together, the oxidized quinone-like component (C3) was relatively more abundant when BP was low, whereas the opposite was true of semiquinone-like components (C4 and C6). In addition, reduced quinone-like components were relatively more abundant in streams with increased agricultural and human land use and reduced wetland area, where the opposite was true of the oxidized quinone-like component.

# Discussion

Across southern Ontario streams, BP and AMA tended to increase in streams with a higher proportion of anthropogenic land use and reduced contributions of wetlands. At the same time, microbial-like and reduced quinone-like DOM characteristics, which are associated frequently with labile and bioavailable organic matter, became relatively more abundant. Agricultural and other human land uses in stream riparian zones acted to increase DOM processing rates in streams from those observed in streams with more natural land uses, which created a positive feedback loop that likely promoted the availability of and increased processing rates on the stream DOM pool.

Characteristics of DOM—We found that DOC correlated with HIX but not with FI or $\beta : \alpha$ ratio, suggesting that much of stream DOC originated from humic-like terrestrial material. Our DOC, FI, and $\beta : \alpha$ ratio values were within the range previously reported for southern Ontario streams (Kelton et al. 2007; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). However, indicators of aromatic humic-like material (HIX and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } ,$ were higher in the current study than in past work in the region (Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). This was likely attributable to the fact that in southern Ontario, September and generally the summer of 2008 were much wetter than normal with higher than average soil moisture levels (Agriculture and AgriFood Canada unpubl. data). In addition, the wet conditions likely influenced the strength of correlation and regression relationships between environmental variables and land use. Wilson and Xenopoulos (2009) found that regression coefficients between DOC and land use tended to decrease when soil moisture increased from normal conditions.

UV-visible optical indices indicated that DOM in southern Ontario streams was primarily of terrestrial origin. The interquartile ranges of HIX, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , and FI were well within the range of values reported as terrestrially derived for several aquatic ecosystems (Fig. 3; McKnight et al. 2001; Hood et al. 2006; Jaffe´ et al. 2008). DOM derived from terrestrial environments typically has higher aromatic (higher $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 , }$ and lignin (low FI) content than microbially derived DOM, which lacks lignin, is less aromatic, and is more aliphatic (i.e., low $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and high FI). Among 12 Long-Term Ecological Research locations in the United States, FI and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ correlate negatively $( r ~ = ~ - 0 . 5 7 )$ , suggesting widespread links between aquatic DOM aromaticity and lignin content (Jaffe´ et al. 2008). This relationship was evident across southern Ontario streams but was weaker $( r ~ = ~ - 0 . 3 7 )$ because of the narrow range in $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and FI observed in the current study and the strongly terrestrial nature of DOM across the stream network.

FI and $\beta : \alpha$ ratio correlated positively with in situ BP (Table 1), indicating the presence of microbially derived DOM in aquatic ecosystems (McKnight et al. 2001; Maie et al. 2006; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). However, these indices described different aspects of the DOM pool, which were sensitive to microbial activity. As such, the FI and $\beta : \alpha$ ratio related differently to PARAFAC components and other optical indices. The $\beta : \alpha$ ratio was inversely related to humic content of DOM (HIX, $r = - 0 . 8 4 )$ but increased with the microbial-derived and protein-like fluorophore group (C5, $r = 0 . 8 3$ ). These results support the hypothesis of Wilson and Xenopoulos (2009) that high $\beta : \alpha$ ratios represent recently produced, microbial-derived DOM. On the other hand, FI correlated $( r = 0 . 3 9  – 0 . 4 8 )$ with quinonelike PARAFAC components C3 and C4, suggesting that FI was responsive to the redox state and/or source of DOM (Table 3; Cory and McKnight 2005; Miller et al. 2006).

EEM-PARAFAC produced six components that had humic-, fulvic-, amino acid–, and/or quinone-like fluorophore properties of terrestrial and autochthonous origin (Fig. 2; Table 2). C1 (a terrestrial, humic-like component) correlated strongly with HIX across southern Ontario streams. An analogous relationship was observed previously in the Occoquan watershed (Holbrook et al. 2006), supporting further the overall humic nature of this component. Similarly, C2 (a terrestrial, humic-like component) was reported previously in a diverse set of aquatic ecosystems (Cory and McKnight 2005; Stedmon and Markager 2005; Jaffe´ et al. 2008). The persistence of C2- like PARAFAC components in many aquatic ecosystems and the fact that C2 did not correlate with microbial activities suggested that C2 is likely a bio-refractory component of the DOM pool. In contrast, C3 (an oxidized quinone-like component), C4 (a reduced semiquinone-like component), C5 (a tryptophan-like component), and C6 (a reduced semiquinone- and soil fulvic acid–like component) were related to microbial activities, autochthonous-linked fluorescence indices ( $\beta : \alpha$ ratio), and/or riparian land use categories (Tables 2 and 3). These aspects of the DOM pool are discussed further to resolve the manor in which microbial activity and land use influenced the optical characteristics of stream DOM.

The influence of land use and microbial activity on DOM— In southern Ontario, agricultural areas are converted from forest and wetland areas, and thus soil organic matter might have a residual wetland and forest quality, which can influence stream DOC concentration and DOM characteristics (Creed et al. 2003; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2008). Moreover, each stream sampled in the current study contained forest and/or wetland riparian areas as well as agricultural and human land uses. As such, PARAFAC components that are present in both wetland and forest areas should also be present in agricultural areas if they are not photo- and/or biodegraded. Conversely, if DOM produced in agricultural areas is distinct from that of natural systems, then fluorophores introduced in the agricultural areas should be less abundant in streams with a high proportion of wetland and/or forest area. Therefore, the relationships observed for DOM optical characteristics and land use (Table 3) could have been caused by altered terrestrial sources of DOM, photodegradation of DOM, and/or microbial transformation and production of DOM.

EEM-PARAFAC modeling and optical indices indicate both terrestrial and autochthonous DOM inputs. DOM with autochthonous optical characteristics was relatively more abundant in streams with greater microbial activity and/or anthropogenic land use. The protein-like PARAFAC component (C5) was present in each stream but its relative abundance was always less than $20 \%$ of the total PARAFAC component fluorescence. C5 correlated negatively with HIX and wetland area but positively with AMA and $\beta : \alpha$ ratio (Table 3). Protein-like fluorophores are thought to be both produced and consumed by microorganisms (Determann et al. 1998; Cammack et al. 2004; Balcarczyk et al. 2009) and these tend to increase in reaches influenced by anthropogenic activity and/or with higher rates of autochthonous production (Baker and Spencer 2004; Hudson et al. 2007; Jaffe´ et al. 2008). The positive relationship between C5 and AMA supports further the link between microbial activity and protein-like components of the DOM pool, where these fluorophores might be produced directly from extracellular peptide hydrolysis or provide suitable substrate that promotes extracellular aminopeptidase enzyme synthesis and activity. Thus, C5 in addition to $\beta : \alpha$ ratio reflected recently produced DOM signatures in aquatic ecosystems, which appeared to originate at least in part through autochthonous BP and extracellular enzyme activity.

Terrestrial-like DOM optical signatures were observed in streams passing through all land use types, but the redox state of a fraction of the DOM pool was affected by agricultural and human land use in the stream’s catchment. The oxidized quinone-like component was more abundant in wetland-influenced streams and the reduced semiquinone-like components were relatively more abundant in streams with increased agricultural land use and BP (Fig. 5). These differences in redox state of the organic matter seemed to be due to dissimilar physical and biological conditions across streams of different anthropogenic and natural land use. If these redox components were produced within a specific land use type, then the relative abundance of each component should be weighted by its land use source. However, this was not observed. The relative abundance of reduced quinone-like components did not appear strictly tied to a land use type, but appeared to be coupled with the disappearance of the oxidized quinone-like component (i.e., C3 and C4 appeared to act as a quinone–hydroquinone couple), which occurred when microbial activity was higher in anthropogenically influenced streams.

In addition to microbial activity, light penetration is expected to increase in agricultural streams because of reductions in tree cover within the catchment. Light can reduce the molecular weight of DOM through direct photodegradation and by stimulating autochthonous production of protein-like components (Moran and Zepp 1997; Obernosterer and Benner 2004; Cory et al. 2007). Moreover, increased autochthonous primary production in combination with light exposure has been observed to promote the formation of oxidized quinone-like DOM moieties in aquatic systems (Miller et al. $2 0 0 9 a )$ ). Conversely, light exposure can decrease FI and the relative abundance of a microbially derived SQ component (i.e., SQ2), but terrestrially derived SQ (i.e., SQ1) and tryptophan-like components are less susceptible to photodegradation (Cory et al. 2007). In our study, tryptophan-like and semiquinone-like (both microbial and terrestrial) PARAFAC components and FI increased in streams that were expected to have greater light penetration (i.e., anthropogenically modified watersheds). Our oxidized quinonelike component increased with wetland influence, where highly colored, humic DOM and vegetation are expected to reduce stream light penetration. The reduced contribution of C5 in wetland streams was consistent with expectations based on light penetration, land use, and microbial activity, but the relative abundance of potentially redox-active PARAFAC components were inconsistent with the effect of light on DOM alone. FI values and the relative abundance of terrestrial (C6) and microbial (C4) semiquinone-like PARAFAC components increased with BP and agricultural land use, suggesting microbial-linked transformation of these PARAFAC components. Light-mediated DOM processes might also influence negatively extracellular enzyme activity in high light streams because photodegradation products (i.e., low-molecular-weight DOM, reactive oxygen, and inorganic nutrients) can inhibit enzymatic activity. However, across our stream network, extracellular enzyme activities did not relate to the relative molecular weight of DOM or catchment drainage area, but increased in areas expected to have greater light penetration based on land use coverage (C.J.W. et al. unpubl. data). We cannot rule out photodegradation as an important control on DOM quantity, character, and bioavailability in our study, but these comparisons suggest that the microbial activity within these streams is an important control on DOM optical characteristics.

The influence of microbial activity on the redox state of DOM—In natural forest and wetland streams, reduced humic-like DOM is expected to be introduced in the wetland and transported into the main stream body, where it is oxidized (Miller et al. 2006). Our results, however, did not conform to these expectations, though longitudinal changes in stream DOM chemistry were not investigated here. In our study, reduced semiquinone-like DOM PARAFAC components (C4 and C6) increased with anthropogenic land use and BP, whereas oxidized quinone-like DOM (C3) increased with increased wetland riparian area (Table 3; Fig. 5). At a broad spatial scale rather than longitudinally along a stream, the redox state of DOM realized by EEM-PARAFAC can vary greatly and the oxidation state of the DOM does not always covary with its suspected source (Jaffe´ et al. 2008). Across the southern Ontario stream network, the reduced, microbial and the terrestrial, soil fulvic acid semiquinone-like PARAFAC components related similarly to riparian land uses and in situ BP, suggesting that the oxidation state of these DOM moieties might be controlled by complex ecosystem scale factors independent of DOM source.

To explore our redox–DOM results further, we calculated the redox index (RI) as proposed by Miller et al. (2006), which represents the ratio of reduced quinone-like PARAFAC components to reduced and oxidized quinonelike components. Here, RI was calculated as the ratio of C4 plus C6 to the sum of C3, C4, and C6. Typical values of RI across stream ecosystems have yet to be resolved fully, but for alpine areas in Colorado, U.S.A., RI values average 0.39 and 0.51 in forest streams and adjacent wetlands, respectively (Miller et al. 2006). In the southern Ontario streams with $\geq 3 0 \%$ riparian wetland area, the average RI value (0.50) was similar to that reported for Colorado wetlands. In our study, RI related negatively to wetland area $( R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 2 3 , p = 0 . { \dot { 0 } } 0 1 )$ ) but positively to cell-specific BP

In addition, weak positive relationships were observed between RI and forage crop and idle land use $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 8$ , $p = 0 . 0 0 4 )$ and monoculture croplands $( R ^ { 2 } ~ = ~ 0 . 0 8$ , $p ~ = ~ 0 . 0 6 1$ ), suggesting a weak increase in relatively reduced DOM in streams influenced by agricultural land use. Stepwise multivariate linear regression suggested that changes in RI between streams were best modeled by a relationship that included temperature (negative slope), cell-specific BP (positive slope), and wetland area (negative slope; $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 7$ , $p <$ 0.001), where the covariate monoculture cropland (positive slope) could be replaced with wetland area without much loss to the explanatory power of the model $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 2$ , $p <$ 0.001). These data suggest that agricultural and human land use change DOM processing and ecosystem function in a way that promotes the formation of reduced DOM. From the water column data alone, we cannot resolve exact mechanisms in these anthropogenically altered streams that would lead to the observed patterns. It is plausible that increased agricultural and human land use enhance soil and hyporheic BP, in addition to water column bacteria. Increased heterotrophy could enhance the reduced DOM signature in these flow paths from what would be expected in streams with more natural land use. In addition, anthropogenic land use is associated with increased suspended solid loading into streams. These particles would provide additional surfaces and microenvironments for DOM oxidation–reduction reactions to occur in the water column. Still, both quinone- and semiquinone-like PARAFAC components appeared to be of terrestrial origin in our study. Microbial activity influenced the reactivity of this terrestrial DOM but did not appear to produce these components internally, which was likely the case for the protein-like component.

Implications for DOM cycling in stream ecosystems—The lability of DOM is dictated largely by its chemical properties and source. DOM originating from microbial and autochthonous production is typically considered more labile than terrestrial-derived DOM. However, DOM leached from wetland and peatland soils can be an important carbon source to downstream microbial communities (Fellman et al. $2 0 0 9 a , b$ ). Chemically, protein-like DOM is typically removed rapidly in stream once it is introduced (Balcarczyk et al. 2009), whereas the contribution of humic-like DOM is removed from the DOM pool more slowly and changes little from upstream to downstream (Wickland et al. 2007; Fellman et al. $2 0 0 9 a , b$ ). Across our stream network, increased protein-like DOM signatures coincided with a relative loss in the freshwater humic-like DOM component (C1) with little change in the refractory humic-like component (C2), but an overall reduction in the humic content and aromaticity of the DOM pool (Table 3; Fig. 4). In temperate forest and wetland streams, protein-like DOM is the best predictor of the bioavailability of DOC, which correlates negatively with $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Fellman et al. $2 0 0 9 a$ ). In the current study, extracellular peptidase activity also increased with the protein-like DOM signature. This microbial activity indicator seemed tied largely to the protein-like DOM pool rather than being a broadscale indicator of microbial transformation of DOM. Thus, an increase in the proteinlike signature of DOM with increases in extracellular enzyme activity reflected recent in situ production and/or transformation of protein-like DOM, which likely increased the overall lability of the DOM pool.

The redox state of DOM might also influence its lability. Miller et al. $( 2 0 0 9 b )$ suggested that DOM in a reduced state is more accessible to microbial decomposition than its oxidized counterparts. In the current study, BP increased with a reduction in the oxidative state of DOM in anthropogenically modified streams, suggesting that bacteria were better able to use DOM in a reduced state and/or that bacterial communities in agricultural streams transformed the redox state of DOM. In addition, the microbial signature of DOM (i.e., FI and $\beta : \mathfrak { a }$ ratio) increased with BP in anthropogenically modified streams. Here, BP seemed linked broadly to DOM characteristics, microbial stream processes, and organic matter transformation, and

BP did not simply indicate a shift from humic-like toward more microbial- and protein-like DOM, as was the case for AMA. Together, these results suggested that the DOM pool present in agricultural and human-influenced watersheds was likely more labile than that found in watersheds with less anthropogenic modification. Increased lability of DOM in anthropogenically modified streams and subsequent loss of these inputs as the labile components are transported downstream (i.e., Fellman et al. $2 0 0 9 a , b ,$ ) can have the general effect of reducing the amount of organic matter that is transported downstream and increasing the abundance of refractory DOM components. The study streams in southern Ontario may have reached a point at which DOC concentration is reduced in agricultural streams (Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). With the observed changes in the DOM characteristics and microbial DOM processing with land use, further conversion of natural forest and wetlands for agricultural use could lead to decreased downstream carbon transport and a relative increase in the abundance of refractory DOM components during downstream transport.

The current study, to our knowledge, was the first concomitant investigation of the influences of land use and in situ microbial activity on DOM optical properties (optical indices and EEM-PARAFAC). In combing these parameters, we gleaned greater insight on the origin and character of DOM throughout stream ecosystems than would be gained investigating microbial activity, optical indices, and EEM-PARAFAC distributions independently. Across streams, the HIX and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ of DOM increased with wetland area in streams. Comparable nonquinone-like humic- and/or fulvic-like PARAFAC components made up $41 \%$ of component fluorescence but did not relate to land use categories, suggesting that the DOM pool of all streams, not just those in wetlands, was comprised strongly of terrestrial humic-like organic matter. The protein-like PARAFAC component followed patterns opposite to those for humic DOM indicators and related positively to AMA and recently produced DOM. These associations support further the notion that proteinlike DOM components arise from autochthonous production in aquatic ecosystems. BP increased in conjunction with the FI, recently produced DOM, and the RI, suggesting that BP is related broadly to the DOM characteristics. In conjunction with agricultural land use, BP increased with the relative abundance of reduced DOM, which is suggested to be more labile than DOM in an oxidized state. Moreover, anthropogenic land use facilitates environmental conditions that allowed DOM to persist in a more reduced state than would be expected in a natural wetland or forest stream. These changes within the DOM pool likely increased the lability of DOM in anthropogenically affected streams, which helped stimulate further microbial processing rates. Ultimately, as more land is converted for agricultural and urban use, more autochthonous, labile DOM will be produced or transformed from terrestrial sources, further stimulating microbial activity, resulting in alterations in stream carbon cycling and changing the quality and quantity of DOM that is exported to lakes, estuaries, and oceans.
