Optical and chemical properties of dissolved organic matter (DOM) were resolved in the mixing zone between the North Sea and the Baltic Sea. The results from four cruises undertaken from August 2006 to February 2007 are presented. Relationships between the optical (absorption and excitation–emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence), dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and dissolved lignin concentrations were determined and carbon stable isotope values $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } { \sf C } )$ of DOM were also used to evaluate DOM sources. Chromophoric DOM (CDOM), measured as absorption at $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , was a strong predictor for DOC and lignin concentrations. The DOM fluorescence (FDOM) characteristics were modeled by parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) and a six component model was derived. The intensity of one terrestrial humic-like fluorescent component (Fmax3) was strongly correlated to dissolved lignin concentrations. DOC concentrations were best modeled by the combination of this component with an amino acid-like fluorescent component. A three end-member mixing model developed for the region using CDOM–salinity relationships was used to estimate end-member lignin and DOC concentrations, which produced results consistent with other published results from the region. The Baltic Sea outflow was determined to be the dominant source of dissolved lignin in these waters. Despite the high riverine influence in the Jutland Coastal Current, the DOM originating from these waters contained little lignin and was predominantly autochthonous. Additionally CDOM and carbon stable isotope measurements revealed substantial autochthonous production in the Kattegat. Finally, we used the fluorescence–DOC relationships to estimate the net export of terrestrial C from the Baltic Sea at 0.8 Tg year−1 , which represents $4 5 \%$ of the calculated net DOC flux $( 1 . 7 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ ).

# 1. Introduction

The flux of terrigenous dissolved organic matter (DOM) through coastal environments represents a major connection between the terrestrial and marine carbon cycles. In coastal waters the quantity and quality of DOM is controlled by the seasonal cycles in riverine loading and marine primary productivity, biotic and abiotic turnover and remineralization, and the extent of mixing. In simple systems with a single dominant freshwater source with high DOM rapidly mixing with marine waters with low DOM, distributions and characteristics can be predicted (e.g., Klinkhammer et al., 2000; Raymond and Bauer, 2001; Stedmon and Markager, 2003). However, in productive eutrophic coastal systems with longer mixing times and/or multiple high DOM freshwater sources the conditions are more complex (Rochelle-Newall and Fisher, 2002; Markager et al., 2011). One such region is the Kattegat and Belt Sea at the interface between the Baltic and North Seas in Northern Europe. Here brackish water from the Baltic Sea flows westward and mixes with saline water from the North Sea, resulting in a classic salt–wedge interface, with strong stratification between the less dense brackish waters at the surface and saline water at the bottom. Due to the large catchment of the Baltic Sea, it is a major source of organic C to the North Sea (Thomas et al., 2005), although little is known of the chemical characteristics of the DOM leaving the Baltic, and hereby its importance and role in the regions carbon cycle.

Matching the flux of water out of the Baltic Sea there is a flux of water from the North Sea into the Baltic as bottom water, which has much lower DOM concentrations and a more marine character (Stedmon et al., 2000). However, this mixing is complicated by the Jutland Coastal Current which transports water with very high DOM concentrations northwards along the west coast of the Jutland peninsula and mixes with the North Sea water entering the Baltic through the Skagerrak (Højerslev et al., 1996; Stedmon et al., 2010). These waters, originating from the German Bight, are heavily influenced by several major Northern European rivers flowing into the southern North Sea. The waters of the Jutland Coastal Current and the Kattegat–Belt Sea are generally eutrophic, thus marine primary productivity also contributes to shaping DOM characteristics.

The optical properties of chromophoric DOM (CDOM) consist of its ultraviolet and visible light absorption, while DOM fluorescence (FDOM) encompasses excitation (absorption) and emission (fluorescence) spectral features. Both provide quantitative and qualitative information on DOM (Coble, 2007). Absorption by CDOM in the 300 to $6 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ wavelength range decreases approximately exponentially and the gradient of this slope can be modeled (S value) and used to differentiate between different CDOM pools (Bricaud et al., 1981; Carder et al., 1989; Stedmon and Markager, 2001). Absorption measurements at a specific wavelength (e.g. a300) can be used to trace CDOM concentrations (Bricaud et al., 1981). When excited with ultraviolet light, CDOM fluoresces at wavelengths between 300 and $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . As with absorption measurements, these spectra also provide information on the amount and characteristics of CDOM (Coble, 1996, 2007). When coupled with multivariate data analysis, fluorescence spectra combined into excitation–emission matrices (EEMs) can be separated into different independently variable fluorescence signals, which can then be traced individually (Stedmon et al., 2003; Stedmon and Bro, 2008).

Chemical biomarkers are also routinely used to separate mixing of DOM sources in coastal waters. Examples are C and N stable isotopes (Bauer, 2002) and lignin (e.g., Benner and Opsahl, 2001; Harvey and Mannino, 2001). Marine DOM derived from phytoplankton typically has carbon stable isotope $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C } )$ values that range from $- 2 0 \text{ - } 2 2 \text{ ‰}$ , while terrestrial DOM derived from C3 land plants typically have $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ values that range from $^ { - 2 8 }$ to $- 2 6 \text{‰}$ (Bauer, 2002). Lignin is a geochemical biomarker unique to terrestrial plants and can be used to trace the mixing and fate of terrestrial organic matter in coastal seawater (e.g., Hernes and Benner, 2003). Further, the ratios of the different phenolic compounds derived from the oxidation of lignin can be used to distinguish between plant sources (e.g. angiosperm vs. gymnosperm, or woody vs. non-woody tissue) and the extent of exposure to degradation (Hedges et al., 1988).

The focus of this study was to describe the chemical and optical properties of DOM in the region in light of resolving the characteristics of the major DOM sources. From previous studies on colored dissolved organic matter (CDOM) absorption it is apparent that three major DOM pools exist; Baltic Sea, North Sea and German Bight, and that conservative mixing of these can explain the majority of the spatial and temporal variability in concentrations and characteristics (Højerslev et al., 1996; Stedmon et al., 2010). Here we test these findings with geochemical measurements on DOM such as stable carbon isotopes and lignin content. In addition we aim to examine the relationships between the optical and chemical characteristics so that future work on tracing the carbon flow in the region can gain from the relatively rapid and cheap optical measurements, which can be carried out extensively and be used to extrapolate the extent of the geochemical data.

CDOM samples were gently filtered through pre-combusted GF/F filters using an acid-washed syringe, directly into acid washed $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m L } }$ brown glass bottles and stored in the dark at $4 ^ { \circ } C .$ Storage time varied between 1 and 2 weeks, as the cruise lasted 5 days and all the samples were measured in the week directly following the respective cruise. GF/F filters have an approximate pore size of $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ and are well suited for organic analysis as they can be combusted before use $^ { \cdot 4 \mathrm { h } }$ , at $5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ). Although they do not remove all microbes, they reduce numbers so that with limited storage little significant change is observed. CDOM absorbance was measured on a Shimadzu UV-2401PC spectrophotometer using MilliQ water as a reference according to the technique described in Stedmon et al. (2000). A $1 0 \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cuvette was used for all samples and measured absorbance (A) was converted to absorption coefficients $( a )$ using Eq. (1).

$$
a _ { \scriptscriptstyle ( \Lambda ) } = 2 . 3 0 3 A _ { \scriptscriptstyle ( \Lambda ) } / L
$$

$L$ is the pathlength in meters $\mathrm { ( 0 . 1 ~ m ) }$ . The spectral slope, S, of the absorption curve over the wavelength range of 300 to $6 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , was obtained by fitting Eq. (1) to the measured data using the non-linear regression technique presented in Stedmon et al.(2000).

DOM excitation–emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence was measured immediately following absorption measurements on a Varian Eclipse spectrofluorometer. Excitation wavelengths ranged from 240 to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ increments, while emission wavelengths were sampled every $2 \mathrm { n m }$ from 300 to $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . EEM fluorescence intensities were corrected for Rayleigh and Raman scattering, Milli-Q water blank-subtracted, and instrument bias in excitation and emission prior to correction for any inner-filtering effect (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Finally, each EEM was calibrated to the water Raman signal so that fluorescence is reported in Raman units $( \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 } .$ ). PARAFAC modeling of EEM fluorescence was done in Matlab using the DOMFluor toolbox (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). The number of components was determined by using split half validation and assessed with random initialization fits and residual analysis according to the approach described in Stedmon and Bro (2008).

DOC samples were filtered into pre-combusted $5 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , 6 h minimum) $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ borosilicate vials capped with Teflon-lined septa using the protocol described above and acidified to $\mathsf { p H } 2$ to 3 with reagent grade $8 5 \%$ $\mathrm { H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { P O } _ { 4 }$ (Fisher Scientific). These samples were stored at $4 ^ { \circ } C$ for $\sim 2$ to 3 weeks prior to shipment to Washington, DC (USA) from Denmark. DOC concentration and stable isotope values analyzed in continuous flow mode on a modified analytical system that coupled an OI1010 TOC wet chemical oxidation carbon analyzer to a Thermo Delta PlusXP isotope ratio mass spectrometer (WCOIRMS; Osburn and St-Jean, 2007). The DOC measurements were made on $2 \mathrm { m L }$ samples after pre-sparging with UHP He for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ to remove inorganic C prior to analysis. Calibration of carbon concentrations was achieved with the response of potassium hydrogen phthalate solutions of known concentration (83 to $8 3 3 \mu \mathrm { M }$ C). Lignin phenols were used as biomarkers for vascular plant contribution (terrestrial OM) to DOM. Lignin was extracted from $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filtrates onto ${ \mathsf { C } } _ { 1 8 }$ resin following Louchouarn et al. (2000) and hydrolyzed in $^ { 2 \mathbb { M } }$ NaOH via microwave-assisted CuO oxidation (Goni and Montgomery, 2000). Trans-cinnamic acid was added as a recovery standard and lignin oxidation products were quantified on a 5-point calibration of eight individual phenol standards (Hernes and Benner, 2002). Separation of lignin phenols was performed on an HP 6890 gas chromatograph fitted with a RTX-5MS capillary column $( 3 0 \mathrm { m } , 0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m m }$ i.d.; Restek) and detection was performed using an HP 5973 mass selective detector. Recovery of cinnamyl phenols was inconsistent, so these were removed from lignin quantification, which was based only on the sum of six vanillyl and syringyl phenols $\left( \Sigma _ { 6 } \right)$ o r normalized to DOC concentration $\left( \Lambda _ { 6 } \right)$ .

The concentration and characteristics of DOM in these waters is greatly controlled by the mixing of three major water masses: North Sea (NS) water originating from the central North Sea and Atlantic, Baltic Sea (BS) water representing the water flowing out of the Baltic, and German Bight (GB) water flowing northwards along the west coast of Jutland from the German Bight to the Skagerrak (Højerslev et al., 1996; Stedmon et al., 2010). The impacts of non-conservative processes on DOM first become apparent once the effects of mixing between these three are removed. Consequently, in the data analysis of the measurements carried out in this study, the contribution of each water mass in each sample derived using a ternary mixing calculation (Stedmon et al., 2010) will be used. In short the relative contribution $( f )$ of each water mass in each sample was estimated based the measured salinity (Salt) and CDOM absorption (a) and the average salinity and CDOM absorption values for each water mass (subscripted GB, NS or BS).

$$
S a l t = f _ { \mathrm { G B } } S a l t _ { \mathrm { G B } } + f _ { \mathrm { N S } } S a l t _ { \mathrm { N S } } + f _ { \mathrm { B S } } S a l t _ { \mathrm { B S } }
$$

$$
a _ { \scriptscriptstyle ( \Lambda ) } = f _ { \scriptscriptstyle \mathrm { G B } } a _ { \scriptscriptstyle \mathrm { G B } ( \Lambda ) } + f _ { \scriptscriptstyle \mathrm { N S } } a _ { \scriptscriptstyle \mathrm { N S } ( \Lambda ) } + f _ { \scriptscriptstyle \mathrm { B S } } a _ { \scriptscriptstyle \mathrm { B S } ( \Lambda ) }
$$

$$
f _ { \mathrm { G B } } + f _ { \mathrm { N S } } + f _ { \mathrm { B S } } = 1
$$

These three equations with three unknowns were solved to estimate $f$ for each water mass in each sample simultaneously using a matrix computation ( $\mathrm { N } = 1 1 4$ ). Thus the estimates of $f$ do not represent a regression or fit to the measured data. The strong agreement between the predicted CDOM absorption spectra $( 3 0 0 -$ $6 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ) and the measured spectra does however enforce the assumption that the mixing of three end members dominated the trends seen in this system (Stedmon et al., 2010).

Using the derived $f$ values, the reverse calculation was carried out on the geochemical parameters measured here ( $\mathrm { 8 ^ { 1 3 } C }$ -DOM, ${ \Sigma } _ { 6 } \mathrm { . }$ ) to estimate the DOM chemical properties of the three sources, for which we did not have as extensive a collection of measurements. For $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values, $N = 1 1 4$ and for ${ \Sigma } _ { 6 }$ values, $N = 1 8$ .

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Water column structure

The oceanographic conditions and optical and geochemical properties of DOM for each sampling are presented in the appendix $\mathrm { N } = 1 1 4$ ; Table A1) and summarized in Table 1 for the samples for which lignin was also measured. The salinity ranged from \~7 at St. 444 in the Arkona Basin, representing the flow out of the Baltic Proper, to $> 3 4$ (bottom depths at St. 905 and 1007) at the northern end of the Kattegat. Distinctive stratification was evident at each station from the Arkona Basin to the Kattegat, and the degree of stratification increased toward the transition from the Kattegat to the Skagerrak, as well as with season (Fig. 2A–F). For example, in August 2006, St. 444 in the Arkona Basin (the freshest end member for the study region) had well-defined surface layer that was warmer and fresher than bottom waters to a depth of $2 0 \mathrm { m }$ . This stratification deepened in February 2007, when the water column was nearly isothermal and the halocline lay between 30 and $4 0 \mathrm { m }$ (Fig. 2D). The Baltic outflow was restricted to approximately $1 0 \mathrm { m }$ at St. 1007, at the transition of the Kattegat to the Skagerrak (Fig. 2C and F).

CDOM absorption quantified at $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \left( a _ { 3 0 0 } \right)$ generally decreased with increasing salinity and with depth (Fig. 2), reflecting high CDOM in the Baltic outflow and low CDOM in the high salinity waters from the North Sea. The freshest samples collected (salinity \~7.4) had $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ values of $5 . 1 0 { \pm } 0 . 1 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Table 1). The most saline sample (salinity 34.9) at St. 905 ( $7 9 . 8 \mathrm { m }$ depth) had the lowest $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ value, $0 . 8 7 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ . DOC concentrations ranged from 79 to $3 3 6 \mu \mathrm { M } \mathrm { ~ C ~ }$ (m ${ \tt t e a n } = 1 9 7 \mu { \tt M } { \tt C }$ ) and followed a similar pattern to CDOM, decreasing with salinity.

# 3.2. Characterization of DOM fluorescence

DOM fluorescence (FDOM) was modeled by PARAFAC on a larger set of samples $N = 1 0 6 4$ ) collected from these cruises, which included the stations reported here. Six fluorescent components were validated and their characteristics are reported in Fig. 3 and Table 2. Five of the components resembled humic-like fluorescent material, while Component 6 resembled that of the amino acid tryptophan (Coble, 1996; Stedmon et al., 2003). Components 1 to 4 (C1–C4) all had excitation maxima centered on $2 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and were mostly distinguished by their emission spectra. Their spectra were largely identical to the humic-like fluorescence components indentified in early studies on Baltic FDOM (Stedmon et al., 2007a,b). Components 1 to 3 have previously been associated with terrestrial humic material (Stedmon et al., 2003; Cory and McKnight, 2005; Murphy et al., 2006; Stedmon et al., 2007b), though C1 also resembled fulvic acid extracted from marine sediments (Klapper et al., 2002). Components 4 and 6 fluoresce in the wavelength regions referred to as M and T, respectively (Coble, 1996) and have been found in both freshwater and oceanic environments (Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Coble, 2007) and are therefore not necessarily as source specific. However the amino acid like fluorescence of component 6 is known to dominate in marine waters where autochthonous production overwhelms the diluted terrestrial humic signal. C5 had two distinct peaks at $2 6 0 / 4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 7 5 / 4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively (Fig. 3E; Table 2) and resembled that found by Murphy et al. (2006, 2008) where it was considered to represent terrestrial material due to its correlation to the terrestrial humic-like fluorescence.

Both C3 and C4 from the PARAFAC analysis had the best correlation to other DOM properties (e.g., $\mathfrak { F } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM and ${ \boldsymbol { \Sigma } } _ { 6 }$ , Table 2). However, a step-wise multivariate regression revealed that the combination of maximum fluorescence values for C3 and C6 (Fmax3 and Fmax6, respectively) best modeled DOC concentration (Table 3). Individually, C3 explained $7 4 \%$ of the variation in DOC concentration and C6 explained $5 6 \%$ . In an earlier study on DOM in the Northern Baltic (Gulf of Bothnia) in seawater and sea ice a very similar relationship to DOC was derived for this C3 fluorescence signal (Stedmon et al., 2007b). For example a C3 fluorescence of 0.1 R.U. predicts a DOC of $1 4 1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ based on the earlier study, while in this study it predicts $1 1 9 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (Table 3). The major difference was that in the winter conditions of the earlier study the majority of the variability in DOC could be explained by C3 fluorescence alone and no significant intercept was found. In this dataset from the Kattegat–Belt Sea region it is clear that the situation is more complex with a background pool of DOC which is not correlated to the fluorescence $( 9 5 \mu \mathrm { { M } ) }$ and an additional source of DOC associated with the amino acid fluorescence of C6.

# 3.3. Salinity relationships with DOM optical and chemical properties

Property-salinity plots for DOM optical and chemical parameters exhibited generally decreasing trends with increasing salinity. For measures of concentration (e.g., $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ for CDOM, DOC), a clearly nonlinear trend was observed over the salinity range of 15 to 34 (Fig. 4A–B). On the basis of CDOM absorption data we know that this trend in optical properties can be explained by the entrainment of water from the German Bight (GB) into the saline North Sea (NS) water, which enters the Kattegat beneath the Baltic outflow layer (Stedmon et al., 2010). At salinities between 28 and 33 in particular, DOC concentrations for some samples were slightly higher than would be expected from mixing of two water masses (Fig. 4B). These samples corresponded to a sub set of bottom water samples from the Central and Northern Kattegat, which contained a contribution of water from the German Bight of $2 0 \%$ or more.

FDOM of each component modeled by the PARAFAC analysis also exhibited a general negative relationship with salinity. The trends for C3 (terrestrial humic A peak) and C6 (tryptophan-like T peak) are shown as an example of two contrasting components that were also found to be the most significant predictors of DOC concentrations (Fig. 4C and D). The fluorescence of C3 decreased linearly with salinity to roughly zero at a salinity of 35. The fluorescence intensities of C6 also decreased with salinity, however, there was more scatter and in contrast to C3, the saline end member had detectable and variable amounts of C6.

The $\mathfrak { F } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values in the Kattegat increased with salinity, exhibiting and upwardly curving pattern as is common for ratio-based estimates of organic matter such as $^ { 1 3 } { \cal C } / ^ { 1 2 } { \cal C } ,$ $\mathrm { { C / N } , }$ etc. (Perdue and Koprivnjak 2007) (Fig. 4E). The $\mathfrak { F } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values appear to represent mixing between terrestrial and marine sources. Typically, terrestrial $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values range from $- 2 5$ to $- 2 8 \text{‰}$ whereas marine $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values range from $^ { - 2 2 }$ to $- 1 9 \text{‰}$ ; estuarine values fall in between these ranges (Peterson and Fry, 1987; Raymond and Bauer, 2001; Bauer, 2002).

The f values from the ternary mixing calculation of the three water masses (GB, NS, and BS; see Methods) was used to estimate the chemical properties for the three end-members to the NS–BS region (Table 3). A ternary conservative mixing line was then calculated from the $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM value estimates, using the following model, adapted from Raymond and Bauer (2001):

$$
\delta ^ { 1 3 } C - D O M _ { \mathrm { t e r } } = \frac { f _ { \mathrm { B S } } \delta ^ { 1 3 } C - D O M _ { \mathrm { B S } } D O C _ { \mathrm { B S } } + f _ { \mathrm { G B } } \delta ^ { 1 3 } C - D O M _ { \mathrm { G B } } D O C _ { \mathrm { G B } } + f _ { \mathrm { N S } } \delta ^ { 1 3 } C - D O M _ { \mathrm { N S } } D O C _ { \mathrm { N S } } } { f _ { \mathrm { B S } } D O C _ { \mathrm { B S } } + f _ { \mathrm { N S } } D O C _ { \mathrm { N S } } + f _ { \mathrm { G B } } D O C _ { \mathrm { G B } } }
$$

The subscript ‘ter’ is the $\delta ^ { 1 3 } { \cal C }$ -DOM value estimated from conservative mixing of the three end-members. The mixing line is the solid black line in Fig. 4E and confidence level estimates are indicated with dotted lines. The ternary mixing line was compared to a binary mixing line, also adapted from Raymond and Bauer (2001):

$$
\delta ^ { 1 3 } C \mathrm { - } D O M _ { \mathrm { b i n } } = \frac { f \delta ^ { 1 3 } C \mathrm { - } D O M _ { \mathrm { B S } } D O C _ { \mathrm { B S } } + ( 1 \mathrm { - } f ) \delta ^ { 1 3 } C \mathrm { - } D O M _ { \mathrm { N S } } D O C _ { \mathrm { N S } } } { D O C _ { \mathrm { B S } } + D O C _ { \mathrm { N S } } }
$$

The subscript ‘bin’ is the value estimated from binary conservative mixing and $f$ in Eq. (8) is the fraction of terrestrial DOM, based on salinity of 8 (for the Arkona Sea) and a salinity of 35 for the North Sea. The binary mixing line appears as a dashed line in Fig. 4E. Both approaches produced very similar mixing lines.

Most of the $\mathfrak { F } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values measured in the Baltic Sea–North Sea mixing zone were less negative at salinities of 24–34 than was expected from marine and estuarine sources, even considering nonlinear conservative mixing (Fig. 4E). The most isotopically enriched value in the data set, $- 2 0 . 6 \text{‰}$ , occurred at St. 1007 (northern edge of the Kattegat; salinity $= 3 4 . 2 1 $ ) and at a DOC concentration $= 1 4 7 \mu \mathrm { { M } \mathrm { ~ C ~ } }$ . At the fresher region of the study area (salinity $\sim 8$ ), $\mathfrak { F } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values reflected a largely terrestrial source. In general, St. 444 $\delta ^ { 1 3 } { \sf C } .$ -DOM values ranged between $- 2 4 . 4$ and $- 2 6 . 4 \text{‰}$ (Fig. 4E).

# 3.4. Lignin characteristics and distribution

Fewer observations of dissolved lignin concentration $( \ N = 1 8 )$ ) were made than of other properties, yet clear trends are apparent. Dissolved lignin concentrations also decreased with salinity and exhibited the most linear trend among the optical and chemical variables measured (Table 1 and Fig. 4F). Lignin concentrations $\left( \Sigma _ { 6 } \right)$ decreased by $\sim 8 5 \%$ with salinity as terrestrial DOM was exported from the Baltic Sea through the Kattegat. In the Arkona Sea (St. 444), ${ \boldsymbol { \Sigma } } _ { 6 }$ values were highest in September $( 6 . 8 3 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) and lowest in August $( 5 . 9 7 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \mu$ ). The overall lowest $\Sigma _ { 6 }$ value $( 0 . 9 7 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ was measured at ca. $8 0 \mathrm { m }$ in the Central Kattegat (St. 905). Lignin was 0.08 to $0 . 2 0 \%$ of total DOC, and the highest carbonnormalized lignin values $\left( \Lambda _ { 6 } \right)$ (ca. 0.17 mg $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g } 0 \mathsf { C } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) occurred at salinities $< 2 0$ (surface waters of the Arkona Sea and the Southern and Central Kattegat). The distribution of $\Lambda _ { 6 }$ values showed less of a dependence on salinity than did ${ \Sigma } _ { 6 }$ . As salinity increased above 20, $\Lambda _ { 6 }$ values decreased by $31 \%$ , which was substantially less than the $\sim 8 5 \%$ decrease measured for ${ \boldsymbol { \Sigma } } _ { 6 }$ .

$S / \mathsf { V }$ ratios indicate vascular plant sources of DOM (Hedges and Mann, 1979) and showed a wide distribution from ca. 0.20 to 0.60 in DOM of the Arkona Sea, and also in DOM across the Kattegat, with no clear trend with salinity. Acid:aldehyde ratios for the vanillyl phenols $\left[ ( \mathsf { A d } / \mathsf { A l } ) _ { \mathrm { v } } \right]$ are indicative of oxidative degradation, with higher values indicating a greater degree of oxidation as aldehydes are oxidized to carboxylic acids (Opsahl and Benner, 1995). The $( \mathsf { A d } / \mathsf { A l } ) _ { \mathsf { v } }$ ratios increased from $0 . 3 0 \pm 0 . 0 8$ in the Arkona Sea to greater than 1.0 at higher salinities, mainly in the deep $( > 7 0 \mathrm { m } )$ Central Kattegat (St. 905).

3.5. Relationship between DOM optical and geochemical characteristics

Several key correlations were found between DOM optical and chemical properties in the North Sea–Baltic Sea mixing zone and are summarized in Table 4. In these statistical expressions, it was clear that optical properties described a large proportion of the variation in both DOC and lignin concentration, as regression coefficients all were ${ > } 0 . 7 5$ . For both DOC concentration and lignin concentration, fluorescence produced slightly higher $r ^ { 2 }$ values than did absorption. DOC concentration regressed on fluorescent components derived from the PARAFAC model was evaluated using a stepwise regression. In contrast to the regression with DOC, the intercept of the lignin $\left( \Sigma _ { 6 } \right)$ regressions did not differ significantly from zero. Although, as presented earlier, the DOC was best explained by two fluorescent components (C3 and C6), $\Sigma _ { 6 }$ was best correlated to terrestrial humiclike fluorescence of C3 alone.

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. DOM in the Kattegat

The results from Table 4 provided clear evidence that DOC and CDOM concentrations in the region were strongly related, and this evidence also suggested a large flux of terrigenous DOM from the Baltic to the North Sea. Most CDOM in coastal waters originates from terrestrial sources (Blough and Del Vecchio, 2002), thus terrestrial biomarkers such as lignin should correlate well with CDOM in coastal waters (e.g., Amon et al., 2003). Binary mixing between terrigenous and marine end-member DOM sources in many estuaries will therefore produce linear relationships in concentration vs. salinity plots (e.g., Rochelle-Newall and Fisher, 2002). The Baltic–North Sea transition zone does not fit this paradigm, because three water masses with distinct DOM characteristics are mixing in these waters (Stedmon et al., 2010). This can be seen in Fig. 4 as “kinks” in concentration vs. salinity plots. The “kink” occurred at a salinity of 32 for $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ (Fig. 4A), DOC concentration (Fig. 4B), and in the C6 fluorescence (Fig. 4D).

Qualitative measurements of DOM based on ratios of values, e.g., $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOM values, will produce curvilinear trends with salinity under binary mixing (Perdue and Koprivnjak, 2007). The binary and ternary conservative mixing lines in Fig. 4E showed similar patterns and indeed were nearly indistinguishable from one another. The dotted lines in this figure are the error associated with $\delta ^ { 1 3 } { \sf C } .$ -DOM measurements; therefore observed $\delta ^ { 1 3 } { \cal C } .$ -DOM values falling within the dotted lines can be considered conservative. The cluster of $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values above and below the conservative mixing lines and at salinities ${ > } 2 8$ can be interpreted as resulting from non-conservative processes. Most non-conservative $\delta ^ { 1 3 } C .$ -DOM values fell above the mixing lines, indicating a presence of isotopically enriched DOM.

In contrast, neither C3 fluorescence (Fig. 4C) nor dissolved lignin concentration (Fig. 4F) showed the “kink” feature indicating the mixing of three discrete signals. Rather, C3 and ${ \Sigma } _ { 6 }$ values showed inverse linear trends with salinity throughout the study area, a pattern similar to binary mixing between distinct river (terrestrial) and marine end-members (de Souza Sierra et al., 1997; Blough and Del Vecchio, 2002). Inverse linear trends in fluorescence with salinity were also shown for A peak fluorescence in the Columbia River estuary (Coble, 1996) and C peak fluorescence in Florida Bay (Clark et al., 2002) and along the Florida Coastal Everglades (Jaffe et al., 2004), the Mississippi River plume (Conmy et al., 2004), the South Atlantic Bight (Kowalczuk et al., 2009), and in Danish estuaries close to this study (Stedmon et al., 2003). Key to these studies is a strong dilution of river water with seawater and a high degree of covariance of C and A peak fluorescence is typically observed (Kowalczuk et al., 2009).

Given the ubiquity with which terrestrial humic fluorescence decreases with salinity, our results indicate a single dominant source of lignin, which exhibited a distinct fluorescence signal (C3) that explained $8 7 \%$ of the variation in $\Sigma _ { 6 }$ (Table 4), and was associated solely with the Baltic outflow (Fig. 5A and B). For samples with little or no Baltic Sea contribution, C3 fluorescence was nearly zero, and lignin concentration was roughly $1 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ . Hence, C3 and the bulk of the lignin originate from the Baltic Sea DOM. These results are supported by the $\delta { } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOM values in the Kattegat (Fig. 5C). Large fractions of German Bight and North Sea water combined corresponded to the most isotopically enriched and marine-like $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values. Further, when only the fraction of German Bight was considered, the pattern remained the same. This result suggested that German Bight water, which contained substantial CDOM and DOC (e.g., Ferrari, 2000; Stedmon et al., 2010), is nonetheless not largely terrestrial in nature, or at least, lignin-poor.

# 4.2. Dissolved lignin characteristics in the Kattegat

The fact that the lignin concentrations correlated best to the fluorescence of C3 contrasts to the findings of Hernes et al. (2009) where wavelengths $\operatorname { E x 2 8 0 }$ and $\mathtt { E m 3 2 5 }$ were found to be best correlated to lignin concentrations in the freshwater Sacramento River/San Joaquin River delta. Fluorescence at these wavelengths corresponds to that of C6 in this study. Although there was a significant correlation between lignin and C6 fluorescence it was not as strong as that with C3. The Hernes et al. study attributed their result to the fact that many of the individual phenolic monomers that comprise lignin do fluorescence at low wavelengths. However these benzoic acid derivatives also exhibit large changes in the fluorescence properties depending on small structural changes. For example, syringealdehyde has a fluorescence maximum at $4 2 6 \mathrm { n m }$ , while syringic acid has a fluorescence maximum at $3 3 8 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ (Stedmon, unpublished data). Syringealdehyde is oxidized to syringic acid during degradation, similar to the vanillyl phenols which produce the $( \mathsf { A d } /$ $\mathsf { A l } ) _ { \mathsf { v } }$ index. The effect on fluorescence is a blue shift in emission as the aldehydes are oxidized to acids. Thus the differences in the fluorescence region best correlated to lignin concentrations between these studies might reflect the different oxidative states of lignin between the two systems (i.e., a marginal sea vs. a freshwater river delta).

Though conservative with salinity, lignin appeared more degraded at higher salinity. This was evidenced by an increase in $( \mathsf { A d } / \mathsf { A l } ) _ { \mathsf { v } }$ values at salinities ${ > } 2 5$ (Table 1) and a general increase in $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM value as $\Sigma _ { 6 }$ decreased (Fig. 6C). These results raise the possibility that this trend is due to photodegradation modifying BS DOM characteristics during its passage to the North Sea as seen in other freshwaters (Opsahl and Zepp, 2001; Osburn et al., 2001; Spencer et al., 2009; Benner and Kaiser, 2011), rather than the trend resulting from mixing alone. Our $( \mathsf { A d } / \mathsf { A l } ) _ { \mathsf { v } }$ values increase with salinity from 0.2 to 1.97 across the BS to NS mixing zone which could be explained by photodegradation (Opsahl and Benner, 1998; Hernes and Benner, 2003; Spencer et al., 2009), but the lignin characteristics of the NS marine end-member derived here agree well with earlier reports from the North Atlantic. Opsahl et al. (1999) found $( \mathsf { A d } / \mathsf { A l } ) _ { \mathsf { v } }$ values of 1.24 and $\Lambda _ { 6 }$ values of $0 . 1 6 9 \mathrm { m g }$ $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g } 0 \mathsf C ^ { - 1 } ,$ ) for the East Greenland Current, at $8 0 \mathrm { m }$ water depth and $\sim 3 4$ salinity. Their values are very similar to the deepest (ca. $8 0 \mathrm { m }$ ) and most saline (\~34 salinity) samples in our data set. This points toward the increase in $( \mathsf { A d } / \mathsf { A l } ) _ { \mathsf { v } }$ values observed here being primarily due to the mixing of comparatively fresh Baltic DOM with a substantially more degraded marine end member from the North Sea, with characteristics similar to lignin in the North Atlantic. The spectral slope data from the region also support this. Much of the overall trends in S values across the mixing gradient in these waters can be explained by the ternary mixing model (Stedmon et al., 2010). Although photodegradation likely plays a role in altering the chemical and spectral characteristics of Baltic DOM further East where residence times are considerably longer (Stedmon et al., 2007a, 2007b), the comparatively short residence time of the Kattegat waters results in mixing explaining the majority of the trends observed.

The lignin results in this study indicated that the waters from the German Bight do not appear to contain substantial amounts of lignin, despite its high CDOM concentration and riverine influence. In fact, when $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOM values were plotted against $\Sigma _ { 6 }$ values (Fig. 6C), the estimate for zero lignin was $- 2 2 . 9 \text{‰}$ , a typical value for marine phytoplankton and in accord with $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -POM values (ca. $- 2 3 \text{‰}$ for the North Sea reported by Laane et al. (1990) and UDOM values $( - 2 2 . 5 \% )$ for the East Greenland Current (Opsahl et al., 1999). It is therefore likely that the terrestrial organic matter signal in the eutrophic waters of the German Bight at salinity of 29 is already degraded and diluted by marine DOM production, thus not strongly influencing the relationships seen in the Baltic–North Sea transition zone.

# 4.3. Estimating sources of DOM in the NS–BS mixing zone

For $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ , the results in Table 3 were exactly the same as was calculated by Stedmon et al. (2010), and, as noted in that study, showed a large contribution of CDOM from the German Bight.

However, DOC concentration was greater in the Baltic Sea end member than in the German Bight end member. This discrepancy between CDOM and DOC corresponds to a difference in the DOC specific absorption. This most likely represents the fact that German Bight contains a substantial amount of autochthonous CDOM produced in the eutrophic estuaries along the coast of Western Europe (e.g., Abril et al., 2002). DOM in the Baltic outflow has experienced a much longer transit resulting in both a greater proportion of non colored DOC and photodegradation of CDOM and FDOM (cf. Stedmon et al., 2010 and references therein). Also, the NS end-member estimate had a DOC concentration of $1 0 5 \mu \mathrm { M } .$ , about $3 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ greater than the lowest DOC concentration measured in the data set. The discrepancy is likely due to an averaging effect of non-conservative processes that add or remove CDOM and FDOM and averaging of water mass inflows (GB, NS) and outflows (BS) over late summer, autumn, and winter.

We cannot rule out resuspension from pore waters as a source of both CDOM and FDOM to the Kattegat. In shallow marginal and coastal areas, deposition both of autochthonous and allochthonous OM can be diagenetically altered to produce distinct signals (Klapper et al., 2002; Komada et al., 2002; Burdige et al., 2004). Each study noted a prominence of the M-peak, which best described C4 from our PARAFAC model (Table 2 and Fig. 3). However, the covariance of C4 with C3 in our data set complicates the use of C4 as a sediment CDOM source.

The $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM end-member estimates compared well with literature values. The BS estimate of $- 2 6 . 1 \text{‰}$ showed a clear terrestrial source of DOM in the low salinity waters of the Arkona Sea. The GB estimate was $- 2 4 . 3 \text{‰}$ , typical of estuarine DOM, and comparable to $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ values for high molecular weight DOM for the lower Scheldt Estuary (van Heemst et al., 2000). The NS estimate was $- 2 2 . 7 \text{‰}$ , which clearly reflected a marine source, similar to the East Greenland Current (Opsahl et al., 1999) and various estimates of North Sea $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values ranging between $^ { - 2 2 }$ and $- 2 3 \text{‰}$ (LeClercq et al., 1997; Ahad et al. 2006). The NS $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values also compared well with $\delta ^ { 1 3 } { \sf C } .$ -POM values in the estuaries and coastal waters of the North Sea (Laane et al., 1990; Megens et al., 2001), which could be derived from phytoplankton biomass (Suratman et al., 2009). The carbon stable isotope evidence argues for autochthonous DOM being imported into the Kattegat from the German Bight and North Sea.

We tested the hypothesis that ternary conservative mixing explained much of the variation in the optical and chemical properties of DOM in the Kattegat by comparing $\delta ^ { 1 3 } { \cal C }$ -DOM values against S values, both qualitative descriptions of DOM sources (Fig. 7). End member estimates for S values were obtained from Stedmon et al. (2010) and combined with the preceding estimates for $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values (Table 3). If the end-member values calculated by the ternary mixing model bracketed all of the data, this result would indicate only conservative behavior in the Kattegat. This was not expected (Fig. 4E), and Fig. 7 reveals information about the non-conservative processes likely occurring in the Kattegat.

It is clear that DOM in the German Bight, North Sea and Baltic Sea waters are distinct from one another with respect to S value and $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM value, and most of the data appear to be mixtures of these end-members. However, encircled in Fig. 7 are two outlier regions (“M” and “K”) that appear to be non-conservative DOM sources. One unknown source (labeled “M” in Fig. 7) was observed and distinguished from the others by S values of $1 4 { - } 1 6 \mu \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ and $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values of $^ { - 2 3 }$ to $- 2 4 \text{‰}$ . The $S$ values strongly point to a phytoplankton signature of autochthonous production that has been observed in the North Sea (Stedmon and Markager, 2001), yet likely difficult to detect via CDOM properties alone (Stedmon et al., 2010). This source was primarily in waters deeper than $3 0 \mathrm { m }$ and at salinities of 34–35. Moreover, the $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values in the “M” region of Fig. 7 support an autochthonous source of DOM.

The second unknown sources (labeled “K” in Fig. 7) was observed and distinguished from the others by $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values of $- 2 1 . 4$ t o $- 2 0 . 6 \text{‰}$ . These samples were primarily found at depths of $2 5 \mathrm { - } 5 0 \mathrm { m }$ . Their S values overlap with the Kattegat values reported in Stedmon et al. (2010) for salinities 20–34. However, their $\delta ^ { 1 3 } { \cal C } .$ -DOM values are the most isotopically enriched in our data set. These values are enriched above the North Sea $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM value predicted by the ternary mixing model $( - 2 2 . 5 \% )$ , but could possibly represent DOM from autochthonous diatom production in the Kattegat. Rolff and Elmgren (2000) found $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ values of $- 2 1 . 8 \pm 1 . 5 \%$ for diatomdominated particles in the Baltic Proper. Waite et al. (2005) demonstrated that sinking diatom remains contained in POM produced very large isotopic shifts toward $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -POM values ca. $- 1 9 \text{‰}$ for Gullmar Fjord, a tributary to the Kattegat, in the spring. Our range of $\delta ^ { 1 3 } { \sf C } .$ -DOM values for this “K” source, when grouped by depth, overlap with the $\delta ^ { 1 3 } { \sf C } .$ -POM values ( $^ { - 2 1 }$ to $- 2 2 \text{‰}$ ) from these studies and originate primarily from St. 1007 in the Northern Kattegat (Fig. 1). Diatoms are abundant along the southern Skagerrak/northern Kattegat, frontal system (Kiørboe et al., 1990; Heilmann et al., 1994), so hydrolysis of sinking diatom remains in these waters could produce the enriched $\delta { } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values and could also explain the rather high DOC concentrations $( > 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ) measured.

Despite the clear dominance of the lignin signal by the Baltic Sea outflow, as evidenced by the conservative behavior of lignin in the Kattegat, the German Bight and North Sea end members were estimated to have measureable amounts of lignin (Table 3), which is understandable given their salinities (29.7 and 35.1) and proximity to land. However, the lignin present in these sources is very likely heavily degraded. Van Heemst et al. (2000) studied the molecular signatures in pyrolyzates of high molecular weight DOM from the Ems–Dollert estuary and discovered a general lack of the alkylphenols that indicate fresh lignin. Rather, these workers found evidence for highly oxidized lignin in DOM. These geochemical results support our findings that the inflowing DOM from the North Sea and German Bight were low in lignin concentration and had $( \mathsf { A d } / \mathsf { A l } ) _ { \mathsf { v } }$ ratios indicative of extensive oxidation.

# 4.4. Terrigenous DOM export from the Kattegat

We used the optical measurement, Fmax3, which we resolved as terrestrial DOM signals through its strong relationship with lignin to compute annual terrigenous DOM export in and out of the Kattegat. These calculations were based on water mass flux estimates from Højerslev et al. (1996) using their two-box model for surface water (flux out) and bottom water (flux in) layers in the Kattegat. Surface water flux out of the Kattegat at St. 1007 is $\sim 1 . 9 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ and bottom water flux in to the Kattegat is $\sim 1 . 4 4 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ . The average DOC concentration at St. 1007 was $1 7 3 \pm 4 1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ in surface waters $\left( 0 { - } 1 0 \mathrm { m } \right)$ and $1 3 3 \pm 4 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ DOC in bottom waters $( > 1 0 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ . These values correspond to an annual flux out from the Kattegat of $4 . 0 \pm 0 . 9$ Tg C year−1 and into the Kattegat bottom waters of $2 . 3 { \pm } 0 . 8 \mathrm { T g } \mathsf { C y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ The net export of DOC from the Baltic is therefore estimated at $1 . 7 \mathrm { T g }$ C year−1 . The terrigenous component of these fluxes was estimated by the Fmax3 term from the two-component fluorescence model used to predict DOC concentration (Table 3). The calculations resulted in an annual terrigenous flux of $1 . 1 \pm 0 . 4$ Tg C year−1 out of the Kattegat surface waters and a flux of $0 . 3 { \pm } 0 . 1 \mathrm { T g } \textsf { C y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ in to the Kattegat bottom waters. The net export of terrigenous DOC is therefore estimated at $0 . 8 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { C y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ .

Thomas et al. (2005) calculated a carbon budget for the North Sea, which included an export of organic C $( \mathsf { D O C } + \mathsf { P O C } )$ of $0 . 4 8 \mathrm { T g }$ $\mathsf { C y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ . Our calculations estimate this organic C flux to be much higher. As similar values for net water flux were used this difference is primarily due to the higher DOC concentrations used by Thomas et al. (2005), which were roughly $45 \%$ of our measured concentrations $( 7 8 \mu \mathrm { { M } }$ ; see their Table 1). It is clear that more work is however required to refine these flux estimates and compare them to riverine loading to the Baltic and expected autochthonous DOC release from pelagic productivity.

# 5. Conclusions

This study has shown that combining optical and chemical tracers with mixing calculations allows us to better resolve how and to what extent the characteristics of DOM change in coastal and shelf systems. In the Kattegat, the combination of rapid mixing times, multiple sources of terrestrial material and considerable autochthonous production due to the trophic status of these water types often make it difficult to separate the effects of different processes on DOM. The approach presented here provides insight into what extent conservative mixing can explain trends observed and the spatial and temporal significance of non-conservative processes. A fraction of DOM fluorescence (Component 3, A-peak, Ex.240/Em. $4 2 4 \mathrm { n m }$ ) was highly correlated to lignin concentrations, which offers an effective approach to vastly expand estimates on the distribution of terrestrial DOM in the region. The dominant source of lignin was the Baltic outflow and the high DOM concentrations in waters originating from the German Bight appear to be greatly influenced by autochthonous production. This fluorescent signal combined with that of the aminoacid like fluorescence signal of Component 6 (T-peak, $\mathtt { E x } 2 8 0 / \mathtt { E m } 3 3 0$ nm), also provided a robust prediction of DOC concentrations. The $\mathfrak { d } ^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -DOM values and S values indicated two additional sources of DOM in the Kattegat in addition to the mixing of DOM transported from the Baltic Sea, German Bight and North Sea. Due to their isotope and spectral slope values these sources were identified as autochthonous production. On the whole we estimate that $1 . 7 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { C y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ is net exported from the Baltic as DOC and $45 \%$ of this load is terrestrially derived DOM.

These results have important implications for DOM biogeochemistry and the use of optics in its detection and quantification. First, CDOM absorption and fluorescence has been shown to be powerful means to create bio-optical algorithms to determine OM sources in coastal waters (cf. Mannino et al. 2008) and can be used to quantify terrigenous DOM fluxes (similar to riverine export, e.g., Spencer et al., 2009). Second, the relative amounts of DOM from different sources can complicate biomarker estimates, yet it is clear that combined optical and chemical analyses can resolve these sources. In fact, the approaches taken here were necessary to evaluate and confirm the utility of using CDOM as a water mass and terrestrial C flux tracer. Our use of DOM-fluorescence and DOM-absorption algorithms validated by a terrigenous biomarker (lignin) improves upon rudimentary CDOM–lignin relationships (Hernes and Benner, 2003) and demonstrates their utility. Terrestrial DOM outflow from the Baltic Sea remains largely in surface waters $< 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ , and drops off precipitously below $1 0 \mathrm { m }$ water depth at the transition from the Kattegat to the Skagerrak. The work presented here suggests that primary production in the Kattegat contributes substantial DOM to the North Sea, building on previous investigations of OM in this region (Waite et al., 2005), yet our estimates for DOC flux are larger than those calculated by Thomas et al. (2005). Historically, the Baltic–North Sea transition zone has had substantial phytoplankton input due to eutrophication (Bianchi et al., 2000) a process continuing today (Carstensen et al., 2004; Conley et al., 2007). These sources of organic matter are clearly accumulating signals that complicate using optical measures of carbon biogeochemistry.
