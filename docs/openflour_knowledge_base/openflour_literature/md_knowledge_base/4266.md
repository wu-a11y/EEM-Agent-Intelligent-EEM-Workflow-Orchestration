# Abstract

Hydrothermal transformations of disolved organic matter (DOM) are governed by temperature and sedimentary organic carbon content, resulting in the release of hydrothermal DOM containing bioavailable compounds fueling benthic microbes.However,the temperature-dependent molecular changes in porewater DOM from organic-rich hydrothermal sediments,and the extent to which these changes contribute to the marine recalcitrant DOM,remain largely unexplored. Here we investigated the DOM composition of hydrothermal porewater and bottom water samples from the Guaymas Basin, Gulf of California, where basaltic sillintrusions generate hydrothermal petroleum in organic-rich sediments. Samples containing hydrothermal petroleum with in situ temperatures from $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $> 1 0 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ were analyzed using Fourier-transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry and paralel factor analysis of excitation-emision matrices from fluorescent DOM (FDOM). We found that the porewater DOM composition was strongly influenced by temperature and petroleum disolution, evidenced by the enrichment of hydrothermal DOM with highly unsaturated,oxygen-depleted aromatic,sulfur-containing molecular formulae and petroleum-associated FDOM compared to a cold reference site. In bottom waters,hydrothermal DOM accounted for $\sim 2 6 \%$ of the DOM molecular formulae,with $8 2 \%$ exhibiting hydrogen-to-carbon ratios $< 1 . 5$ ，indicating their recalcitrance. The remaining $\sim 1 8 \%$ of the hydrothermal molecular formulae were aliphatic and saturated, representing the release of bioavailable DOM to the ocean. Our results show that hydrothermal sediments are a source of both bioavailable and recalcitrant DOM,releasing water-soluble petroleum-derived compounds to the deep ocean. Our study highlights the need for more quantitative research on the contribution of hydrothermal sediments to deep-sea DOM cycling.

Oceanic dissolved organic matter (DOM) constitutes a large pool of reduced carbon on Earth,containing approximately $6 6 2 \mathrm { P g }$ of dissolved organic carbon (DOC; Hansell 2013). Marine DOMis mainly derived from primary producers in the euphotic zone.Most of this DOM is labile and quickly degraded by heterotrophic microorganisms within hours to days (Hansell 2013). The remaining,accumulating fraction of DOM makes up about $9 5 \%$ of the DOC pool in global oceans,with turnover times of $\sim 4 0 0 0$ to 600O years. It is thus defined as refractory DOM (Bauer,Williams,and Druffel 1992;Hansell 2013). The contributions of hydrothermal systems to the marine carbon cycle are complex.Temperaturesabove $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ cause transformations which can lead to the removal of refractory DOM, converting it primarily into $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ along with smaller，biolabile DOM compounds such as low-molecular weight organic acids (Hawkes et al. 2015a; Hawkes et al. 2O15b). These transformations occur through thermal degradation，including abiotic oxidation, decarboxylation，and dehydration (Seewald 2Oo1；McCollom and Seewald 20O3; Hawkes et al. 2015a). In contrast, hydrothermal systems with temperatures of $\sim 1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ can facilitate processes like petroleum formation,as well as the production and mobilization of bioavailable DOM (Simoneit 1985；Rossel et al.2015; Lin et al. 2O17).Here,hydrothermal fluids may also contain DOM of biological origin (Yang et al. 2O17; Longnecker et al. 2018). At temperatures between $\sim 6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ hydrothermal processes can be either source or sink mechanisms for marine organic matter (Sert et al. 2O22).Furthermore,hydrothermal systems can be sources of recalcitrant thermogenic DOM,such as polycondensed aromatics，which can persist in the ocean for millennia (Dittmar and Koch 2OO6;Dittmar and Paeng 2O09；Druffel et al. 2O21； Yamashita， Mori, and Ogawa 2023).Hydrothermally mobilized DOM may be discharged from sediments into the ocean via hydrothermal fluid flow (Lin et al. 2017),potentially influencing the DOM distribution in the deep ocean to a yet unknown extent (Hawkes et al. 2015a; Rossel et al. 2015; Sert et al. 2022).

Here,we examined the molecular composition and optical properties of DOM released at an expanding hydrothermal spreading center in the Guaymas Basin,Gulf of California, located at $\sim 2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ water depth.At this location,magmatic sill intrusions heat up overlying organic-rich sediments,generating hydrothermal petroleum that impregnates the sediments (Einsele et al. 1980； Simoneit 1985；Teske，Lizarralde，and Hofig 2O2O),especially at localized hydrothermal hotspots in the shallow subsurface (Ondréas et al. 2O18;Mara et al. 2022). Active venting sites host microbial mats and highly active benthic communities (Teske,Callaghan，and LaRowe 2014; McKay et al. 2016; Teske et al. 2016) covering hot,sulfidic, methane-rich and at times oil-impregnated sediments.These sediments contain particulate and dissolved aliphatic and aromaticpetroleum hydrocarbons including polycondensed aromatics and polysulfides,as well as low-molecular weight organic acids and hydrocarbons (Simoneit 1985;Martens 1990; Zhuang et al. 2019; Song et al. 2021; Mara et al. 2022).Laboratory experiments have shown that hydrothermal heating of Guaymas Basin sediments can release bioavailable DOM (Lin et al.2O17).At the same time,the release of water-soluble petroleum compounds can also be a source mechanism of refractory DOM in petroleum impacted systems (Podgorski et al.2O21;Brünjes et al.2O22). The relative contributions of either bioavailable or refractory DOM from hydrothermal petroleum in the porewater or even the deep ocean via hydrothermal discharge remain to be determined.

A source of refractory DOM is the abiotic formation of dissolved organic sulfur (DOS),which can occur via sulfide incorporation into DOM under sulfidic conditions-a process known as sulfurization (Pohlabeln et al. 2017).An accumulation of DOs underanoxicconditions isfrequently observed innonhydrothermal sediments (Schmidt et al. 20o9; Seidel et al. 2014; Gomez-Saez et al. 2021). While DOM sulfurization has been documented in shallow hydrothermal systems (Gomez-Saez et al. 2O16) it has not yet been reported in deep-sea hydrothermal environments. Sulfur-enriched porewater DOM from sulfidic systems as well as petroleum-derived DOS (Brunjes et al. 2O22) can be transported to the deep ocean where they contribute to the marine DOM pool (Phillips et al. 2022; Ibrahim and Tremblay 2023). In the Guaymas Basin,advective porewater fluxes lead to elevated DOC concentrations in the bottom waters, ${ \sim } 3 { - } 5 0 $ times higher compared to the deep ocean (Lin et al. 2O17).We therefore anticipate that discharged porewater DOM containing refractory components,including DOs,is transferred to the water column.

Dissolved organic matter transformations in organic-matter rich and hydrothermally-influenced environments with petroleum generation，such as the Guaymas Basin，are largely unexplored as previous studies have focused on low-organic matter hydrothermal systems (Hawkes et al. 2O15b;Rossel et al. 2015; Rossel et al. 2017; Yang et al. 2017; Longnecker et al.2018; Sert et al. 2O22).To address this issue,we analyzed the molecular composition of solid-phase extracted DOM $\mathrm { ( D O M _ { S P E } ) }$ via Fourier-transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FT-ICR-MS),supplemented with optical analysis of whole water chromophoric DOM(CDOM) and fluorescent DOM (FDOM) properties. We investigated DOM compositional changes in hydrothermal porewaters along steep temperature gradients and compared these with DOM from the overlying bottom water to trace the fate of hydrothermal DOM in the deep ocean.We hypothesize that organic-rich hydrothermal sediments are a source of recalcitrant DOM to the deep ocean, originating from discharge of sulfurized DOM and hydrothermal petroleum. Our objectives were to (1) investigate the relationship between sediment temperature and porewater DOM composition，(2） determine the role of sulfurization and petroleum dissolution in shaping the porewater DOM composition,and (3) assess the potential contribution of hydrothermal porewater discharge as a source of recalcitrant DOM to the deep ocean.

# Material and methods

# Sampling sites and sample collection

Seven push cores between 16 and $4 8 \mathrm { c m }$ length were retrieved by the Deep Submergence Research Vehicle Alvin during expedition AT42-05 with R/V Atlantis in November 2018 (Fig.1, Table 1) targeting previously explored sampling areas in the southern Guaymas Basin in water depths of about $2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ (Teske et al.2016). In addition, nine bottom water samples were taken with Niskin botles during Alvin dives,10 to $3 0 0 \mathrm { c m }$ above the sediment surface of mat-covered hydrothermal sediments adjacent to the push core samples (details about bottom water samples in Supporting Information Table S1).On the ship,aliquots of sediments containing porewater and water samples were distributed to $1 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ pre-rinsed high-density polyethylene bottles in $_ { 4 \mathrm { - C m } }$ intervals and subsequently stored frozen at - $1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ Two cores (4992-40,5001-41) were frozen intact in the core liner and split into $4 \mathrm { - c m }$ segments right before porewater sampling in the laboratory. All sediment and water samples were stored and transported frozen before final processing in the laboratory between November 2O19 and April 2O20.Frozen sediment and water samples were gradually brought to room temperature by letting them sit in a thermally insulated box overnight.All sediment cores except the reference core exhibited a petroleum smell and individual subsamples partly contained visible oil droplets (Supporting Information Fig. S1). Sediments were then transferred to $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ pre-rinsed Falcon tubes and centrifuged for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $4 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ .Separated porewater and seawater was then filtered through pre-combusted $( 4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } , \ 5 \mathrm { h } )$ glass fiber filters (GF-75,Advantec) with a nominal retention of $0 . 3 \mu \mathrm { m }$ within a polypropylene flter holder (Advantec) using pre-rinsed $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ polypropylene syringes.Porewater from cores 4992-4O and 5001-41 was filtered by $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ hydrophilic polypropylene membrane filters (GHP,Acrodisc).All glassware used was combusted $( 4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } , ~ 5 \mathrm { h } )$ and non-combustible material was pre-cleaned by overnight-soaking in acidified ultrapure water (HCl, pH 2） and subsequent thorough rinsing with ultrapure water.

# Optical analysis

Filtered water samples were stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark until analysis.Absorbance measurements of CDOM were conducted ona UV-VISabsorptionspectrophotometer(UV-1280, Shimadzu) in a $1 0 \mathrm { m m }$ quartz cell directly before fluorescence measurements.Fluorescence measurements were performed using a fluorescence spectrophotometer (Agilent Cary Eclipse, USA) at room temperature in the same quartz cell. Excitationemission matrices (EEMs) were recorded from 220 to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ excitation in $5 \mathrm { n m }$ steps and $2 5 0 { - } 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ emission in $2 \mathrm { n m }$ steps at room temperature. Samples with very high fluorescence intensities were diluted with ultrapure water up to 10-fold. The fluorescence spectrum of ultrapure water was measured at the beginning and end of each measurement day and subtracted from the raw data set. Fluorescence signals were normalized to the Raman peak area of ultrapure water. Inner-filter effects were corrected using absorbance spectra. Specific absorption at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { ( S U V A _ { 2 5 4 } ) }$ ）was calculated by dividing the absorption coefficient $\mathbf { \tau } ^ { ( \mathrm { m } ) }$ at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ by the DOC concentration $( \mathbf { m g L ^ { - 1 } } )$ ．Chromophoric dissolved organic matter slope ratios were calculated based on the slope of absorption spectra between 275-295 and $3 5 0 -$ $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ .A seven-component PARAFAC model was calculated on porewater excitation-emission matrices $( n = 5 2 )$ ）using the StaRdom package (Pucher et al. 2019) in $\mathrm { ~ R ~ v 4 . 2 . 1 }$ . Raman and Rayleigh scattering were thoroughly removed and interpolated.Samples were normalized to unit variance because of large differences in fluorescence intensities. The final model was validated by split-half tests after splitting it into four subsets, recombined to six splits (Murphy et al. 2013; Supporting Information Figs. S3， S4). The loadings of PARAFAC components were reported as the relative abundance of each component to track qualitative differences in FDOM among samples (Fig.2).We further calculated a PARAFAC model on re-dissolved SPE extracts to assess the influence of extraction

# Statistical analysis

A principle coordinate analysis (PCoA） was calculated on normalized intensities of $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ molecular formulae within a Bray-Curtis dissimilarity matrix using the vegan package utilizing the functions vegdist and cmdscale (Oksanen et al. 2022) in R v4.2.1. Intensity-weighted averages of molecular parameters, $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ compound group assignments as well as centered normalized CDOM，FDOM and environmental parameters were further correlated to this matrix using envfit.A PERMANOVA was performed to assess the differences in molecular and environmental parameters based on a Bray-Curtis dissimilarity matrix,with sediment core locations as the grouping factor, using vegdist and adonis2. Correlations between environmental and molecular parameters were assessed using Kendall's $\tau _ { i }$ a non-parametric test (most of the resulting parameters were not normal-distributed, test for normality using a Shapiro-Wilk test)which is considered robust to outliers and nonlinearity (Newson 2Oo2).Differences between subsets of molecular formulae were assessed using a Wilcoxon test. To investigate the influence of in situ sediment temperature on the porewater DOM composition,a linear model between log-transformed sediment temperature and (1) $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ Bray-Curtis dissimilarity and (2) FDOMpARAFAc Bray-Curtis dissimilarity Was calculated, comparing the hottest,presumably sterile sample $( > 1 0 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ to all other hydrothermal samples.For approach (1), $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } } ,$ relative intensities of all detected molecular formulae were used as the input，whereas for approach (2)，FDOMpARAFAc，relative abundances of the seven PARAFAC components were used.

# Discussion

# Sediment temperature and petroleum impregnation govern the porewater dissolved organic matter composition

We observed significant trends between sediment temperature and DOM composition，specifically $\mathrm { D O C } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ concentrations were significantly lower $( R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 5 6$ ， $p < 1 0 ^ { - 8 }$ ， $\mathrm { d f } = 4 0$ Fig. 4a) and dissimilarities of $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ and FDOM decreased with increasing temperatures $( R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 1$ ， $\mathrm { p } < 1 0 ^ { - 1 1 }$ ， $\mathrm { d f } = 4 0$ ， Fig. 4b; $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 4 9$ $p < 1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ $\mathrm { d f } = 3 7$ ,Fig. 4c).Furthermore, we noticed an increasing sulfurization of DOM at high temperature compared to low temperature sites (AbioS, $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 6 3$ ， $p < 1 0 ^ { - 9 }$ ， $\mathrm { d f } = 4 0$ ，Fig. 4d; Supporting Information Table S3). Principle coordinate analysis revealed that the molecular composition of surficial porewater DOM $\left( 0 { - } 4 \thinspace \mathrm { c m } \right)$ was comparable between all sampling locations,independent of local thermal regimes and microbial mat coverage (samples clustering in gray ellipsis labeled“surficial” in Fig. 5a; Supporting Information Fig. S6). The compositional similarity of DOM aligns with a recent study investigating sedimentary organic matter from the Southern Guaymas Basin which showed similar carbohydrate compositions in surface sediments of hydrothermal and non-hydrothermal sites (Gajendra et al. 2O24). In our study, surficial porewater $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ was enriched with unsaturated aliphatic, N-rich compounds along with protein-like FDOM component C4 (compare direction of respective vectors and gray ellipse in Fig. 5b). Such molecular signatures are indicative for microbial and bioavailable DOMin anoxic porewaters (Schmidt et al.2017; Zhou et al. 2O22).Besides high abundances of aliphatic and N-containing molecular formulae,also amino acids were previously observed to be formed in laboratory heating experiments with Guaymas Basin sediments (Lin et al. 2017). Our field study thus confirms that DOM with labile molecular characteristics is being mobilized，as previously proposed by Lin et al.(2O17).However,it remains difficult to differentiate between abiotically heat-mobilized and microbially mobilized DOM due to their similar molecular properties.

Across hydrothermally influenced sites,sediment temperature was a major factor related to changes in $\mathrm { D O C } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ concentrations and DOM composition; temperature emerged as a significant explanatory variable in linear models (Fig. 4a-d),and in the temperature vector aligning with PC2 (Fig. 5b). Sediment temperature also controls microbial abundance in Guaymas Basin sediments (Lagostina et al. 2O21) with microbial activity typically being observed onlybelow $\sim 6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Meyer et al. 2013;McKay et al.2O16).With declining microbial activities at temperatures above $\sim 6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ thermal modifications of sedimentary organic matter and heat-driven hydrolysis of macromolecules become increasingly dominant (Lin et al. 2O17; Gajendra et al. 2024). The relationships between $\mathrm { D O C } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ concentrations and $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ composition with sediment temperature can thus be explained by (1）heat mobilization of DOM at high temperatures where DOM is transported to the sediment surface by advective fluxes, leaving behind porewater with low $\mathrm { D O C } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ concentrations,and (2）increasing microbial transformations in shallow subsurface sediments at $< 6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ where heat-mobilized DOM and microbiallyderived labile DOM are likely to share molecular signatures.

Dissolved organic matter from Aceto Balsamico was molecularly distinct compared to the other samples in PCoA (Fig. 5), although it fit well into the linear model relating temperature to $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ dissimilarity (Fig.4b). $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ and CDOM at Aceto Balsamico site were more reduced (low $\mathrm { O } / \mathrm { C }$ ratios)，more aromatic and hydrophobic,and had relatively low molecular masses (Fig. 5b; vectors of $\mathrm { O } / \mathrm { C } .$ $\operatorname { A I } _ { \mathrm { m o d } } ,$ $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } ,$ $\mathrm { N S O } : \mathrm { C } , m / z )$ compared to the other sites.These molecular signatures of DOM from Aceto Balsamico were indicative of petroleum-derived DOM (Seidel et al. 2016;Harriman et al. 2017;Podgorski et al. 2021; Brunjes et al.2O22)，which was further supported by highest intensities of petroleum-associated PARAFAC components C1 and C3 at this site (Fig. 2a,b). Our findings therefore highlight the complex interplay between thermal and microbial processes in shaping DOM composition and DOC concentrations in hydrothermal porewaters.

# Sulfurization of dissolved organic matter in hydrothermal sediments

IntheGuaymasBasinhydrothermalporewater, S-containing molecular formulae in $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ were strongly correlated with sediment temperature $\left( \tau = 0 . 3 8 \right.$ ， $p < 0 . 0 0 1$ $n = 4 7$ ； Figs.5b,4d; Supporting Information Table S3). High DOS contents in porewater $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ is consistent with previous studies of non-hydrothermal sulfidic sediments (Schmidt et al. 2009; Seidel et al． 2014； Pohlabeln et al.2O17) and shallow hydrothermal systems with elevated temperature (Gomez-Saez et al. 2016). Abiotic sulfurization，where sulfide reacts with organic matter, including DOM,is a common process resulting in sulfurenriched compounds presumably resistant to microbial degradation (Kohnen et al. 1989;Pohlabeln et al. 2017).

The high sulfide concentrations in Guaymas Basin sediments,reaching several millimolar just below the surface (McKay et al. 2012; Teske et al. 2016) are in support of abiotic sulfurization processes.However,also the dissolution of the abundant S-containing petroleum compounds in sediments and porewaters (Simoneit 1985；Simoneit 1988; Mara et al. 2O22) could contribute to the high abundance of porewater DOS.

The strong correlation between weighted-average S-content and the index for abiotic sulfurization of DOM，AbioS $( \tau = 0 . 5 3$ ， $p < 0 . 0 0 1$ ， $n = 4 7$ )，however，suggests sulfurization as a key process behind the high DOS contribution in the porewater. Additionally, S-containing molecular formulae are positively correlated with sediment temperature, but not sediment depth $( \tau = 0 . 3 8$ $p < 0 . 0 0 1$ $n = 4 7$ and $\tau = 0 . 1 4$ $p > 0 . 0 5$ $n = 3 4$ ； Supporting Information Table S3)，as evident from comparing vectors of temperature,AbioS,and S (Fig. 5b).This relationship indicated that high temperatures increase DOM sulfurization rates,as has been observed in previous laboratory experiments (Kok，Schouten，and Sinninghe Damsté 2000; Pohlabeln et al.2O17).The sum of AbioS molecular formulae reached up to $0 . 4 3 \%$ of total signal intensity in hot sediment porewater DOM,which was up to 1O times higher than in cold sulfidic porewater from Black Sea sediments (Gomez-Saez et al.2O21)，and cold deep Fennoscandian Shield fracture waters (Osterholz et al. 2022).

Porewater $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ near the colder sediment water interface contained fewer S-containing molecular formulae compared to hot samples (Figs. 5b,4d). The observed DOS loss along the temperature gradient suggests microbial degradation，considering the presence of sulfate-reducing and hydrocarbon degrading bacteria in surface sediments (Ramirez et al. 2021).Furthermore，precipitation due to changes in solubility or mixing with low DOS seawater could explain the apparent DOS decrease in surface sediments.While sulfurization is generally considered to increase DOM recalcitrance (Pohlabeln et al. 2O17),recent studies suggested that most DOS is bioavailable(Osterholz et al. 2022;Hu et al. 2023;Ibrahim and Tremblay 2023). Yet, $8 { - } 3 9 \%$ of oceanic DOS is derived from sulfurization (Phillips et al.2022;Ibrahim and Tremblay 2023) and $3 8 \%$ of S-containing molecular formulae from hydrothermal DOM in our study were found in the overlying water column (Fig. 6c; Table 2). Our study thus indicates a considerable recalcitrance of DOS and the potential for hydrothermal sources to contribute to oceanic DOs.

# Hydrothermal porewater is a source for oceanic dissolved organic matter

Sediment porewater, transported via diffusive fluxes between sediment and water column,is generally considered a DOM source to oceanic bottom waters (Burdige and Komada 2015; Chen et al. 2O23).In the Guaymas Basin,advective fluxes accelerate this process，explaining the observed elevated bottom water DOC concentrations $( 9 0 3 \pm 4 7 1 \mu \mathrm { M } _ { \mathrm { \scriptscriptstyle { H } } }$ ：Supporting Information Table S1).Elevated bottom water DOC concentrations between 110 and $2 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ were also previously reported from the Southern rift zone (Lin et al. 2O17； Ziervogel and Arnosti 2O2O). The majority of DOC in bottom waters was not extractable by the applied SPE method (see Supporting Information Discussion about extraction efficiencies).Parts of the non-extractable DOC at hydrothermal sites were shown to be bioavailable (Rossel et al. 2O15) and have been suggested to be comprised of low molecular-weight organic acids and amino acids (Hawkes et al. 2O15b),which are in fact present in high concentrations in Guaymas Basin porewaters (Martens 1990; Teske et al. 2016; Zhuang et al. 2019).We consequently infer that a substantial portion of hydrothermally mobilized DOC transported to the water column of the Guaymas Basin is likely bioavailable, consistent with previous experimental findings (Lin et al.2O17). However, the elevated bottom water $\mathrm { D O C } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ concentrations,representing extractable DOC (average $7 7 \pm 4 4 \mu \mathrm { M }$ $n = 9$ ）thatis approximately 3.5 times higher than those in the deep Pacific Ocean (ca. $2 2 \pm 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ $\mathrm { D O C } _ { \mathrm { S P E } } ;$ Bercovici, Dittmar, and Niggemann 2O23） indicate that a significant fraction of more recalcitrant hydrothermal $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ is transported to the ocean.This is also in line with laboratory incubation experiments showing that a subfraction of hydrothermal $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ is not readily biodegradable (Rossel et al. 2015).

Bottom water and porewater DOM shared 7598 $( 5 3 \% )$ molecular formulae,with only $3 \%$ of all detected molecular formulae being exclusive to bottom water DOM (Fig. 6a; Table 2). This molecular overlap indicates extensive exchange of porewater with bottom water DOM.Hydrothermally driven porewater fluxes therefore represent an important mechanism transporting DOM to the water column.Additionally，2177 molecular formulae exclusive to hydrothermally active sites were detected in bottom water $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } } ,$ accounting for up to $2 6 \%$ of all molecular formulae detected in bottom water samples (Fig. 6b). The majority of these molecular formulae $( 8 2 \% )$ had $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C }$ ratios $< 1 . 5$ ,athreshold used to identify recalcitrant DOM (D'Andrilli et al. 2015). A smaller proportion of aliphatic and saturated compounds $( 1 8 \% )$ are presumed to be more bioavailable but occurred only at relatively low abundances (Fig.6b；yellow bubble sizes $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C } \geq 1 . 5 )$ . In contrast，highly unsaturated and carboxyl-rich alicyclic molecules were abundant $( 5 7 \%$ and $3 1 \%$ ；Table 2). Carboxyl-rich alicyclic molecules are a major structural component of refractory oceanic DOM (Hertkorn et al. 2Oo6) and can also be derived from biodegradation of petroleum-derived DOM (Podgorski et al. 2021). Additionally,aromatic and polycondensed aromatic molecular formulae were abundant( $1 7 \%$ and $9 \%$ ；Table 2)，likely representingpartlybiodegradedpetroleum-derived DOM. These more recalcitrant compounds are likely derived from the selective degradation of aliphatic compounds,as shown by previous laboratory incubations with petroleum-derived DOM (Harriman et al. 2O17; Brünjes et al. 2O22) and a study at a terrestrial oil spill site (Podgorski et al. 2021).

Hydrothermal DOM detected in bottom water contained 836 S-containing molecular formulae $( 3 8 \%$ ；Fig.6c)，likely resulting from both abiotic sulfurization and petroleum dissolution，aspreviously discussed.We notably observeda decrease of the relative contribution of sulfurized DOM from hot sediments toward the sediment-water interface,along with the absence of AbioS molecular formulae in bottom water samples (Fig. 4d; Supporting Information Fig. S5). This pattern suggests that hydrothermal DOS exported to the water column originated primarily from petroleum dissolution, whereas sulfurization of porewater DOM was the major contributor for DOS at greater sediment depths in our study. Petroleum-derived DOs can be more polar and water-soluble compared to hydrocarbons consisting solely of carbon and hydrogen (Liu and Kujawinski 2O15). Therefore, these compounds are often relatively enriched in terrestrial environments (Dvorski et al. 2016; Islam et al. 2016),and laboratory incubation experiments (Hegazi et al. 2O12; Liu and Kujawinski 2015; Brunjes et al.2O22).We identified a prominent cluster of oxygen-deficient，aromatic S-containing molecular formulae (Fig. 6c)，indicative of benzothiophene-derivatives (Hertkorn et al.2O16).Polycyclic aromatic sulfur heterocycles such as benzothiophenes are resistant to microbial degradation (Kropp and Fedorak 1998;Hegazi et al. 2012）and accumulate in petroleum-rich Guaymas Basin sediment and porewater (Mara et al.2O22). Our findings therefore suggest that hydrothermal sediments release petroleum-derived DOS with long residence times into the ocean,consistent with the working hypothesis that natural petroleum sepages are sources of recalcitrant DOs in deep-sea environments (Bruinjes et al. 2022).

The presence of a recalcitrant hydrothermal DOM fraction in bottom water was further demonstrated by the high molecular similarity ( $6 6 \%$ ：0.44 Bray-Curtis dissimilarity） between bottom water and $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ from hot sites $\mathrm { ( > 1 0 6 ^ { \circ } C ; }$ Fig. 4b). Bottom water samples contained 791 molecular formulae that were also present at $> 1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ sediment temperature (Table 2; Supporting Information Fig. S6b),indicating thermal stability and resistance to microbial degradation over timescales of advective upward porewater flow (approximately $5 0 \mathrm { c m } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ Teske et al. 2016). Notably, $\sim 1 0 \%$ (336) of the molecular formulae present in a reference sample of NEqPIW $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ were also found in hydrothermal bottom water $\mathrm { D O M } _ { \mathrm { S P E } }$ . Since NEqPIW DOM represents mesopelagic water containing deepsea refractory DOM (Green et al. 2014; Osterholz et al. 2015), this similarity suggests that hydrothermal sediments release DOM comparable to refractory deep-sea DOM.

Our data indicate that the generation and dissolution of hydrothermal petroleum contributes to the release of recalcitrant DOM into overlying water. Hydrothermal petroleum generation in deep-ocean spreading centers is not limited to the Guaymas Basin，but also occurs in the Pescadero Basin in the Gulf of California (Paduan et al. 2O18), Escanaba Trough in the northeast Pacific Ocean (Kvenvolden and Simoneit 1990), Middle Valley at the northern Juan de Fuca Ridge (Simoneit

1994)and Rainbow Field at the Mid-Atlantic Ridge (Simoneit et al.2OO4).Further research is needed to evaluate the global contribution of hydrothermal (petroleum） to deep-sea DOM and its impact on the marine carbon cycle.

# Conclusions

We combined optical and molecular analyses to investigate hydrothermal porewater DOM and its potential release into the ocean.We found significant relationships between in situ sediment temperature, DOM composition,and DOC concentrations, probably driven by(1) thermal desorption and breakdown of sedimentary organic matter，with increasing DOM sulfurization at high temperatures,and (2) the accumulation of hydrothermally mobilized DOM and microbial DOM at lower temperatures below the sediment surface. Our findings align with previous laboratory experiments showing the mobilization of bioavailable DOM through hydrothermal heating of organic-rich sediments.Furthermore,we detected highly unsaturated and particularly sulfur-rich DOM and petroleum-like FDOM components in the extractable porewater DOM. These hydrothermally-mobilized compounds were also present in bottom water DOM, suggesting their contribution to recalcitrant marine DOM，including oceanic DOs. While high-temperature samples contained highly sulfurized DOM，recalcitrant DOS compounds released into the bottom water were predominantly associated with water-soluble petroleum compounds.Our study highlights the role of organic-rich hydrothermal sediments in releasing both,potentially labile and recalcitrant DOM to the ocean, indicating that petroleum generation in these systems contributes to marine recalcitrant DOM. These results underscore the need to integrate natural hydrothermal petroleum seepages into oceanic carbon and nutrient budgets.Quantifying the release of recalcitrant DOC as well as dissolved organic S,N,and $\mathrm { P }$ are critical to assess the impact of hydrothermal sediments on the marine DOM cycle.
