# Abstract

Permafrost thaw may increase the production of neurotoxic methylmercury (MeHg) in northern peatlands, but the downstream delivery of MeHg is uncertain.We quantified total mercury $\mathrm { ( T H g ) }$ and MeHg concentrations in lakes and streams along a $1 7 0 0 \mathrm { k m }$ permafrost transect in boreal western Canada to determine the influence of regional permafrost extent compared to local lake and catchment characteristics.In lakes,we assessed sediment microbial communities and modeled potential rates of water column photodemethylation (PD). Regardless of permafrost conditions, peatlands were the primary sources of MeHg across the transect as MeHg concentrations in streams increased with aromatic disolved organic carbon (DOC),iron,and lower pH. Higher DOC and greater catchment peatland extent were further asociated with higher stream %MeHg (MeHg/THg). Peatland lakes were potential MeHg sinks，with lower MeHg concentrations than streams (i $\mathrm { n e a n } \pm 1 \mathrm { S D }$ ： $0 . 1 9 \pm 0 . 2 3$ and $0 . 4 7 \pm 0 . 7 7 ~ \mathrm { n g ~ M e H g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ , respectively),and larger stream catchments had lower %MeHg where PD may occur in abundant smallakes. Microbial communities in lake sediments showed that abundance of $\mathrm { H g }$ reducing genes (merA) predominated over $\mathrm { H g }$ methylating (hgcA) and MeHg demethylating (merB) genes. The effects of permafrost extent on MeHg processes in lakes were secondary to the influence of local catchment characteristics,but lakes in regions with less permafrost had higher DOC concentrations,higher $\% \mathrm { M e H g } ,$ and lower potential rates of PD. Our study highlights a need to understand the impacts of climate change on MeHg source and sink processes,particularly as mediated through changes to peatland DOC,to improve projections of future MeHg concentrations in northern catchments.

Climatewarmingandpermafrostthaw(Gibson et al.2O21） will likely affect concentrations of neurotoxic methylmercury (MeHg) in northern peatland-rich catchments (Gordon et al. 2016; Tarbier et al. 2021). Many northern boreal catchments have widespread peatlands with large stocks of atmospherically deposited mercury $\mathrm { ( H g ) }$ bound to soil organic matter (Schuster et al. 2O18).Favorable environmental conditions for MeHg production render peatlands as important sources of MeHg in boreal catchments (St.Louis et al. 1994),but the presence of permafrost in peatlands may influence MeHg production，degradation，and transport through its influence on groundwater connectivity and soil environmental conditions (Gordon et al. 2O16; Poulin et al.2019; Tarbier et al. 2021). Still, linkages between permafrost conditions and downstream MeHg delivery in northerncatchmentsarepoorlyunderstood(Zolkos etal.2O2O).Peatlands and peatland-rich catchments contain abundant shallow lakes connected to the stream network (Olefeldt et al. 2021),yet in these lakes, the balance between MeHg production and degradation through microbial and photochemical processes is unknown.Understanding spatial trends of MeHg concentrations in northern catchments can inform our ability to project future changes to MeHg cycling and how these changes may impact Indigenous peoples'cultural and physical well-being who rely on fish as healthy traditional food (Wheatley and Wheatley 2000).

Microbes with the hgcAB gene cluster methylate inorganic divalent $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ into MeHg，but methylation additionally depends on environmental conditions and $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ bioavailability (Parks et al. 2013; Bravo and Cosio 202O). High $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ methylation efficiency is often associated with anoxic conditions rich with alternative terminal electron acceptors (e.g., sulfate $[ \mathrm { S O _ { 4 } } ]$ or ferric iron; Bravo and Cosio 2O2O),although elevated $S \mathrm { O } _ { 4 }$ concentrations may limit methylation in lakes (Gilmour et al.1992; Shao et al. 2012). Labile dissolved organic matter (DOM) can further stimulate microbial activity and $\mathrm { H g }$ bioavailability (Chiasson-Gould et al. 2O14； Klapstein and O'Driscoll 2O18). Thus, certain regions in wetlands and lakes areoften favorable locations for methylation(Mitchell et al. $2 0 0 8 a _ { i } ^ { \cdot }$ ：Branfireun et al. 2O2O).In addition,permafrost thaw in peatlands often leads to land surface collapse, inundation，and increased groundwater interactions,creating $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ methylation hotspots(Gordon et al. 2016； Fahnestock et al. 2019; Poulin et al. 2019; Tarbier et al. 2021).

Degradation of MeHg through microbial and photochemical processes can influence MeHg concentration in freshwaters following its production.The microbial pathways of $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ reduction and reductive demethylation are associated with the detoxifying mer operon,where merB cleaves MeHg into $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ and methane,and merA reduces $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ into volatile gaseous $\mathrm { H g ^ { 0 } }$ that can be emitted to the atmosphere (Boyd and Barkay 2O12).Whereas microbial MeHg production occurs primarily in anoxic environments,the mer operon occurs in both aerobic and anaerobic microbes (Christakis et al. 2021). Solar breakdown (photodemethylation [PD]) of MeHg is considered the primary MeHg sink in northern lakes (Lehnherr and St. Louis 2O09).A key control on PD is water column ultraviolet (UV） radiation intensity. High DOM concentrations in small peatland lakes can rapidly attenuate UV radiation through the water column，inhibiting demethylation (Klapstein et al. 2O18).The balance between $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ methylation and MeHg demethylation can vary among lakes,affecting lake MeHg concentrations and downstream transport (Hammerschmidt et al. 2OO6; Lehnherr et al. $2 0 1 2 b$ ；Girard et al. 2016; Branfireun et al. 2020).

Here,we assessed concentrations of $\mathrm { H g }$ and MeHg within lakes and streams within peatland-rich catchments on a $1 7 0 0 \mathrm { k m }$ latitudinal and permafrost transect in the Interior Plains region of northwestern Canada (Fig.1). Some of the most rapid warming on Earth has occurred in this region, leading to extensive permafrost thaw in peatlands (Gibson et al.2O21).The transect provides a space-for-time substitution for projecting climate change;southern sites can be considered an analog for future conditions in northern sites where climate and permafrost conditions vary along otherwise similar physiography and land cover (Kuhn et al. 2O21). Our focal research aims were: (i) determining how permafrost conditions and peatland extent interact in determining MeHg concentrations in lakes and streams and (i) examining how microbial and photochemical processes may influence MeHg concentrations in small,organic-rich lakes.We anticipated that higher MeHg concentrations would covary with a greater extent of peatlands and occur within the rapidly thawing discontinuouspermafrost regions. In northern freshwaters, reduced PD is predicted to occur with permafrost thaw and the associated release of DOM (Chételat et al.2022). Our investigation of microbial-driven methylation was exploratory since connections between hgcA gene abundance and MeHg concentrations remain unclear (Christensen et al. 2O19).Our study will thus provide knowledge to help us understand future MeHg concentrations and export from northern peatland-rich catchments.

# Materials and methods

# Study area

We sampled 25 lakes and 47 streams within the Interior Plains of northwestern Canada,spanning a $1 7 0 0 \mathrm { k m }$ latitudinal gradient from $5 5 . 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ to $6 8 . 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ (Fig. 1; Supporting Information Fig.S1). The streams ranged in size from headwaters to major rivers (Table 1),and the small lakes and streams were nested within larger catchments at several sites (se Supporting Information Fig. S1).The study encompassed lands of the Dehcho Dene,Gwich'in，Inuvialuit，Dene Tha'，Woodland Cree，Big Stone Cree,and Métis people. Surficial geology is predominantlyglacialandglaciolacustrinesediments (Fulton 1995)，and the catchments contain widespread peatlands (Hugelius et al. 2O2O; Fig. 1). The sampling locations were clustered in six regions (Table 1),where within each region， all samplingsiteswere $< 2 0 0 \ \mathrm { k m }$ apart (Supporting Information Fig. S1).White spruce (Picea glauca), balsampoplar(Populusbalsamifera)，balsam fir(Abies balsamea),and trembling aspen (Populus tremuloides) were the main tree species in upland forests,with deciduous species decreasing with latitude.Peatlands in the study area from $5 9 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } N }$ and northward were complexes of permafrost peat plateaus and permafrost-free bogs and fens (Ecosystem Classification Group 2009；Olefeldt et al. 2021)，and at $5 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ complexes of permafrost-free bogs and fens, either non-treed or containing sparse canopies of black spruce (Picea mariana), and tamarack (Larix laricina) (Olefeldt et al.2014). Small (area $< 0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ k m } ^ { 2 }$ ）to midsize $( < 1 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 } )$ organic-rich lakes are a common landscape feature of the catchments (Olefeldt et al. 2021). The regional climate is cool and sub-humid, with mean annual air temperature (MAAT) ranging from $1 . 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the southernmost sites to $- 9 . 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the northernmost sites (Table 1; Fick and Hijmans 2017).

Catchment boundaries and area (Table 1) were determined with the hydrology toolset on ArcGIS 10.3 using the Canadian DigitalElevationModel(CDEM;NaturalResources Canada 2O13)，with manual adjustments required in some regions of low relief using the National Hydro Network (Natural Resources Canada 2O16) as reference. The proportion of lowland in each catchment (Table 1) was determined using the slope tool on ArcGIS 10.3.After masking waterbodies from the National Hydro Network (Natural Resources Canada 2016),areas with slopes $< 1 ^ { \circ }$ were classified as lowland and presumed proportional to peatland cover (Olefeldt et al.2O14).Although lowlands are dominated by organic peatland soilsin the region(Ecosystem Classification Group 2Oo9),some small, disconnected peatlands or uplands may have been misclassified due to the $3 0 \times 3 0 \mathrm { m }$ resolution of the CDEM.

# Gene abundance and diversity of hgcA and merAB in lake sediments

Near the edge of 2O lakes (excluding $6 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ sites)，we collected the top $1 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ of sediment using a sterile trowel and sterile gloved hands for microbial analyses at three locations. Samples from the three locations per lake were combined, then mixed and stored in sterile Whirl-Paks (Nasco) with the air pressed out to minimize oxygen exposure at $2 { - } 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until transferred to a $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ freezer within 5 d and subsequently to a $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ freezer within $1 4 \mathrm { d }$ ：

DNA was extracted in duplicate from $0 . 6 5 \mathrm { g }$ of homogenized wet sediments using the DNeasy PowerSoil kit (QIAGEN N.V.). Duplicates were pooled and concentrated by ethanol precipitation $( 3 \times$ volume) with sodium acetate (1/1O volume） and resuspended in Tris-Cl ( $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ ，pH 8.5). A nuclease-free wateronly negative control and a positive control consisting of a ZymoBIOMICS Microbial Community Standard (Zymo Research Corporation) were also extracted in duplicate and pooled.

We prepared sequencing libraries from $2 5 0 \mathrm { n g }$ DNA per sample.DNA extracted from the sediment of one lake in the $6 3 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } N }$ region (W1) was of insufficient quantity to perform further analysis.The negative control yielded insufficient DNA for library preparation.Libraries were prepared with the NEBNext@ UltraTM II FS DNA Library Prep Kit for llumina (New England Biolabs).All nucleic acid concentrations and libraries were quantified using a Qubit dsDNA High Sensitivity Assay Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) on a Qubit 3.O Fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific).Library quality was confirmed on a Bioanalyzer HS DNA chip (Agilent) before pooling at equimolar concentrations into a single multiplexed sample. Samples were sequenced on a NextSeq 5Oo using a High Output Kit v2 (3oo cycles，paired-end) (llumina, Inc). Negative and positive controls revealed no significant contamination. Sequencing yielded an average of 37 (standard error $= 2 . 2 5$ SE) million reads per sample after quality fltering using Fastp (Chen et al. 2018),with a percentage of 92 $( \mathrm { S E } = 0 . 0 0 1 $ ）and 87 $\mathbf { S E } = 0 . 0 0 2 )$ of bases,respectively.Microbial data are available in the European Nucleotide Archive at EMBL-EBI with access number PRJEB36558.

We used the hgcA gene as a genetic marker for microbial $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ methylation and merA and merB as genetic markers for $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ reduction and MeHg demethylation.We assessed gene abundance using GraftM, identifying gene clades (i.e.,representative monophyletic groups） in shotgun metagenomics data and assigning taxonomic classifications based on phylogenetic placements into gene trees built from reference sequences (Boyd et al. 2O18). GraftM gene-specific packages were created using curated reference sequences of hgcA $( n = 2 4 3$ ; Gionfriddo et al. 2020),merA $( n = 2 1 9$ ； Boyd and Barkay 2012),and merB $( n = 3 5 9$ ; Mistry et al. 2O21).We used the microbial housekeeping gene 16S rRNA to normalize hgcA,merA,and merB gene abundances,providing an estimated proportion of $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ methylators, $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ -reducing,and MeHg demethylating microbes,respectively，within each site.16S rRNA sequences were identified using the default gene package provided by GraftM. We expected the ratios of hgcA,merA,and merB relative to 16S to represent the gene's proportional presence in the microbial communities.

# Statistical methods

We used permutational ANOVA (perANOVA) tests to examine the variability among waterbody type and region for MeHg concentrations, THg concentrations,and MeHg to THg ratio (hereafter $\% \mathrm { M e H g }$ ，a proxy for net MeHg formation; Bravo and Cosio 2O2O),including an interaction parameter, with lmPerm (Wheeler and Torchiano 2O16).We used the same method to examine variability in additional water chemistry parameters among waterbody type and region, including concentrations of DOC,Fe, S,and pH, $\mathrm { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ,and EC.The interaction parameter tested the hypothesis that the region would influence lake or stream characteristics.

Linear regressions were used to estimate the strength and direction of the relationship between water chemistry variables and MeHg,exploring water chemistry parameters that have been previously shown to covary with MeHg, including DOC concentrations and composition，pH，Fe，THg，and S (Lavoie et al. 2019; Bravo and Cosio 202O). Data were logtransformed if Shapiro-Wilk tests indicated non-normality in distributions of the residuals.Path analysis was used to examine potential direct (DOC concentrations) and indirect drivers (permafrost region， peatland coverage， catchmentsize) influencing MeHg concentrations in lakes and streams with the piecewiseSEM package (Lefcheck 2O16; code adapted from

St Pierre et al. 2O19)；further details are described in the Supporting Information document. We conducted analyses in RStudio@ utilizing R version 3.6.1 (RS Team, 2016).

To compare hgcA and merA gene diversity across the transect,we estimated Aitchison distance matrices between each pairwise combination of sites for the abundances of the clades with DEICODE(Bolyen et al. 2019;Martino et al. 2019) and tested if gene proportions and diversity varied across permafrost extent using perANOVA and permutational multivariate ANOVA, respectively.

# Results and discussion

# Variability of $\mathrm { T H g }$ and MeHg in lakes and streams

Lakes and streams had high variability across the transect in both filtered and unfiltered $\mathrm { T H g }$ and MeHg concentrations, with MeHg concentrations varying by more than two orders of magnitude (Fig. 2). Council of Ministers of the Environment 2O03).Most of the $\mathrm { { M e H g } }$ and $\mathrm { T H g }$ were in the dissolved form,which has the potential to be more bioavailable than particle-bound forms (Le Faucheur et al. 2014),with $8 6 \% \pm 1 3 \%$ of MeHg and $8 1 \% \pm 1 8 \%$ of $\mathrm { T H g }$ in dissolved form in streams and $7 0 \% \pm$ $1 8 \%$ of MeHg and $7 5 \% \pm 2 1 \%$ of $\mathrm { T H g }$ in lakes. # Drivers of MeHg and %MeHg in lakes and streams

The regions sampled across the continental permafrost transect had distinct water chemistry characteristics. Some parameters exhibited latitudinal trends,with Fe concentrations increasing and $\mathrm { p H }$ decreasing toward northern sites (Fig. 1B,C). Other parameters were regionally distinct, such as concentrations of DOC,S，and EC (Fig.1D-F).Widespread permafrost was generally associated with low EC and $\mathrm { p H } ,$ likely indicating low hydrological connectivity with deeper groundwater sources (Frey and McClelland 2Oo9).Regions with lower permafrost coverage had more variable concentrations of DOC, ions,and nutrients,suggesting that land cover, surficial geology，and topography of the sampled regions influenced the connectivity to groundwater discharge and groundwater-fed fen peatlands (Frey and McClelland 2009; Gordon et al. 2016; Kuhn et al. 2O21).Lakes and streams at $5 9 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } N } .$ ，a region with low permafrost extent,had particularly high indicators of groundwater connectivity, that is, EC and S (Frey and McClelland 2009; Kuhn et al. 2021). There was also a consistent trend of higher Fe concentrations, $\mathrm { p H }$ ，and EC in streams than in lakes (Fig.1).

Relationships varied between MeHg concentrations and water chemistry parameters among streams and lakes (Fig. 3). Concentrations of MeHg increased with DOC concentrations and $\mathrm { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ in both streams and lakes,but streams had higher MeHg concentrations than lakes at similar levels of DOC and $A _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Fig. 3A,B). $A _ { 2 5 4 }$ was a better predictor of $\mathrm { { M e H g } }$ concentrations for streams based on $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 }$ compared to DOC and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Fig.3A,B; Supporting Information Fig. S2), suggesting higher absolute quantities of aromatic carbon may drive MeHg transport vs.higher concentrations of the total DOC pool or higher percent aromaticity. Slopes of $\mathrm { T H g }$ and MeHg against DOC have been explored in many aquatic environments,and we found differences in the strength of relationships among lakes and streams using untransformed data (THg-DOC:lake slope $= - 0 . 0 3 8 ~ \mathrm { n g } ~ \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } .$ 、 $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 3$ ， $p = 0 . 0 4$ ； stream slope $= 0 . 0 4 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 }$ $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 0 7$ $p = 0 . 0 5$ MeHg-DOC: lake slope $= 0 . 0 0 6 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 }$ $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 5$ $p = 0 . 0 3$ stream slope $= 0 . 0 5 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 }$ ， $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 5 1$ ， $p < 0 . 0 0 1$ ). DOC concentrations explained $\leq 1 5 \%$ of the variability in lake

MeHg concentrations and lake/stream THg concentrations, while stream MeHg-DOC had the strongest relationship. These results suggest a decoupling of $\mathrm { T H g }$ and organic matter input in lakes,and a stronger linkage between MeHg and DOC concentrations in streams as has been observed in other wetland-influenced catchments (Lavoie et al. 2019; Branfireun et al. 2020).

Stream and lake DOM also differed in their chemical composition.Stream DOM was relatively more aromatic and dominated by humic acids (associating with higher humification index, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } ,$ and PARAFAC Component 2; see Supporting Information Figs. S3,S4; Table S3),which can facilitate binding with MeHg (Klapstein and O'Driscoll 2018) and reduce MeHg bioavailability (Tsui and Finlay 2O11). In contrast, lake DOM had lower aromaticity and was relatively more influenced by amino acids (associating with higher biological index and PARAFAC Component 4; see Supporting Information Figs. S3, S4; Table S3)，which can stimulate MeHg production in lake sediments (Klapstein and O'Driscoll 2018). Therefore,DOM in streams was likely associated with terrestrially derived sources,while lake DOM may have been relatively more influenced by autochthonous， algal DOM production,littoral vegetation exudates,or selective photochemical degradation of humic DOM (Tank et al. 2011; Kellerman et al. 2O15),as has been observed in other Canadian ecozones (Aukes et al. 2O21). The dominance of terrestrial sources in streams aligns with findings from $\mathrm { H g }$ stable isotopes in a headwater peatland catchment,where aqueous $\mathrm { H g }$ was primarily derived from dry deposition on vegetation (Woerndle et al. 2018).

Lake and stream water chemistry indicated peatlands as primary sources of DOM and MeHg. Concentrations of MeHg in streams increased with lower pH and higher concentrations of Fe and THg (Fig. 3C-E), suggesting that MeHg was delivered to streams in runoff generated from peatlands with acidic and reducing conditions (St. Louis et al. 1994; Bourbonniere 2009). For lakes,we found that MeHg concentrations increased with groundwater indicators (i.e.， S,EC, $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ ，K, $\mathrm { S O } _ { 4 } .$ S； Fig. 3F; Supporting Information Fig. S5；Frey and McClelland 2009; Kuhn et al. 2021). These relationships may point to groundwater-rich lakes as high in situ MeHg production sites with the exchange of MeHg from sediment to the water column，potentially due to the supply of $S O _ { 4 } – S$ for sulfatereducing bacteria (Bravo and Cosio 2O2O).However，we also found that higher concentrations of $S O _ { 4 } – S$ and MeHg were associated with a lower proportional abundance of the hgcA $\mathrm { ( H g ^ { I I } }$ methylating) gene in lake sediments (Supporting Information Figs. S5D,S5H; Fig.3H).Although the relationship between the abundance of the hgcA gene and in situ biological methylation is unclear, these results suggest that its potential activity may have been decreased at higher MeHg and $S O _ { 4 }$ -S concentrations.As groundwater-rich fens are abundant in the Interior Plains (Ecosystem Classification Group 2Oo9; Quinton et al.2Oo9)，they are potentially important external MeHg sources for these lakes,as fens readily convey water in their channels (Quinton et al. 2Oo9) and have been previously identified as $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ methylation hotspots in boreal sites (Gordon et al.2016; Fahnestock et al. 2019; Poulin et al. 2019; Tarbier et al.2O21).Lakes that receive high DOM inputs from their surrounding catchment,as is typical for peatland lakes in the Interior Plains (Olefeldt et al. 2O13), have also previously been shown to have high MeHg concentrations but low rates of in situ methylation (Bravo et al. 2O17; Branfireun et al. 2020).

# Gene abundance and diversity of hgcA and merAB in lake sediments

Peatland lakes generally had a greater abundance of microbial genes associated with reducing rather than methylating $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ .The microbial communities in lake sediments had a $\sim$ ninefold greater_abundance of gene sequences for $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ reduction than $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ methylation,based on merA and hgcA genes, respectively (Fig. 5A). These results may be unsurprising given that $\mathrm { H g } ^ { \mathrm { I I } }$ methylators are relatively low in abundance in nature.When present,methylators are expected to constitute $< 5 \%$ of the bulk microbial community (Podar et al. 2015; Christensen et al. 2019; Christakis et al. 2021). The merA gene is more ubiquitous and was found in $\sim 8 \%$ of microbial genomes in public databases (Christakis et al. 2O21). By contrast, the gene for MeHg demethylation,merB,had the lowest abundance compared to hgcA and merA (Fig. 5A). The proportion of merB in microbial communities is expected to be lower than for merA,as it is present in only $2 \%$ of genomes in public databases (Christakis et al. 2O21).Furthermore,gene duplication could contribute to greater relative proportions of merA than merB (Christakis et al. 2O21).Nevertheless,our data suggest that detoxification potentially occurs via merA over the merB pathway.With permafrost thaw, emission of $\mathrm { H g ^ { 0 } }$ may be a dominant pathway of $\mathrm { H g }$ mobilization from permafrost regions (Schaefer et al. 2O2O)，and direct measurements of thermokarst ponds in Tibet suggest a $1 5 \%$ increase in annual $\mathrm { H g ^ { 0 } }$ emissions in the past decades (Ci et al. 2O2O). The abundances of merA demonstrate that microbial communities in lake sediments have the potential to cause aquatic $\mathrm { H g }$ losses through $\mathrm { H g ^ { 0 } }$ emissions.

# PD in the water column of lakes

By modeling the rate of UV MeHg PD based on a representative PFD for each site and measured DOC and MeHg concentrations (Lehnherr and St. Louis 2Oo9),we found that the maximum depth of PD increased toward northern sites in line with lower DOC concentrations (Fig. 6). The depthspecific PD rates approached zero at $0 . 3 { - } 0 . 7 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ in the continuous permafrost zone at $6 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ in comparison to $0 . 0 5 { - } 0 . 2 \ \mathrm { m }$ at the southern regions of $5 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ and $5 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } .$ ，Modeled areal fluxes of UV PD ranged from 0.29 to 9.23 ng MeHg $\mathrm { ~ m ~ } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ (Fig. 6D),with the highest rates in the northernmost region. Lehnherr and St. Louis 2009) Alaska $( 5 { - } 1 3 \ \mathrm { n g \ M e H g \ m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 } )$ Hammerschmidt et al. 2006) and Nunavut $( 2 { - } 3 3 \ \mathrm { n g \ M e H g }$ $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ ； Lehnherr et al. 2012b).Although a mass balance would be required to contextualize areal fluxes of PD in the lakes,the daily rates represent an average of $1 . 0 \% \pm 0 . 5 \%$ of the total MeHg in these lakes (concentration $\times$ volume); thus, PD is likely an important MeHg sink in the studied lakes.If climate change and permafrost thaw lead to increased DOC concentrationsinsurfacewatersaspredicted(Freyand Smith 2005；Olefeldt et al. 2014;Hugelius et al. 2020)，PD rates may decrease and thus reduce the MeHg sink capacity of lakes in the continuous permafrost zone (Lehnherr and St. Louis 2009; Klapstein et al. 2018).

# MeHg concentrations under current and future climates

Peatlands were the dominant sources of MeHg to streams across the transect,as stream MeHg concentrations increased with acidic,reduced conditions and humic DOM associated with peatland runoff (Bourbonniere 2Oo9). The %MeHg in streams also increased with peatland catchment coverage. Although permafrost thaw and thermokarst expansion in peatlands create local hotspots for MeHg production (Gordon et al.2016;Fahnestock et al. 2019; Poulin et al. 2019; Tarbier et al. 2O21),we did not find elevated MeHg concentrations in streams of the rapidly thawing discontinuous and sporadic permafrost zones. Thermokarst wetlands, however， occupy a minor fraction of peatlands (Gibson et al. 2O21) and may not have a detectable influence on downstream MeHg concentrations,and the variability of stream catchment size potentially precluded the detection of a direct permafrost influence.As the export of peatland-derived aromatic DOC is expected to increase with permafrost thaw (Frey and Smith 2OO5; Olefeldt et al.2014; Hugelius et al. 202O),continued research at different spatial and temporal scales is needed to understand the potential for permafrost thaw to increase downstream delivery of MeHg from peatlands.

In lakes，potential drivers of MeHg degradation include biotic demethylation (Boyd and Barkay 2O12) and UV PD (Lehnherr et al.2O12a). In this study,we modeled areal fluxes of UV PD ranging up to $9 . 2 \ \mathrm { n g } \ \mathrm { M e H g } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ . If climate change and permafrost thaw increase the delivery of both DOM and MeHg from peatlands,our study suggests that this could affect the sink capacity of lakes by decreasing potential UV PD given the highest modeled rates in the continuous permafrost zone.

This study provides insight into the importance of regional biogeochemical factors that influence MeHg concentrations in lakes and streams of the Interior Plains during the summer period. As our study was based on a synoptic survey during moderate to low flow conditions,it cannot give estimates of annual catchment MeHg yields as concentrations can markedly fluctuate during high-flow periods such as spring freshet (Mitchell et al. 20o8b) or summer storms(Coleman Wasik et al.2O15). Still, the summer sampling conditions of this study prevail for the majority of the ice-free season.We show that both terrestrial delivery of MeHg into aquatic ecosystems and solar degradation of MeHg in lakes of the Interior Plains of boreal Canada is likely to be linked to changes in the delivery and downstream degradation of peatland DOM as permafrost thaws.Post-thaw, the factors that dictate groundwater dynamics and inputs of DOM, ions,and nutrients into aquatic environments (e.g.，landcover shifts,landscape position，and surficialgeology； Frey and McClelland 2Oo9； Gordon et al. 2016; Gibson et al. 2021) will in turn influence MeHg production and downstream delivery. Therefore，continued monitoring of MeHg in surface waters and fish of the Interior Plains and additional work on the mass balance budgets of peatland catchments is recommended to enable the detection of changes associated with permafrost thaw and assess potential impacts on the physical and cultural health of Northern Indigenous communities (Wheatley and Wheatley 2000).
