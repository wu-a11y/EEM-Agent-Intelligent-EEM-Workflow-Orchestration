# ABSTRACT

When dissolved organic matter (DOM) from terrestrial and freshwater sources is mixed with estuarine waters at the land-sea interface,the change in salinity has been suggested to cause fast aggregation and an increase in the bioavailability of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and nitrogen (DON).These processes were investigated in different Danish freshwaters. Aggregation of DOC in short-term (hours） mixing events at increasing salinity was low. In one stream with forest and wetland runoff and in a humic lake, the decrease of DOC overa 0 to 25 ppt salinity gradient was 2 to $5 \%$ .Optical analyses by absorption and fluorescence,revealed changes in the composition of the humic components due to salt. The bioavailability of terrestrial DOC was also investigated and found not to change moving from limnic to estuarine conditions.Although the yield of freshwater bacteria cells was about twice the yield of estuarine bacteria, the utilization of DOC was identical and not influenced by the bacterial communities.In addition, optical analyses showed that although utilization of DOC was equal, freshwater and estuarine bacterial coinmunities differed in their preference to the humic fractions.

Three processes of some quantitative importance have been identified to remove terrestrial DOC from the water column during mixing in estuaries; (1) Aggregation of DOC due to a change in ionic strength and an increase in divalent cations when freshwater is mixing with sea water (Sholkovitz 1976, Mulholland 1981),(2) Direct bacterial uptake and utilization of the bioavailable fraction (Hopkinson et al.1998),and (3) Photochemical reactions,where DOC is degraded directly to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ or photo-transformed to compounds more available for bacterial uptake (Wetzel et al.1995, Granéli et al.1996). Among these three processes, the photochemical processes are reasonably well investigated (reviewed by Moran and Covert 2003) although some uncertainty still exists on how the source of DOC might affect photochemical reactions and render DOC more or less biodegradable (Tranvik and Bertilsson 2001).The two former processes are treated in the present investigation.

Over the past two decades the view of allochthonous dissolved organic matter (DOM） as ecologically "inert”has changed and DOM is considered an important component in biotic and abiotic reactions in aquatic ecosystems (Sinsabaugh & Findlay 2003), providing an important energy and nutrient subsidy for microbial activity and phytoplankton (Graneli et al.1999,Wetzel 200l).With respect to abiotic reactions，properties like photon absorption, metal chelating and colloid formation are important features (Williamson et al. 1999).Large amounts of terrigenous derived dissolved organic carbon (DOC)

Aggregation of dissolved compounds to particles during estuarine mixing is a process where by dissolved material is made directly available to filter feeders and to benthic feeders after sedimentation. Furthermore, bacterial colonisation of the aggregated colloids and particles can enhance decomposition (Kepkay 1994, Kerner et al. 2003). The quantitative importance of aggregation is well known for Pand Fe (Forsgren et al. 1996) while fewer studies have addressed to what extent DOC contributes·to aggregation.Although a relatively high fraction (60 to $\bar { 8 } 0 \%$ ）of dissolved humic acids and high molecular weight carbon are preferentially and rapidly aggregated (Sholkovitz et al.1978,Powell et al.1996) a salinity dependent DOC removal has mostly been reported to be below $1 0 \%$ of the total in laboratory studies (Sholkovitz 1976,Powell et al.1996,Forsgren et al. 1996).Mulholland (198l) estimated that $2 5 . 3 0 \%$ of the DOC load in rivers and streams from southeastern US may aggregate and be retained within the estuarine system.Apparently the process initiates between salinities of 2 and 5 ppt with no further enhancement above 20 ppt (Sholkovitz 1976, Powell et al. 1996). Salinity independent sorption processes and polymeric gel formation of dissolved compounds can also contribute to formation of sub-micron particles (Chin et al.1998,Kerner et al. 2003) but these are rather slow processes (days) compared with the short-term (minutes/hours) physicochemical reaction found by Sholkovitz et al. (1978)and Mulholland (1981).

Bacterial DOC degradation is an important re moval process of terrestrial DOC in estuaries (e.g. Raymond and Bauer 20o0, Wiegner and Seitzinger 2001).It has been suggested that the bioavailability of allochthonous DOC and dissolved organic nitrogen (DON) increases when transported from limnic to estuarine conditions (Wikner et al. 1999, Stepanauskas et al. $1 9 9 9 \mathrm { a }$ .Wikner and coworkers reported that degradation of terrigenous DOC by estuarine bacteria could increase 4fold at a salinity of 4 ppt compared with degradation in freshwater. Furthermore,lower growth efficiencies were observed for estuarine bacteria utilizing riverine DOC. Similar results for DON were achieved by Stepanauskas et al. (1999a,1999b) showing a two-fold larger DON availability for bacteria at high salinity (17 ppt) compared with limnic conditions. Changed coiling of molecules and enhanced aminopeptidase activity at higher salinity were proposed as explanations. It cannot be excluded that formation of sub-micron particles (Kerner etal. 2O03) with a high bacterial colonisation affinity can explain some of the observed differences in both cases, but then salinity dependent particle/ colloid formation has to be present.

The purpose of this study was to verify whether a change in salinity causes DOC aggregation and/or alteration in the optical signatures of DOM in shortterm (hours) experiments.Furthermore,it was the purpose to find out if limnic and estuarine conditions and the origin of bacterial communities change the removal of allochthonous DOC at carbon limited growth conditions.Aggregation was tested with water from four Danish streams,a humic lake and an algal culture.A change in bioavailability during estuarine mixing was tested with water from one stream enteringHorsens Fjord,Denmark.Sample sites are characterised with respect to catchment and drainage in Table 1. The analytical methods included biological, chemical and optical techniques.

A significant fraction of DOM is coloured dissolved organic matter (CDOM). The optical properties of CDOMs can be used as a proxy for DOC concentrations and as an indicator of its composition and source (Kalle 1966, Coble 1996).Absorption and fluorescence spectroscopy provide a rapid,cheap and sensitive method of following and assessing changes occurring to DOM, which may not be apparent by sinple chemical concentration measurements. Fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs） present a detailed mapping of the fluorescence properties of DOM and have successfully been used to characterize different allochthonous and autochthonous fractions of DOM (Coble 1996).Recent developments in data analysis (Stedmon et al. 2003） now allow us to decompose the fluorescence signal into different independent fluorescent fractions,further improving our ability to trace and characterize changes in DOM in natural waters.

# DISCUSSION

Huge amounts of terrestrially derived dissolved organic matter are exported in rivers and streams to coastal areas (Mulholland 20o3) and may have major influence on estuarine ecology exerted by its optical, chemical and microbial properties.Although the major part of terrestrial DOM is recalcitrant to bacterial degradation (Sgndergaard & Middelboe 1995) the concerted interaction between microbes and photochemical processes together with aggregation may play a key role for DOM retainment.

Terrestrial DOM signatures,e.g. lignin derivatives,are largely absent in open oceans (Opsal & Benner l997) so estuaries and coastal water are functioning as diagenetic sieves removing most terrestrial signals from the DOM. Furthermore, there are some indications in the literature (Wikner et al.1999, Stepanauskas et al.1999a） that a larger fraction of terrestrial DOC and DON is more easily degraded in estuaries than in fresh water. In the present study we have investigated the fate of terrestrial DOM in saline water.

The effects of salinity on aggregation and optical signatures. The methods used to investigate DOC aggregation in salinity gradients are not standardised and include experiments with both pre-filtered and unfiltered samples and a large variability of different filters and mixing times.The use of unfiltered samples would be the natural choice but also include the problem that biotic and abiotic processes cannot be separated. The apparently most common procedures use the addition of artificial salt mixtures followed by mixing/stirring,a filtration step and measurement of DOC in the filtrate and/or particulate organic carbon on filters. Disintegration of aggregated particles during filtration might occur so any filtration step would result in a minimum estimate of aggregation.In the present investigation we followed a protocol with measurements of DOC in the filtrates and linear regressions to detect significant slopes in DOC versus salinity.

In previous investigations, the aggregation process has been found to be fast and controlled by a change in the physicochemical environment, i.e. the divalent cations in sea salt (Sholkowitz 1976,Mulholland 1981).Here we used one hour mixing time in one experiment and from 1 to 24 hours in another. Salt independent slow formation of sub-micron particles and aggregates over longer times scales (days and weeks） more relevant for estuarine turnover times (Kerner et al. 2003) is not included in our experiments.

Low but measurable decreases in DOC were only found in unfiltered samples with the highest DOC concentrations,which are also the samples dominated by the humic fluorophore component 1, i.e. Lake Grib and Station 14. The loss of DOC over a salinity gradient of 25 ppt was between 3 and $5 \%$ of the initial concentrations.The low levels of DOC removal are in accord with some previous findings with aggregation values below $10 \%$ and often $< 5 \%$ of the total (Sholkovitz 1976,Powell et al.1996,Forsgren et al. 1996). The change in the environment from freshwater to higher salinities had a quantitatively modest effect on DOC concentrations. We therefore suggest that salinity independent long-term aggregation processes are more inportant for the abiotic transformation of terrestrial DOC to particles，as found by Kerner et al. (2003).

Previous studies by Sholkovitz et al. (1978) and Powell et al. (l996) have shown that dissolved humic acids and high molecular weight carbon are preferentially and rapidly aggregated.This is in accord with our findings for Stations 12 and 14, where we detected a removal of component 1 fluorescence,albeit an increased fluorescence of the other components (Fig. 4C).The absorption measurements also revealed a general decrease in molecular weight with salinity，with decreasing @375 and increasing S for Station 12 DOM.However, the increase in S was not evident for Station 14. This could be due to the fact that absorption is less sensitive than fluorescence spectroscopy, although it could also point toward a source dependent variability, not yet understood.

It is well documented that increases in pH can lead to changes in DOM fluorescence signals and the sensitivity of DOM to subsequent photo-oxidation (e.g. Laane 1982， Vodacek 1992). In this study we incubated in the dark to eliminate changes due to photo-oxidation, however, the DOM was still susceptible to changes in $\mathrm { p H }$ as a result of the salt additions. For Lake Grib this was clearly a problem as the lake is humic with low $\mathrm { p H }$ and low buffering capacity. As a result of the salt addition $\mathsf { p \bar { H } }$ increased from 3.6 to 7.9 and this most likely lead to the considerable increases in fluorescence observed.However, for the samples from Station 12 and 14 the $\mathtt { p H }$ was 8.5 and 8.1, respectively, and did not change as a result of the salt addition, suggesting that the fluorescence changes occurred as a result of salinity increases.

We conclude that the quantitative importance of short-term DOC aggregation due to a change in salinity is low for the tested samples. There seemed to be no direct linkage between changes in the optical properties and changes in DOC concentrations at a level we could detect chemically. This uncoupling suggests that the changes seen in the optical measurements reflect a restructuring of the DOM pool rather than a removal of a certain fraction from the dissolved phase.

The effects of salinity on DOC bioavailability. Urup Stream is mainly receiving water draining from agricultural land and some forests,and at Station 14 the stream has just passed a small wetland area. The stream is one of the two dominant sources of DOM to Horsens Fjord. The DOM sources used here are rather typical for Danish rivers and streams.The optical signature verified the humic and “agricultural" influence. The organic matter in the stream is transported to Horsens Fjord where estuarine mixing is taking place with the marine endmember. Station 14 may accordingly be used as a model for the fate of DOM upon estuarine mixing in inner Danish coastal areas where the marine end-member salinity ranges from 25 to 30 ppt. Water for the degradation experiments was sampled in January and May and represents the range in concentration and bioavailability found in a seasonal study (Sgndergaard and Borch,unpublished).

Three DOM degradation experiments from Station 14 at limnic and estuarine conditions with either an artificial sea salt mixture or estuarine water revealed no quantitative effects of salt or bacterial inocula type on the level of DOC bioavailability, except for freshwater bacteria at 5 and 10 ppt. However, the ability of the freshwater and estuarine bacteria to remove humic fluorescent components differed.

Freeze-dried DOM was used in order to avoid artificial seawater and to be able to add DOM without diluting the salinity of the water from Horsens Fjord. The fluorescence analysis showed quantitative and qualitative recovery when re-dissolved in distilled water (Fig.2) and almost exactly the same bioavailability as in the experiment with water collected and used for experimentation within I2 hours.Thus,the isolation was successful. The addition of freezedried DOM to Fjord water slightly changed the optical properties,but as found by Hopkinson et al. (1998) freeze-drying did not significantly change the DOC bioavailability.

In the study by Wikner et al. (1999) it was shown very convincingly that estuarine conditions (4 ppt） can dramatically increase the bioavailability of terrestrial dominated river DOC compared with limnic conditions. The explanations forwarded were that either this was caused by a salinity induced change of the DOC making it more accessible for bacterial degradation or that estuarine bacteria were more capable of using terrestrial DOC. Although major genotypic differences can be found among freshwater, estuarine and marine bacterial communities (Foreman & Covert 2003,Langenheder et al. 2003) and seasonal changes in organic matter source can shift bacterioplankton community structure (Crump et al. 2003),it has to our knowledge never been shown that a site specific bacterial community should have the ability to extract more carbon from a common source than other communities.

In one experiment we tested how freshwater bacteria and estuarine bacteria performed in a salinity gradient. Aside a reduced performance of freshwater bacteria at 5 and 10 ppt, the bacterial communities extracted identical amounts of DOC,and the estuarine bacteria performed equally well between O and 25 ppt (Fig. 6B). The reason(s) for low DOC consumption by freshwater bacteria at higher salinities is not known.We may speculate that the salt tolerant bacteria selected from the freshwater community cannot extract the more recalcitrant DOC components or that changes in the molecular structure observed with the optical measurements impeded a fast utilization.For the fate of terrestrial DOC in estuaries this observation should have no consequence as estuarine bacteria are present in the mixing zone and removed similar amounts of DOC over the entire salinity gradient and, the same amount as freshwater bacteria at limnic conditions.

Langenheder et al. (2003) have recently published a study where estuarine and freshwater bacteria were provided a common terrestrial DOC source and exposed to limnic (0 ppt) and estuarine (4 ppt) conditions in batch incubations. Although the bacterial communities differed in composition and some functional attributes (e.g. growth rate), similar amounts of DOC was removed. The deviation of these results from those presented by Wikner et al. (1999) was suggested to results from seasonal changes in the DOC pool, which could affect the bioavailability at different salinities and that the samples for DOC and bacterial inocula were collected at different times.The invariant DOC bioavailability found by Langenheder et al.(2003) is in accord with our results for samples collected at Station 14 in January and May.

One of the findings by Wikner et al (1999) was that estuarine bacteria extracted more DOC but the yields of bacterial biomass during limnic and estuarine conditions were similar. Thus, the carbon growth efficiency of estuarine bacteria was almost half than for freshwater bacteria. Our results showed major differences in cell yields at limnic and estuarine conditions with the lower abundances at estuarine conditions.In flow cytometric cell analyses side- and forward scatter,and to some extent fluorescence,have been used to estimate cell sizes (Marie et al. 1997). The measured signals for the limnic and estuarine samples did not differ in our experiments. Thus,if bacterial abundance equivalate the biomass,then the growth efficiency for the estuarine bacteria was lower than for freshwater bacteria and in accord with the findings by Wikner et al. (1999). Langenheder et al. (2003) came to the conclusion that riverine and estuarine bacteria in their natural environments had similar growth efficiencies growing on a common DOC source. However, a decrease in growth efficiencies was found when riverine bacteria were disturbed by growth at 4 ppt and estuarine bacteria by growth at O ppt. Their conclusion was that the functional performance of riverine and estuarine bacteria can change due to salt. It seems clear that our empirical knowledge is yet too meagre to make solid conclusions.

A comparison of bacterial cell or biomass yield among the performed experiments relies on the assumption that cell loss by grazing is absent or very low. In the experiment with water sampled at Station 14 in May, and inoculated with estuarine and freshwater bacteria in a salinity gradient (Fig. 6) a plateau level in cell densities was not found. In most samples the densities decreased after two to six days and with some increases in the latter part of the experiment. These fluctuations are most probably due to grazers and the results are excluded from further interpretation. In all experiments with water from Station 14 collected in January the bacteria showed logistic growth and approached plateau levels in abundance, which indicate low mortality (Fig.5B). In our experience a plateau value in bacterial density never occurs in batch cultures when flagellates are present. Thus,we feel safe to conclude that resources controlled the yields of bacteria in those experiments, i.e. the availability of DOC.

To our knowledge the study by Wikner et al. (1999) is the only study so far that experimentally has found higher biodegradability of terrestrial DOC at estuarine than at limnic conditions. The reason(s） for their finding is not obvious. Although we could not detect aggregation to be of any quantitative importance the experiments by Wikner et al. (l999) may have resulted in the formation of aggregates and colloidal material (Kerner et al. 20o3) and colonisation by bacteria may have improved access to otherwise recalcitrant DOC at the time scale of the experiment (days) (Kepkay 1994). This explanation may have a parallel in the study of Stepanauskas et al. (1999a),who suggested that the increased bioavailability of terrestrial DON at estuarine conditions was due to a change in the accessibility of molecules. Our results showed distinct changes in optical properties as a function of salinity and may provide some credit for the speculations concerning re-configuration of the molecules.

Another suggested explanation for the higher bioavailability of terrestrial DOM to estuarine bacteria, is that estuarine bacteria have a different, and possibly more diverse，set of extracellular enzymes, and are therefore more capable of utilising other fractions of DOM (Stepanauskas et al. 1999a). This hypothesis is supported by the fluorescence results presented here (Fig.7). It is clear that the two bacterial communities differ in their preferences to degrade different fractions.However, we did measure equal abilities to extract DOC at two different seasons. Our results stress that it is important to consider qualitative changes as well as quantitative changes in DOM, when comparing bioavailability between different sources and in different environments.

# CONCLUSIONS

Fast aggregation (within hours） of terrestrial DOC caused by salinity changes during estuarine mixing was not found to be a major removal process in the tested Danish rivers. Optical analyses identified five fluorescent humic components in freshwater DOM.A sources and salinity dependent variation in the mixture of the humic compounds was observed. The microbial bioavailability of DOC in one model stream was not influenced by limnic (freshwater）or estuarine (salinity $> 2 . 5$ ppt) conditions.However, the yield of freshwater bacteria was about double at limnic conditions compared with estuarine conditions suggesting that bacterial growth yield based on the utilisation of terrestrial DOC can be low in estuaries. The reason(s） for this difference remains unknown,but could be related to the fact that the different bacterial communities showed different capabilities to remove distinct fluorophore components. Thus，terrestrial DOC seems to be utilized with low efficiency in estuaries and possibly restructured with respect to its composition.

Biodegradation. Limnic conditions during a degradation experiment are defined as an experiment without salt addition and inoculated with freshwater bacteria from the stream. Estuarine conditions are defined by the presence of salt and the addition of bacteria from the estuary. The microbial inocula were in all cases 2 times GF/F filtered water from Station 14 collected mid-stream at about $0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m }$ depth or surface water from the outer part of Horsens Fjord (about 24 ppt).All inocula were added to $5 \%$ v/v. Two series of biodegradation experiments were carried out with water collected from Station 14. In the second experiment we used either limnic or estuarine bacteria as inocula for separate bottles with individually increasing salinities (0,2.5,5,10, 20 and 25 ppt).

First experiment: The water from Station 14 was sampled in January and $0 . 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filtered (PolycapTM, Watman) after return to the laboratory.Part of the water was distributed in 250 ml acid cleaned BOD bottles and freeze-dried. Duplicate bottles of the remaining water was prepared for limnic conditions by adding a Station 14 inoculum and two other bottles were added salt to 25 ppt and added an estuarine inoculum.These bottles were then incubated in darkness at room temperature $( 2 0 \pm 2 ^ { \mathrm { { o } } } \mathrm { { C } ) }$ for 13 days and sub-sampled at intervals for DOC, bacterial counts and optical measurements. The samples for DOC and optical analyses were filtered (GF/F) before measurements.

The freeze-dried material made it possible to add terrestrial DOM to estuarine water without a decrease in salinity. As all minerals and organic material are preserved during freeze-drying we assume that the original stream environment is re-established by the addition of water. In a previous study Hopkinson et al. Second experiment: An additional degradation experiment was carried out late May and followed the protocol with respect to the nonfreeze drying pre-treatment. However, now the ability of fresh water bacteria to extract organic carbon was tested over a salinity gradient from 0 to 10 ppt and the estuarine inoculum was tested over a gradient from 0 to 25 ppt.Replication was sacrificed for the salinity gradient except for the limnic condition,which was done in duplicate.

Analytical procedures. Organic carbon.Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) was analysed after GF/F filtration (also $0 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ filtration in the first aggregation experiment） by high-temperaturecatalytic-oxidation in a Shimadzu TOC-5000 analyser equipped with automatic sample injection and a platinum coated silicate catalyst (Cauwet 1994).A four point calibration curve was used.Blanks and calibrated standards were included in each run. All procedures followed the recommendations by Sharp et al. (2002) and the standard ampoules provided by J. Sharp were used to control the quality of our blank (MilliPoreQ-water) and standards.

Bacteria were counted by flow cytometry (Becton-Dickinson FacsCalibur). Samples were immediately fixed with glutaraldehyde $( 2 \%$ final vol.） followed by staining with SYBR Green 1 (Marie et al. 1997). The analysed volume was calibrated by weight and the counting procedure was validated by the addition of fluorescent beads in known concentrations.

$$
a _ { \lambda } = a _ { \lambda \mathcal { O } } e ^ { S ( \widehat { \lambda } \mathcal { O } - \widehat { \lambda } ) } + \mathbf { K }
$$

where wavelength is denoted by $9 , \ a$ is the absorption coefficient, $s$ is the spectral slope coefficient and $\mathbf { K }$ is a constant. Fluorescence measurements were made using a Varian Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer.A series of emission scans $( 3 0 0  – 5 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m } )$ were collected over excitation wavelengths ranging from 300 to $4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ with $5 \mathrm { n m }$ increments.Bandwidths were set to $5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ for both excitation and emission.The spectral calibration procedure consisted of three stages.Firstly, spectra were corrected for inner-filter effects using the absorption measurements (McKnight et al.20o1).
