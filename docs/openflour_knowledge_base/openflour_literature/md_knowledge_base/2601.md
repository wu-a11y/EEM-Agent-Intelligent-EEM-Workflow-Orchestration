# a b s t r a c t

This study investigated the applicability of parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) of fluorescence excitationemission matrices (EEM) spectra to assess the formation potentials (FP) of carbonaceous and nitrogenous disinfection byproducts (C-DBP and N-DBP) and the FP reduction by the magnetic ion exchange resins, MIEX $^ { \mathrm { \textregistered } }$ DOC and MIEX® GOLD. Two source waters of different nature d a surface water and a secondary treated wastewater effluent d were studied. The samples were analyzed for formation potentials of trihalomethanes $\left( \mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 } \right)$ , haloacetonitriles $\mathrm { ( H A N _ { 4 } ) }$ , haloketones $\left( \mathrm { H K } _ { 2 } \right)$ , and chloropicrin (CPN). A 4- component PARAFAC model was developed from 150 EEM samples generated from the raw source waters and their treatment with ${ \mathrm { M I E X @ } }$ resins. Components C1, C2, and C3 corresponded to humic-like dissolved organic matter (DOM) while C4 corresponded to protein-like DOM. Both MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ resins preferentially removed components C1, C2, and C3 over C4, indicating affinity with humic materials. MIEX® resins were shown to be more effective to treat surface water than secondary effluent, including effective removal of DBP precursors with extended bed volume treatment. Among all parameters investigated, $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP strongly correlated with humic-like component C3, while $\mathrm { H A N } _ { 4 }$ -FP strongly correlated with protein-like component C4 $\dot { \rho } > 0 . 8 9$ and $p < 0 . 0 1$ ); CPN-FP and $\mathrm { H K } _ { 2 }$ -FP both correlated with anthropogenic DOM C2 $\dot { \mathbf { \rho } } _ { \mathbf { 0 } } > 0 . 8 9$ and $p < 0 . 0 1$ ). Our results indicate that EEMePARAFAC was valuable for assessing DBP formation potentials and removal of their precursors by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ resins in different water sources.

# 1. Introduction

Due to water scarcity and increase in water demand, interest in treated wastewater effluent as a source water for human consumption (e.g., through indirect or direct potable reuse) is increasing. Further, effluent discharge from wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) infiltrates surface and ground waters that are sources for downstream drinking water treatment plants (DWTPs), causing indirect and unintentional water reuse. During the disinfection process at DWTPs, chemical disinfectants have been widely used for pathogen control to ensure clean and safe water. Organic matter in the water can react with these chemicals, especially chorine, and result in formation of carcinogenic halogenated disinfection byproducts (DBPs) such as trihalomethanes (THMs), haloacetic acids (HAAs), and haloacetonitriles (HANs) (Symons et al., 1999). Since DBPs have been reported to have adverse health effects, they have become a focus of attention in water treatment. In 2001, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) promulgated the Stage 1 Disinfectants and Disinfection Byproducts Rule which set maximum contaminant levels (MCLs) of ${ 8 0 \ \mu \mathrm { g / L } }$ for total trihalomethanes $\left( \mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 } \right)$ and $6 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ for haloacetic acids (five, HAA5) (U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, 2001).

Upstream direct and indirect effluent discharge unavoidably affects the quality of water sources of DWTPs because organic matter in treated wastewater effluents (EfOM) has different chemical characteristics compared to natural organic matter (NOM) in surface water (Sirivedhin and Gray, 2005b). NOM and EfOM together are hereinafter referred to as dissolved organic matter (DOM). EfOM can lead to formation of DBPs different from those typically formed during chlorination of natural water (Krasner et al., 2008; Sirivedhin and Gray, 2005a). EfOM from biological wastewater treatment processes contains refractory substrates not biodegraded by bacteria, degradation products, and soluble microbial products (SMP) (Krasner et al., 2009). SMP are enriched in nitrogen, which could lead to formation of emerging nitrogenous DBPs (N-DBPs) (e.g., HANs and chloropicrin (CPN)). Moreover, most EfOM waters have high levels of DBP precursors and contain other inorganic substances (e.g., ammonia, nitrite, and bromide), which influence DBP formation (Krasner et al., 2008).

N-DBPs have received significant attention due to an increase of nitrogen-based contaminants that are N-DBP precursors in water sources and utilities switching from chlorine to chloramine disinfection. Even though concentrations of N-DBPs in tap water were reported to be much lower than C-DBPs (Baytak et al., 2008; Krasner et al., 2008), toxicity of N-DBPs are higher than regulated CDBPs (Plewa et al., 2008; Richardson et al., 2007). For example, halonitromethanes (HNMs) and haloacetonitriles (HANs) have cytotoxicity higher than that of haloacetic acids (HAAs) (Muellner et al., 2007). The World Health Organization (2004) provides guideline values for dichloroacetonitrile (DCAN) and dibromoacetonitrile (DBAN) at 20 and $7 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ , respectively. N-DBPs, including HANs and CPN, were cited as research priorities by the U.S. EPA (US EPA, 2016).

Coagulation is a conventional process for DBPs precursor removal. It is relatively inexpensive, but not necessarily the most effective process (Symons et al., 1999). To increase precursor removal, extensive research has been focused on enhanced coagulation, including increasing the coagulant dose and reducing pH. However, high cost of chemicals and sludge disposal is a limitation of enhanced coagulation (Symons et al., 1999). Besides conventional processes, recent research has highlighted advantages of magnetic ion exchange (MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ ) resins for dissolved organic carbon (DOC) removal. MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ is a strong base anion exchange resin with magnetic properties and a small resin bead size (IXOM Watercare, 2019). The magnetic properties make the resin beads act as weak individual magnets, which enhances particle agglomeration and facilitates rapid particle settling. When the resin is in contact with water, negatively charged organic acids in the DOC are removed from the water by exchanging with chloride ions at the active sites on the resin surface. The small resin beads, 2 to 5 times smaller than conventional resins, provide high surface areas that enhance DOC removal kinetics and regeneration capacity (Johnson and Singer, 2004).

The first generation of ${ \tt M I E X } \mathrm { \textcircled { R } }$ resin, namely ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC, was reported to be more effective than coagulation at removing DOC, including THM and HAA precursors, from a broad range of source waters (Boyer and Singer, 2005; Fearing et al., 2004). For N-DBPs, specifically, halonitromethanes (HNM) precursors removal with ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC ranged from 10 to $3 3 \%$ in surface waters and effluent impacted waters (Gan et al., 2013), and HANs precursors removal ranged from 42 to $7 2 \%$ in model and surface water samples (Rajca et al., 2017). Notably, there is very limited published literature investigating the performance of MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC resin to remove DOM and/or DBPs precursors from effluent-impacted water (Gan et al., 2013), or even synthetic wastewater (Nguyen et al., 2011).

Recently, a second generation of MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ resin, MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD, was developed by IXOM Watercare (Australia) with rougher and increased surface area, and larger pore openings to enhance the DOC removal rate in waters with organic matter of high molecular weight (Lopez-Ortiz et al., 2018  ). Both ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC and GOLD achieved $8 0 \%$ removal of humic acid within $6 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ of resin contact experiment (Imbrogno et al., 2018). In some waters, MIEX $^ { \mathrm { i } }$ GOLD achieved better performance in terms of DOM removal. For example, ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD achieved similar UVA removal at a treatment rate of 1000 bed volumes (BV) compared to 600 BV of ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC, thus decreasing regeneration cost (Gibson and Golubovic, 2015). To our knowledge, there are no studies in the literature that have reported the performance of MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD resin for removing C-DBP and N-DBP precursors, nor the correlation between performance and organic matter properties.

Chemical and physical properties of DOM have an important role in the interactions of DOM with disinfectants, and consequently, DBP formation. For example, THM formation potential (THM-FP) was shown to strongly correlate with specific UV absorbance (SUVA) (Symons et al., 1999), which is a proxy of DOM aromaticity. Thus, measures of chemical and/or physical properties of DBP precursors could potentially be used as surrogates to determine DBP precursor reactivity with disinfectants and their removal mechanism by treatment processes (Lyon et al., 2014; Pifer and Fairey, 2012). Optical properties, such as UV light absorption and fluorescence emission, can be used to characterize DOM (Leenheer and Croue, 2003  ). Fluorescence excitation-emission matrix (EEM) spectroscopy, which is a series of emission scans from a range of excitation wavelengths, has proven successful in DOM characterization (Coble et al., 2014; Stedmon et al., 2003). EEM spectroscopy is both qualitative and quantitative and able to provide information regarding the type, structure and abundance of functional groups (Coble et al., 2014). The fluorescence signal of DOM can be used to distinguish between a humic-like and a protein- or amino acid-like DOM (Stedmon et al., 2003). EEM spectroscopy has also been used to track DOM in both drinking water and wastewater treatment plants (Baghoth et al., 2011; Murphy et al., 2011). Correlations between fluorescence surrogate parameters (e.g., fluorescence intensity of humic-like fluorophores) and THM-FP were found to be substantially stronger than correlations between THM-FP and UVA, SUVA, or DOC (Hao et al., 2012; Jutaporn et al., 2019; Pifer and Fairey, 2012).

EEM spectra contain a large amount of information, requiring multivariate data analysis to process and interpret the data effectively. Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) is a multi-way data analysis technique that is widely applied in the study of DOM fluorescence (Hambly et al., 2015; Lyon et al., 2014; Murphy et al., 2011; Stedmon et al., 2011). PARAFAC modeling can be applied across a large data set of hundreds or thousands of EEMs, which makes the statistical analysis more robust and makes determining fluorescence characteristics over a wide range of water sources possible. EEM spectroscopy, without PARAFAC, was previously used to develop a predictive model for THM and HAA formation (Hao et al., 2012). Given that PARAFAC modeling allows the user to distinguish between humic-like and protein-like DOM in a statistically more robust manner than traditional EEM spectrocopy, PARAFAC analysis could potentially enhance the computational ability of researchers to correlate measures of chemical/physical properties to DBP precursors to DBP formation, and treatment outcomes. In addition, the resolution of the humic-like and proteinlike components that PARAFAC modeling affords can be used to determine the concentration of DBP precursors in the water samples. Treatment protocols could then be tailored accordingly.

Accordingly, the objective of this study was to evaluate the use of PARAFAC analysis to assess the removal of C-DBP and N-DBP precursors by two different ${ \tt M I E X } \textcircled { \tt R }$ resins, MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC and ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD. The MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ treatment ranged from 500 to 5000 BV and was evaluated in two source waters of different nature, a surface water and a secondary treated wastewater effluent. We performed PARAFAC modeling on fluorescence EEM spectral data to characterize DBP precursors in both NOM and EfOM. Although studies of C-DBP precursors removal by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC has been well established in natural water sources (Boyer and Singer, 2005, 2006; Johnson and Singer, 2004), the effectiveness of ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ resin treatment to remove emerging N-DBP precursors is quite limited (Gan et al., 2013; Rajca et al., 2017), especially in secondary effluent. In this study, we established correlations between fluorescence surrogate parameters obtained from a PARAFAC model and two DBP formation potentials, THM-FP and HAN-FP).

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Water samples

One-time grab samples of $5 0 ~ \mathrm { L }$ were obtained from two water sources containing organic matter of different nature, surface water and secondary effluent. The surface water was collected from an intake at a drinking water treatment plant (DWTP) in Chapel Hill, NC, and had a dissolved organic carbon (DOC) content of $6 . 5 7 \mathrm { m g / L }$ $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ absorbance $\left( \mathrm { U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 } \right)$ of $0 . 1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ , specific UV Absorbance (SUVA) of $2 . 2 8 \mathrm { \ L / m g . m }$ , and total dissolved nitrogen (TDN) of $0 . 4 3 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ . The source of the sampled surface water was University Lake, Chapel Hill, NC, a natural surface water. Collected in October 2013, DOM in University Lake had predominantly terrestrial character from fall foliage. The secondary treated domestic wastewater effluent was collected from the discharge stream of a wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) in Carrboro, NC and had a DOC of $7 . 1 7 \mathrm { m g } /$ $\mathrm { L } , \mathrm { U V } \mathrm { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ of $0 . 1 1 4 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ , SUVA of $1 . 5 9 \mathrm { L } / \mathrm { m g . m }$ , and TDN of $9 . 5 9 \mathrm { m g / L }$ . The WWTP is a conventional activated sludge process with nitrification and the samples were collected after secondary clarifier before UV disinfection in January 2014. All samples were stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in the dark and allowed to reach room temperature $( 2 0 \pm 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ prior to MIEX $^ { \mathrm { i n } }$ treatment experiments.

# 2.2. MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ pretreatment

The two resins studied were ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC and ${ \mathrm { M I E X @ } }$ GOLD, obtained from IXOM Watercare, Inc. (Australia). MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD resin presents similar bead size $1 5 0 { - } 1 8 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ (Neale and Schafer, 2009 € ) and exchange capacity to the ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC resin (Gibson and Golubovic, 2015). The manufacturer indicates (IXOM Watercare, 2019) that the main characteristics differentiating ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD from ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC are the increased roughness and larger pore openings of MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD, which result in increased surface area and more accessible functional groups. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images illustrating resin bead size, pore size, and roughness of the two resins are shown in Fig. S1 (Supporting Materials). The images show a greater density of pores per unit area of resin surface in ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD than in ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC, with average pore diameters of 2.35 and $2 . 2 4 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , respectively (i.e., $1 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ larger in MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD than in MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC). According to the resin supplier, ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD has ability to remove both low molecular weight materials ( ${ < } 5 0 0 0 \mathrm { D a }$ ), as well as high molecular weight DOC (IXOM Watercare, 2019). The two resins were stored in $5 \%$ sodium chloride and then rinsed with laboratory-grade deionized water prior to resin contact experiments. The volume of the resins was determined after $1 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ of settling in a graduated cylinder. The resin contact experiments followed the Multiple-Loading Jar Test Protocol using a six-paddle stirrer (Phipps and Bird Inc., VA) as described elsewhere (Myat et al., 2012) and the Supporting Materials. In this protocol, the resin is exposed to a given volume of fresh untreated water several times, effectively mimicking resin reuse without regeneration. Targeted levels of treatment were quantified by bed volumes (BV) which is calculated as the ratio between the cumulative volume of water treated and the volume of ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ resin. Thus, a larger BV indicates a larger number of consecutive resin uses. The resin was used for up to 6 consecutive times, resulting in treatment levels of 500e5000 BV. Resin fines were removed from settled water using a vacuum filter and a $1 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size filter (Whatman GF/C). All the experiments were done in triplicate and average values are reported. Removal efficiency was calculated as the difference in the value of a measured water parameter in raw and MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ treated waters divided by the raw water value (i.e., Removal efficiency $= 1 0 0 \times$ (Raw-Treated)/Raw).

# 2.3. Analytical methods

The DOC $\mathrm { ( m g \ C / L ) }$ and TN $( \mathrm { m g ~ N / L } )$ concentrations in water samples was measured using a TOC-V organic carbon analyzer (Shimadzu). $\mathrm { U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ measurements were made using a $1 \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cell and a U-2000 spectrophotometer (Hitachi Instruments, Inc.). SUVA values were calculated as $\mathrm { S U V A } = 1 0 0 \ \times \ ( \mathrm { U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } / \mathrm { D O C } ) .$ . Chlorine demand and DBP formation potential (DBP-FP) were evaluated under uniform formation conditions (UFC) (Summers et al., 1996). The UFC corresponds to the chlorination conditions that yield $1 \mathrm { m g / L }$ free chlorine residual at $\mathtt { p H } 8$ after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ incubation at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in the dark. After $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ , chlorine residuals were measured by a DPD (N,N-diethyl-p-phenylenediamine) colorimetric method (Ha et al., 2004) and DR890 Portable Colorimeter (HACH). Samples with chlorine residuals of $1 \pm 0 . 2 \mathrm { \ m g / L }$ as $\mathrm { C l } _ { 2 }$ were quenched with ascorbic acid (Sigma Chemical Co.), transferred to $6 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ glass vials capped with PTFE-lined septa for DBPs analysis, and extracted with methyl tert-butyl ether (MTBE) within $^ \mathrm { ~ 4 ~ h ~ }$ of quenching. The following DBPs were targeted for analysis: regulated THMs (i.e., $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ ; chloroform (CF), bromodichloromethane (BDCM), dibromochloromethane (DBCM), and bromoform (BF)), four HANs, (i.e., $\mathrm { H A N _ { 4 } }$ ; dichloroacetonitrile (DCAN), trichloroacetonitrile (TCAN), bromochloroacetonitrile (BCAN), and dibromoacetonitrile (DBAN)), two haloketones (i.e., $\mathrm { H K } _ { 2 }$ ; 1,1-Dichloro-2-propanone (DCP) and 1,1,1-Trichloropropanone (TCP)), and chloropicrin (CPN). All samples were analyzed on a Hewlett-Packard 5890 gas chromatograph (GC) with electron capture detector (ECD) using a Zebron (Phenomenex) ZB-1 capillary column ( $. 3 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ length, $0 . 2 5 \mathrm { \ m m }$ inner diameter, 1.0- $\mu \mathrm { m }$ film thickness). We used a modified version of EPA Method 551.1 (1995) after Lyon et al. (2014) (see Supporting Materials). All samples were analyzed in duplicate and 1,2- dibromopropane $9 9 \%$ , Acros Organics) was used as an internal standard.

# 2.4. EEM and PARAFAC modeling

EEM spectra of water samples were acquired in a $1 \mathrm { c m }$ cell using a Fluorolog-321 spectro-fluorometer with a xenon arc lamp as the excitation source and synapse CCD detector (Horiba JobinYvon). Excitation wavelengths of $2 4 0 { - } 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ in $5 \ \mathrm { \ n m }$ intervals and emission wavelengths of $3 2 0 { - } 5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals were used. Blank EEM spectra of deionized (DI) water were also collected daily prior to sample analysis. Each sample was analyzed in triplicate. To account for inner filter effects, a matrix of correction factors was created from absorbance spectra of the samples (Cory et al., 2010) measured with a diode array UVeVis spectrophotometer (Hewlett Packard) in the range of $2 0 0 { - } 6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . Sample EEM spectra were corrected for instrument response using excitation-emission correction factors, corrected for inner filtering effect, then blank subtracted to minimize the effect of Raman scattering. The fluorescent intensities were normalized to the area under the waterRaman peak $\mathrm { { ' e x } ~ 3 5 0 ~ n m / e m ~ 3 6 8 { - } 4 5 0 ~ n m ) }$ to report data in

Raman units (RU) (Stedmon et al., 2003). EEM data processing and PARAFAC modeling was conducted using Matlab (MATLAB R2016a, MathWorks) and DOMFluor Toolbox (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). The PARAFAC model was developed from 150 EEM spectra of the raw and MIEX $^ { \mathrm { { ( R ) } } }$ -treated waters. Among these, 2 outlier EEM spectra from instrumentation error were identified through both visual inspection and leverage plot scores, thus were removed prior to further analysis.

EEM data was modeled according to

$$
x _ { i j k } = \sum _ { n = 1 } ^ { N } a _ { i n } b _ { j n } c _ { k n } + \varepsilon _ { i j k } ,
$$

where $x _ { i j k }$ is the fluorescence intensity of sample $i ( i = 1 , . . . , I )$ measured at emission wavelength $j ~ ( j = 1 , . . . , J )$ and excitation wavelength $k$ $( k = 1 , . . . , K )$ . The outcomes of the model are the parameters $a , \ b$ and $c _ { \mathrm { { r } } }$ , which represent concentration, emission spectra, and excitation spectra of the underlying fluorophores, respectively. The number of fluorophores components in the model is represented by $n$ . The matrix of the sum of the squares of the residuals, $\varepsilon _ { i j k }$ , was minimized across the data set and represents the unexplained variation in the model (Stedmon et al., 2003). The proper number of components for each model was validated by four-way split-half analysis (Murphy et al., 2013; C. Stedmon et al., 2003), examination of residual fluorescence EEM spectra (i.e., the difference between the model and the measured excitationemission matrix) and random initialization (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). The fluorescence intensities of the components from the model outputs were reported as the sample-specific maximum fluorescence $\left( F _ { m a x } \right)$ . The $F _ { m a x }$ value of a component represents the concentration of that PARAFAC component in Raman units (RU) (Murphy et al., 2013; Stedmon et al., 2003). Excitation and emission loading of each component was compared to the OpenFluor database of PARAFAC aquatic fluorescence components (Murphy et al., 2014) to determine type and source of the component. Statistical correlation between two parameters was evaluated using Spearman’s rank correlation coefficients (r) (Stata 13.0, StataCorp) and statistical significance of the difference between two outcomes was calculated using pairwise t-tests.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. DOC removal

The impact of MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ treatment on DOC concentration in surface water and secondary effluent is illustrated in Fig. 1. For the two water sources tested, there was no significant difference between performance in DOC removal by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC and MIEX $^ { \mathrm { { ( R ) } } }$ GOLD resins. For surface water, DOC removal initially increased and then decreased progressively, but after 6 consecutive resin uses (when BV treatment was 5000 BV), the removal was not substantially different than for fresh resin (500 BV), at approximately $30 - 3 5 \%$ . It should be noted that a 5000 BV corresponds to a substantially higher water to resin ratio compared to the 600 BV that is typically used in DWTPs (Gibson and Golubovic, 2015; Myat et al., 2012). Therefore, the results suggest that, for surface waters, ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ resin may be used to achieve greater bed volume treatments than currently practiced.

For secondary effluent, DOC removal was lower than for surface water, and DOC removal decreased as BV decreased. Removals of approximately $1 8 \%$ at $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { B V }$ and $8 \%$ at 5000 BV were achieved by both resins. Thus, greater bed volume treatments than currently used in practice caused a significant decrease in DOC removal efficiency in secondary effluent.

The greater removal of DOC from surface water than secondary effluent in this work is consistent with literature reporting that greater DOC removals by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC were observed in waters with higher SUVA (Boyer and Singer, 2006). SUVA strongly correlated to the aromaticity (Weishaar et al., 2003) and waters with high SUVA are generally enriched in hydrophobic materials (Leenheer and Croue, 2003  ). Given the higher SUVA of surface water $( 2 . 2 9 \ \mathrm { L } / $ mg.m) compared to the SUVA of secondary effluent $\left( 1 . 5 9 \mathrm { L / m g . m } \right)$ , the DOM in the surface water was more hydrophobic in nature compared to the secondary effluent. Preferential removal of hydrophobic over hydrophilic fractions of DOM by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC resin was also previously reported (Fearing et al., 2004). In contrast, there is no evidence in the peer-reviewed literature that ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD preferentially removes specific DOM fractions over others. However, considering that MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD has the same quaternary ammonia functional groups and presents similar characteristics to MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC resin (IXOM Watercare, 2019), affinity with hydrophobic materials and similar performance to MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC resin was also expected.

Overall, for the water sources studied, MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC and ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD resins were more effective at treating surface water than secondary effluent, had similar DOC removal efficiency, and exhibited preferential removal of hydrophobic DOM.

Haloacetonitriles formation potential ${ \mathrm { R A N } } _ { 4 }$ -FP) (Fig. 2b) was more than ten times higher in secondary effluent $\left( 1 4 3 \ \mathrm { \mu g / L } \right)$ compared to in surface water $( 1 0 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L } )$ . This result agrees well with literature (Gan et al., 2013), which reported that higher nitrogen content corresponded to higher N-DBP formation; in this study, the TDN of secondary effluent was $9 . 5 9 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ (with correspondingly higher $\mathrm { H A N _ { 4 } – F P }$ ) and that of the surface water was $0 . 4 3 ~ \mathrm { { \ m g / L } }$ (with correspondingly lower $\mathrm { H A N _ { 4 }  – F P }$ ). For $\mathrm { H A N _ { 4 } }$ speciation, DCAN was the dominant HAN species in these two water sources. However, as with the THMs, secondary effluent was shown to form higher concentrations of the two brominated HANs, BCAN and DBAN. TCAN-FP was minimal in both water sources, with formation potentials of 0.02 and $0 . 8 9 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ in surface water and secondary effluent, respectively.

Secondary effluent also presented higher chloropicrin formation potential (CPN-FP, $8 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L } )$ compared to surface water $( 0 . 9 8 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L } )$ (Fig. 2c). For the two haloketones investigated, TCP-FP was higher than DCP-FP in both water sources (Fig. 2c). Haloketones formation potential $\mathrm { [ H K _ { 2 } }$ -FP) was $2 . 5 3 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ in surface water and $2 4 . 4 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ in secondary effluent.

Overall, EfOM in secondary effluent yielded greater formation potentials of C-DBPs and N-DBPs compared to NOM in surface water despite their similar DOC concentration. The only DBP that was formed at greater concentrations in surface water was CF. Therefore, the results indicate that, consistent with literature (Krasner et al., 2008), the source of DOM played a major role in $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 } – \mathrm { F P }$ and $\mathsf { H A N } _ { 4 }$ -FP and that DOC concentration alone is not a reliable indicator of relative differences in DBP-FP between different water sources.

# 3.2.2. DBP formation potential removal by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ resins

Fig. 3 presents results for DBP-FP removal by ${ \tt M I E X } \mathrm { \textcircled { R } }$ treatment. Results show that $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP, $\mathrm { H A N _ { 4 } }$ -FP, CPN-FP and $\mathrm { H K } _ { 2 }$ -FP removals were in the ranges of $3 5 \mathrm { - } 5 1 \%$ , $3 0 { - } 4 7 \%$ , $1 6 - 3 4 \%$ and $4 2 \mathrm { - } 6 2 \%$ , respectively, for surface water and $1 1 - 2 1 \%$ , $1 7 - 2 3 \%$ , $3 9 - 6 4 \%$ and $2 3 \mathrm { - } 3 8 \%$ , respectively, for secondary effluent. Results from pairwise t-tests showed that the two resins achieved significantly $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ ) greater $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP, $\mathrm { H A N } _ { 4 }$ -FP, $\mathrm { H K } _ { 2 }$ -FP removal in surface water than in secondary effluent. On the contrary, the two resins achieved significantly $( p \ < \ 0 . 0 1 )$ greater removal of CPN-FP in effluent compared to in surface water. This result suggests that CPN precursors in surface water differed from those in secondary effluent. The different FP removal levels across DBP groups indicates that their major precursors were different, which agrees well with previous research (Gan et al., 2013). For each DBP group, observed FP removals in surface water using multiple load testing were relatively low compared to results from published batch studies. For example, $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP removal by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC resin in surface waters was reported to be $2 6 { - } 6 9 \%$ (Boyer and Singer, 2005) and as high as $9 8 \%$ (Rajca et al., 2017) for resin doses of $2 { - } 5 ~ \mathrm { m L / L }$ and $1 0 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { L } ,$ respectively. The ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ treatment levels of 500e5000 BV used in this study are equivalent to resin doses of $0 . 2 { - } 2 ~ \mathrm { m L / L }$ in batch operation. Thus, the lower equivalent resin doses used in our study explain the lower FP removal levels observed.

For all experiments, either fresh resin (500 BV) or 1000 BV treatment had the highest FP removal; the FP removal performance decreased at greater bed volume treatments, consistent with the trend observed for DOC removal. It should be noted that the changes in resin performance were not substantially different after 2000 BV treatment, suggesting that the resins maintained their removal efficiency between 2000 and 5000 BV. Decline in resin performance with increasing BV was more substantial in secondary effluent. For example, for surface water, $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP removal by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC resin at 500 BV was $4 9 \%$ and decreased to $3 5 \%$ at 5000 BV; however, the same treatment levels caused $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP removal to drop from $2 1 \%$ to $1 1 \%$ in secondary effluent. For $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP and $\mathsf { H A N } _ { 4 }$ -FP, removals by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD resin were significantly better $\ b { p } < 0 . 0 1 )$ in surface water compared to secondary effluent, similar to the behavior of ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC in this and previous studies (Gan et al., 2013). For example, in this study, removal of $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD resin at 500 BV was $4 6 \%$ for surface water and $1 9 \%$ for secondary effluent. Thus, the different nature of NOM and EfOM not only affected the concentration and speciation of DBPs formed, but also affected precursor removal by MIEX $^ { \mathrm { i } }$ treatment.

Pairwise t-tests were also used to investigate the differences between ${ \tt M I E X } \mathrm { \textcircled { R } }$ DOC and ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD in FP removal. Results showed no significant difference $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ in the performance of the two resins. Gibson and Golubovic (2015) reported that, for some waters, ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD resin provided little to no improvement in resin performance over MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC resin, consistent with our results.

# 3.3. EEM spectra and PARAFAC model

The fluorescence character of 150 EEMs generated from the two water sources with and without MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ treatment was represented well by four fluorescent components (Fig. 4). Table 1 lists the wavelengths of excitation (Ex) and emission (Em) maxima of each component compared to those in previous studies. We interpreted the component spectra based on the existing literature in the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al., 2014) with minimum similarity score (congruence coefficient) of 0.95 (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Component C1 corresponds to ‘Peak A’, humic-like DOM of terrestrial origin (Hambly et al., 2015; Shutova et al., 2014). Component C2 corresponds to anthropogenic DOM in wastewater/ nutrient enriched environments (Murphy et al., 2011). Component C3 was previously identified to correspond to ‘Peak C’ or humiclike, terrestrially derived DOM (Cohen et al., 2014). Component C4 corresponds to the protein-like fluorophores of microbial origin ‘Peak T’, which has excitation/emission maxima in the same region with tryptophan (Stedmon et al., 2011; Yamashita et al., 2011). Spectra from split-half validation results are presented as line plots to the right of each EEM contour plot with excitation (black, left) and emission (red, right) loadings. The solid lines from the complete dataset and dotted lines from one of the independent halves have high degree of similarity with congruent coefficient ${ > } 0 . 9 5$ , thus the same components were found in both halves of the data set indicating four components are the proper number of components for this EEM data set (Stedmon and Bro, 2008).

Despite the similar DOC concentrations in surface water $6 . 6 \mathrm { m g C / L } )$ and secondary effluent $( 7 . 1 7 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ C / L } )$ , the different $F _ { m a x }$ signatures observed in Fig. 5 indicate that the fluorescent DOM compositions of the two waters were different. The differences of the $F _ { m a x }$ scores across fluorescence components were evaluated using ANOVA with Bonferroni correction. For raw surface water, C1 was dominant with significantly higher $F _ { m a x }$ compared to all other components $( p < 0 . 0 1 { \dot { } }$ ), while there was no significant difference between the $F _ { m a x }$ scores of C2, C3, and C4 ${ \boldsymbol { p } } = 1 . 0 0 { \mathrm { \Omega } }$ ). For raw secondary effluent, C1 also had the highest $F _ { m a x } ,$ followed by $\scriptstyle { \mathsf { C } } 2 { \mathsf { > } } { \mathsf { C } } 4 { \mathsf { > } } { \mathsf { C } } 3$ at significant levels $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ . The $F _ { m a x }$ of C2 and C4 was substantially greater in raw secondary effluent compared to in raw surface water. Together, the $F _ { m a x }$ scores indicate that NOM in raw surface water was dominantly humic-like and terrestrially derived (C1), while EfOM in raw secondary effluent had a greater variety of fluorophores, containing more protein-like materials (C4) and anthropogenic humic-like DOM (C2), consistent with a previous study reporting that wastewater was enriched in proteinaceous tryptophan-like matter (Cohen et al., 2014). Protein-like component (C4), which resembles tryptophan-like fluorescence found in aquatic environments (Yamashita et al., 2011), corresponds to fluorescence of aromatic amino acids associated with biological activity (Bridgeman et al., 2013). Thus, the greater $F _ { m a x }$ scores of C4 found in secondary effluent are associated with high microbial abundance (Coble et al., 2014), and are consistent with studies that reported domestic wastewater effluent had higher fluorescence intensity for the components associated with protein-like materials compared to natural water (Bridgeman et al., 2013; Carstea et al., 2016).

After 500 BV MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ -treatment, $F _ { m a x }$ values of the four components in surface water and secondary effluent decreased substantially, with ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC and MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD providing similar removal patterns (Fig. 5). For statistical comparison of component removal, ANOVA with Bonferroni tests were conducted (Fig. 6). For surface water, (Fig. 6a and b), MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ DOC and MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD resins exhibited greater removal of C1, C2 and C3 over C4, indicating that both resins have affinity for humic substances. The preferential removal of humic materials by ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ treatment changed the composition of the surface water producing treated water with enriched microbial character, consistent with previous studies (Arias-Paic et al., 2016; Gan et al., 2013). The predominant presence of humic-like components (C1eC3) in surface water and the apparent affinity of ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ resins for humic substances resulted in effective DBP precursor removal in surface water. For secondary effluent (Fig. 6c and d), removals of the four components for both resins ranked as $\scriptstyle \subset 2 > \mathbf { C } 1 > \mathbf { C } 3 > \mathbf { C } 4 .$ . Although differences in removal efficiencies across components were not substantial, the order of the component removal (i.e., C4 as the least removed component) indicates resin affinity for humic materials (i.e., C1, C2, and C3) over protein-like EfOM (C4), but to a lesser extent for secondary effluent than in surface water.

Among the four groups of DBPs studied, the strongest correlation with $\mathrm { U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ was seen with $\mathrm { T H M _ { 4 }  – F P }$ (Spearman’s rank correlation coefficients, $\rho \ = \ 0 . 7 5 2$ , $p < 0 . 0 1 $ ) (Fig. 7a). Correlations between $\mathrm { U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and other DBPs resulted in $\rho = 0 . 6 1 4 \mathrm { - } 0 . 6 3 4$ $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ (not shown). DOC concentration also significantly correlated with $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP, $\rho \ = \ 0 . 8 4 9$ , $p < 0 . 0 1 $ ) (Fig. 7b) and to a lesser extent with $\mathsf { H A N } _ { 4 }$ -FP, CPN-FP, and $\mathrm { H K } _ { 2 }$ -FP $\mathsf { p } = 0 . 7 8 7 \mathrm { - } 0 . 8 2 7$ , $p < 0 . 0 1$ ) (not shown). However, Fig. 7a and b show that for each $\mathrm { U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and DOC, the data points from surface water and effluent are on distinctly different trend lines. Therefore, the results indicate that $\mathrm { U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and DOC are not appropriate surrogates to assess DBP-FPs across different water sources; however, when studying single water sources $\mathrm { U V } \mathsf { A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and DOC may potentially serve as appropriate indicators of DBP-FPs.

# 4. Conclusions

The following conclusions were drawn from our study on the removal of C-DBP and N-DBP precursors from surface water and secondary treated effluent by MIEX $^ { \mathrm { { ( } \overline { { \mathbb { R } } } ) } }$ DOC and MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ GOLD resins:

Both ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ resins more effectively treated surface water than secondary effluent, including greater removal of $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP, $\mathsf { H A N } _ { 4 } .$ -FP, and $\mathrm { H K } _ { 2 }$ -FP. This was because ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ preferentially removed humic substances over protein-like DOM and the surface water had greater abundance of terrestrial humic substances than secondary treated effluent.   
 There was no significant difference in the performance of ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ DOC and ${ \tt M I E X } \exp$ GOLD resins for DOC, $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP, $\mathsf { H A N } _ { 4 }$ - FP, CPN-FP, and $\mathrm { H K } _ { 2 }$ -FP removals.   
The EEM-PARAFAC approach together with statistical analysis revealed that $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 } – \mathrm { F P }$ strongly correlated with humic-like Component 3, $\mathsf { H A N } _ { 4 } .$ -FP significantly correlated with microbial protein-like Component 4, and CPN-FP and $\mathrm { H K } _ { 2 }$ -FP significantly correlated with the humic-like anthropogenic DOM Component 2.   
Strong correlations were found between (a) the removal of humic-like Component 3 and $\mathrm { T H M } _ { 4 }$ -FP removal, and (b) the removal of protein-like Component 4 and $\mathsf { H A N } _ { 4 }$ -FP removal.

Overall, using one surface water and one effluent, our results indicate that EEM-PARAFAC analysis is a promising tool to predict the formation of C-DBPs and N-DBPs and their removal by MIEX $^ \mathrm { \textregistered }$ resins. Therefore, EEM-PARAFAC could potentially serve as a tool to optimize water treatment process design for controlling DBP formation in these water sources. It remains to be evaluated whether this finding is applicable to other water sources (e.g., ground water for potable water supply) and future work should be directed in this area.
