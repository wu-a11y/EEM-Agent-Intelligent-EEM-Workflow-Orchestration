This study developed an intelligent method for identifying and quantifying water pollution sources in estuarine areas. It characterized the excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectra from seven end-members, including seawater, rainwater, and five pollution sources typical of these areas. A deep learning model was established to identify and quantify these pollution sources in mixed water bodies. The model was fed either the original EEM or a combined EEM and gradient input. The results indicated that the combined input enhanced classification and quantification accuracy; Although model accuracy declined with an increasing number of mixed pollution sources, the combined input still improved classification accuracy by $3 . 1 ~ \%$ to $6 . 8 ~ \%$ ; When the proportion of rainwater and seawater was below $7 0 \%$ , the model maintained a classification accuracy of $5 7 . 4 \%$ with original input and $6 1 . 3 \%$ with combined input, with root mean square error values for the pollution source proportion being $1 2 . 2 ~ \%$ and $1 1 . 4 ~ \%$ , respectively.

# 1. Introduction

Tracing the sources of water pollution has been studied extensively in the field of environmental supervision. In estuaries, fresh water from rivers and streams mixes with seawater. Estuaries become polluted from incoming water upstream of seagoing rivers, domestic and industrial sewage, mariculture, livestock and poultry breeding, agricultural and forestry drainage, and atmospheric rainfall, under the effects of tide and runoff. As such, it is extremely difficult to distinguish pollution sources in estuaries, and this is not conducive to the management of marine pollution.

There are various analytical techniques currently used to determine environmental pollutants, such as mass spectrometry, inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry, capillary electrophoresis, gas chromatography, atomic absorption spectrometry, high-performance liquid chromatography, and chemiluminescence (Yadav et al., 2023). These technologies have several drawbacks that limit their practicality, such as the need for extensive sample pretreatment, complicated handling, and expensive analysis. Therefore, in order to identify various pollutants in the environment, highly reliable, low-cost, and fast methods are needed. In recent years, the combination of fluorescence spectroscopy and chemometrics in pollution source analysis has gained increasing attention. These strategies offer several advantages, such as high sensitivity, speed, simplified sample preparation, and on-site detection (Derrien et al., 2017). Moreover, excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence combined with parallel-factor (PARAFAC) analysis (Carstea et al., 2016; Derrien et al., 2019; Duan et al., 2022), regional integration (Chen et al., 2003), and/or principle components analysis (PCA) (Osburn et al., 2012; Graeber et al., 2012) allows one to obtain comprehensive and quantifiable information about fluorescent compounds in complicated mixed water samples in a short time. Due to the specificity of fluorescent fingerprints from different pollution sources, analyzing the characteristics of fluorescent fingerprints from pollution sources and using similarity comparison algorithms for pollution tracing is also a feasible method (Wu et al., 2006, 2016; Liu et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2021). Various fluorescence indices, such as the humification index (HIX), fluorescence index (FI), and biological index (BIX), have been successfully used to distinguish different sources of pollution (Derrien et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2023). Furthermore, attempts were also made to quantify the sources of dissolved organic matter (DOM) pollution through end-member mixing analysis (EMMA) based on fluorescence indices (Tak et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023). However, in complicated estuary water samples, the fluorescence components from different pollution sources may be very similar and overlap each other, making their identification and quantification challenging.

Recently, deep learning methods have been applied to deal with intricate relationships in complex samples. In the field of analytical chemistry, Zhu et al. (2021); Nguyen et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023) used convolutional neural networks (CNNs), famous for their superior ability of feature extraction and image recognition, to classify and regress spectroscopic signals. Compared to traditional chemometric methods, CNNs are more sensitive to capturing slight differences in the peaks. They are also more tolerant of nonlinear data structures and growing datasets, and they are more generalizable to unseen data (Chen et al., 2023). However, the practicability of CNN authenticity identification models relies on sufficient and diverse training samples to some extent.

Previously, we constructed a sufficient number of mixed-water EEMs based on end-member mixing, and used them to train a multi-task and multi-label convolutional neural network (MTML CNN) to quantitatively trace the sources of mixed water in estuarine areas, confirming the feasibility of the method (Zhao et al., 2022b, 2024). Herein, we propose a rapid, efficient, and practical analytical method for identifying and quantifying multi-source pollution in mixed water bodies in estuaries. Firstly, the differences between fluorophores were characterized using the chemometrics method for the exploratory analysis of seven endmembers in the estuary. Then, deep learning was combined with the EEM fluorescence spectra of 283 actual measured end-member samples and 10,000 mixtures to establish authenticity identification models. To improve the accuracy of the models, two types of model inputs were designed and tested: the original EEM, and the combined use of the EEM and its gradient in the $x$ and $y$ directions. One of the models can distinguish different end-members (Case 1) and the other model can identify and quantify pollution mixtures (Case 2) and multiple pollution sources in estuaries (Case 3). This study hopes to realize the intelligent analysis of EEM fluorescence fingerprints through deep learning, that is, automatically and rapidly determining the category and proportion of pollution sources, and to expand the application of EEM fluorescence to authenticity detection.

# 2.2. Fluorescence spectrum analysis, optical indices, and PARAFAC modeling

The EEM spectra of the samples were obtained using a fluorescence spectrometer F-7000 (Hitachi Co., Japan). The excitation (Ex) wavelengths ranged from 200 to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and the emission (Em) wavelengths ranged from 250 to $5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . Excitation and emission were both maintained at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ , and the scanning speed was $3 0 , 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m / m i n }$ .

PARAFAC analysis was performed on the EEM data acquired from the seven end-member samples. The model was constrained to nonnegative values, and the results were validated using split half analysis. All data handling and analysis described above were performed in MATLAB $2 0 1 9 \mathbf { a }$ (MathWorks) using the drEEM toolbox (Murphy et al., 2013). Detailed comparisons and validation for the PARAFAC model were made using the Tucker congruence coefficient (TCC).

Three optical indices were calculated: a) HIX, for which values ${ \sim } 1 { - } 2$ are associated with non-humified material, and values ${ > } 1 0$ are typical for fulvic acid extracts (Zsolnay et al., 1999); b) FI, for which values ${ \sim } 1 . 3$ suggest a dominant terrestrial, higher-plant DOM source, and values ${ \sim } 1 . 8$ suggest a predominant microbial DOM source (McKnight et al., 2001); and c) BIX, for which values ${ \sim } 0 . 6 \substack { - 0 . 8 }$ indicate that the contribution of DOM is mainly due to terrestrial inputs, and values $^ { > 1 }$ are mainly from biological or bacterial sources (Huguet et al., 2009).

# 2.3. Dataset description

Three datasets were prepared to identify and quantify the pollution sources of mixed water in estuarine areas. Firstly, exploratory analysis was implemented to distinguish the seven end-members with the EEM data acquired directly from the 283 samples (labeled as Dataset 1). Then, pollution mixtures that mixed one or more pollution sources (Dataset 2) were classified and quantified. Dataset 2 was formed using random mixed EEM spectra. In the random mixing process, it was ensured that the probability of each pollution source appearing and not appearing was approximately 0.5, and that the mixing ratio of each pollution source remained random. If the mixing ratio was $< 5 ~ \%$ , it was considered that the pollution source could be ignored. Dataset 2 contained 10,000 EEM data. A more practical situation was further explored to distinguish water pollution sources in estuarine areas, where the water is not only a mixture of pollution sources but also mixed with seawater and rainwater (Dataset 3). The formation of Dataset 3 was based on Dataset 2. Rainwater and seawater were randomly mixed to obtain a clean water mixture. Then, the pollution mixtures of Dataset 2 and the clean water mixture were randomly mixed. As the clean water mixture served merely as a background and not as the primary subject of recognition, the proportion of clean water mixture was kept within 95 $\%$ . Dataset 3 also contained 10,000 EEM data. The three datasets were each divided into a training set and a test set for deep learning. The ratio of the training set to the test set was 8:2. To make the test set invisible to the deep learning model, for Dataset 2 and 3, we first extracted $2 0 \%$ of the data of each end-member and ensured that these data were not used for training. Then, we randomly mixed these data to form the test set (with the same steps as above).

# 2.4. Classification and quantitative analysis models

In this study, two CNN models were employed: a single-task and single-label CNN model (named STSL CNN) for the purpose of endmember classification, and an MTML CNN model that takes advantage of multi-task learning and multi-label learning, for effective mixed pollution source classification and quantification.

The architecture of the MTML CNN is shown in Fig. 1. A six-layer CNN model was utilized to extract the shared features of EEM. The two related tasks of pollution source classification and quantification were extracted. For authenticity identification purposes, the output of the CNN classification layer (FC1) consisted of a vector of five neurons, recording the probability distribution of different pollution sources in the mixed environmental water. Since there may be a mixture of multiple pollution sources in the natural water body, the multi-hot code was used in FC1. For quantitative purposes, five neurons were outputted by the CNN quantification layer (FC2), directly mapping the proportion of each pollution source in the mixed environmental water. The softmax function was applied to FC2 to constrain its sum to be equal to one. For pollution classification, the MultiLabelSoftMarginLoss was used. While in the task of pollution quantification, the model was trained using a mean squared error (MSE) loss function:

$$
R _ { \mathrm { M S E } } = \frac { 1 } { n } \sum _ { i } ( y _ { i } - x _ { i } ) ^ { 2 } .
$$

For STSL CNN, seven neurons were outputted by the CNN layer and the one-hot code was used in the FC layer, directly mapping the probability of each end-member. The softmax function was applied to the FC layer to constrain its sum to be equal to one. The CrossEntropyLoss was used instead for STSL CNN.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Fluorescence characteristics of end-members

# 3.1.1. PARAFAC analysis

In this study, the fluorescence characteristics of seven typical endmembers in estuaries were analyzed first. Raman and Rayleigh scattering were nonlinear factors that adversely affected the models based on a linear data structure. Therefore, the contribution of Raman scattering was eliminated by directly subtracting the average of solvent blanks. First- and second-order Rayleigh scattering was removed and interpolated before subsequent analysis. The typical EEM fluorescence spectra of the seven end-members were pre-processed and shown as contour maps (Fig. S1). These EEM fluorescence spectra are different from each other, and they can be regarded as fingerprints, since they reflect the intrinsic fluorophores and microenvironments of different sources.

The EEM differences in the seven end-members were characterized by the PARAFAC algorithm to mathematically separate the fluorescence bands and provide their qualitative and quantitative adulteration information. Due to the lower fluorescence intensity of rainwater and seawater compared to other five pollution end-members (mariculture, domestic, municipal, soil, and plants), they were analyzed separately.

For five pollution end-members, six fluorescent components (labeled C1, C2, C3, C4, C5, and C6 in order; see Table S1 and Fig. S2 for details) were characterized from 212 samples, and $9 9 . 1 \ \%$ of the EEM variation was explained. Based on previous studies (Brogi et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2022), C1 was assigned as peak T and is identified as a tryptophan-like component. C2 is a UV humic-like component that conforms to the characteristics of peak A. C3 is a marine-derived humic-like component with a fluorescence peak M. C4 and C6, assigned as peak C, are identified as humic-like components. C5 is consistent with the fluorescence spectra of the mixing of C and A peaks. It is identified with humic-like and UV humic-like components. Fig. S3 further describes the relative concentration scores of six components together with their normalized excitation and emission loadings. The composition of each individual pollution end-member differs. In marine aquaculture drainage, the primary components are C3, C2, and C1, which respectively represent marine humic-like substances, UV humic-like substances, and humiclike substances. Fluorescent DOM in mariculture water mainly comes from high-density shrimp biomass and its foraging, excretion, and death processes (Zhu et al., 2019). The fluorescence spectra of untreated domestic wastewater are characterized by intense peaks in the region Em $< 3 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ —especially peak T, associated with high microbial abundance—and by lower intensity peaks A and C fluorescence (Baker, 2001; Hudson et al., 2010; Bridgeman et al., 2013). In the present study, the C1 component (peak T) of domestic sewage is notably higher than the other components (see Fig. S1 and Fig. S3), similar to previous research (Carstea et al., 2016). Domestic wastewater contains the solid and liquid discharges of humans and animals, contributing millions of bacteria, viruses, and non-pathogenic and pathogenic organisms. It also contains sanitary products, cleaners and detergents, trash, garbage, and any other substances that are poured or flushed into the sewer system (Drinan and Spellman, 2012). Nevertheless, its fluorescence characteristics remain stable. In addition to collecting domestic wastewater, public treatment facilities collect industrial effluents. Thus chemicals, dyes, acids, alkalies, grit, and detergents can be found in municipal wastewater (Drinan et al., 2000). Among the peaks, T and C seem to be present in most municipal wastewater samples and may serve as indicators of wastewater contamination (Carstea et al., 2016). The fluorescence spectra of municipal sewage here have a higher concentration of C1, C2, C3, and C4 and show a higher prevalence of peaks T, A, M, and C (see Fig. S1 and Fig. S3). In the topsoil of forests and vegetable fields, the C2 and C6 components are higher (peaks A and C). The fluorescence spectra of forest and grass soil showed the humic-like peak C and humic-like peak A (Coble, 1996). Peak A represents poly-aromatic humic acid (PAHA), whereas peak C represents poly-carboxylate humic acid (PCHA), which is typically derived from the breakdown of plant and animal substances (Lee et al., 2008; Liu et al., 2019). The dissolved organic matter in soil mainly includes humus-like substances and tyrosine-like substances, and the distribution of these components is not significantly different in different climatic regions (Gao et al., 2020). For riparian plants and fallen leaves in forests, the C5 (C6) component is higher in riparian plants (fallen leaves in forests). The fluorescence spectra of riparian plants and fallen leaves showed a strong humic-like peak C. The degree of humification of DOM in terrestrial plants is greater than that in aquatic plants (Xie et al., 2013).

For seawater and rainwater end-members, the five-component PARAFAC model was considered suitable, and $9 9 . 0 ~ \%$ of the variation in 71 EEMs was explained. Table S1 and Fig. S4 show that the maximum excitation/emission pair of C1, C2, C3, C4, and C5 was $2 9 5 ( < 2 5 0 ) / 4 0 5$ , 275/340, 270/305, 250(340)/440, and $2 7 0 / 3 8 0 \ \mathrm { \ n m }$ , respectively, representing the marine humic-like component (peak M), tryptophanlike component (peak T), tyrosine-like component (peak B2), UV humic-like component (peak A), humic-like component (peak C), and the component related tophytoplankton (peak N). The fluorescence intensity of seawater and rainwater is significantly lower than that of other end-members (see Fig. S5). In the seawater, the position of the overall fluorescence maximum occurs at shorter wave-lengths for marine surface samples (see Fig. S1). T-type and N-type fluorescences are generally the highest in the seawater samples. C-type and M-type fluorescence also exists in seawater samples, similar to previous research (Coble, 1996). The rainwater samples collected in South China show three characteristic fluorophores: marine and UV humic-like substances (peaks M and A), tryptophan-like substances (peak T), and tyrosine-like substances (peak B2)(see Fig. S1 and Fig. S5), consistent with previous research (Catherine et al., 2008; Wu et al., 2015). The rainwater in this area is significantly affected by monsoons. UV humic-like substances (peak A) originate from terrestrial particulate matter, while the marine humiclike substances (peak M) are due to the high concentration of marine humic-like substances in the air blown from the Pacific Ocean (Wu et al., 2015). Sources of tryptophan-like and tyrosine-like compounds are likely to include plant matter, bacteria, yeast, spores, and pollen associated with terrestrial areas (Milne and Zika, 1993), and high concentrations have also been detected in oceans (Determann et al., 1996). Though the precise locations of the peak excitation and emission wavelengths can vary between samples and events (storm type and source area, wind speed, air quality before rainfall, impact of local human activities, etc.)(Catherine et al., 2008), all the rainwater samples show similar EEM spectra compared to other sources.

Consequently, each end-member can be characterized by the load capacity of PARAFAC, and their differences and similarities can be mapped to scores. This difference provides for the possibility of distinguishing different sources of pollution in mixed water bodies at estuaries, but it requires a more powerful model to capture qualitative and quantitative information differentiate pollution sources.

# 3.1.2. Spectroscopic indices and PCA results

Based on previous literature (Lee et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2022), HIX, BIX, and FI were the possible optical descriptors that could be taken into account for DOM source tracers. Therefore, the fluorescence indices of each end-member were also studied. Table S2 provides the HIX, BIX, and FI values for each end-member. Fig. S6 further shows the different distributions of the seven end-members in FI-HIX space. Among these endmembers, seawater and rainwater have comparable HIX and FI values, both with lower HIX values. However, seawater has a higher BIX value than rainwater, indicating that seawater DOM is mainly derived from biological or bacterial sources. In the effluent groups, their FI values are generally ${ > } 2$ . The municipal sewage treatment plant effluent exhibited higher BIX (1.17) but lower HIX (3.12) values, indicating that this endmember includes large amounts of microbial sources that originated from biological treatment processes (Kim et al., 2022). The relatively lower HIX (1.37) values in domestic sewage suggest a significant presence of fresh biological organic matter. The high HIX values of soil, riparian plants, and fallen leaves indicate the intense humification of DOM. The above results show that the fluorescence indices of each endmember used in this study are different, but there is also some overlap, such as soil and aquaculture, municipal sewage and domestic sewage, rainwater and seawater, and so on.

The results indicate that relying solely on spectroscopic indices may not be able to distinguish the end-members well. In addition, although the spectroscopic indices behaved conservatively, indices such as HIX, BIX and FI changed in non-linear patterns with the mixing ratios (Yang and Hur, 2014). This limits their application in EMMA (Tak et al., 2021), a popular method for quantifying the relative contribution of different sources.

PCA was also fitted on the EEM fluorescences of the seven endmembers. As shown in Fig. S7, the first two PCA component variance ratios of EEM were 0.566 and 0.256, explaining up to $8 2 . 2 \%$ of the total variance. The majority of plants were grouped separately, while mariculture, domestic sewage, and municipal sewage were grouped together. Seawater and rainwater were also separately grouped together. The overlap of end-members in the first and second principal component space seems to be similar to their distribution in the FI-HIX space.

# 3.2. Intelligent traceability of water pollution in estuary regions

In estuarine waters, various natural and anthropogenic pollution sources mix with each other, posing great challenges to pollution tracing. This study attempts to reproduce this mixing process by generalizing the mixed water bodies in estuarine areas into five pollution sources (aquaculture, domestic sewage, municipal sewage, soil sources, and plant sources) and a mixture of relatively clean water bodies (rainwater and seawater). On this basis, the STSL CNN model was first used based on Dataset 1 to evaluate the ability of deep learning model to identify the different end-members (Case 1). The MTML CNN model was then adopted to identify the types of pollution sources and quantitatively evaluate the proportions of pollution sources in mixed water bodies in estuarine areas. Case 2 uses Dataset 2 to classify and quantitatively identify mixtures from five types of pollution sources. Case 3 uses Dataset 3, which adds the influence of seawater and rainwater on the basis of Case 2, making it more realistic.

# 3.2.1. Intelligent recognition of end-members

An attempt was made to use the STSL CNN model to recognize the seven end-members in Dataset 1. Several studies used derivatives to find UV–vis spectral features, compare their differences in terms of the effects of various derivatives, and propose the most suitable derivative order for specific research (De Luca et al., 2009; Ichimura, 2017; Zhu et al., 2021). The introduction of derivatives can strengthen the characteristics but also amplify the random error (Zhu et al., 2021). Similarly, the various gradients of EEM may also strengthen the fluorescence features, but so far there has been no research in this area. Hence, the combined use of the EEM gradient was adopted in this study. Two types of model inputs were designed for the models: 1) the original EEM, and 2) the combined use of EEM and its gradient in the $x$ and $y$ directions $( \nabla _ { x } \mathrm { E E M }$ and $\nabla _ { y } \mathrm { E E M } )$ . It is worth noting that the CNN model input for the original EEM is designed as a single channel, while the model input for the combined input is designed as three channels, because the combined input has three matrices (EEM, $\nabla _ { x } \mathrm { E E M }$ and $\nabla _ { y } \mathrm { E E M } )$ .

Table 2 shows the classification accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and precision on the test set of Dataset 1 in Case 1. During the crossvalidation process, the accuracy obtained by the original input and combined input was $9 7 . 6 { \pm } 3 . 6 \%$ and $9 7 . 9 { \pm } 2 . 6 \%$ , respectively, proving that the combined input improves the classification accuracy. Among all end-members, except for the decrease in specificity and precision of the aquaculture end-member, the sensitivity, specificity, and precision of the other end-members was maintained or improved. Regardless of the original or combined inputs, the sensitivity, specificity, and precision values were $1 0 0 \%$ for seawater and rainwater, indicating that seawater and rainwater can easily be identified by the algorithm, even in the test group. As can be seen, municipal and mariculture sewage were the most confusing classes because they have similar component composition (see Fig. S3 and Fig. S7), resulting in some misclassification by both types of inputs. But overall, their accuracy is above $9 0 \%$ , and the total accuracy of the combined inputs is higher.

The results demonstrate that compared to spectroscopic indices and PCA results, deep learning model can better distinguish these endmembers. However, it is worth noting that STSL CNN model is suitable for distinguishing water bodies with a single source of pollution, but cannot effectively identify multi-source pollution.

# 3.2.2. Classification and quantitative analysis of mixed pollution sources

According to the scores of PARAFAC in previous section, the difference in EEM fingerprints between end-members is mainly due to different combinations of several fluorescent components. The fluorescence spectrum of end-member mixtures is generally a linear superposition of the fluorescence spectra of these end-members (Chen et al., 2023). This is the basis for tracing pollution sources through fluorescence spectroscopy. However, at the same time, the mixture of different end-members may cover up weaker fluorescent signals, making it difficult to recognize the end-member well. Therefore, it is a more complicated task to identify the pollution sources in pollution mixtures, and this requires a more powerful model. On the basis of end-member classification, we extended the STSL CNN model to the MTML CNN model for multi classification and quantitative analysis of mixed pollution sources (Case 2). The multi-task model we use here has been proven to improve model accuracy in quantitative analysis tasks (Zhao et al., 2022a). Similarly, both model inputs (the original input and combined input) were conducted.

The MTML CNN model was trained and estimated on Dataset 2, and its overall performance is shown in Table 2. # 3.3. Model application and future suggestions

The MTML CNN model of combined input were used to analyze the source of pollution in the basin of Qianshan River. Qianshan River is a transboundary river between cities. It starts from Lianshiwan, Zhongshan City in the west, introduces Xijiang River water from Modaomen Waterway to the east, passes Zhongshan City and Zhuhai City, and flows into the Pearl River Estuary at Shijiaozui, with a total length of about 23 km and a drainage area of $3 2 8 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ (see Fig. 3). The pollution sources in the Qianshan River basin are diverse, including aquaculture, domestic sewage, municipal sewage, agricultural cultivation, forest land, and so on (see the land cover in Fig. 3(b)). A total of 23 station samples were collected in Qianshan River (see Fig. 3(a)).

It can be seen that the types of sewage at stations T1-T12, T25 and T26 are mainly municipal sewage or domestic sewage, while T16-T19, T21-T23 are mainly mariculture, which is basically consistent with the distribution of the land use (see Fig. 3(b)). Furthermore, the proportion of municipal sewage is often high near sewage treatment plants. Ultimately, it can be roughly determine that the main sources of pollution in section T1 of the river entering the sea are municipal sewage $( 4 7 \% )$ , domestic sewage $( 3 0 \% )$ , and marine aquaculture drainage $( 1 9 \% )$ , in descending order.

Our model employs a multi-task, multi-label structure, which is relatively complex but has been proven to enhance the model’s accuracy and practicality (Zhao et al., 2022a, 2022b, 2024). In practical applications, we only need to collect water samples from the key sections of rivers for analysis, and the MTML CNN model can predict the composition of pollution sources in these sections. Moreover, if the model is applied to automatic monitoring stations, it is possible to constantly understand the composition and changes of pollution sources, which is beneficial for environmental management.

It is important to note that the sources of pollution in estuarine areas are extremely diverse, and the composition of pollution sources (for example, there may also be ship pollution sources, various industrial pollution sources, sediment source and aquatic organisms, etc.) and ecological environments (such as climate, terrain, vegetation, organisms, etc.) in different estuarine areas may also be completely different, leading to different sources and migration and transformation processes of DOM. Due to various physical, chemical, and biological processes that affect the migration and transformation of DOM, the timeliness of source tracing is generally within a few hours to several days. For DOM that has undergone significant changes in the natural environment, its tracing accuracy will be reduced. In estuarine areas, changes in water salinity alone do not cause significant changes in the optical characteristics of DOM (Han and Guo, 2008). However, temperature can affect the fluorescence intensity, so it should be noted that all samples collected for model building should be at the same temperature level, which means they should be collected in the same season as much as possible. Additionally, since MTML CNN model primarily identifies fluorescence components rather than fluorescence intensity for quantitative source tracing (as each EEM input will be standardized, and fluorescence intensity information will be lost), fluorescence intensity only affects the process of forming the dataset from the mixed pollution end-members, which minimizes model errors that could be introduced due to differences in fluorescence intensity.

Additionally, this method may not be suitable for estuaries with overly complex pollution sources, such as the mixed discharge of a large number of different types of industrial wastewater. This is because the mixing of too many different types of pollution sources may significantly reduce the accuracy of the results. In our study, it may be more appropriate to have 2 to 7 types of pollution sources.

When using this method for pollution source tracing in a new estuary, certain rules must be followed: first, the selected types of pollution sources must be representative, requiring the selection of appropriate pollution source types to ensure that these pollution source types are representative in that estuary; Second, the selected pollution source types must have specificity in their fluorescence, which means that the fluorescence spectra of the same type of pollution sources should be as consistent as possible, while the fluorescence spectra of different types of pollution sources should have significant differences, which directly determines the accuracy of model recognition; Third, the fluorescence characteristics of the selected pollution source types should be relatively stable, the more stable the fluorescence, the longer the traceability time, therefore, when sampling pollution sources, samples with more stable spectra should be collected as much as possible (for example, samples that have been exposed to the natural environment and where DOM has been fully photodegraded). Furthermore, it is important to collect samples, if possible, during the same season.

# 4. Conclusions

In this study, a technique was proposed that integrates EEM fluorescence spectroscopy with deep learning for the intelligent and automatic detection of pollution sources in estuaries. A diverse dataset comprising seven end-members—seawater, rainwater, mariculture, domestic, municipal, soil, and plants—was analyzed for better practicality in estuary scenarios. Taking advantage of mathematical separation, PARAFAC roughly realized spectral characterization and provided qualitative and quantitative information on fluorophores in the seven end-members.

A single-task, single-label convolutional neural network (STSL CNN) was developed to identify the seven end-members (Case 1), while a multi-task, multi-label CNN (MTML CNN) was designed to identify and quantify pollution mixtures (Case 2) and multi-source pollution in estuaries (Case 3). The STSL CNN achieved, $9 7 . 6 { \pm } 3 . 6 \ \%$ and $9 7 . 9 { \pm } 2 . 6 \ \%$ accuracy with the original and combined inputs, respectively, but it was limited to single-source pollution detection. The MTML CNN, an extension of the STSL CNN, demonstrated higher sensitivity, specificity, and precision with combined inputs, achieving an accuracy of $6 3 . 6 \pm 9 . 0 \%$ ( $3 . 2 \%$ higher than the original input). The root mean square error (RMSE) of the pollution sources proportions derived from the original input and combined input was $1 2 . 3 { \pm } 2 . 3 \%$ and $1 1 . 8 { \pm } 1 . 9 \ \%$ , respectively. The model’s accuracy decreased with more mixed sources but was more robust with combined inputs. In estuaries, when the proportion of rainwater and seawater was below $7 0 ~ \%$ , the model using the original input maintained a classification accuracy of $5 7 . 4 ~ \%$ , while with combined input, it was $6 1 . 3 ~ \%$ . The RMSE of the pollution proportion was $1 2 . 2 \%$ for the original input and $1 1 . 4 \%$ for the combined input. Therefore, the results suggest that the combined input improved the overall accuracy of both the STSL CNN and MTML CNN models, particularly in identifying multiple pollution sources.

The findings indicated that the MTML CNN model, when integrated with the combined EEM input, was more sensitive to subtle variations in the EEM fluorescence signatures of different end-members and could effectively apply to new, unseen samples. Although this approach has preliminarily succeeded in intelligently determining the types and ratios of pollution sources in mixed water bodies, further validation is needed in actual estuarine source tracing, such as comparing the results with isotope tracing to prove the reliability of the model. And further research is required to enhance the model’s accuracy in identifying multiple pollution sources. This enhancement will involve integrating additional characteristics of pollution sources, such as UV–visible absorption spectra, and evaluating potential model refinements. Following this, the model’s performance will be assessed in scenarios featuring a higher number of mixed pollution sources across various estuarine regions.
