# a b s t r a c t

The structural compositions of dissolved organic matter (DOM) could profoundly affect formation and evolution of black-odor waterbodies (BOWs). In this study, 81 samples of BOWs were collected from three different latitudinal rural regions in eastern China, including low, middle and high latitude regions. Based on fluorescence index (FI) and biological index (BIX) deduced from excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) of rural BOWs, biological source of DOM was dominant in low latitude, while DOM derived from both biological and terrestrial in midlatitude and high-latitude. Furthermore, humification degree of DOM in the former was lower than those in the latter based on humification index (HIX) deduced from EEMs. Seven fluorescence components of DOM were extracted by EEMs combined with parallel factor analysis: components 1 and 2 (C1 and C2) known as tryptophan-like substances, C3 and C4 associated with tyrosine-like, C5 related with biological byproducts, C6 relative to fulvic-like, and C7 referred as humic-like. The roughly decreasing order of percentages in DOM fractions from the rural BOWs was tyrosine-like $>$ tryptophan-like $>$ fulvic-like $>$ microbial byproduct $>$ humiclike in three regions. According to hierarchical cluster analysis and redundancy analysis, the autochthonous fresh DOM was dominant in low latitudinal rural BOWs, which was relative to actions of phytoplankton and microorganisms. However, humification degree of DOM increased with a rise in latitude, which could attribute to variations of climate and agriculture industrial structure. Based on structure equation model, the C5 and FI were the potential factors of the rural BOWs, which suggested that microbial activity and pollution sources should affect formation and evolution of rural BOWs. These findings are conductive to reveal composition and fluorescence properties of DOM and in recognizing the potential factors of forming mechanism in rural BOWs, which could provide basic theoretical support for policymakers to regulate and treat it.

# 1. Introduction

Aquatic environment, because of its various functions that included domestic, agricultural, industrial, and recreational purposes, plays an irreplaceable role in not only the natural ecological environment but also human health and social development (Li et al., 2021). However, multiple anthropogenic activities impact on waterbody resulted in deterioration of water quality and the phenomenon of black-odor waterbodies (BOWs), i.e., waterbodies become black with unpleasant smell (Cao et al., 2020; Miao et al., 2021). The BOWs are found all over the world, such as the Thames in London, the Cheonggye Stream in Seoul, the Seine in Paris, the Rhine in Europe, and the Suzhou River in China (Cai et al., 2019; Miao et al., 2021; Sarigai et al., 2021). The water that is black and smelly loses its basic function, affects living conditions of residents, and threatens health of aquatic ecosystem.

With social and economic rapid development, environmental livability gains more attention and many restoration programs of water environment have been conducted in North America, Europe, and East Asia (Choe et al., 2014). In China, the BOWs are frequently reported and become a serious environmental issue that receives high attention from the government. The Chinese Ministry of Housing and UrbanRural Development (CMOHURD) and the Chinese Ministry of Ecology and Environment (CMOEE) released working guidelines for the treatment of BOWs in urban and rural, in 2015 and 2019 respectively. The corresponding standards and indices were defined by CMOHURD and CMOEE for identification and classification of the urban and rural BOWs (Table S1). Up to now, the proportion of treated urban BOWs is now up to $8 0 \%$ (2313 BOWs have now been managed) (http://gz. hcstzz.com). Compared to the urban BOWs, rural BOWs, known as small waterbodies in villages, are widespread in China, which are even more inducive to ecosystem deterioration. It is urgent to conduct surveys for investigating pollution status of rural BOWs.

Generally, the BOWs are caused by excessive organic pollutants loaded into water, as well as imbalance of oxygen supply and consumption (Liang et al., 2018). Decomposition of organic matter consumes large amounts of dissolved oxygen, which resulted in the water form an anaerobic and strong reducing condition. It promotes the formation of compounds that cause black and odor, including ferrous sulfide, manganese sulfide, hydrogen sulfide, ammonium, and so on (Song et al., 2017; Xin et al., 2012; Zhao et al., 2020). It is indispensable that to clarify the origin and composition of DOM for insight into the formation and evolution of BOWs. Labile DOM, from either autochthonous or allochthonous organic matter, had been found as a critical vector that control oxygen concentration which indirectly induced water blackening along Siak river, a typical black river in central Sumatra, Indonesia (Rixen et al., 2010). Autochthonous organic matter, e.g., aquatic animals, plants and plankton, was an important factor contributing to black bloom with odor in lake Taihu, China (Feng et al., 2014). DOM pool is mainly derived from autochthonous sources in a black water river in Venezuela, Orinoco (Battin, 1998). Black water layer, occurring in Mystic Lake (US), was the by-product of sulfate-reducing bacteria mediated through chemolithotrophic or heterotrophic process (Duval and Ludlam, 2001). Hence, BOWs are rich in DOM derived from autochthonous origin, which is observably different from fresh water, where allochthonous DOM predominate (Hudson et al., 2008). However, there are few studies on variations of dissolved organic matter (DOM) in rural BOWs and how DOM fractions potentially affect forming mechanism of BOWs. Therefore, it is significant to investigate composition of DOM in rural BOWs and reveal potential factors of the BOWs formation.

Fluorescent dissolved organic matter makes up a substantial fraction of DOM, and studies of its optical properties could provide information about the composition, quantity, and transformation of DOM pool (Ishii and Boyer, 2012; SanClements et al., 2012). Sources of DOM in water could be categorized as allochthonous and autochthonous materials, which are derived from outside and within the ecosystem, respectively (Goldman et al., 2012). DOM from different sources has specific optical characteristics and components. Fluorescence spectroscopy is an effective tool to measure and identify characteristics of DOM, which can absorb wavelengths of light and then re-emit those energies as fluorescence. By measurement, fluorescence data with comprehensive information are presented in three-dimensional excitationemission matrices (EEMs) spectroscopy. The measuring intensity of re-emission energy can be used to quantify DOM (Osburn et al., 2012). Moreover, parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis is a mathematical method for decomposing overlapped fluorescence components, which allowed a much more accurate quantitative analysis of EEMs data than peakpicking or regional integration (FRI) method (Yu et al., 2015). EEMs coupled with PARAFAC have been widely used to characterize DOM in environmental samples (Qian et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2017).

This study investigates the composition of DOM by EEMs combined with PARAFAC in 81 BOWs samples from three different latitudinal rural regions. Moreover, structural equation models (SEMs) were applied to gain insight into the potential influence of DOM fractions and sources on forming mechanism of rural BOWs. The objectives of this study were (a) to characterize variations of DOM fractions in different latitudinal rural BOWs; (b) to discriminate main pollution sources of rural BOWs; (c) to reveal interactions between optical properties and fractions of DOM from rural BOWs and environmental variables; (d) to explore potential factors influencing formation and evolution of BOWs.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study area and sample collection

In this study, three cities with different latitude in eastern China that faced heavy pollution waterbodies in their rural areas, namely Shenyang (SY), Qingdao (QD), and Foshan (FS), were selected for studying of the variations and composition of DOM fractions in rural BOWs. SY $4 1 ^ { \circ } 4 8 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { N }$ , $1 2 3 ^ { \circ } 2 5 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { E }$ ), the capital of Liaoning province, is located in high latitude region, which belongs to the temperate zone with annual average precipitation of $7 5 0 \mathrm { m m }$ and annual average temperature of ${ 9 ~ ^ { \circ } C } .$ QD $3 6 ^ { \circ } 0 5 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { N }$ , $1 1 9 ^ { \circ } 4 5 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { E }$ ), a vice-provincial city of Shandong province, is located in the temperate zone of middle latitude region with annual average precipitation of $6 8 0 \mathrm { m m }$ and annual average temperature of $1 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . FS $2 3 ^ { \circ } 0 2 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { N } .$ , $1 1 3 ^ { \circ } 0 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { E }$ ), a prefecture city of Guangdong province, is located in the low latitude region, which is a subtropical city with annual average precipitation of $1 3 1 0 \mathrm { m m }$ and annual average temperature of $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ .

A total of 173 water samples were obtained from 173 suspected BOWs, which selected through remote sensing interpretation, in rural regions of three cities in three field surveys. According to the standard of CMOEE, 81 of the 173 water samples were identified as rural BOWs. The BOWs were in different villages and towns, consisting of 17 samples in the SY, 33 samples in the QD, and 31 samples in the FS (Fig. 1). Due to waterbodies were freeze in winter, especially in middle and low latitude regions of China, the field surveys were conducted in October in 2020 and April in 2021. The samples of SY were obtained from tributaries of an urbanized river in October, those of QD were gathered both from ponds and rivers in April, and the samples of FS were also collected from rivers in April. Surface water sample collection was carried out with a 5-L Van Dom water sampler. Three water samples were collected from central of each waterbody, which were completely mixed with the equal amounts and put into a polyethylene bottle. Those samples (1 L) were shipped to the lab in a cooled container for further analysis.

# 2.2. Measurements of physico-chemical parameters

According to the corresponding standards defined by CMOHURD and CMOEE (Table S1), Dissolved oxygen (DO) and Secchi depth (SD) were measured using a YSI portable multiparameter water quality tester and a standard Secchi disk with black and white quarters in situ.

# 2.3. Fluorescence measurements and optical indexes

Water samples were filtered using glass fiber filters (Millipore, $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fiber $\varnothing$ ) before the fluorescence determination. The fluorescence EEMs of the BOWs were determined using a Hitachi fluorescence spectrophotometer (F-7000) with a 150 W Xenon lamp and a $1 \mathrm { \ c m }$ quartz cuvette. The scan speed, PMT voltage, excitation (ex) and emission (em) range were set at $1 2 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m \ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ , 700 V, 200–450 nm (step $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ), and $2 6 0 { - } 5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (step $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ), respectively (Guo et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2013). The excitation and emission data were corrected for specific equipment responses to reduce inner filtering effects (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). For adjusting the EEMs data, spectra of a blank Milli-Q water solution were subtracted from the original data by FL-solution software (Murphy et al., 2013).

Optical indexes are calculated by the data from EEMs, such as fluorescence index (FI), biological index (BIX), and humification index (HIX), which can provide effective information about sources, composition, and properties (Zhang et al., 2020a). The three indexes are calculated by the following formulations:

$$
\mathrm { F I } = { \frac { I _ { E m 4 5 0 \ n m , E x 3 7 0 \ n m } } { I _ { E m 5 0 0 \ n m , E x 3 7 0 \ n m } } }
$$

$$
\mathsf { B I X } = \frac { I _ { E m 3 8 0 \ n m , E x 3 1 0 \ n m } } { I _ { E m 4 3 0 \ n m , E x 3 1 0 \ n m } }
$$

$$
{ \mathrm { H I X } } = { \frac { \sum I _ { E m 4 3 5 - 4 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m } , E x 2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m } } } { \sum I _ { E m 3 0 0 - 3 4 5 \ \mathrm { n m } , E x 2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m } } } }
$$

where Ex represents the excitation and Em represents emission, I is the fluorescence intensity.

FI can be used to describe the sources and degree of degradation of DOM (Zhang et al., 2020b). BIX can be associated with the intensity of recent biological activities, i.e., an index that measures the degree of autochthonous pollution (Huguet et al., 2009). HIX showed the humification degree, a higher degree of humification with a more stable DOM that can exist longer in the environment (Zsolnay et al., 1999).

# 2.4. PARAFAC analysis

To perform the PARAFAC analysis, a dataset with information of excitation wavelength, emission wavelength, and sample numbers was built containing 81 EEMs of BOWs samples. The dataset was modeled with PARAFAC utilizing the MATLAB 2019a software with DOMFluor toolbox (www.models.life.ku.dk, version 1.7) (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). No outlier samples were found by leverage comparison and the correct number of fluorescence components was determined by plots of the sum of squared residuals in the excitation and emission directions for different models.

# 2.5. Statistical analysis

Hierarchical cluster analysis (HCA) was performed to reveal the intrinsic correlation or similarity between sampling sites of different regions by Origin 2021b. Redundancy analysis (RDA) was applied to investigate the effects of environmental variables on fluorescence parameters by CANOCO 5. Structural equation model (SEM), an a priori approach, was widely applied to study and predict interactive relationships among observed and latent variables and causality between latent variables (Xie et al., 2019). The ratio of Chi-square and degrees of freedom and significance coefficient were used for confirming the applicability and overall goodness of fit for SEM, i.e., Chi-square/df is less than 5 and $P$ value is less than 0.05 (Hooper et al., 2008). The SEM was conducted in IBM SPSS AMOS 25.

# 3. Results and discussions

# 3.1. Fluorescence spectroscopy characteristics of rural BOWs

Eight representative pollution sources types were obtained in the rural BOWs with nine fluorescence peaks or shoulders by visual inspection (Fig. 2): Peak T1 (tryptophan-like, $\mathrm { e x } / \mathrm { e m } = 2 7 5 / 3 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ); Peak T2 (tryptophan-like, $\mathrm { e x / e m } = 2 2 5 \mathrm { - } 2 3 7 / 3 4 0 \mathrm { - } 3 8 1 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ); Peak B1 (tyrosinelike, $\mathrm { e x } / \mathrm { e m } \ = \ 2 7 5 / 3 1 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ); Peak B2 (tyrosine-like, $\mathrm { e x } / \mathrm { e m } \ =$ 225–237/309–321 nm); Peak M (microbial byproduct, $\mathrm { e x } / \mathrm { e m } =$ $2 9 0 - 3 1 0 / 3 7 0 { - } 4 1 0 \ \mathrm { \ n m } )$ ; Peak A (fulvic-like, $\mathrm { e x / e m } \ = \ 2 3 7 – 2 6 0 /$ $4 0 0 { - } 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ); Peak C (fulvic-like, $\mathrm { e x / e m } = 3 0 0 - 3 7 0 / 4 0 0 { - } 5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ); Peak H (humic-like, $\mathrm { e x / e m } = 2 6 0 - 3 0 0 / 4 7 5 \mathrm { - } 5 1 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ); and Peak F (humic-like, $\mathrm { e x / e m } = 3 7 0 - 3 9 0 / 4 8 0 - 5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m } )$ (Guo et al., 2018; Han et al., 2020; Henderson et al., 2009). Waterbodies affecting by different sources of pollution were measured and plotted as EEMs that exhibited various advantageous peaks. Peak T1 and T2 were the predominant peaks in phytoplankton release and litter leachate type of BOWs (Fig. 2a). Waterbody containing poultry and livestock wastewater was dominated by peak T1 (Fig. 2b). Overflow from swine and cattle lagoon, a structure used to store and compost manure, flew into the water that increased content of peak A and T1 (Fig. 2c). In the waterbody affecting by non-point pollution (agriculture effluent), Peak M, A, and T1 was the predominant fluorophore (Fig. 2d). Peak B1 dominated BOWs which are mainly affected by leaves decomposition (Fig. 2e). Peak T2 and T1 dominated waterbody that mainly polluted by sediment release (Fig. 2f). “Untreated sewage effluent” was raw sewage in the municipal pipes, which inflowed into water due to overloading of wastewater treatment plants that increased fluorescence intensity of peak T2, T1 and B2 (Fig. 2g). Waterbody that polluted by fishery wastewater was dominated by peak T1, T2 and A (Fig. 2h).

Classification and implications of optical indexes (FI, BIX and HIX) are shown in Table S2. The FI values of rural BOWs ranged from 1.61 to 2.45 and average value of those was 1.92 in eastern China. This indicated that biological source of DOM was more significant than that of terrestrial source in rural BOWs (Table S2). The FI mean value of FS $( 2 . 0 3 \pm 0 . 1 2 ) $ was significantly higher than that of SY $( 1 . 9 3 \pm 0 . 1 6 )$ and QD $\left( 1 . 8 1 \pm 0 . 1 4 \right)$ , which suggested that microbially derived components were the dominant components in DOM pool from FS, while the most samples of QD dominated by DOM from blended sources (Table S2). The waterbodies in SY were dominated by both microbial and mixed sources. Values of FI had been found that were negatively correlated with the aromaticity of DOM (Birdwell and Engel, 2010), which indicated that, compared to the samples of FS, those in SY and QD exhibited stronger aromaticity that might derived from plant residues or soil fulvic-like acids (McKnight et al., 2001).

The BIX values increase with the increasing of contributions from recently produced DOM of biological activity (Table S2). The BIX values of rural BOWs ranged from 0.65 to 1.37 and average value was 0.97 in eastern China. The range of BIX in FS from 0.93 to 1.17 (average value: $1 . 0 3 \pm$ 0.06) presented the minimum amplitude of variation, while that of SY and QD varied from 0.67 to 1.15 (average value: $0 . 9 4 \pm 0 . 1 2 $ and 0.65 to 1.37 (average value: $0 . 9 2 \pm 0 . 1 2 $ . This indicated that the highest proportion of components derived from microbial or aquatic bacterial metabolism in DOM fractions was in the FS, followed by SY and QD.

The HIX values of rural BOWs ranged from 0.22 to 6.14 and average value was 1.78 in eastern China. The mean value and standard deviation of HIX in SY ( $1 . 9 6 \pm 1 . 3 8 )$ were comparable with that in QD $[ 1 . 9 7 \pm$ 1.28), and the FS $( 1 . 4 7 \pm 0 . 3 3 )$ ) exhibited the lowest HIX mean value. This indicated that humification degrees of DOM in BOWs from SY and QD were higher than that in FS (Fig. 3b). Additionally, four samples of SY and three samples of QD exhibited strong humification levels (Fig. 3b, Table S2). Furthermore, most samples of SY and QD exhibited relatively high humification levels of DOM, while DOM in most samples of FS derived from biological or aquatic bacteria. It could attribute to high temperatures and strong light in low latitude region, which might accelerate proliferation of algae and microorganisms that released more endogenous organic matter into water (Zhang et al., 2020a).

# 3.2. Composition of FDOM in rural BOWs

From the plot of the sum of squared residuals, it seems clear that the step from the 7 to 8 component model offers little improvement of fit, suggesting that 7 components are more adequate for this data (Fig. S1). To provide further context to the results, the excitation and emission maxima of the seven components were compared with those identified earlier in published works of literature using the OpenFluor database (www.openfluor.org), which contains samples of river/stream, lake, seawater, estuary, wastewater, drinking water and other various environmental samples (Murphy et al., 2014). The Tucker convergence coefficient value of matched components exceeded 0.95, and the detailed information about components of this study and their probable origin described in previous studies are provided in Table S3.

The 7 components were extracted from 81 BOWs samples by PARAFAC model, including four protein-like substances (component 1, component 2, component 3, and component 4) and three humic-like substances (component 5, component 6, and component 7) (Fig. 4). Component 1 (C1), with the peak excitation wavelength at $2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and peak emission wavelength at $3 6 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , and Component 2 (C2), with ex/em maxima at 230/340 nm, were assigned to tryptophan-like and traditional peak T (Henderson et al., 2009). Component 3 (C3) had ex/em maxima at $2 7 5 / 3 2 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ might be relative to tyrosine-like (B1) or tryptophan-like (T) fluorescence substance (Rodriguez-Vidal et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020a). Component 4 (C4) had ex/em maxima at 225/305 nm, which was assigned to tyrosine-like and traditional peak B2 (Chen et al., 2017; Henderson et al., 2009). In the polluted water, the peak B and T generally originated from microbial activity (Hudson et al., 2007; Hudson et al., 2008). And peak T has been found that was related to anthropogenic input, while peak B could be regarded as recent biological activity and algal production (Baker and Spencer, 2004; Catalan et al., 2021; Wauthy et al., 2018). Component 5 (C5) appears the peak excitation wavelength at $3 1 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and emission wavelength at $4 1 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , suggesting an association with biological activity and traditional peak M (Lambert et al., 2017; Osburn et al., 2016). Component 6 (C6) had two peak wavelengths at $\mathrm { e x } / \mathrm { e m } 2 5 0 / 4 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and 320/410 nm were formerly defined as traditional fulvic-like peaks A and C (Henderson et al., 2009; Vines and Terry, 2020). Component 7 (C7), had two excitation maxima (at 270 and $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ), corresponding to a single emission maximum (at $4 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ), were assigned to peak H, which is similar to a terrestrial humic-like substance from lignin and other terrestrial plant– derived precursor material (Lutz et al., 2012). Noticeably, based on the OpenFluor Database, C4 has not been reported previously (Table S3).

# 3.3. Sources and variations of DOM in different latitude rural BOWs

The optical indexes (FI, BIX, and HIX) and fluorescence components (C1 to C7) were used to HCA for exploring the sources and spatial similarity or dissimilarity of DOM in different latitude rural BOWs. Ward clustering method with Euclidean distance were selected to generate a dendrogram of samples, and the Ward method with Pearson correlation coefficients were applied to explore relationship between the ten indicators. Before performing the HCA, the dataset was first standardized to avoid errors caused by numerical ranges and units of the original variables (Kannel et al., 2007).

The ten indicators were organized into three clusters: cluster I with indicators of BIX, FI, C2, and C4; cluster II with HIX and C6; and cluster III with C1, C3, C5, and C7. Cluster I was relative to autochthonous origin that DOM derived from biological activity and sediment release, and refuse degradation could also be a possible origin of cluster I (Table S3). Cluster II represented DOM of allochthonous sources, which attributed to manure composting and surface runoff. Cluster III was characterized by mixed sources, including DOM derived from microbial origin, autochthonous origin, and terrestrial origin.

As we expect, the 81 sampling sites were distinctly divided into three clusters according to their variations of climate and pollution sources, when the distance was 9 (Fig. 6). The 44 samples of BOWs containing all samples of FS and 13 samples from QD and SY were clustered into the green cluster with higher value of indicators of cluster 1, which indicated that the DOM of those BOWs had strong autogenic characteristics (Fig. 6). The other 37 samples of SY and QD were assembled in the blue and red clusters (Fig. 6). Blue cluster with indicators of cluster 3 exhibiting higher value represented BOWs that dominated by DOM from blended sources. Red cluster had higher values of indicators of cluster 2, which indicated that BOWs in that cluster presented blended sources characteristics with higher degree of humification.

According to these results and field surveys, the BOWs of FS were mainly polluted by fresh DOM from autogenous origin, which derived from actions of phytoplankton and microorganisms and metabolic products of organisms in sediment, attributing higher temperature in subtropical monsoon climate that activated microbial activity. Most BOWs of SY and QD were dominated by DOM from both autogenous and allochthonous origin with higher degree of humification, which was attributed to variations of agriculture industrial structure and climate. In rural areas of SY and QD, villagers mainly rely on swine and cattle farming and corn cultivation for their livelihood. The lagoons of swine and cattle were built near waterbodies and large amounts of cornstalk stacked on the banks that contributed to input of allochthonous humic-like material from manure composting and plants. However, fishery and domestic wastewater were main pollution in FS.

Furthermore, seasonal differences might also be a factor in DOM composition changes. The samples of BOWs in SY were collected in autumn, while the sampling campaigns were conducted in spring in QD and FS. In the heavy polluted waterbodies, biomass of phytoplankton is higher in spring and summer with low oxygen production (Duan et al., 2014; Lei and Wei, 2010). As the primary productivity increased, the content of organic matter in the waterbodies exhibited a higher level, which caused occurrence of BOWs. Therefore, samples that obtained from the sampling campaigns in spring were likely to have stronger endogenous levels. Despite the fact that samples in QD were also sampled in spring, compared with BOWs in FS, BOWs in QD were higher in latitude with lower temperature and precipitation, inducing that the waterbodies were smaller in size and worse in hydrodynamic conditions, which were even more conductive to humification of organic matter.

# 3.4. Interactions between fluorescence parameters of DOM and environmental variables

RDA was conducted to explain the comprehensive correlations and impacts between response variables (PARAFAC components and optical indexes) and environmental variables (water quality indexes and geographical coordinates). In the plots of RDA, the length of arrow indicates how important is the factor, and the angle between the arrow and arrow or the coordinate axis implicates correlation of them (the smaller the angle, the stronger the relationship). The AX1 of water quality indexes was mainly affected by SD $p = 0 . 6 6 6 )$ and $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ -N ${ \boldsymbol { p } } =$ 0.002), and the SD was negatively correlated with the $\sf N H _ { 3 } { - } \Nu$ (Fig. 7a). The C1, C3, C5, and C7 exhibited significant positive correlations with AX1, especially C5 (Fig. 7a). This suggested that C5 was more indicative of the changes of $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ -N. The significant negative correlations were acquired between C2, C6, HIX, BIX, and FI and AX1, especially FI and BIX, which represented that the BOWs dominating by autogenous origin exhibited higher SD values. Particularly, the C4 was negatively correlated with AX2, which was dominated by DO ${ \boldsymbol { p } } =$ 0.008) (Fig. 7a).

On analyzing the relationship between the response variables and geographical coordinates of BOWs, the C1, C3, C5, C7, and HIX were positively correlated with geographical locations (longitude, $p = 0 . 0 0 4$ ; latitude, $\mathsf { p } = 0 . 0 0 2$ ) of the BOWs, especially HIX, C5 and C7 (Fig. 7b). This suggested that latitude and longitude increased with a decline in degree of humification, which could be attributed to the variations of climate among south and north China that led to changes in DOM fractions (Chai et al., 2019; Kellerman et al., 2014). The significant negative responses were accessed between optical indexes (FI and BIX) and geographical locations of BOWs, which indicated that the BOWs in low latitude and longitude regions exhibited strong autogenic features (Fig. 7b). High temperature in low latitude regions will stimulate metabolism of microbes that would produce more fresh DOM, i.e., protein-like and microbial humic-like components. However, microorganism prefer to store DOM and decompose proteins to maintain its basic function in low temperature condition (Zhu et al., 2020). Then, humic-like substances as products of decomposition could release into waterbodies. Thus, humification degree of DOM increased with a rise in latitude, while the autogenous origin contribution to DOM decreased.

# 3.5. Influence of fluorescence parameters on the forming mechanism of rural BOWs

The variables and parameters of the structural equation model (SEM) were set according to the CCA results and the previous studies by Bagozzi and Yi (1988) and Hooper et al. (2008). The goodness of fit for model with modest sample sizes (50–100) was verified by Chisquare/df, i.e., the fit of model with the Chi-square/df range from 2 to 5 is acceptable (Hooper et al., 2008; Iacobucci, 2010). The model that we created had a Chi-square/df value of 4.944, which indicated that the results of SEM were credible. The 11 observed variables (C1, C3, C4, C5, C6, HIX, BIX, FI, SD, DO, and $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ -N) were selected to conduct the SEM, and these observed variables were divided into 4 latent variables (Humus, Protein, Source, and BOW), according to their potential relevance (Fig. 8). Particularly, component of microbial origin (C5) was set as an observed variable of “BOW” due to its most significant correlation with the indexes which could be used to classification the BOWs (Fig. 6a). Furthermore, according to the results of CCA and the previous studies found that microbial activity has been associated with protein-like peak and humic-like peak (Henderson et al., 2009; Stedmon and Markager, 2005). Thus, the C5 was set as an observed variable both for the “Humus” latent variable and the “Protein” latent variable. Each latent variable corresponds to a measurement model, i.e., the “Humus” with three observed variables (HIX, C5, and C6), the “Source” with two observed variables (FI and BIX), the “Protein” with four observed variables (C1, C3, C4, and C5), and the “BOW” with four observed variables (DO, SD, $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ -N, and C5) (Fig. 8).

Structural equation model was applied to depict the direct and indirect influences of pollution sources (Source), humus substances (Humus), and protein substances (Protein) on rural black-odor waterbodies (BOW), thus revealing the influence of DOM on the forming mechanism of rural BOWs. The C5 presented the highest factor loading, even more than the factor loadings of indicators (DO,

This indicated that the C5 presented the most significant direct correlation with the latent variable of “BOW”, which was attributed to the diverse microorganisms existing in the BOWs (Li et al., 2012; Wu et al., 2019). With the condition of high organic compound concentrations and low dissolved oxygen content, the bacteria become more active in sediment metabolism (Cao et al., 2020), which caused release of bacterial metabolizing organic matter from sediment into water. C5 have been also demonstrated that is similar to phytoplankton degradation release component due to microbial activity (Zhu et al., 2020). The algal metabolites have been demonstrated to be the primary cause of odor in waterbodies (Watson, 2003). And biodegradation becomes more important than photodegradation in heavily polluted waters (Knapik et al., 2019). Therefore, this indicated that microbial metabolism is extremely vigorous in formation and evolution of rural BOWs.

Among the three direct regression paths to the “BOW”, the highest coefficient of regression path was between the “Source” and “BOW” $\left( - 0 . 6 9 \right)$ , followed by the “Protein” (0.32) and the “Humus” (0.19). This indicated that sources of DOM and protein-like substances deeply affected the BOWs. Moreover, the increasing order of factor loadings of the “Source” measurement model was FI $( 0 . 7 7 ) < \mathrm { B I X }$ (0.66). Thus, FI and BIX had significantly indirectly negatively effects on BOWs mediated by the latent variable of Source, especially FI. This could be attributed to that excessive DOM from allochthonous origins loaded into waterbodies was transferred into autochthonous DOM by microbial metabolism, which indirectly indicated that autochthonous origins were the dominate factor of the rural BOWs. The factor loadings of “Protein” measurement model increased in the order of C4 $( 0 . 9 ) < \mathbb { C } 3$ $( 0 . 5 5 ) < \mathsf C 1$ $( 0 . 4 5 ) < C 5$ (0.04), which indicated that C4 exhibited the most significantly indirectly positive effects on BOWs mediated by the latent variable of the “Protein”. And the highest factor loading of the “Humus” measurement model was obtained in C6 (0.78), followed by HIX (0.6) and C5 (0.12).

In a word, the formation of rural BOWs was mainly influenced by microbial activities (C5) and variations of pollution sources (the latent variable of “Source”). Therefore, we proposed to use C5 and FI, an indicator with the highest measurement weights of the latent variable of “Source”, as the indicators for identifying and recognizing the BOWs. In addition, C4 could also be regarded as a potential factor of the BOWs, which is the indicator with the highest measurement weight of the “Protein” and the only component that is negatively correlated with DO (Figs. 7a and 8).

# 4. Conclusion

Variations of DOM fractions and potential factors of rural BOWs in three of different latitudinal rural regions were explored. In the rural BOWs, the biological source of DOM was more significant than that of the terrestrial source, especially in the low latitude regions. However, the rural BOWs of middle and high latitude regions were dominated by DOM from mixed sources with relatively high humification degree. Seven fluorescence components of DOM were obtained in rural BOWs: C1 and C2 known as tryptophan-like substances, C3 and C4 associated with tryptophan-like, C5 related with biological byproducts, C6 relative to fulvic-like, and C7 referred as humic-like. The protein-like substances dominated the rural BOWs, which exhibited obvious advantage in proportion of DOM fractions. And the roughly decreasing order of percentages in DOM fractions from the rural BOWs was tyrosinelike $>$ tryptophan-like $>$ fulvic-like $>$ microbial byproduct $>$ humiclike in three regions. The BOWs in low latitude regions were mainly polluted by fresh DOM from actions of phytoplankton and microorganisms and release of sediment, and the BOWs in middle and high latitude regions were dominated by mixed sources with higher humification degree, which contained stable DOM from plants degradation and manure composting. Autogenous origin contribution to DOM decreased with a rise in latitude, while humification degree of DOM increased. The formation and evolution of rural BOWs were mainly influenced by microbial activities (C5) and variations of pollution sources (the latent variable of Source). Therefore, we proposed to use C5 and FI, indicators with the highest measurement weights of their latent variables, as the potential factors of rural BOWs.
