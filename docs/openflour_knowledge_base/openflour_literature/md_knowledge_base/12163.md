# ABSTRACT

Characterization of the biochar-derived dissolved organic matter (BDOM) is essntial to understanding the environmental efficacy of biochar and the behavior of heavy metals.In this study,the binding properties of BDOM derived from diferent pyrolysis temperatures,wetland plants,and plant organs with Cu was investigated based on a multi-analytical approach.In general, the pyrolysis temperature exhibited a more significant impact on both the spectral characteristics of BDOM and Cu binding behavior than those of the fedstocks.With the pyrolysis temperature increased,the dissolved organiccarbon,aromaticity,and fluorescence substance of BDOM decreased and the structure became more condensed. Humic-and tryptophan-like substance was more susceptible to the addition of Cu for BDOM pyrolyzed at $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ,respectively.In addition, the more tyrosinelike substance is involved in Cu binding at higher pyrolysis temperature $( 5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ .However,the fluvic-like substance occurred preferentially with Cu than the other fluorophores.Moreover,the higher binding capacity for Cu was exhibited by the humic-like substanceand by BDOM derived from the higher pyrolysis temperature

# 1．Introduction

Biochar is a carbon-based and porous material with a large surface area,high cation exchange capacity,long-term stability,and ample functional surface groups,which is derived from the thermochemical conversion of a wide range of biomass,such as agricultural, forestry, animal residues, food waste, and sludge (Shaheen et al.,2O19; El-Naggar et al., 2019a; Wang et al., 202la). In addition, due to its low cost and eco-friendly character,biochar has been widely used in soil amendment (El-Naggar et al., 2019b; Xiong et al., 2017). During the application of biochar in the environment,the biochar-derived dissolved organic matter (BDOM) is inevitably released,which is the most active and mobile component among all the dissolved fractions including dissolved salts and minerals (Joseph et al., 2010).

BDOM contains abundant reactive functional groups (e.g., carboxyl, hydroxyl,carboxylic,and phenolic) which can affect the effect of biochar in soil remediation and change the stabilization of soil (Quan et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021). BDOM is also known to promote microbial activities and population (Quan et al., 2O2O).Moreover, previous studies have reported that BDOM had strong interactions with heavy metals, nitrogen,and organic pollutants,which greatly affects the transport, bioavailability,and fate of these contaminants (Huang et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2021). However, BDOM provides extra bioavailable organic matter not only for the plant yield and quality but also for the natural water bodies which may lead to eutrophication (Zhang et al., 2020; Mukherjee and Zimmerman, 2013). Besides, BDOM also showed toxicity to some alga (e.g.blue-green alga and eukaryotic green alga) and inhibited the seed germination of rice,corn,and wheat (Smith et al.,2013;Liao et al.,2014).Hence,the characteristics of BDOM are significant to the application of biochar and the ecosystems.

With regards to the properties of BDOM,both hydrophilic- and hydrophobic fractions were found in BDOM,and both aliphatic and aromatic components were found in the hydrophilic and hydrophobic BDOM (Li et al., 2017, 2018).Another significant feature of BDOM is that of abundant functional groups and different molecular weights, which could further control the migration, transformation,and fate of heavy metals through hydrogen bonding,cation bond bridges,ligand exchange,and complexation (Xu et al., 2018; Borggaard et al., 2019). As the dominant binding way,BDOM could bind with heavy metals and form soluble complexes through complexation,thus changing their sorption behavior of them onto the other environmental sorbents (Borggaard etal., 2019; Huang et al., 202O). Although some studies have paid atention to this area, only a few are concerned with the binding mechanism of the fluorescent BDOM with heavy metals.For instance, Wu et al., (2022) found that sludge BDOM-Cu binding occurred preferentially in the fulvic-like substances and the hydrophilic groups in BDOM had a stronger binding affinity towards Cu (Wu et al., 2022). Song et al.,(2021) verified that the sequential binding of Cu by BDOM generally followed the order: lipid/aliphatic／peptide-like compounds tannin-like compounds condensed aromatic compounds (Song et al.,2O21). In particular, there is stillimited knowledge on the pyrolysis temperature taken to exert the binding behaviors of BDOM with heavy metals. Huang et al.,(2021) revealed that the binding sequence of BDOM with heavy metals under pyrolysis temperature interference was highly variable and bidentate carboxylic-Cu complexes appear to be the predominant binding pattern for Cu to BDOM (Huang et al., 2021). Guo et al.,(2022) demonstrated that BDOM produced at $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ pyrolysis temperature had more fluorescent substance combined with Cu than those at $3 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $7 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Guo et al., 2022).In addition, the study comprehensively dealt with the effect of the pyrolysis temperature and feedstock on characterizing both BDOM and the BDOM-Cu binding

system is much scarcer so far.

In fact, the characteristics of BDOM were primarily dependent on the feedstock and the pyrolytic temperature (Sun et al., 2O21). It had been reported that the humic-like materials in BDOMare mainly impacted by temperature,while the protein-like substances are influenced by the feedstocks (Rajapaksha et al.,2O19). However,due to the profound complexity,heterogeneity,and polydispersity of BDOMas well as the different feedstocks,the molecular-level characterization of BDOM needs further investigation (Huang et al., 2Ol9). Many previous studies focused on BDOM were fed from agricultural crops,animal manure,and sludge (Li etal., 2017;Wei et al., 2019), while there is a paucity of information on wetland plants.As an effective approach for the remediation and purification of wastewater, the constructed wetland is widely used due to its low cost and environmental friendliness (Parde et al., 2020).Recently, biochar derived from constructed wetland biomass used in soil and water remediation received more attention because this is an efficient way to manage a large amount of plant biomass (Parde et al.,202O).Accordingly,the relevant studies about BDOM derived from constructed wetlands also got attention (Wu et al., 2018, 2019). However,as the important source of constructed wetland plants,the information of BDOM derived from natural wetland plants is much scarcer so far.

Nowadays,BDOM properties such as structures，compositions, fluorescence, functional groups,aromaticity,and humification degree could be comprehensively characterized by multiple techniques and methods.Therein,spectroscopic techniques including UV-visible absorption (UV-vis) (He et al.,2O2l),synchronous fluorescence spectra combined with two-dimensional correlation spectroscopy (2D-SFS-COS) (Huang et al., 2O22), fluorescent excitation-emission matrix,and parallel factor analysis (EEM-PARAFAC) (Yang et al.,2O19) have been widely used for BDOM characterization and obtaining their influence by heavy metal stimulation owing to their non-invasive,rapid,and in situ measurement,which has been proven capable of tracing the changes of BDOM structures and compositions.In addition, some chemometric algorithm such as self-organizing map (SOM) has been successfully combined with PARAFAC for visualization of the fluorescence composition of fluorescence DOM (FDOM) because it needs fewer input variables, shorter running time,higher reliability,and nondestructive results (Yan et al., 2018).

Therefore,multiple types of natural wetland plants from different water levels are selected in this study to produce biochar via different pyrolysis temperatures (i.e.,without pyrolysis, $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ,and 700 $^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ .Copper (Cu),as mainly immobilized in the application of biochar, was selected as the representative heavy metal in this study due to its biogeochemistry severely interacting with organic matter (Huang et al, 2021；Phillips et al.,2O13). The objectives of this study are to (1) investigate the influence of the pyrolysis temperature and feedstock on the spectral properties of BDOM using EEMs-PARAFAC-SOM;(2) explore the effects of the pyrolysis temperatures and feedstocks on the BDOM-Cu binding behavior based on 2D-SFS-COS;and (3) explore the BDOM-Cu binding mechanism including substances,sequence,and capacity.The results of this study could provide the significant data for understanding the fate of biochar after application.

# 2．Materials and methods

# 2.1．Sample collection and treatment

According to the flooding condition of plants,seven types of plants from the Poyang Lake wetland were collected in December 2020, including Carex cinerascens (CC),Carex ischnostachya (CI),Triarrhena

Iutarioriparia (TI),Phragmites australis (PA),Cynodon dactylon (CD), Polygonum orientale (PO) and Zizania latifolia (ZL).A detailed description of the wetland plants was shown in Table S1. Once transported to the laboratory,all the plants were washed with water in order to remove the attached sediment or soil.Then all types of plants were divided into roots and leaves except for PO and ZL whose roots were adhesion to leaves.Notably,the stems and leaveswere collectively referred to as leaves hereinafter because they were difficult to separate and basically withered in this season.Then the plant samples were cut,dried (at 60 ${ } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ ,and sieved to $< 2 \mathrm { m m }$ particles prior to pyrolysis.

# 2.2.BDOM extraction

$0 . 0 5 g$ biochar and $1 2 \mathrm { m L }$ of Milli-Q water were both added into a 15 mL centrifuge tube to extract DOM from biochar.The sealed tube was then placed on a shaker at a speed of $1 5 0 \mathrm { r m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ and shook at ambient temperature in dark.After shaking for $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ ,the resulting solution was centrifuged at $3 6 0 0 \mathrm { r m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ and then filtered using a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter membrane. # 3．Results and discussion

# 3.1. Characterization of biochar and BDOM derived from the wetland plants

# 3.1.1． Elemental composition

Results of the elemental analysis showed that the two main elements (C and O) of biochar and RS accounted for $3 2 \mathrm { - } 7 3 \%$ and $2 { - } 4 4 \%$ respectively;and the proportion of the other elemental contents (H, N, and S) was less than $1 0 \%$ (Fig.S1).Besides,the proportion of ash varied greatly from $4 \%$ to $6 0 \%$ among different samples.As the pyrolysis temperature increased,the proportion of C elementin biochar gradually increased with the average value increasing from $3 8 \%$ to $5 6 \%$ ；similarly, the proportion of ash gradually increased with the average value increasing from $1 4 \%$ to $3 4 \%$ .In contrary, the proportion of O element in biochar gradually decreased with the average value decreasing from $2 1 \%$ to $7 \%$ with the pyrolysis temperature increased.In addition, the proportion of Cand ash in biochar derived from different wetland plants was also compared.Results showed that the proportion of C element both for roots and leaves of the plants grown far away from the lake (e. g., TI and PA) was higher than that of plants grown near the lake (e.g., CC and ZI)；while the proportion of ash showed the opposite trend (Fig.S2).These results were also reported in previous studies (Wei et al., 2019; Conz et al., 2017).

In addition,as the pyrolysis temperature increased,the proportion of H element also gradually decreased and the proportion of N element firstly increased and then decreased. The variation tendency of C, O, H and N proportion in biochar with the pyrolysis temperature increased in this study was in accordance with that in the biochar derived from rice straw,pine wood,pig manure,and sewage sludge reported in the literature (Wei et al., 2O19). The main reason for these changes could be attributed to that with the pyrolysis temperature increased:1） the carbonization ratio of biochar gradually increased,which resulted in the increase of C content (Keiluweit et al., 201O; Wei et al., 2017); 2) the O-H bonds in organic matters including hemicellulose,cellulose,and lignin reduced which gave rise to the decreased of O and H content (Usman et al., 2015); 3) some organic matter containing N element referred to the aromatization and formation of heterocyclic N (such as nitriles-N,pyrrole-N,pyridine-N,amino-N,and ammonium-N) gradually formed and had relatively higher stability when the pyrolysis temperature increased to $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ，but the formed structure of N was gradually decomposed at the temperatures higher than $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (such as alkaloid-N,and free amino acid-N) (Gaskin et al., 2O08; Liuet al., 2018; Lu et al., 2Ol2). It might be the reason for the changing trend of the N element which increased firstly and then decreased.

# 3.1.3.UV-vis parameters

The UV-vis parameters of BDOM/RDOM (i.e., $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ,α355, $s _ { \mathrm { { R } } } )$ at different pyrolysis temperatures are shown in Fig.2. $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ value is usually used as an indicator of the aromaticity of DOM,which is also related to $\scriptstyle { \mathsf { C } } = 0$ bonds and $\scriptstyle \mathrm { C = } 0$ bonds in aromatic rings,as well as the humic-like substances in DOM (Zhang et al.,2014).The higher the $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ value,the higher the content of humic acid in DOM (Yeh et al., 2014)．Theaverage $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ valueofBDOM/RDOMwas $6 . 7 2 \mathrm { L m } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 }$ for the wetland plant from Poyang Lake.As pyrolysis temperature increased,the $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ value gradually decreased,and this result was agreed with the biochar derived from the dewatered sludge and the fresh human manure (He et al.,2O21). It indicated that the aromaticity of BDOM from all plants decreased at the higher pyrolysis temperature.Besides,the $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values of BDOM from both roots and leaves were higher in CC,CD,and PO but lower in PAand ZI.This result suggested that there was no relationship between the $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ value and the plant with different water conditions,as well as the proportion of leaf and root,which demonstrated that wetland plant feedstocks made little effect on the aromaticity of BDOM.

The average CDOM concentration (i.e., $\alpha _ { 3 5 5 }$ value) of BDOM/RDOM was $2 6 . 4 6 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ for the wetland plant from Poyang Lake,which was comparable to those derived from pinecones biochar and the corresponding modified biochar $( 1 2 . 5 0 { - } 3 8 . 3 3 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ） (Wang et al., 2021b). With the similar changing trend of $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ value, the CDOM concentration of BDOM also decreased as the pyrolysis temperature increased. Moreover,the CDOM concentration of BDOM from the different plants was also higher in CC,CD,and PO;and this value in leaves was higher than that in roots.These results indicated that the CDOM concentration of BDOM was lower at higher pyrolysis temperature,especially for the plant with more roots.

The $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values are often used to represent the molecular weight of DOM. The larger the $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ value,the smaller the molecular weight (Helms et al., 2008). The average $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ value of BDOM/RDOM was 1.96 for the wetland plant from Poyang Lake,which was in the range of the $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values for BDOM from bamboo biochar (1.14-2.40) (Fu et al., 2016).As the pyrolysis temperature increased, $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values increased.Although there was no significant difference in $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ value in BDOM among different wetland plants,a higher $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ value of BDOM derived from roots was found.These results suggested that the molecular structure of BDOM was further condensed at higher pyrolysis temperature,which resulted in smaller molecules,especially for the roots of plants.

# 3.1.4.Fluorescence index

Fn28o and Fn355 could represent the concentration of the protein-like and the humic-like substances in DOM, respectively.As shown in Fig. S3, the concentrations of the protein-like and the humic-like substances of BDOM/RDOM at a lower temperature (i.e.，without pyrolysis and $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ were much higher than those of BDOM ata higher temperature (i.e., $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ .In detail, the concentration of the protein-like substance was ordered as: without pyrolysis $> 3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \ \gg \ 5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ $\approx 7 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ while the concentration of the humic-like substance decreased as the following order: $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } >$ without pyrolysis $\gg 5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } > 7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ Additionally, the concentration of the protein-like substances was always higher than that of humic-like substances except for the pyrolysis temperature of $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ，These results indicated that the fluorescence substance was high in RDOM,and as the pyrolysis temperature increased,the protein-like substance gradually decreased and the humic-like substance sharply increased; but when the pyrolysis temperature exceeded $5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ，the fluorescent substances significantly decreased and the protein-like substance became the dominant fluorophore in BDOM.This finding was in accordance with the results detected by the synchronous fluorescence spectroscopy in the previous study (He et al., 2O2l).The effect of different water conditions on the concentration of the fluorescent substance in BDOM from the wetland plants was inconspicuous,but a significant difference in the fluorescent substance in BDOM was displayed between roots and leaves that a higher concentration was found for the BDOM from leaves.These results revealed that compared with roots,biochar derived from the leaves could dissolve more fluorescent substances compared to the roots for most of the plants,which was of greater significance to the fate of pollutants.

The FI,HIX,and BIX values of BDOM/RDOM were 1.00-2.90, 0.16-2.81,and O.27-1.72 for the wetland plant from Poyang Lake, respectively (Fig.S4).Moreover, the values of the three fluorescence indices of RDOM were all the lowest (Fig.S5),which demonstrated that these DOM contributed more to the terrestrial and non-autochthonous source and contributed smaller to the humification degree of the DOM in the aquatic environment.As the pyrolysis temperature increased to $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ，the terrestrial and autochthonous characteristics gradually expressed and then turned to the opposite character when the pyrolysis temperature continued to increase.The humification degree of BDOM also showed a similar changing trend with FIand BIX values,which was consistent with the above result that the highest concentration of the humic-like substance was observed at $3 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ .Furthermore,the difference in the FI,HIX,and BIX values was not significant between $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Fig.S5). These results suggested that BDOM produced under low pyrolysis temperature contributed more terrestrial,autochthonous,and a higher degree of humification DOM to the environment compared to those produced at the higher pyrolysis temperature,which was of great significance for understanding the effectof biochar application.It has been reported that BDOMcould be used to trace the effect of biochar migration and the downward co-migration of the heavy metals in soil and the fluorescence indices were a useful supplement to traditional characterization methods for structural and chemical characteristics of the DOM in soil (Jiang et al.,2022). In addition, there was no significant difference between these three fluorescence indices among different wetland plants and fractions of plants (Fig.S4),indicating that the source and humification degree of BDOM were less affected by the feedstocks of plants.

# 3.2.Binding mechanism of BDOM and Cu

# 3.2.1.Binding characteristics of BDOM with Cu by SFS

To investigate the sensitivity of the impact of Cu on the conformation of BDOM, SFS was introduced in this study (Bi et al.,2Ol6).As the concentration of Cu increased， the notably decreased peak at $2 7 5 { - } 2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (tryptophan-like substances), $3 2 0 { - } 3 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (fluvic-like substances),and $3 6 0 { - } 3 7 8 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (humic-like substances) for all DOM was observed,indicating that Cu was complexed with these fluorophores (Fig.5).In detail, the extent of the decreased tryptophan-like substances was greater forRDOM and BDOM pyrolyzed at $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (excluding CC L 5),while a higher reduction of the fluvic-and humic-like substance was observed for BDOM pyrolyzed at $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ 、It might be attributed to the higher concentration of the protein-like component in RDOM and BDOM pyrolyzed at $5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and of the humic-like component in BDOM pyrolyzed at $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ,respectively.Besides,owing to the more fluorophores in BDOM derived from roots,the number of the decreased peaks was more than that in BDOM derived from leaves.This phenomenon was also found between DOM from CC and PA that more decreased peaks were observed for DOM from CC.It might be related to the high biomass in leaves and in CC. (Fig. 6).

When Cu was added to the DOM solution, there are two quenching processes: static and dynamic quenching.The former indicates the formation of a non-fluorescent complex between DOM and Cu,while the latter refers to the collision between DOM and Cu (Yan et al., 2O19; Xu et al.,20l3).In this study,Cu was strongly binding with the fluorescence components in DOMas a static quenching process,which was verified by the increased absorbance of DOM with an increasing concentration of Cu (Fig. S8).

# 3.2.2.Binding sequence of BDOM with Cu by 2D-COS

2D-COS based on SFS was applied to reveal the binding sequence of BDOM with Cu. The synchronous and asynchronous correlation maps for BDOM from CC and PA with Cu titrated were presented in Figs.S9 and S10. Tables S4 and S5 provided the corresponding detailed spectral assignments.In the synchronous map,many positive auto-peaks were observed near 200,210,280,327,340,and $3 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ on the main diagonal,corresponding to the tyrosine-like,tryptophan-like,fluvic-like,and humic-like components (such as $2 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ for CC L 5, $2 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for PA L 5, $2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ for CC R0,327 nm for CC L 3, $3 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for PA R 5,and $3 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for CC R 3),indicated that these fluorophores could be complexing with Cu in varying degrees.In particular,the tryptophan-like substance $( 2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m } )$ in RDOM and BDOM pyrolyzed at $5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ characterized with the highest fluorescence intensity indicated that the tryptophan-like substance was more susceptible to the addition of Cu,while for BDOM pyrolyzed at $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ,the humic-like substance was more sensitive when adding Cu.Moreover,some positive cross-peaks were displayed at 280/ 340,280/378,280/360,and 280/328 nm along the off-diagonal lines for the synchronous map,suggesting that the tryptophan-like substance and the fluvic-and humic-like substance was reduced proceeded in the same direction with increasing the concentration of Cu.In contrast, some negative cross-peaks were observed at 200/280,200/328,and $2 0 0 / 3 3 9 \mathrm { n m }$ in the synchronous map,revealing that the tyrosine-like substance was decreased and proceeded in the opposite direction to the tryptophan-and the fluvic-like substance with the addition of Cu.

Besides,the binding sequence of these fluorophores was gained by comparing the signs (positive and negative) of the cross-peaks in both synchronous and asynchronous maps (bottom-right) (Table S4 and S5). According to Noda's principle (Noda and Ozaki, 2Oo4), the sequence of the fluorescence components for RDOM participating in binding Cu was ordered as $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m } < 2 8 0 \mathrm { n m } < 3 4 0 \mathrm { n m } ;$ the binding sequence for BDOM pyrolyzed at $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ was: $3 6 0 \ \mathrm { n m } < 2 7 8 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $3 6 0 \mathrm { n m } < 3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 1 0 \mathrm { { n m } < 3 3 2 / 3 6 0 \mathrm { { n m } < 2 0 0 \mathrm { { n m } ; } } }$ ：the preferential binding of Cu withBDOMpyrolyzedat $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ wasorderedasfollows: $2 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m } < 2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m } < 3 3 9 \ \mathrm { n m }$ In general, it could be concluded that the fluvic-like substance reacted fastest with Cu,followed by the tryptophan-,the tyrosine-,and the humic-like substance.However,some exceptions still existed,confirming the relative difference in the complexation of BDOM derived from different pyrolysis temperatures and feedstocks with Cu.Furthermore,the pyrolysis temperature showed a stronger impact on the binding sequence of the fluorophores in BDOM with Cu.As pyrolysis temperature increased,the numbers of the Cu binding substance (such as the tyrosine-like substance) with BDOM increased,which was in agreement with the binding effect of BDOM from wheat straw and corn straw with Cu (Phillips et al., 2013). Compared with pyrolysis temperature,the influence of feedstocks (CC and PA) on the binding sequence between BDOM and Cu was very small. For BDOM derived from leaf and root,a small difference in the binding effect between BDOM and Cu was observed in the corresponding synchronous map, but a great difference was found in the corresponding asynchronous map.It demonstrated that the fluorophores that participated in the complexation were similar in BDOM from leaf and root, but their binding sequence was not,which might be attributed to the different kinds and proportion of fluorescence components in BDOM from leaf and root.

Specifically, the quenching percentage of C1-C3 ranged from $4 7 \%$ to $8 4 \%$ $5 8 – 9 5 \%$ ,and $4 6 { - } 9 4 \%$ when the concentration of Cu reached $1 0 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ ,and the counterpart of RDOM and BDOM pyrolyzed at $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ was in the range of $4 6 { - } 9 4 \%$ $5 2 \mathrm { - } 8 7 \%$ and $4 6 { - } 9 4 \%$ .However,Fig.S13 showed that no significant difference in the quenching percentage was observed among different fluorescence components and different DOM.

Therefore,in order to visually displayed the diferent binding affinity of Cu by the fluorescence components,Fig.5 A-C plotted the fluorescence intensity of the components with Cu at O and $1 0 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ ,and Fig. 5 D pairwise compared the fluorescence intensity of the components with the addition of Cu at all the concentrations.It showed that except for the fluorescence scores of C2 and C3 in BDOM pyrolyzed $5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ,the others were close to the X-axis revealing that the complexation between C1, C2, or C3 and Cu occurred.Additionally,the fluorescence scores of C1 vs C2 and Cl vs C3 were both closed to the x-axis of C1,while the counterpart of C2 vs C3 was slightly below the diagonal. This result illustrated that the binding ability between C3 and Cu was slightly higher than that of C2 and further both stronger than that of C1,i.e.,the humic-like substance showed a stronger binding affinity with Cu than that of the protein-like substance.

Furthermore,the Ryan-Weber model was introduced to quantify the binding affinity of Cu onto the fluorescence components and Table 1 showed the binding parameters.The fitting results indicated that both protein-and humic-like substances had a vital role in the binding Cu to BDOM.However,the stability constant $( \mathrm { l o g } K _ { \mathrm { M } } )$ for the protein-like substances (average $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { \mathrm { M } }$ for Cl was 4.44) was generally lower than that of the humic-like substances (average $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { \mathrm { M } }$ for C3 was 5.58),but higher than that of the short wavelength of humic-like substances (average $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { \mathrm { M } }$ for C2 was 4.08).This range is similar to those in the previous studies for Cu and other DOMs (such as straw DOM,BDOM, and DOM in the sewer) (Huang et al., 2021, 202O; Liao et al., 2021). Moreover, the $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { \mathrm { M } }$ of the fluorescence components in the BDOM-Cu system increased by approximately 0.5 from RDOM (average:4.47) to BDOM pyrolyzed at $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (average:4.98) and $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (average:5.36). This phenomenon was also found for BDOM derived from the wheat straw and corn straw that the counterpart increased nearly one order of magnitude with the pyrolysis temperature ( $1 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ steps) (Huang et al., 2021).This might be ascribed to the loss of aromaticity materials and oxygen-containing functional groups from BDOM when the pyrolysis temperature increased.Besides,we found that the $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { \mathrm { M } }$ values for BDOM of CC (5.90) were generally higher than those of PA(5.01), this could be attributed to the higher DOC and CDOM concentration,humic acid,and fluorescence intensity in BDOM of CC,as the results obtained by UV and fluorescence analysis.

# 4.Conclusions

This study systematically promoted of the understanding the characteristics of BDOM derived from different pyrolysis temperature, wetland plants,and plant organs and their influence on the BDOM-Cu binding behaviors through multiple spectral technologiesand methods.Results of elements showed that the pyrolysis temperature and feedstocks of different wetland plants both impacted the proportion of C, O,and ash in biochar.DOC and CDOM concentration,aromaticity,and molecular weight of BDOM gradually decreased as the pyrolysis temperature increased,and the values of these features were generally higher for the plant from the lower elevation (e.g.,CC) and from leaves. Moreover,the 3DEEM-PARAFAC-SOM analysis revealed that BDOM derived from the lower pyrolysis temperature and the leaves characterized by higheraromaticity and fluorescence intensity and the proteinlike substances were the dominant fluorophores except for $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ Further exploring the effect of pyrolysis temperature and feedstocks on BDOM-Cu binding mechanism discovered that both protein-and humiclike substances could be complexed with Cu generally occurring from the fulvic-like substance to the tryptophan-,the tyrosine-,and the humic-like substance.In addition, the more tyrosine-like substance was binding with Cu ata higher temperature $( 5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ .Although the fluorophores in BDOM that participated in the complexation were similar between leaf and root,the binding sequence was not.Besides,the higher binding capacity for Cu was exhibited by the humic-like substance and by BDOM derived from the higher pyrolysis temperature and the lower elevation plants.
