# ABSTRACT

Dam construction and fish culture can change the biogeochemical processes in river, yet their impact on the spectral properties of particulate organic matter (POM) remains to be studied. This was investigated in a reservoir-type river (Minjiang river, SE China) using absorption spectroscopy and fluorescence excitation-emission matrices-parallel factor analysis (EEMs-PARAFAC). Five fluorescent components were identified from POM with PARAFAC. Four components C1eC4 were affected by the seasonal variations of rainfall and runoff, indicating the influences of hydrological condition and terrestrial inputs. The Chlorophyll $a$ concentration (Chl a) correlated significantly with the humic-like C3 $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ and the protein-like C4 $\left( p < 0 . 0 1 \right)$ , indicating phytoplankton was an important source of C3 and C4. The Chl a and fluorescence intensities of ${ \sf C 3 - C 4 }$ were higher in the fish culture zones than in other areas, and the absorption coefficient $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ and ${ \sf C 1 - C 4 }$ were lower downstream the dam. These results indicated that fish farming in the reservoir probably promoted the production of POM. The $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ and C1 per unit mass of suspended particulate matter $( a _ { 3 0 0 } / \mathrm { T S M }$ and $\mathsf { C 1 } / \mathrm { T S M } )$ correlated significantly with the median particle size $\left( p < 0 . 0 1 \right)$ , which might be related to the contribution of micro-phytoplankton. The absorption spectra of POM showed a shoulder peak at $\sim 2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and its intensity correlated significantly and positively with Chl a $\left( p < 0 . 0 1 \right)$ . These results indicated that the peak was probably derived from phytoplankton production. Our results have implications for better understanding the influences of human activities on the dynamics of river POM.

# 1. Introduction

Particulate organic matter (POM) in river represents an important source of material and energy for aquatic biomes, which is derived from both allochthonous (e.g., terrestrial plants and soil) and autochthonous sources (e.g., plankton and fecal debris; Lu et al., 2014; Lobbes et al., 2000). It plays an important role in the global carbon cycle (Ellis et al., 2012), and is important in determining the water quality and safety (Lee et al., 2019). For example, approximately $_ { 0 . 2 \mathrm { P g } }$ of particulate organic carbon (POC) is discharged to the ocean by global rivers every year, accounting for $4 3 \%$ of the total fluvial organic carbon flux (Blair and Aller, 2012). POM can complex with pollutants such as heavy metals in aquatic environments, which has a significant impact on the migration and transformation of pollutants (Liu et al., 2016; Hu et al., 2019). In addition, POM can release dissolved organic matter (DOM), which is affected by sediment re-suspension (Yang et al., 2013; He et al., 2016). The released DOM from POM can react with oxidants to form harmful disinfection byproducts in water treatment plants (Lee et al., 2019). Therefore, it is important to study POM in the river basin for better understanding the biogeochemistry, ecosystem function and water quality in aquatic environments.

Absorption spectroscopy and fluorescence excitation-emission matrices-parallel factor analysis (EEMs-PARAFAC) are widely used in studying the source, chemical composition and transformation of DOM in aquatic systems (Stedmon et al., 2003; Fellman et al., 2010; Yang et al., 2015a, 2015b). In contrast, there are few studies applying these techniques for characterizing aquatic POM. Osburn et al. (2012) first studied the quality of base-extractable components of POM (BEPOM) in the Neuse River Estuary using EEMPARAFAC. Yang et al. (2013) characterized the spectral properties of water-soluble POM in the Jiulong Estuary and the adsorptiondesorption process between POM and DOM. The average extraction efficiency of POC using NaOH is $3 8 \%$ , which shows no evident relationship with different station locations and seasons (Brym et al., 2014). It is demonstrated that the extracted organic compounds are representative of the characteristics and source of bulk POM (Chen and Hur, 2015). These techniques have also been successfully applied for investigating the dynamics of POM in other studies (Brym et al., 2014; Lü et al., 2019). Dam construction and/or fish farming is in general a representative and critical human activity to cause the deterioration of water quality associated with POM (Syvitski et al., 2005; Ballester-Molto et al., 2017  ). However, there is little information on the effects of dam construction and fish farming on the optical properties of river POM.

Minjiang River is the largest river in southeast China, which is $5 7 7 \mathrm { k m }$ long and covers a basin area of $6 0 , 9 9 2 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ (Yang et al., 2019). A reservoir of ${ \sim } 1 0 0 \mathrm { k m }$ long has formed in the middle reach of Minjiang River, due to the interception of the Shuikou hydropower station (Zhu et al., 2018). There are a plenty of fish cage culture activities in the reservoir, with \~20,000 cages. The culture species mainly include grass carp, bighead carp and silver carp, and their growth rate may depend on seasonal changes of environment conditions such as water temperature. This has an important impact on the water quality of Minjiang River, due to the contamination from underutilized fish feed (Zhu et al., 1998). The dynamics of POM may be also affected by the dam and fish culture, which have implications for the biogeochemical processes and ecosystem function in both the freshwater and the downstream coastal water. Therefore, this study examined the changes of colored and fluorescent POM (i.e. CPOM and FPOM) when the river flowed through the Shuikou Reservoir and fish culture areas, for revealing the factors influencing the dynamics of POM in river.

# 2. Material and methods

# 2.1. Sample collection and optical measurements

Surface water samples were collected in the middle reach of Minjiang River in April, July and December, 2018 (Fig. 1). Stations A01-A06 were located in the reservoir upstream the Shuikou hydropower station, while stations A07-A09 were downstream the dam. In addition, many fish cages were present at sites A03-A06, but not at other sampling locations. These three months were chosen for assessing the effects of seasonal changes of both hydrological condition (section 4.1) and fish cultural activity (section 4.2). Samples were filtered through pre-combusted $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m } \mathrm { G F } / \mathrm { F }$ filters, and the POM on filters were stored in the dark at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ before the extraction and analysis of BEPOM. The particles on filters were dried at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ , and were weighed for calculating the concentration of total suspended particulate matter (TSM). Then, POM was extracted with $\bar { 0 . 1 } \mathrm { m o l } \bar { \mathrm { L } } ^ { - 1 }$ NaOH in the dark at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , and the extracted solution was neutralized with $1 \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { H C l }$ to avoid the precipitation of humic acids (Osburn et al., 2012). The solution was filtered through pre-cleaned $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate filters for optical measurements.

The absorption spectra of BEPOM were scanned from 240 to $8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ using a Shimadzu UV-1780 UVeVis spectrophotometer, and the Milli-Q water was used as the blank (Yang et al., 2013, 2019). The absorption coefficient at $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \left( a _ { 3 0 0 } \right)$ was calculated as an index for the abundance of CPOM, and the spectral slope over 275e295 nm (S275-295) was calculated as an index for the average molecular weight of organic matter (Helms et al., 2008). A shoulder peak was present around $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in the absorption spectra of CPOM (Fig. S1), which was probably associated with primary production and algal debris (Mayer et al., 2009; Brym et al., 2014). Thus, Da280 was calculated for assessing the intensity of the shoulder peak (Tilstone et al., 2010). Briefly, the log-transformed absorbance over $3 0 0 { - } 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ were fitted with the linear regression $( r ; 0 . 9 8 9 - 0 . 9 9 9 )$ , which was extrapolated to $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to obtain the fitted value of a280. Da280 was the difference between the measured and the fitted values of a280.

Fluorescence spectra of BEPOM were scanned using a Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer (Yang et al., 2013, 2019). The excitation wavelength spanned from 240 to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with an interval of $5 \mathrm { n m }$ , while the emission wavelength was $2 8 0 { - } 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with an interval of $1 \mathrm { n m }$ . The fluorescence spectrum of each sample was corrected for inner-filter effects based on the absorption spectrum, subtracted the blank signals of Milli-Q water, and was normalized by the Raman peak of Milli-Q water (i.e., the Raman Unit, RU; Stedmon and Markager, 2005a; Yang et al., 2013, 2019). The fluorescence intensities were further corrected for the volumes of filtered water sample and the NaOH and HCl solutions for extraction and neutralization (Osburn et al., 2012). Humification index (HIX) was used to indicate the degree of humification, which was calculated as the ratio of the fluorescence peak area over the emission wavelengths of $4 3 5 { - } 4 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that over $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 5 \mathrm { n m }$ at the excitation wavelength of $2 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ (Zsolnay et al., 1999; Birdwell and Engel, 2010). Biological index (BIX) was used to indicate the source of organic matter, which was the ratio of the emission intensity of $3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $4 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at the excitation wavelength of $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Huguet et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2019). A total of 67 EEMs were modeled with PARAFAC using MATLAB 7.0 with the DOMFluor toolbox 1.7. The number of fluorescent components was determined with the split-half validation, and the fluorescence intensity of each component was represented with $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ (RU; Stedmon and Bro, 2008).

# 2.2. Measurements of basic parameters and statistical analysis

The Chlorophyll a (Chl a) concentration and the grain size of TSM were further measured in December, 2018, for testing their influences on the optical properties of POM. The Chl a was extracted with acetone $( 9 0 \% )$ and was determined by measuring the absorbance at 750, 664, 647 and $6 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Strickland and Parsons, 1972). The grain size of TSM was measured using a Malvern Mastersizer 3000 laser particle size analyzer (Callesen et al., 2018).

Correlation analysis, $t$ -test and principal component analysis were performed using IBM SPSS 22.

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Identifying the source of POM with spectral analysis: effects of hydrological changes

The five fluorescent components identified with PARAFAC were compared with those in the OpenFlour database (Murphy et al., 2014). C1 had a long emission wavelength of $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , similar to the terrestrial humic-like component (Kulkarni et al., 2018) and fulvic acids in soil and sediment (Osburn et al., 2016). C2 was similar to the microbial humic-like component in the sediment from Arctic Ocean (C2, Chen et al., 2016). C3 resembled a ubiquitous fulvic acid-like component (Heibati et al., 2017). C4 contained tyrosine-like and tryptophan-like fluorophores, which were related to the phytoplankton production (Stedmon and Markager, 2005b). C5 was similar to a tyrosine-like component, which can be derived from both autochthonous production and sediment re-suspension (Chen et al., 2018).

HIX and BIX were used to infer the degree of humification and the source of organic matter, respectively (Huguet et al., 2009; Birdwell and Engel, 2010; Yang and Hur, 2014; Lee et al., 2018). Lower values of HIX $( < 4 )$ indicated a lower humification degree, while lower values of BIX (0.6e0.7) indicated a weaker contribution from autochthonous sources (Huguet et al., 2009). HIX and BIX were 0.86e4.71 and $0 . 5 0 – 0 . 9 3$ (mean: $2 . 4 5 \pm 1 . 1 5$ and $0 . 6 5 \pm 0 . 1 0 )$ in this study, indicating that BEPOM generally had a low degree of humification and a mixed source from terrestrial and biological/ bacterial end-members. HIX was highest while BIX was lowest in July (paired $t$ -test, $p < 0 . 0 5$ ; Table 2), indicating a higher humification degree and enhanced terrestrial inputs. This was possibly related to the higher rainfall in summer, which resulted in increased flowing of plant detritus and anthropogenic wastes into the river through surface runoff.

Hydrological condition generally has a notable impact on the flow of allochthonous POM into river (Hein et al., 2003; Yang et al., 2019). C1eC4 and TSM were generally highest in July in this study, which were consistent with the seasonal variations of runoff and rainfall (paired $t$ -test, $p < 0 . 0 5$ ; Table 2, Fig. S4). The monthly runoff was $1 . 2 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 }$ , $2 . 0 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 }$ and $1 . 6 9 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 0 } \mathrm { m } ^ { \dot { 3 } }$ in the middle reach of Minjiang River in April, July and December, respectively. The rainfall for the antecedent 7 days before sampling was 14, 90 and $5 \mathrm { m m }$ in April, July and December, respectively (http://slt.fujian. gov.cn/; Fig. S4). Plant detritus, soil and domestic wastes are important sources for river organic matter (Finlay and Kendall, 2007). The increased rainfall and runoff can enhance the flushing of these allochthonous POM from the watershed (Lienart et al., 2018). Thus, the seasonal variation of C1eC4 indicated an important role of terrestrial sources in determining the dynamics of POM in the Minjiang River.

The TSM concentration, C1, C2 and $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ were elevated at two sites A08 and A09 in July (Figs. S1B and S3A, S5A-B). The site A08 was located near a sand quarry, while the site A09 was near residential areas. On one hand, the sand quarrying activity could cause sediment re-suspension, and the sand washing thereafter could further increase the TSM concentration. These processes probably resulted in the elevated levels of TSM and the associated POM at A08 in July (Bao et al., 2014). On the other hand, the higher TSM and POM parameters at A09 in July might be attributed to intensified flushing of domestic wastes, increased sediment re-suspension, and/or stronger soil erosion under higher rainfall and runoff in July (Lienart et al., 2018  ). In addition, the TSM at A08 and A09 also increased in April and December, which might be partly attributed to the trapping of sediment in the reservoir, and stronger erosion of river bank or bed downstream the reservoir (Yu et al., 2011; Bao et al., 2014). Overall, C1 and C2 correlated significantly with the TSM concentration (Fig. 4), indicating an important influence of TSM on the dynamics of organic matter in the Minjiang River (Liu et al., 2019).

# 4.2. Effects of fish culture on the production and quality of POM

The $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ and $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of ${ \mathsf { C } } 3 { \mathrm { - } } { \mathsf { C } } 4$ were significantly higher in the fish culture zones than in other areas in July, except for the site A09 (independent samples t-test, $p < 0 . 0 5$ ; Figs. 2 and S1). They were also significantly higher in the reservoir than in the downstream zone (independent-samples t-test, $p < 0 . 0 5$ ; Figs. S1B and S5). Uneaten fish feed and fish fecal are important sources of POM in the fish culture zone (Rubio-Portillo et al., 2019). In addition, they could increase the levels of nutrients and promote the growth of phytoplankton, which would enhance the autochthonous production of

CPOM (Mazzola et al., 2001). This was supported by the higher level of Chl a in the fish culture zones and the significant correlations between Chl a and a300, C3, C4 (Table 1). It is consistent with the results in previous studies that both protein-like and refractory humic-like components can be produced from the growth and degradation of phytoplankton (Kramer and Herndl, 2004; Lü et al., 2019). Furthermore, the higher S275-295 indicated lower molecular weight of POM in the fish culture zones than in other areas (Fig. S1C), which was probably related to increased contribution from the autochthonous source. However, the influence of fish culture was less significant in December (Figs. 2 and 5). This might be attributed to less feed input and slower growth rate of fish at lower water temperature in winter.

Principal component analysis was performed using the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1eC5, HIX, BIX, $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ , and TSM. Two principal factors were identified, which explained $5 0 . 9 \%$ (PC1) and $2 8 . 5 \%$ (PC2) of the total variance of the dataset, respectively (Fig. 5A). PC1 correlated positively with C1eC2, TSM and HIX but negatively with BIX, indicating that it represented the input of terrestrial organic matter which was dominated by humic-like components. PC2 correlated significantly with ${ \mathsf { C } } 3 { \mathrm { - } } { \mathsf { C } } 4 .$ , indicating that it represented levels of humic-like and protein-like materials. The score of PC2 for each sample correlated significantly with the Chl a concentration $\scriptstyle { r = 0 . 8 4 7 , }$ , $p < 0 . 0 1$ ), indicating that PC2 was mainly affected by phytoplankton production. PC1 was generally low in April and December (Fig. 5B), indicating a dominance of autochthonous sources in these months. The non-fish cultural zones showed highest PC1 and low PC2 score in July, which might be affected by higher rainfall and more terrestrial inputs in the rainy season. In addition, the fish culture zones showed higher PC2 and lower PC1 score than in other areas in April and July, indicating elevated autochthonous production of CPOM from phytoplankton growth in spring and summer.

The influence of grain size on the spectral properties of POM is largely unknown. The ratios of $a _ { 3 0 0 } / \mathrm { T S M }$ and C1/TSM can be used to assess the levels of CPOM and C1 per unit mass of particles. They both correlated significantly with the median grain size $\mathrm { D } \mathbf { x } ( 5 0 )$ in this study $( r ; 0 . 8 0 0 - 0 . 8 3 5$ , $p < 0 . 0 1$ ; Fig. 4), indicating that CPOM and C1 were enriched in larger particles. This might be related to the contribution of micro-phytoplankton which was affected by fish culture. The size of micro-phytoplankton was $2 - 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ (Hoffmann et al., 2006), which was close to the grain size of suspended particles in the Minjiang River $\mathrm { ( D x ( 5 0 ) }$ : $1 1 . 0 { - } 1 8 . 9 \mu \mathrm { m } \mathrm { , }$ ). It has been reported that the size of phytoplankton is larger at higher nutrient concentration (Hein and Reimann, 1995). Thus, the input of fish feed could increase the nutrient concentration, the size and biomass of phytoplankton. This was supported by the higher $\mathrm { D } \mathbf { x } ( 5 0 )$ and Chl a in the fish culture zones than in other areas $( 1 7 . 3 \pm 1 . 2 $ vs. $1 2 . 9 \pm 2 . 3 \mu \mathrm { m }$ and $4 . 0 \pm 0 . 8$ vs. $1 . 9 \pm 1 . 3 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ , independent samples $t$ -test, $p < 0 . 0 5$ ).

The absorption spectra of CPOM were characterized by a broad shoulder peak at ${ \sim } 2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. S1A). Similar shoulder peaks have been identified over $2 5 0 { - } 3 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for DOM from algae and zooplankton in the North Atlantic Ocean (Steinberg et al., 2004). Ortega-Retuerta et al. (2010) also found the same shoulder peak around $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for DOM near the Fantasy Island of Antarctic where the Chl a level was elevated. The intensity of the shoulder peak $\Delta a _ { 2 8 0 }$ correlated significantly with $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and Chl a in our study (r: $0 . 8 5 3 \mathrm { - } 0 . 8 6 2$ , $p < 0 . 0 1$ ; Fig. 3A and B). To our knowledge, this is the first time reporting the result of correlation analysis between $\Delta a _ { 2 8 0 }$ and Chl a, which supports the hypothesis that autochthonous production is an important influencing factor for this shoulder peak (Brym et al., 2014). The positive correlation between $\Delta a _ { 2 8 0 }$ and S275- 295 further suggested that the autochthonous organic matter probably had a lower average molecular weight, since S275-295 correlates negatively with the average molecular weight of organic matter (Helms et al., 2008; Yang and Hur, 2014). In addition, $\Delta a _ { 2 8 0 }$ correlated strongly with ${ \mathsf { C } } 3 { \mathrm { - } } { \mathsf { C } } 4$ $r \colon 0 . 9 1 3 { - } 0 . 9 6 9$ , $p < 0 . 0 1$ , Fig. 3C and D), the two components which correlated strongly with Chl a (Table 1). This indicated that the shoulder peak might be related to aromatic amino acids (e.g., tyrosine and tryptophan) from phytoplankton production (Norman et al., 2011). Our results were consistent with the presence of a similar shoulder peak in the absorption spectra of algal debris (Mayer et al., 2009) and DOM from algae and zooplankton (Steinberg et al., 2004). Lee et al. (2018) also found the shoulder peak over $2 4 0 { - } 2 9 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for algal DOM, and the degree of the shoulder decreased with increasing proportion of allochthonous source in the DOM samples mixed with two contrasting sources.

Overall, our results showed that POM in the reservoir-type river was affected by hydrological condition and phytoplankton production, and fish culture promoted the autochthonous production of POM. The difference of POM between the fish culture zones and other areas was revealed by spectroscopic analysis and PCA for the first time. The correlation between $\Delta a _ { 2 8 0 }$ and Chl a further indicated the potential of using $\Delta a _ { 2 8 0 }$ to probe algal organic matter. Generally, POM plays an important role in the biogeochemical cycles in aquatic environments, and is important in determining the water quality and safety (Blair and Aller, 2012; Bao et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2019). Our results have implications for better understanding the dynamics of river POM under the influences of human activities including dam construction and fish culture (Syvitski et al., 2005; Yu et al., 2011; Ballester-Molto et al., 2017  ).

# 5. Conclusions

(1) PARAFAC identified five fluorescent components for POM, in which the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C1eC4 were highest in July, indicating the influence of hydrological condition and terrestrial inputs. In addition, C3 and C4 correlated significantly with the Chl a concentration, indicating an important contribution from phytoplankton production.   
(2) Human activities such as fish culture and dam construction had a notable impact on POM. Fish culture increased the Chl a concentration and C3eC4. C1eC4 and $a _ { 3 0 0 }$ were higher in the reservoir than downstream the dam. These results were probably related to the eutrophication and enhanced production of POM in the fish culture zone of the reservoir.   
(3) Component C1 and CPOM were enriched in larger particles, which might be associated with the contribution from micro-phytoplankton.   
(4) CPOM showed a broad shoulder peak around $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and the peak intensity $\Delta a _ { 2 8 0 }$ correlated significantly with Chl a and C3eC4, indicating that the shoulder peak was affected by the aromatic amino acids from phytoplankton production.
