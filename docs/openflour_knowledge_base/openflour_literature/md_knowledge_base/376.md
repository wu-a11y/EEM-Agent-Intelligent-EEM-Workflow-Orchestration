# Competition between photochemical and biological degradation of dissolved organic matter from the cyanobacteria Microcystis aeruginosa

Thais B. Bittar,\*1 Armando A. H. Vieira,2 Aron Stubbins,1 Kenneth Mopper3 1 Skidaway Institute of Oceanography, Department of Marine Sciences, University of Georgia, Savannah, Georgia 2 Laboratory of Phycology, Department of Botany, Federal University of Sao Carlos, S \~ ao Carlos, S \~ ao Paulo, Brazil \~ 3 Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry, Old Dominion University, Norfolk, Virginia

# Abstract

Extracellular and intracellular dissolved organic matter (E-DOM and I-DOM, respectively) produced in axenic cultures by the cosmopolitan cyanobacteria Microcystis aeruginosa were characterized, and photoreactivity, bioreactivity, and the effects of phototransformations on bioavailability of E-DOM and I-DOM were assessed in laboratory experiments. Chromophoric (CDOM) and fluorescent (FDOM) properties were analyzed by spectrophotometry, and chemical signatures were obtained by ultrahigh resolution Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FTICR-MS). E-DOM was more heterogeneous regarding optical characteristics as indicated by the presence of multiple CDOM peaks and the more even distribution of FDOM components, and had less absolute and relative numbers for molecular formulae containing nitrogen (N) than I-DOM. E-DOM was more photolabile, with greater photochemical removal of dissolved organic carbon (DOC), CDOM and FDOM, and exhibited more pronounced photochemical alterations in molecular formulae composition than I-DOM. E-DOM was less biolabile, with less biological removal of DOC, CDOM, and protein-like fluorescent material, and supported lower rates of bacterial productivity than I-DOM. Phototransformations reduced the bioavailability of E-DOM and I-DOM, as shown by lower bacterial productivity and longer DOC and CDOM half-lives in bioassays with photoirradiated DOM in comparison to those with nonirradiated DOM. Analyses of CDOM, FDOM, and FTICR-MS revealed that the decrease in bioavailability was caused primarily by photolysis of the biolabile DOM, indicating competition between photochemical and biological removal of cyanobacterial DOM. These results provided insights into mechanistic effects of independent and combined photochemical and biological degradation on M. aeruginosa-derived DOM.

Phytoplankton play a key role in global carbon cycling by mediating the transformation of inorganic carbon into biomass and dissolved organic carbon (DOC). DOC fuels secondary microbial productivity, regulating the flow of carbon through the microbial loop (Azam 1998). In highly productive lakes (Horner-Devine et al. 2004), and in the open ocean (Luo et al. 2010), primary production is the most important source of DOC. Close coupling between phytoplankton primary productivity, release of DOC, and bacterial growth and activity has led to the understanding that autochthonous phytoplankton-derived dissolved organic matter (DOM) is highly bioavailable (Chrost and Faust 1983; Teeling et al. 2012). The bioavailability of DOM can be described as a continuum, with pools that have half-lives from minutes to multiple millennia (Hansell 2013). In this continuum, biolabile DOM (BDOM) is characterized by rapid turnover rates, with timescales of minutes to days (Carlson 2002), and provides substrates that meet energy requirements of heterotrophic bacteria (Keil and Kirchman 1991). The diverse composition of phytoplankton BDOM mediates specific interactions between phytoplankton and bacteria (Bell 1980). For example, throughout the course of a phytoplankton bloom, bacterial succession is driven by a sequence of ecological niches created by availability of different pools of phytoplankton-derived BDOM (Teeling et al. 2012). In addition to exudation by growing phytoplankton, cell death also results in release of BDOM (Agust-ı et al. 1998). Between $2 5 \%$ and $6 5 \%$ of cellular C consists of soluble compounds, such as soluble proteins and carbohydrates directly released as DOM on cell lysis (Myklestad and Swift 1998). At the onset of the senescent phase of phytoplankton cultures, increases of $4 8 – 6 0 \%$ in DOC concentrations have been associated with cell lysis and release of intracellular DOC into the media (Chen and Wangersky 1996), and the lysis of a small fraction $( 3 \% )$ of cells of the cyanobacteria Anabaena flos-aquae resulted in a DOC increase of $3 4 – 6 0 \%$ in cultures (Lee and Rhee 1997). With loss of cellular membrane integrity on cell death, the input of soluble cellular components into the water column is often accompanied by an increase in bacterial abundance and per-cell activity, indicating that intracellular DOM is an important source of BDOM that supports microbial productivity (Pinhassi et al. 2004).

In addition to primary production, photochemical transformations of biorecalcitrant DOM are another source of BDOM (Mopper et al. 2015). However, the exposure of originally biolabile, phytoplankton-derived DOM to sunlight, particularly at shorter wavelengths (UV region), can reduce DOM bioavailability (Tranvik and Kokalj 1998; Obernosterer et al. 2001). Such reduction can be due to the photolysis of BDOM or to cross-linking reactions and formation of humic substances that are less biolabile (Kieber et al. 1997). Thus, photochemical transformations can have varying effects on DOM bioavailability depending, among other factors, on the initial biological lability of DOM. Freshly produced, phytoplankton-derived DOM with high BDOM can become less biolabile after phototransformations, while, humic and recalcitrant DOM can become more biolabile (Tranvik et al. 2000; Tranvik and Bertilsson 2001).

Both individual and combined effects of photochemical and biological degradation of DOM are important removal processes of DOC in aquatic systems (Mopper et al. 2015). The objectives of this study were to identify photochemical and biological transformations of freshly produced phytoplankton DOM and to extend previous investigations of photochemically induced transformations of BDOM. We used axenic (i.e., bacteria-free) cultures of the cyanobacteria Microcystis aeruginosa to obtain fresh unaltered extracellular and intracellular DOM. M. aeruginosa is a cosmopolitan species of cyanobacteria that often dominates massive and recurrent cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms (CyanoHAB) in fresh and brackish waters around the world (Sivonen and Jones 1999), producing significant amounts of DOM and BDOM. The occurrence and magnitude of cyanobacteria blooms are expected to increase with future global warming and eutrophication (Paerl and Huisman 2008). In addition, Microcystis are ineffectively grazed by microzooplankton, thus, the main flow of carbon fixed by Microcystis to secondary producers is via bacterial degradation of the DOM released from live cells and/or during cell death (Kluijver et al. 2012). While extensive research has focused on M. aeruginosa toxicity, the fate of DOM produced by this species is not well known (Paerl and Otten 2013). To address this knowledge gap, the photochemical and biological reactivity (i.e., aspects of removal and transformation) of freshly produced M. aeruginosa-derived DOM was assessed in controlled experiments using solar simulator irradiations and bioassays. Parallel and sequential degradation experiments were performed. In parallel experiments, photochemical and biological degradation of DOM were investigated independently from one another. In sequential experiments, the effects of photochemical degradation on the biological lability of extracellular and intracellular dissolved organic matter (EDOM and I-DOM, respectively) were investigated. This experimental design gave insights into whether photodegradation and biodegradation act in synergy, resulting in complementary degradation of DOM, or in competition. DOM transformations were assessed by changes in bulk DOC concentration, in optical signatures of chromophoric and fluorescent DOM (CDOM and FDOM, respectively), and in chemical properties using ultrahigh resolution Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FTICRMS). Bioavailability was assessed by half-lives of DOC, CDOM, and FDOM, and by bacterial productivity.

# Materials and methods

# M. aeruginosa cultures

The cyanobacteria M. aeruginosa, previously isolated from the Barra Bonita Reservoir (Tiet^e River, ${ 2 2 ^ { \circ } 2 9 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { S } , }$ $4 8 ^ { \circ } 3 4 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { W }$ Sao\~ Paulo, Brazil), is currently maintained in axenic conditions in the Culture Collection of Microalgae at the Botany Department, Federal University of S\~ao Carlos (World Data Center for Microorganisms No. 835). Axenic $M$ . aeruginosa was cultured in batch mode by adding $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of inoculum to $5 \mathrm { ~ L ~ }$ of autoclaved $( 1 2 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ $4 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ ) pH 7 ASM-1 (Artificial Seawater McLachlan, where 1 refers to the $1 ^ { \mathrm { s t } }$ iteration of ingredients) medium containing high concentrations of inorganic nitrogen $( \mathrm { N a N O } _ { 3 } ,$ , $2 \ \mathrm { m m o l \ L } ^ { - 1 }$ ) and phosphorus $\mathrm { ( K _ { 2 } H P O _ { 4 } }$ and ${ \mathrm { N a } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { H P O } } _ { 4 }$ , $0 . 2 \ \mathrm { m m o l \ L } ^ { - 1 }$ ) (Gorham et al. 1964). In spite of the word “seawater” in the acronym, the ASM-1 medium does not contain NaCl in its composition. The DOC concentration of the medium was $< 1 0$ lmol-C $\mathrm { ~ L ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ . Cultures were lightly stirred and maintained in a temperature-controlled room $( 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ under a light : dark cycle of $1 2 : 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ and light intensity of $1 5 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 }$ $\mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ photon flux (40 W fluorescent tubes, Philips TLT 40W/75 Extra Day Light). M. aeruginosa growth rates were calculated from in vivo chlorophyll a (chl a) fluorescence using a Turner 10-AU fluorometer calibrated with chl a standards (Sigma Chemical). M. aeruginosa cultures reached the late exponential/early stationary growth phase in $3 0 \ \mathrm { d }$ (data not shown), at which point extracellular and intracellular DOM were collected.

# Extracellular and intracellular DOM separation

DOM is operationally defined here as passing through a $0 . 2 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter. E-DOM refers to DOM released by live cells of M. aeruginosa, which accumulates during culturing. I-DOM is composed of materials that were originally held as soluble compounds within the cells and were transferred directly into the surrounding aqueous solution as free-DOM on cell lysis. E-DOM was separated from cells using tangential flow filtration through $0 . 2 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size polypropylene cassette modules (Pellicon Millipore) under low pressure $( \leq ~ 5$ psi).

The filtration system was prewashed with $5 \%$ NaClO and rinsed thoroughly with ultrapure deionized water (MilliQ, Millipore). Total organic carbon concentration measured in the ultrapure water after running through the ultrafiltration unit was $< 1$ $\mu \mathrm { m o l - C ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ . The E-DOM was immediately sterilized by vacuum filtration through acid-washed, sterile (autoclaved) $0 . 2 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size polycarbonate membrane filters (GTTP Isopore, Millipore), and sterile glassware. This $2 ^ { \mathrm { n d } }$ filtration step through $0 . 2 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ membranes was done to ensure that E-DOM was kept sterile after the separation in the tangential flow filtration system, as the setup of the tangential flow system is more susceptible to contamination with airborne bacteria than the sterile vacuum filtration system. Unlike the tangential filtration, the vacuum filtration could be performed inside a sterile laminar flow hood. The DOC concentration of the E-DOM solution was $\sim 6 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l { - } C }$ $\mathrm { ~ L ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ .

For the preparation of I-DOM, M. aeruginosa cells were concentrated in the tangential flow filtration system described above. To remove any remaining E-DOM from the retentate cells, three consecutive rinsing steps were performed. In each step, one volume of sterile, tenfold diluted ASM-1 medium (ASM-1/10) was added to the cells and filtration was resumed, flushing remaining E-DOM while reconcentrating the cells. This procedure was tested prior to the experiments with two types of DOM solutions: E-DOM from a test-culture of M. aeruginosa (obtained by vacuum filtration through a $0 . 2 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ membrane) and a solution of blue dextran (Sigma-Aldrich). DOC concentrations of these DOM solutions were measured before and after (on the filtrate) running through the tangential filtration system as described above and yielded $1 0 0 . 8 \%$ $( \pm ~ 4 \% )$ recovery of E-DOM. The cells concentrated by tangential flow filtration were frozen at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for two weeks, then thawed and sonicated at room temperature for $1 0 \mathrm { \ m i n }$ . The freeze–thaw–sonication cycle was repeated one more time and the cell lysate sample was sequentially filtered through $1 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $5 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , and $0 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ precombusted glass fiber filters to remove undisrupted cells $\middle <$ $1 0 \%$ , calculated by the difference between cell counts before and after lysis) and cell fragments, and finally through acidwashed, sterile $0 . 2 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate membranes. This procedure recovered $8 0 \%$ of cellular organic carbon, calculated by the difference between total organic carbon concentrations of the cell lysate before and after the serial filtration. The I-DOM sample yielded by this method had a concentration of $\sim 1 5 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l - C } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ . I-DOM was diluted to $\sim 8 5 0$ $\mu \mathrm { m o l - C ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ to provide a DOC solution of comparable concentration and absorbance to the E-DOM sample. I-DOM was diluted with sterile ASM-1/10 to provide inorganic constitution similar to the E-DOM sample, considering that at late exponential/early stationary phase of $M .$ . aeruginosa growth, inorganic constituents of the medium are reduced to $\leq 1 0 \%$ of original concentrations (based on total dissolved nitrogen measurements, data not shown). Filtrations through $0 . 2 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ were performed under a sterile laminar flow hood with sterile membranes and glassware to ensure that E-DOM and I-DOM were unaltered until the initiation of the UV irradiation experiments and bioassays.

# Simulated irradiation and bioassays

The nonirradiated and nonbiodegraded DOM samples are referred to as “Initial” in this study. Triplicate aliquots of Initial E-DOM and Initial I-DOM were separately irradiated in autoclaved and combusted $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ quartz flasks for $^ \textrm { \scriptsize 7 d }$ in a solar simulator equipped with 12 UV340 bulbs (cutoff at $2 9 5 { - } 3 6 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , Q Panel) (Stubbins et al. 2012). Intensities of UVA and UVB reaching the flasks $2 5 ~ \mathrm { W ~ m } ^ { - 2 }$ and $3 \mathrm { ~ W ~ m ~ } ^ { - 2 }$ , respectively) were similar to those measured at the surface of the Barra Bonita Reservoir in the summer, using a portable radiometer (StellarNet). The total experimental UV irradiation was equivalent to $\sim 1 2 \mathrm { ~ d ~ }$ , assuming $1 3 \ \mathrm { h }$ of sunlight per day in the summer months in the Barra Bonita Reservoir. Samples irradiated are hereafter referred to as UV E-DOM and UV I-DOM.

Triplicates of Initial DOM and UV DOM were inoculated with $5 \%$ volume of a bacteria inoculum. Water for the inoculum was collected from the Little Creek Reservoir (Virginia) at $5 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ depth in a sterile glass flask, returned to the laboratory, and filtered through combusted $1 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size glass fiber filters to remove grazers, phytoplankton, zooplankton and detritus. The filtrate was then centrifuged at $1 0 , 0 0 0 \times g$ for $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ (Beckman J2-21). Pelletized bacteria were resuspended in sterile ASM-1/10 medium and used as the inoculum. Incubation bottles were kept in the dark at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and sampled regularly for bacterial productivity. The bioassays were stopped after bacterial productivity plateaued. Bioassays performed with Initial E-DOM and UV E-DOM were incubated for 27 d and those performed with Initial I-DOM and UV I-DOM were incubated for 41 d. Bioassays with E-DOM were sampled on days 0, 1, 2, 5, 8, 13, 19, 27 and bioassays with I-DOM were sampled on days 0, 1, 2, 4, 7, 12, 21, and 41. In addition to bacterial productivity, samples were collected for measurements of DOC concentration, and DOM optical and chemical characterization. Samples taken from bioassays with Initial DOM and with UV DOM are referred to as Bio DOM and $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio DOM, respectively.

# Bacterial productivity

Rates of bacterial incorporation of tritiated thymidine $( \mathrm { [ H ^ { 3 } ] }$ TdR) were determined to assess bacterial productivity using eight $1 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ aliquots of each sample spiked with 57 $\mu \mathrm { C i }$ $\left( 0 . 7 4 \ \mathrm { n m o l } \right)$ of $[ \mathrm { H } ^ { 3 } ]$ TdR, four of which were immediately killed with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $7 9 \%$ trichloroacetic acid (TCA, killed controls) (Smith and Azam 1992). Killed controls and live aliquots were incubated at room temperature in the dark for four hours, and then live aliquots were killed with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ o f $7 9 \%$ TCA. Two centrifugation-resuspension cycles with $5 \%$ TCA and cold $8 0 \%$ ethanol were performed to remove the excess $[ \mathrm { H } ^ { 3 } ]$ TdR and excess TCA, respectively. After a final centrifugation, pelletized bacteria were air-dried overnight and then resuspended in scintillation solution (Cole-Parmer) and the radioactivity of the tubes was measured in disintegrations per minute using a scintillation counter (TriCarb Packard HP). Calibration curves and instrument efficiency and reproducibility were verified by running $[ \boldsymbol { \mathrm { H } } ^ { 3 } ]$ TdR in the range of $0 . 2 5 { - } 2 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m o l \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ along with the samples, in each run. As direct bacterial counts are not available, $[ \mathrm { H } ^ { 3 } ]$ TdR incorporation rates (in nmol $\mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { h } ^ { - 1 }$ ) were converted to cells $\mathrm { ~ L ~ } ^ { - 1 } \bar { \mathrm { ~ h ~ } ^ { - 1 } }$ assuming a conversion factor of $2 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 8 }$ bacteria cells produced per mol of TdR incorporated (Riemann et al. 1990), and to units of C as lmol-C $\mathrm { ~ L ~ } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { ~ h ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ assuming a cellular content of $2 0 \mathrm { \ f g - C \ c e l l ^ { - 1 } }$ (Lee and Fuhrman 1987). Due to the use of these conversion factors, the production rates presented should only be used for relative comparisons between DOM treatments within this study.

# DOM characterization

Bulk concentrations of DOM are reported as nonpurgeable DOC and were obtained by high temperature combustion $( 6 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ and nondispersive infrared detection using a total organic carbon analyzer (TOC-Vcph, Shimadzu). The instrument was calibrated with standard solutions of potassium hydrogen phthalate from $1 0 0 \phantom { \frac { 1 } { 2 } } \mu \mathrm { m o l - C } \phantom { \frac { 1 } { 2 } } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ to 1000 $\mu \mathrm { m o l - C } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ (Stubbins and Dittmar 2012).

Optical characterization of DOM was obtained by analysis of its chromophoric (absorbance) and fluorescent properties (CDOM and FDOM, respectively). Ultrapure water was used as blanks for CDOM and FDOM analyses. Absorption spectra of CDOM from $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ to $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ at $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals were obtained using a UV-visible diode array spectrophotometer (Agilent 8453) and a five-centimeter acid-washed quartz cuvette. Spectra were corrected for light scatter by subtracting absorption from $7 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ to $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , and Naperian light absorption coefficients were calculated $( a \mathrm { ~ m ~ } ^ { - 1 } )$ (Green and Blough 1994). The background absorption of the ASM-1/10 medium was recorded and subtracted from the CDOM absorption spectra of the samples. Absorption of the medium was $< 3 \%$ of Initial E-DOM and Initial I-DOM at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . Variation in CDOM in the samples is reported at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (a254). Spectral slopes $( \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) between $a 2 7 5$ and $a 2 9 5$ $( S _ { 2 7 5 }$ 295) and $a 3 5 0$ and $a 4 0 0$ $( S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 } )$ were calculated according to Helms et al. (2008). Specific UV absorbance $\left( \mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } \right)$ was calculated using the decadic light absorption coefficient at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m ~ ( m ^ { - 1 } ) }$ normalized to DOC $\mathbf { \Phi } ( \mathbf { m } \mathbf { g } \mathbf { C } \mathbf { \Lambda } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ;$ ) and used as a proxy for aromaticity (Weishaar et al. 2003). $1 ^ { \mathrm { s t } }$ and $2 ^ { \mathrm { n d } }$ derivative absorption spectra were determined using linear regression over sliding $2 1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals. The $2 ^ { \mathrm { n d } }$ derivative was used to accurately determine peaks that appeared as shoulders in the absorption coefficient spectra (Loiselle et al. 2009).

Emission–excitation matrix (EEM) spectra for FDOM were determined using a spectrofluorometer (Fluoromax 2) and a one-centimeter acid-washed quartz cuvette (Spencer et al.

2007). Each raw EEM was composed of 45 emission spectra from $2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ to $5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ in $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals, at excitation from $2 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ to $4 2 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ in $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals and both emission and excitation slits were set to $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . Blank EEMs were collected from ultrapure water and subtracted from sample EEMs. EEMs were corrected using instrument-specific excitation (xcorrect) and emission (mcorrect) correction files and for inner-filter effects using Matlab version 7.14 (Cory et al. 2010). Due to low signal to noise ratios at excitation wavelengths shorter than $2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { { \ n m } }$ and emission wavelengths shorter than $3 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , the EEMs were trimmed to excitation and emission wavelengths between $2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $4 2 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively (Stedmon and Bro 2008). Corrected and trimmed EEMs were normalized to Raman units and FDOM components were identified by parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC). A PARAFAC model was developed using EEMs for 122 samples from M. aeruginosa E-DOM and I-DOM using the DOM Fluor toolbox in Matlab version 7.14 (Stedmon and Bro 2008). Given the relative homogeneity of the sample set, no extreme outliers were found. The model was validated using random split-half analysis and the number of components was determined by visual examination of the residuals from models with gradually increasing number of components. Intensities of each component in the samples were calculated as the maximum fluorescent intensity of each component in the model, i.e., using maxval values (Stedmon and Bro 2008).

Four samples from each DOM type (E-DOM and I-DOM) were selected for Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FTICR-MS) analysis: Initial (nonirradiated, nonbiodegraded); UV (seven days UV-irradiated, nonbiodegraded); Bio (nonirradiated, biodegraded, i.e., from Bio DOM bioassays); and $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio $1 ^ { \mathrm { s t } } ~ 7$ d UV-irradiated, then biodegraded, i.e., from $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio DOM bioassays). Bio DOM and $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio DOM samples were collected from E-DOM and I-DOM bioassays on days 2 and 7, respectively. These sampling days corresponded to the first third of the exponential phase of bacterial productivity. Samples were kept frozen at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $< 6 0$ d prior to analysis. Molecular characterization of DOM was obtained by electrospray ionization (ESI, Apollo II) FTICR-MS (Bruker Daltonics 12 Tesla Apex Qe), at the College of Sciences Major Instrumentation Cluster (COSMIC), Old Dominion University, Virginia (Sleighter and Hatcher 2008). Samples were analyzed in negative ionization mode with an infusion rate of $1 2 0 \mu \mathrm { L } \mathrm { h } ^ { - 1 }$ into the ESI ion source. Whole samples (unfractionated) were prepared by dilution $( 5 0 : 5 0 \mathrm { ~ v / v } )$ with ultrapure methanol plus $0 . 1 \%$ ammonium hydroxide $\mathrm { ( p H ~ 8 ) }$ ). Ion accumulation time in the hexapole was $_ \mathrm { ~ 1 ~ s ~ }$ and 300 transients were collected with a 4M word time domain (Chen et al. 2011). Mass spectra of peaks with signal to noise ratio $> 4$ ranged from $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m } / z$ to $8 5 0 \ \mathrm { ~ m / z }$ and were internally calibrated using data lists of fatty acids and a list of peaks common to all samples (Sleighter et al. 2008). Molecular formulae assignment was performed based on exact masses following the criteria described in Stubbins et al. (2010), except for $\mathrm { C } \le 6 0$ and $\mathrm { N } / \mathrm { C } < 0 . 7$ . This relatively high N/C was allowed in the molecular assignment because DOM from M. aeruginosa is likely to contain amino acids, such as arginine, which has an $\mathrm { N } / \mathrm { C } > 0 . 6 6 7$ (Fang et al. 2010). Only single-charged peaks and unambiguously assigned molecular formulae (Sleighter et al. 2008) were included in the final list of compounds comprising each DOM sample. Using this set of rules, formulae were assigned to $3 7 – 4 1 \%$ of peaks in E-DOM and $2 4 { - } 3 1 \%$ of peaks in I-DOM. The unassigned peaks had on average lower intensity $( 3 . 2 6 ~ \times ~ 1 0 ^ { 6 } )$ ) compared to those that were assigned a single formula $( 2 . 3 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 7 } )$ , and many had mass defects $> 0 . 5$ , indicative of salt-DOM adducts.

Formulae were categorized in three elemental classes regarding presence/absence of the heteroatoms N, P, and S in the molecular formulae. The elemental classes are defined as CHO-only; $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { N }$ : formula with N, regardless of the presence of $\mathrm { P }$ or S (CHON, CHONS, CHONP, and CHONSP); and $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { P } / \mathrm { S }$ : formulae with $\mathrm { P }$ and/or S, but without $_ \mathrm { N }$ (CHOP, CHOS, and CHOPS). Formulae were also categorized into likely structural groups, based on double bond equivalents (Mclafferty and Turecek 1993), modified aromaticity index $\mathrm { ( A I _ { m o d } ) }$ (Koch and Dittmar 2006), the elemental ratios of $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C }$ and $\mathrm { O } / \mathrm { C } ,$ and the number of $_ \mathrm { N }$ in the formula. The structural groups and their definitions used here are: aromatics $\left( \mathrm { A I } _ { \mathrm { m o d } } \geq 0 . 5 \right)$ ); moderately unsaturated $( \mathrm { A I } _ { \mathrm { m o d } } < 0 . 5$ , $\mathrm { O } /$ $C < 0 . 9$ , and $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C } < 1 . 5 )$ ); unsaturated aliphatics $( \mathrm { N } = 0$ , $\mathrm { O } /$ $C < 0 . 9$ , and $2 \leq \mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C } \geq 1 . 5 )$ ); peptide-like $( \mathrm { N } > 0$ , $\mathrm { O } / \mathrm { C } < 0 . 9$ , and $2 \leq \mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C } \geq 1 . 5 )$ ); saturated fatty acids $\mathrm { ( O / C < 0 . 9 }$ and $\mathrm { H } /$ $\mathrm { C } > 2 ,$ ); and sugars $\mathrm { ( O / C } \geq 0 . 9 )$ ).

# Interpretation of FTICR-MS data

Parallel UV and Bio experiments: A comparison of FTICRMS formulae present in Initial DOM vs. UV DOM samples, and in Initial DOM vs. Bio DOM samples revealed six lability-defined pools of DOM: (1) Photoresistant (present in both Initial and UV samples); (2) Photolabile (present in Initial, absent from UV samples); (3) Photoproducts (absent from Initial, present in UV samples); (4) Bioresistant (present in both Initial and Bio samples); (5) Biolabile (present in Initial, absent from Bio samples); and (6) Bioproducts (absent from Initial, present in Bio samples). The “competition” pool of FTICR-MS formulae mentioned in the discussion is comprised of formulae common to photolabile and the biolabile pools.

Sequential UV $\longrightarrow$ Bio experiments: A comparison of FTICRMS formulae in Initial DOM, UV DOM, and $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio DOM samples allowed the definition of four pools of DOM: (7) Biolabile photoproducts (absent from Initial, present in UV, absent from $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio samples); (8) Bioresistant photoproducts (absent from Initial, present in UV, present in $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio samples); (9) UV $\longrightarrow$ Bio products (absent from Initial, absent from UV, present in $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio samples); (10) UV Bioresistant (present in Initial, present in UV, present in UV $\longrightarrow$ Bio samples).

# Statistical analyses

Bacterial productivity data were fit with exponential curves (SigmaPlot v. 12.0 from Systat Software Inc) and specific growth rates $( \mu , \mathrm { ~ d ~ } ^ { - 1 } )$ of bacteria were calculated using a linear regression of the exponential growth phase determined from semilog plots of C incorporation vs. time for each bioassay. DOC concentrations, $a 2 5 4$ , and fluorescence intensity of FDOM components throughout the bioassays were fitted with a two-pool decay function (SigmaPlot v. 12.0 from Systat Software) and half-lives $( t ^ { 1 / 2 }$ , d) were calculated as $t ^ { 1 / 2 } = \ln ( 2 ) \ \times$ decay constant $( \lambda ) ^ { - 1 }$ . A paired, nonparametric test (Wilcox test, $p { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ was used to determine significant differences in bacterial productivity rates, DOC concentration, a254, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } ,$ , and fluorescence of each of the FDOM component between Bio-DOM and $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio DOM (i.e., over the course of the bioassays with preirradiated and postirradiated DOM). An unpaired, nonparametric Mann–Whitney test $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ was used to determine significance of differences in molecular formulae composition between E-DOM and I-DOM, and between median of molecular mass of lability-defined groups (labile, resistant, products). Shifts in molecular mass are presented as values of median throughout this study. The Jaccard similarity index (JSI) was used to assess similarity between E-DOM and IDOM regarding molecular composition and was calculated as $\mathrm { J S I } = \#$ of formulae common to both samples $\times$ (# formulae unique to $\mathrm { E - D O M } + \#$ formulae unique to $\mathrm { I - D O M } + \#$ formulae common to both samples) $\cdot ^ { - 1 }$ (Jaccard 1912).

# Results

# Characterization of Initial E-DOM and Initial I-DOM from M. aeruginosa

Analyses of optical and chemical properties showed significant differences between E-DOM and I-DOM regarding chromophoric (CDOM), fluorescence (FDOM), and chemical (FTICR-MS) composition. E-DOM had higher $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 ) }$ , a shallower $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ , and a steeper $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ than I-DOM (Table 1). The two most evident peaks in the $2 ^ { \mathrm { n d } }$ derivative CDOM absorption spectra of E-DOM were at $2 6 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and $\sim 3 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , with two additional peaks at $3 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. 1C). In I-DOM, the most pronounced CDOM peak was at $2 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , with an additional peak at $3 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. 1F). Three PARAFAC-derived components were identified in FDOM (see Supporting Information). Peak T, $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A } _ { i }$ , and M comprised $4 3 \%$ , $3 7 \%$ , and $2 0 \%$ of the total fluorescence in Initial E-DOM, respectively, and $8 3 \%$ , $1 6 \%$ and $1 \%$ in Initial I-DOM, respectively (Table 1).

# Characterization of UV-irradiated E-DOM (UV E-DOM) and UV-irradiated I-DOM (UV I-DOM)

Simulated solar UV exposure (7 d) removed $1 7 \%$ of the DOC from E-DOM and $< 4 \%$ from I-DOM and caused substantial loss of DOM absorbance and total fluorescence in both (Table 1). The absorbance coefficient at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ( a254, $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ) and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ decreased $4 7 \%$ and $3 6 \%$ , respectively in UV E-DOM, and $3 0 \%$ and $2 6 \%$ , respectively in UV I-DOM, following photodegradation (Table 1). UV E-DOM displayed two distinct peaks in the $2 ^ { \mathrm { n d } }$ derivative CDOM absorption spectra at $2 9 3 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $\sim 3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ; $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ were not calculated for UV E-DOM as these peaks caused substantial nonlinearities in the log normalized CDOM spectrum (Fig. 1). In UV I-DOM, the $2 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ peak shifted to $2 5 8 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. 1), while $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ exhibited minimal change (Table 1).

Total DOM fluorescence decreased 15-fold due to photodegradation in UV E-DOM and 7.5-fold in UV I-DOM. When considering absolute fluorescence loss, the component with highest initial fluorescence intensity, peak T, was reduced the most (17-fold in UV E-DOM and 20-fold in UV I-DOM), followed by component $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A } .$ , which had intermediate fluorescence intensities initially (16-fold reduction in UV E-DOM and 2.4-fold in UV I-DOM), and component M, which had the lowest initial intensity (decreasing tenfold in UV EDOM, and increasing 1.7-fold in UV I-DOM; Table 1).

Photochemically mediated shifts in UV E-DOM relative to Initial E-DOM were more pronounced than those in UV IDOM relative to Initial I-DOM, regarding both elemental and structural classes (Fig. 2) and molecular mass (Table 2). In UV E-DOM, photolabile formulae had significantly greater median of molecular mass (454 Da) compared to photoresistant ( $3 1 2 \mathrm { \ D a }$ ; $p { < } 0 . 0 1 $ ), while UV E-DOM photoproduced formulae (318 Da) were not significantly different than photolabile and photoresistant regarding molecular mass $( p > 0 . 0 5$ ; Fig. 3A). In UV I-DOM, photolabile (481 Da), photoresistant $( 4 5 8 ~ \mathrm { D a } )$ , and photoproduced (486 Da) formulae were not significantly different regarding molecular mass $( p > 0 . 0 5$ ; Fig. 3B). Photodegradation resulted in overall decrease in molecular mass of both E-DOM and I-DOM (Table 2).

# Discussion

# Signatures of Initial and degraded M. aeruginosa DOM

The main components of phytoplankton biomass are peptides/proteins, polysaccharides, lipids, pigments, and nucleic acids (Hellebust 1974). The relative composition of a phytoplankton cell and released DOM varies with the physiological status of the cells, such as in response to nutrient and light availability (Granum et al. 2002; Bittar et al. 2013). For instance, in phytoplankton growing under optimal light but

N limitation, cell division is limited relative to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fixation, N is retained intracellularly and photosynthesis continues to occur, resulting in excess of C assimilation and release of Cenriched and N-depleted DOM relative to the intracellular content (Myklestad and Haug 1972; Fogg 1983). Phytoplankton growing under light- and nutrient-replete conditions, conversely, have greater $\mathrm { N } / \mathrm { C }$ ratios of both cellular and extracellular material (Myklestad 1995). The ratio of polysaccharides to amino acids of phytoplankton-derived DOM can influence its bacterial utilization and, consequently, the fate of these organics in the environment (Obernosterer and Herndl 1995). The composition of cellular and extracellular material is also species-specific, with DOM from cyanobacteria, such as M. aeruginosa, generally displaying greater ratios of protein to polysaccharides relative to eukaryotic phytoplankton (green algae and diatoms) (Henderson et al. 2008). In this study, cultured M. aeruginosa was not limited by light or nutrients and, although the DOM was collected at the beginning of the stationary phase, at the onset of nutrient limitation, the batch cultures were at exponential growth during $\sim 3 0$ d. Consequently, both E-DOM and I-DOM in this study mainly represent material from cells under replete resources, which is reflected in the relatively high N/C ratios of both E-DOM and I-DOM relative to DOM from natural waters (Rossel et al. 2013). While the character of the extracellular material reflects the cellular physiological status, the compositions of intracellular and extracellular material are not necessarily identical (Myklestad 1995; Pivokonsky et al. 2014). In this study, the optical and chemical characterization of E-DOM and I-DOM showed differences in relative composition between the internal and external pools of organics in M. aeruginosa. E-DOM had a shallower $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ , a greater number of CDOM peaks, a more even distribution among FDOM components (Table 1), as well as lower molecular mass, and was enriched in CHO-only and aliphatics (Nfree), but depleted in peptide-like formulae relative to IDOM (Table 2). Conversely, I-DOM exhibited a steeper $S _ { 2 7 5 }$ 295, one significant CDOM peak, dominance of peak T $( 8 3 \% )$ within FDOM (Table 1), greater median of molecular mass, and was enriched in $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { N }$ formulae and peptide-like formulae but depleted in aliphatic formulae (Table 2). In concert, these results indicated that E-DOM is a more optically heterogeneous, lower molecular mass, and N-depleted mixture of compounds relative to I-DOM (Fig. 1; Tables 1, 2). The differences in chemical composition were reflected in an overall low similarity index (0.20) between E-DOM and IDOM (Table 2). The high similarity of CHO-only and aliphatics (0.50) between E-DOM and I-DOM may indicate that these compounds are abundant metabolites in the soluble intracellular pool and, consequently, the excess is released as DOM by the live cells (Mague et al. 1980). The exudation mechanism of DOM by live phytoplankton is dependent, among other factors, on membrane permeability, which decreases steeply with molecular mass (Raven 1984). It is generally suggested that when present in excess intracellularly, small molecules such as free amino acids $( 7 5 { - } 2 0 4 ~ \mathrm { D a } )$ ) and glucose $( \sim 1 8 0$ Da) are passively diffused through the cellular membrane (Hellebust 1974; Mague et al. 1980), while the excretion of larger molecules $( > 3 0 0$ Da) requires a membrane transporter (Lignell 1990). In this study, it is likely that exudation of the compounds detected by FTICRMS (200-850 Da) in E-DOM occurred via mediated transport through the cell membrane, but passive diffusion of smaller compounds, such as free amino acids, is also likely. As DOM exudation is dependent on membrane permeability and transporters selectivity, E-DOM has generally lower molecular mass relative to I-DOM (Lee and Rhee 1997).

Many functional and structural cellular components, such as proteins, nucleic acids, and chlorophyll, have high N/C ratios and, thus, the intracellular material is generally enriched in N, relative to the extracellular DOM exuded by phytoplankton (Fang et al. 2010). M. aeruginosa and other cyanobacteria typically store high molecular mass copolymers of aspartic acid and arginine in cyanophycin granules as N intracellular storage (Lee 2008) and produce toxins, such as microcystins, that also contain N and are more abundant intracellularly (Harada 2004). In addition to relative content of N, the types of N-containing compounds also differ between extracellular and intracellular matter in many phytoplankton (Granum et al. 2002). Thus, the lower abundance of aliphatics relative to peptide-like in I-DOM observed in this study may be attributed to higher amounts of the amino acids arginine, lysine, and glycine, and lower concentrations of aliphatic amines (diethylamine and ethylamine) relative to E-DOM (Fang et al. 2010). The greater number of molecular formulae in I-DOM relative to E-DOM could also be due to the more hydrophobic nature of EDOM produced by M. aeruginosa relative to I-DOM (Henderson et al. 2008; Pivokonsky et al. 2014). Although cyanobacteria are generally constituted of higher ratios of proteins to polysaccharides compared to other classes of phytoplankton (Henderson et al. 2008), polysaccharides are important components of E-DOM as well as cellular constituents, such as storage (as cyanobacterial starch) and cell wall structures (peptidoglycans) (Lee 2008). Polysaccharides are nonchromophoric compounds and have low ionization rates, being poorly represented in CDOM, FDOM, and FTICR-MS analyses, which means that this class of compounds is poorly assessed in this study.

CDOM absorbance $< 3 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ was greater in I-DOM than E-DOM (Fig. 1), which is consistent with a higher relative fluorescence intensity of the short wavelength excitation and emission peak T, and coincides with a greater relative content of $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { N }$ and peptide-like formulae compared to E-DOM (Tables 1, 2). These results suggest that both the absorbance $< 3 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and peak T fluorescence are possibly due to proteinaceous chromophores (Maie et al. 2007; Fellman et al. 2010), particularly in I-DOM, given that the relative content of peptides/protein can reach $6 3 \%$ of bulk IDOM in cultured M. aeruginosa, compared to $4 7 \%$ of E-DOM (Pivokonsky et al. 2014). Both $\mathrm { C D O M } < 3 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and peak T pronounced signals, particularly in I-DOM, could also be associated with production of the toxin microcystin and the nontoxic peptide aeruginopeptin, which can contain the chromophoric and fluorescent amino acid tyrosin (Meriluoto et al. 1990; Harada et al. 2001) and are enriched intracellularly (Wiedner et al. 2003). The CDOM peaks between $3 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and $4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ identified in this study (Fig. 1) could be associated with two classes of UV protectants in cyanobacteria: micosporine-like amino acids (MAAs) and scytonemin. Scytonemin is an extracellular alkaloid with absorbance maxima between $2 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 8 6 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (Sinha and Hader € 2008). Although this compound has not yet been isolated from strains of Microcystis, it has been found in other species of cyanobacteria (Karsten et al. 1998). MAAs have a range of UV absorbance maxima between $3 1 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , as a result of variations in chemical structure, such as side groups and N substituents (Sinha and Hader 2008). The two types of € MAAs, shinorine and Porphyra-334, previously isolated from M. aeruginosa have maximum absorbance at $3 3 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , and its production has been associated with $M _ { ☉ }$ . aeruginosa’s competitive advantage relative to other phytoplankton in developing and maintaining surface blooms in high UV radiation surface waters (Liu et al. 2004). As the location of MAAs (i.e., extracellular or intracellular) is unknown for most cyanobacteria (Sinha and Hader 2008), shinorine and Porphyra-334€ could be among the chromophores in E-DOM and/or IDOM. While the $1 ^ { \mathrm { s t } }$ and $2 ^ { \mathrm { n d } }$ derivative spectra of CDOM absorbance are informative of DOM optical properties (Loiselle et al. 2009), the above species-specific metabolites are only suggested chromophores and further isolation and targeted analysis are necessary to verify the chemical identity of compounds that contribute to CDOM bulk absorbance in M. aeruginosa DOM. While the presence of nitrate in the ASM-1/10 culture medium can result in a secondary absorption shoulder at $3 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , the absorption spectrum of the sterile ASM-1/10 was subtracted from the CDOM spectra of the samples (see Materials and methods section), thus, CDOM peaks are likely not significantly influenced by the background absorption of the medium constituents.

The larger number of CDOM peaks in E-DOM (Fig. 1) is in agreement with the highest relative intensity of fluorescence of the humic-like peaks $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ and M) detected in EDOM (Table 1) relative to I-DOM. E-DOM had a more even distribution of elemental classes (i.e., relative content of CHO-only, $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { N } ,$ , $\mathrm { C H O } + { \mathrm { S } } / { \mathrm { P } } )$ ), but its structural composition was dominated by aliphatics with minor aromatic contributions (Table 2). This apparent inconsistency between the relative humic FDOM and FTICR-MS aromatics is attributed to the unknown chemical properties of peaks $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ and M. While peaks $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ and $\mathbf { M }$ are typically associated with humic terrestrial or vascular plant-derived material and byproducts of microbial respiration, respectively (Coble 1996), the prevalence of these peaks in DOM from a variety of environments suggests that they might have distinct chemical character depending on source (Ishii and Boyer 2012). Furthermore, neither fluorescence spectra nor FTICR-MS are quantitative analyses. DOM fluorescence represents an unknown group of fluorophores, with unknown fluorescence quantum yields (Fellman et al. 2010), while FTICR-MS cannot be interpreted quantitatively due to differences in relative ionization efficiencies and charge competition among molecules during electrospray ionization (Kujawinski 2002). Consequently, much still remains to be learned about the overlap between the analytical windows of FDOM (i.e., the fluorescent fraction) and FTICR-MS (i.e., the ionizable fraction), thus, a direct comparison between the two remains poorly constrained and requires further investigation (Stubbins et al. 2014).

E-DOM and I-DOM were distinct regarding photochemical and biological reactivity. E-DOM had higher percentages of DOC, CDOM, and FDOM removed by UV (Table 1) and its elemental and structural composition was more evidently affected by irradiation relative to I-DOM (Figs. 2, 3). The preirradiation of E-DOM had more pronounced negative effects on its bioavailability compared to I-DOM (Figs. 4, 5). While less altered by UV, I-DOM had a higher percentage of DOC removed by biodegradation, shorter biological half-lives of DOC, CDOM, and FDOM, supported higher rates of bacterial productivity, and underwent greater bioalterations in elemental and structural composition compared with E-DOM. In summation, these results suggest that E-DOM is more photoreactive than I-DOM, while I-DOM is more bioreactive than E-DOM. The greater photoreactivity of E-DOM was likely due to its higher content of optically active compounds, as indicated by its greater CDOM absorbance, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Weishaar et al. 2003), and fluorescence of peaks $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ and M (Moran et al. 2000), when compared to I-DOM. These chromophoric and fluorescent compounds absorb photons, initiating photochemical primary reactions and producing reactive transients that result in secondary photochemical pathways of DOM transformation (Mopper et al. 2015). In addition to being the initiators of photochemical reactions in natural waters, chromophoric compounds are on average more photolabile (Spencer et al. 2009; Stubbins et al. 2010) and less biolabile (Kothawala et al. 2012) than the bulk DOC pool. This pattern has been observed in this study, with $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values decreasing due to UV irradiation and increasing during the $1 ^ { \mathrm { s t } }$ days of bioassays. The greater biolability of I-DOM is consistent with its relatively lower $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Jaff-e et al. 2008), higher peak T fluorescence (Fellman et al. 2010), and greater proportion of $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { N }$ and peptide-like formulae (Benner and Kaiser 2011) compared to E-DOM.

Besides the CDOM peaks that were lost by photodegradation $2 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ in E-DOM and $3 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ in IDOM), the $2 ^ { \mathrm { n d } }$ derivative CDOM spectra showed that the remaining peaks were shifted toward shorter wavelengths (Fig. 1). These shifts in CDOM peaks suggest a decrease in the degree of conjugation, aromaticity and/or molecular size due to photodegradation (Helms et al. 2008). Consistent with this trend, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values, $\mathrm { { A I } _ { \mathrm { { m o d } } } }$ and molecular mass decreased following UV exposure, in both E-DOM and I-DOM (Tables 1, 2), indicating that photodegradation reduced the degree of molecular conjugation and size in both Microcystis-derived DOM pools, which is consistent with previous optical and

FTICR-MS studies of photodegraded DOM (Gonsior et al.   
2009; Stubbins et al. 2010; Rossel et al. 2013).

In this study, peak T fluorescence decreased during UV irradiation (Table 1; Fig. 6A,D). While the photolability of peak T is not unprecedented (Moran et al. 2000; Xu and Jiang 2013), other photoirradiation studies have shown contrasting effects of UV irradiation on peak T’s fluorescence intensity (Clark et al. 2008) or an absence of effects (Cory et al. 2007). Such cross-study variability shows that the protein-like peak T has a variable degree of photolability, which is potentially related to its chemical composition and source (Maie et al. 2007). Furthermore, in studies that report photoresistant peak T, photochemical removal of peak B occurred (Stedmon and Markager 2005; Cory et al. 2007), indicating that the composition of the protein-like material might play a role in its degree of photolability. While peaks T and B are defined as distinct protein-like fluorescent material based on their relative amount of tryptophan and tyrosine (Coble 1996), their maxima ex/em pairs often overlap in cross-study comparisons, leading to conflicting conclusions regarding photolability of protein-like fluorescent material. The type of irradiation (UV, visible or UV 1 visible, natural or simulated) used in the experiment and the length of exposure also seem to play a role in the degree of photolability of protein-like fluorescent peaks. In this study, the light sources in the solar simulator have a cutoff at $\sim$ $2 9 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , and, therefore, E-DOM and I-DOM were irradiated at somewhat more energetic wavelengths than typically found in the solar irradiance spectrum at the Earth’s surface (Mopper et al. 2015). This is in agreement with findings by Stedmon and Markager (2005) showing that peak B (ex/em 280/338, closest to this study’s peak T pair, 275/338) was photodegraded by UV-only and not by $\mathrm { U V } +$ visible radiation. Irradiation experiments that resulted in photolysis of peak T were overall carried out for longer periods (6 d, Xu and Jiang (2013); up to $^ { 7 0 \mathrm { ~ d ~ } }$ , Moran et al. (2000)), such as in this study (7 d), when compared to those in which no change is reported $( \leq 2 4 \ \mathrm { h } ,$ , Stedmon and Markager (2005); Cory et al. (2007)). Losses of peak T during longer irradiation periods may indicate that secondary photochemical reactions are important in the destruction of DOM compounds that give rise to peak T fluorescence, including toxins from cyanobacteria (Welker and Steinberg 2000).

Our results showed that peaks T and $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ were both biolabile, with the former displaying shorter half-lives than the latter (Fig. 6), which is in agreement with previously reported FDOM lability profiles that show preferential removal of protein-like FDOM relative to humic-like FDOM (Balcarczyk et al. 2009; Guillemette and Del Giorgio 2011). The significant biological removal of peak $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ in this study (Fig. 6B) has been previously reported (Moran et al. 2000; Sleighter et al. 2014), while others found no changes or production of these peaks in bioassays (Stedmon and Markager 2005; Cory et al. 2007). Such contrasting results seem to be related to DOM source and history of degradation. In support of that, previous studies have shown that “fresher” terrestrially derived humic-like FDOM with more limited degradation history were more readily bioavailable (Mann et al. 2012; Sleighter et al. 2014) when compared to highly processed humic-like FDOM (Spencer et al. 2010; Cory and Kaplan 2012, 2013). Therefore, it is apparent that a complex lability spectrum can be expected for humic-like FDOM components depending on chemical composition and degradation history (Kothawala et al. 2012). The application of FDOM bioreactivity concepts, developed mainly from studies with natural DOM (Fellman et al. 2010), to freshly produced, cyanobacteria-derived DOM must be attempted with great care, given that DOM from natural waters are more complex mixtures and have undergone considerable processing (due to multiple sources and extensive history of degradation) than the unprocessed DOM from M. aeruginosa presented in this study. In fact, FTICR-MS data showed that M. aeruginosaderived DOM has a remarkably different molecular signature when compared to DOM from natural waters (Table 2). Typically, peaks $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ are described as terrestrially- or vascular plant-derived humic material and considered biologically more recalcitrant than peak T (Fellman et al. 2010). Yet, in this study peak $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ was undoubtedly a product of $M .$ . aeruginosa metabolism that accumulated prior to the initiation of the bioassays. With such distinct origins and histories of degradation, it is likely that the biolability of peak $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ found in this study differs from that of peaks $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ from terrestrially influenced waters. Patterns of DOM and FDOM reactivity are unlikely universal across distinct aquatic systems and DOM sources, and much still remains to be learned regarding chemical composition, photoavailability and bioavailability of distinct fractions of DOM (Jaff-e et al. 2014). Recent efforts have been made to resolve molecular characteristics of FDOM across ecosystems (e.g., Stubbins et al. (2014)), and have revealed that FDOM components are extremely diverse in their chemical features as well as their degree of lability, spanning from labile to recalcitrant (Sleighter et al. 2014).

In this study, E-DOM had less relative abundance of $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { N }$ and peptide-like formulae relative to total assigned formulae and a lower relative fluorescence intensity of peak T (relative to total FDOM) when compared to I-DOM. Conversely, it was detected a relative increase in peptide-like formulae due to biodegradation in all bioassays, while the peak T fluorescence did not increase in most bioassays. The relationship between FDOM, FTICR-MS formulae, and concentration of N-rich DOM moieties is far from being straightforward. As discussed above, fluorescent molecules represent only an unknown fraction of the total C and N within DOM. Similarly, ESI-FTICR-MS has its own analytical window and does not account for nonionizable compounds or for shifts in competition for change in the ionization source. None of these techniques provide quantitative data, thus, it is not surprising that some discrepancies arise compared to the monocular view provided by either technique in isolation. Photodegradation can reduce fluorescence signals of DOM moieties (such as peak T), but that does not mean that the molecules comprising these moieties have undergone complete photo-mineralization or photooxidation (transformation of DOM into CO or $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ ) (Mopper et al. 2015). In a recent study, the molecular (FTICR-MS) signatures associated with classical PARAFAC components, including the B and T peaks were described (Stubbins et al. 2014). Of the 244 molecular formulae assigned to the protein-like peaks B and T, only $3 1 \%$ contained N. These relatively low N levels contrast with previous assumptions that peaks B and T are the most N-rich FDOM components. Use of solid phase extraction and negative mode ESI in Stubbins et al. (2014) may have discriminated against N-rich compounds. However, B- and T-associated formulae were of lower N-content than the formulae associated with peaks M and N (marine/microbial humics), and $\mathrm { B } / \mathrm { T }$ molecular formulae were not enriched in N compared to bulk DOM detectable via solid phase extraction and ESI FTICR-MS. Although the compounds responsible for peak $\mathrm { B } / \mathrm { T }$ fluorescence require further elucidation, the formulae correlated with peak $\mathrm { B } / \mathrm { T }$ had a distinct aliphatic-enriched molecular signature (Stubbins et al. 2014), which is in agreement with our current data for E-DOM and I-DOM, as both have high levels of protein-like fluorescence and aliphatic molecular formulae compared to DOM samples from rivers and the oceans. Aliphatics of course do not fluoresce, yet that does not mean they cannot track a fluorescent signature in natural waters or in the current experiments.

Both E-DOM and I-DOM had very strong aliphatic signatures, before and after photodegradation and biodegradation (Table 2; Figs. 2, 3), but significant shifts regarding molecular mass in E-DOM and I-DOM caused by UV and biological activity were detected. In E-DOM, the photolabile formulae had significantly greater molecular mass than the photoresistant formulae, indicating that photodegradation preferentially removed larger molecules reducing the overall size of the photodegraded DOM (Stubbins et al. 2010). E-DOM photoproducts were not significantly different than the photolabile group regarding molecular mass $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ , suggesting that some compounds could have undergone small photochemical alterations, such as removal of side chains, hydroxyl, carbonyl or alkyl groups, while others may have been completely photo-oxidized, as indicated by the loss of DOC. For I-DOM, photolabile, photoresistant, and photoproducts were not significantly distinct $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ , indicating that photoirradiation did not strongly alter molecular mass in I-DOM. In summation, regarding chemical composition and mass, E-DOM underwent more pronounced alterations than I-DOM, which is in agreement with a greater photoreactivity of E-DOM relative to I-DOM revealed by optical analyses.

The biolabile pool in Bio E-DOM was not significantly smaller than the bioresistant pool $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ , while bioproducts were significantly greater than biolabile and bioresistant pools $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ , suggesting that bacteria can produce high molecular mass material as a by-product of DOM degradation, particularly peptide-like molecules (Fig. 2B). In I-DOM, bacteria preferentially removed larger compounds $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ leaving a smaller, bioresistant pool, and produced significantly smaller molecules $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ ), potentially by breaking compounds down into smaller units. These opposite trends in biodegradation between Bio E-DOM and Bio I-DOM regarding molecular mass reflect the initial differences between these two DOM pools, with Initial I-DOM constituted of significantly larger molecular mass formulae than Initial E-DOM to start with $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ , and with the greater biolability of I-DOM relative to E-DOM, revealed by bulk and optical DOM analyses, and bacterial productivity rates. For Bio I-DOM, biodegradation resulted in an increase in aliphatics and a decrease in moderately unsaturated compounds, neither of which was observed in Bio E-DOM (Fig. 2). In other studies, the formulae comprising this cluster of moderately unsaturated structures can represent multiple, biogeochemically distinct isomers, including nonchromophoric, steroid-like, carboxylic-rich alicyclic molecules (CRAM) (Hertkorn et al. 2006) or chromophoric, terrigenous, photolabile aromatics, such as lignin and other phenols (Sleighter and Hatcher 2008; Dittmar and Stubbins 2014), all of which are relatively biorefractory in natural waters (Hertkorn et al. 2006; Dittmar and Stubbins 2014). Thus, the identification of biolabile cluster of compounds with these elemental features in this study is unprecedented. In this study, these formulae contain both N and P, and are abundant in Initial I-DOM but absent in Initial E-DOM, indicating that they could represent subunits and/or fragments of functional cellular components, such as nucleic acids or pigments. Nucleotides (i.e., the building blocks of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and ribonucleic acid (RNA)), chlorophyll and phycocyanin (a cyanobacterial light harvesting accessory pigment) contain heterocyclic rings (i.e., both C and N in the ring structure) and $\mathrm { H } / \mathrm { C }$ ratio ranging from 1 to 1.3, consistent with the molecular features of the cluster of moderately unsaturated formulae abundant in I-DOM.

# Competition between photodegradation and biodegradation

The decrease in bioavailability following UV irradiation of labile phytoplankton DOM has been demonstrated in a number of previous studies (Tranvik and Kokalj 1998; Tranvik et al. 2000; Tranvik and Bertilsson 2001) and attributed to photoproduction of inhibitory substances (Haverstock et al. 2012), removal of biolabile DOM (BDOM) through photolysis, and/or photo transformation of BDOM into more biorecalcitrant DOM through condensation, cross-linking reactions and photohumification (Kieber et al. 1997). In this study, reduced bioavailability of UV preirradiated M. aeruginosa-DOM was detected in the sequential experiment $\mathrm { U V } {  } \mathrm { B i o } _ { i }$ , as shown by longer DOC half-lives and lower productivity rates in $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio DOM than in Bio DOM, particularly for E-DOM (Fig. 4). The patterns that arose from our CDOM and FTICR-MS data indicate that competition between photochemical and biological pathways of degradation was the mechanism responsible for the decrease in DOM bioavailability, and that the overlap between photochemical and biological removal of BDOM was greater in EDOM than in I-DOM (Figs. 1, 2, 7). The “competition pool” is defined in this study as the material (CDOM or FTICR-MS formulae) common to photolabile and the biolabile pools, i.e., compounds concurrently susceptible to photochemical and biological removal (Fig. 7).

The competition pool of molecular formulae accounted for $1 5 \%$ and $1 1 \%$ of the total number of assigned formulae, $2 9 \%$ and $2 1 \%$ of all biolabile formulae, and $5 0 \%$ and $1 8 \%$ of biolabile $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { N }$ formulae in E-DOM and I-DOM, respectively (Fig. 7). This suggests that the number of biolabile compounds, particularly $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { N }$ compounds, remaining after UV-irradiation in the sequential $( \mathrm { U V } \to \mathrm { B i o } )$ ) experiment was smaller in E-DOM than in IDOM, in agreement with the findings from the optical data. In summation, CDOM and FTICR-MS analyses indicate that photochemical and biological processes compete to remove a chromophoric and N-containing fraction of M. aeruginosaderived DOM. The competition is more pronounced in EDOM than in I-DOM, perhaps because E-DOM is more photoreactive and less bioreactive than I-DOM to start with. In concert, these results are in agreement with previous studies showing that reduced bioavailability results from the photolysis of freshly produced phytoplankton-derived material (Benner and Biddanda 1998; Obernosterer and Benner 2004).

# Broader significance

The relative losses of DOC by photooxidation in E-DOM $( 1 7 \% )$ and I-DOM $( 4 \% )$ were within the range of or slightly lower than those previously determined for riverine water $( 2 0 \%$ , Congo River, (Spencer et al. 2009); $1 6 \%$ , Satilla River, (Moran et al. 2000)) and deep ocean water $( 7 \% )$ (Stubbins et al. 2012)), in solar simulator experiments of similar timeframes. However, accurate comparisons of photochemical DOC loss between experiments should take into account the total absorbed photon dose, which varies depending on a number of factors, such as solar simulator settings, irradiation pathlengths, and the optical thickness of the sample. These caveats should be kept in mind when comparing DOC loss and they prevent direct quantitative comparison of results here. Qualitative trends revealed that photodegradation of CDOM, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 ) }$ , and FDOM were greater than DOC losses (Table 1), which is a pattern consistent with many other studies (Spencer et al. 2009; Mopper et al. 2015).

Both E-DOM and I-DOM were highly biolabile compared to typical freshwater and open ocean DOM (Guillemette and Del Giorgio 2011). This high biolability was accompanied by dominance of peak T (Table 1) and the high contents of $\mathrm { C H O + N }$ , aliphatic, and peptide-like compounds in M. aeruginosa E-DOM and I-DOM (Table 2), relative to natural DOM. These observations are consistent with previous studies that suggest that the peak T fluorescence signature is indicative of freshly produced, highly biolabile organics, including aliphatics and peptide-like DOM (Guillemette and Del Giorgio 2011; Stubbins et al. 2014) and that bioavailability is closely related to a high ratio of aliphatics to aromatics (Sun et al. 1997). The synchronous removal of peak T, Nrich moieties, and DOC demonstrated that DOM from M. aeruginosa is comprised of N and C pools that were readily available to fuel bacterial productivity and/or respiration. At the same time, the photochemical removal of CDOM and the subsequent decrease in DOC bioavailability and bacterial productivity in $\mathrm { U V } \to$ Bio bioassays suggest that a fraction of the photodegradable CDOM pool is biolabile on the timeframes of the current incubations. The high bioavailability of E-DOM and I-DOM in this study may also have been related to the balanced nutrient ratios of the medium $\mathrm { ( N / P \sim }$ 12.5) in which M. aeruginosa was grown. It is not clear if the biolability of DOM from M. aeruginosa would be as high under nutrient levels of natural waters, as evidence exists that DOM released by phytoplankton growing under N- or P-limitation is less biolabile than that released under nutrient-replete conditions (Obernosterer and Herndl 1995).

Previous studies have shown the importance of phytoplankton as a source of chromophoric and fluorescent DOM in the environment (Romera-Castillo et al. 2010), including cyanobacteria (Steinberg et al. 2004). In this study, the use of axenic cultures of M. aeruginosa allowed the unequivocal identification of CDOM and FDOM as M. aeruginosa metabolites. Net microbial consumption (Fig. 6A,B,D, black dots) and production (Fig. 6A,B,C,F, white dots) of FDOM was observed in this study in Bio DOM and $\mathrm { U V } $ Bio DOM bioassays, respectively. As distinct patterns of regulation, e.g., associated with DOM composition and bacterial activity, result in simultaneous removal and production of FDOM by bacteria, the intensities of FDOM components reflect the net balance between heterotrophic consumption and production of FDOM (Guillemette and Del Giorgio 2012). Heterotrophic production of FDOM, particularly peak M (Fig. 6C,F, white dots), from nonfluorescent precursors in photobleached EDOM and I-DOM was also observed in this study. Similarly, bacterial production of FDOM from glucose (Kramer and Herndl 2004) and the amino sugars glucosamine, galactosamine, and mannosamine (Biers et al. 2007) has been previously reported. These are common and abundant organic constituents in phytoplankton (Benner and Kaiser 2003) and both glucose and glucosamine have been identified in the strain of M. aeruginosa used in this study (Gouvea et al. 2005), suggesting that these and other nonfluorescent compounds could have given rise to FDOM in the bioassays. Nonetheless, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ of both E-DOM and I-DOM were at the low end of the range of $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values for natural waters $( 0 . 5 \mathrm { - } 7 . 5 \ \mathrm { \ m g C \ L ^ { - 1 } \ m ^ { - 1 } } )$ ) (Weishaar et al. 2003; Jaff-e et al. 2008), and close to those for open ocean DOM (0.56- $1 . 0 1 \ \mathrm { \ m g { C } \ L ^ { - 1 } \ m ^ { - 1 } }$ (Helms et al. 2013)). Therefore, while phytoplankton are a potential source of chromophoric and fluorescent material, DOM from $M .$ . aeruginosa is apparently depleted in aromatic, chromophoric carbon relative to terrigenous DOM.

The similarity of $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ between E-DOM $( 0 . 0 1 \ \mathrm { \ n m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) and riverine and estuarine waters dominated by terrestrial inputs $( 0 . 0 1 { - } 0 . 0 2 \ \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 } )$ (Helms et al. 2008; Spencer et al. 2009; Fichot and Benner 2012) and the presence of a fluorescent peak $\mathrm { C } + \mathrm { A }$ in the same EEM region as that of terrestrially derived fluorescent humics, show that M. aeruginosa is a potential source of material of a spectral quality similar to DOM from other sources, contributing to the complexity of determining DOC source based on optical signatures within lakes, nearshore, and estuarine waters (Jaff-e et al. 2008). Significantly, the fewer FTICR-MS formulae and higher relative content of $\mathrm { C H O } + \mathrm { N }$ and aliphatics compared to DOM from natural waters (Sleighter and Hatcher 2008; Stubbins et al. 2010), indicate that M. aeruginosa DOM is less diverse than DOM from rivers and the ocean. These results are, therefore, consistent with the understanding that multiple sources and transformation processes tend to increase the diversity and complexity of the DOM mixture in natural waters (Dittmar and Stubbins 2014).

Regardless of the differences in growth conditions and processes of DOM release and cell lysis between naturalgrowing and laboratory-cultured M. aeruginosa, this study provides novel information on molecular-level composition of unaltered cyanobacteria DOM. For the most part, the results presented here showed good agreement between optical and chemical properties of E-DOM and I-DOM. This study also provided novel mechanistic information on photoreactivity and bioreactivity of DOM freshly produced by cyanobacteria. In the environment, photodegradation and biodegradation occur simultaneously, but to attribute optical and chemical shifts to one pathway or the other, the isolation of these processes under laboratory-controlled conditions is critical. The results presented in this study represent a $1 ^ { \mathrm { s t } }$ step toward identifying, tracking, and resolving the fate of DOM produced by M. aeruginosa, including during cyanobacterial harmful algal blooms (CyanoHAB) events dominated by this species.
