Systematic water sampling for characterization of chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM) in the coastal South Atlantic Bight, was conducted as part of the long term Coastal Ocean Research and Monitoring Program (CORMP). Water samples were collected during a 3.5 year period, from October 2001 until March 2005, in the vicinity of the Cape Fear River (CFR) outlet and in adjacent Onslow Bay (OB). During this study there were two divergent hydrological and meteorological conditions in the CFR drainage area: a severe drought in 2002, followed by the very wet year of 2003. CDOM was characterized optically by the absorption coefficient at $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { \ n m }$ , the spectral slope coefficient (S), and by Excitation Emission Matrix (EEM) fluorescence. Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC) was used to assess CDOM composition from EEM spectra and six components were identified: three terrestrial humic-like components, one marine humic-like component and two protein-like components. Terrestrial humic-like components contributed most to dissolved organic matter (DOM) fluorescence in the low salinity plume of the CFR. The contribution of terrestrial humic-like components to DOM fluorescence in OB was much smaller than in the CFR plume area. Protein-like components contributed significantly to DOM fluorescence in the coastal ocean of OB and they dominated DOM fluorescence in the Gulf Stream waters. Hydrological conditions during the observation period significantly impacted both concentration and composition of CDOM found in the estuary and coastal ocean. In the CFR plume, there was an order of magnitude difference in CDOM absorption and fluorescence intensity between samples collected during the drought compared to the wet period. During the drought, CDOM in the CFR plume was composed of equal proportions of terrestrial humic-like components (ca. $6 0 \%$ of the total fluorescence intensity) with a significant contribution of proteinaceous substances (ca. $2 0 \%$ of the total fluorescence). During high river flow, CDOM was composed mostly of humic substances (nearly $7 5 \%$ of total fluorescence) with minor contributions by proteinaceous substances. The impact of changes in fresh water discharge patterns on CDOM concentration and composition was also observed in OB, though to a lesser degree.

# 1. Introduction

Dissolved organic matter, DOM, is by far the largest pool of organic matter in the sea. About $9 7 \%$ of all organic carbon in the sea is incorporated in DOM with an estimated 685 Giga tons as dissolved organic carbon (DOC) (Hansell and Carlson, 2001). The mass of DOC in the sea is comparable with the mass of the carbon in the Earth's atmosphere, as ${ \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ , and the amount of carbon in terrestrial ecosystems (Hedges, 2002). An understanding of the mechanisms and processes regulating the amount of DOM in the sea is critical to better constrain the global carbon cycle. Therefore research on marine DOM has intensified over the last 30 years. The dominant source of organic matter in the world's ocean is autochthonous production, accounting for more than $9 5 \%$ of the total organic matter. The input of terrestrial DOM represents only $2 - 3 \%$ of the total oceanic DOM pool, although it may be a dominant source of DOM in coastal zones (Opsahl and Benner, 1997). In the past, DOM has been regarded as a large inert reservoir of carbon in the ocean, which below the oceans' mixed layer is excluded from the current carbon cycle. Results of recent studies have revealed that DOM is an active and dynamic component in carbon biogeochemical cycles and plays important roles in marine ecosystems (e.g. Mopper et al., 1991).

The optically active fraction of DOM, chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM), is one of the major determinants of the optical properties of natural waters, directly affecting both availability and spectral quality of light in the water column (Jerlov, 1976; Blough and Del Vecchio, 2002; Hargreaves, 2003). Through its effects on the attenuation of light in the water column, CDOM may stimulate or hinder primary production, temperature stratification and exposure of marine organisms to harmful UV radiation (e.g. Mopper and Kieber, 2002). Photochemical reactions of CDOM produce inorganic carbon, low-molecular-weight organic compounds, trace gases, and phosphorus- and nitrogen-rich compounds (e.g. Vähätalo and Zepp, 2005; Stedmon et al., 2007). CDOM has the ability to complex with trace metals, which are later released to the marine environment during DOM remineralization. Therefore, the ability to differentiate and quantify sources of CDOM in the oceans and the factors underlying its variability is fundamentally important to understand biogeochemical cycles in the oceans.

DOM consists of a complex mixture of organic compounds resulting from the breakdown of bacteria, algae and/or higher plants and their continuous reworking by photochemical and microbial processes. Because of their complexity most organic compounds dissolved in the ocean $( > 8 5 \% )$ have not been characterized (Benner, 2002). The compounds that have been identified, are mainly low or medium molecular weight organic molecules: hydrocarbons, carbohydrates, fatty acids, and amino acids (Bronk 2002; Benner 2002). There is insufficient knowledge about the remaining fractions of organic matter, which consist mainly of medium and high molecular weight compounds: e.g. proteins, lipids and their polymers and complexes with phenols and metals. There are two major fractions of humic substances present in aquatic environments: humic and fulvic acids. Humic and fulvic acids differ from each other by molecular weight, chemical composition, chemical properties, aromaticity and optical properties (Harvey et al., 1983; Carder et al., 1989). Components of DOM, especially humic substances, absorb light causing water to appear yellowish. For that reason, this colored fraction of DOM was called ‘Gelbstoff’ or yellow substances (Kalle, 1966) in the past, but is now commonly referred to as CDOM.

The optical properties of CDOM in natural waters have been studied for many decades by researchers of ocean color remotesensing and aquatic optics (see review by Blough and Del Vecchio, 2002 and references therein). Optical properties of CDOM enable the application of remote sensing methods to study its distribution and dynamics on global and regional scales (Siegel et al., 2002, 2005). Many physical, chemical and biological processes influence the distribution and optical properties of CDOM. Among the most important in open marine areas is dilution of terrestrially-derived CDOM, photochemical bleaching, bacterial degradation, and autochthonous production of CDOM by plankton (Whitehead et al., 2000; Blough and Del Vecchio, 2002; Rochelle-Newall and Fisher, 2002; Osburn and Morris, 2003; Zepp, 2003).

A component of CDOM fluoresces (FDOM). This property of DOM has been known for a long time (Duursma, 1974) and it has been used to estimate CDOM levels in marine waters. Numerous investigators have observed linear relationships between fluorescence and absorption (Ferrari and Tassan, 1991; Hoge et al., 1993; Vodacek et al., 1997; Ferrari and Dowell, 1998; Ferrari, 2000). Recent advances in fluorescence spectroscopy have resulted in the development of Excitation Emission Matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectroscopy. EEM spectra (EEMs) are obtained by acquiring emission spectra at a series of successively increasing excitation wavelengths. The emission spectra are concatenated to generate a plot in which the fluorescence is displayed as a function of excitation and emission wavelengths. Although slower to collect, EEMs provide a more complete picture of CDOM emission properties and can often be used to discriminate among different classes of fluorophores of terrestrial, marine and anthropogenic origin based on their excitation/emission maxima. It is also possible to use EEMs to track changes in CDOM resulting from biological or physical processing of the material. Coble (1996) was first to successfully apply this method to field data analysis with descriptions of CDOM in the Caribbean, Arabian Sea and Gulf of Mexico (e.g. Coble et al., 1998; Del Castillo et al., 1999). The EEMs of fluorescent DOM from natural waters are composed of various types of overlapping fluorophores, and it may be difficult to assess dynamics of DOM aquatic environment based solely on the EEMs “peak picking” technique (Coble, 1996). Recently, Stedmon et al. (2003) applied a statistical modeling approach called Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC) to decompose EEMs into individual fluorescent components. This new approach provides a considerable advantage over traditional methods in interpreting the multidimensional nature of EEMs data sets. The PARAFAC model has been used to study variability of DOM in coastal areas (Stedmon and Markager, 2005a), to observe effects of production and degradation processes of DOM fluorescence in marine environments (Stedmon and Markager, 2005b), and to trace anthropogenic pollutants in oceanic DOM (Murphy et al., 2006).

The fluorescent properties of DOM in the South Atlantic Bight have been studied using EEMs by the traditional “peak picking” technique described by Kowalczuk et al. (2003). The objectives for this study were i) to apply the PARAFAC model to a considerably larger data set and characterize fluorescent properties of DOM in three different environments: the Cape Fear River estuary, the coastal ocean of Onslow Bay and Gulf Stream influenced oceanic waters. ii) to study the dynamics of identified fluorescence components, iii) to describe interannual variability in CDOM absorption, fluorescence and composition, and iv) to relate those changes with contrasting hydrological conditions in the study area during a 3.5 year period of field observations.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. The study area

The South Atlantic Bight, (SAB) as defined herein, is the shoreline and the continental shelf from Cape Hatteras down to Cape Canaveral, and consists of a coastal plain that grades gently into a wide (50– $1 0 0 \ \mathrm { k m } \cdot$ ) shelf. The wide shelf is bordered on its east by the Gulf Stream. Much of the shoreline consists of barrier islands separated by shifting inlets and backed by riverine estuaries, sounds, lagoons, salt marshes and the Intracoastal Waterway. Riverine input is sporadic. Some areas like southern Onslow Bay are sediment-starved with little riverine input, whereas just south of this area, North Carolina's largest and most industrialized riverine system, the Cape Fear River watershed, drains into the coastal ocean off North and South Carolina (Mallin et al., 2000). The whole region is subject to frequent flooding, numerous catastrophic storms and chronic erosion problems. Summaries of a number of previous studies of physical, chemical and biological properties of the continental shelf of the South Atlantic Bight have been given by Atkinson et al. (1985).

Due to the social and economic importance of the Carolinas' coastal region, environmental monitoring is increasingly important in the SAB. The University of North Carolina Wilmington has begun an inter-disciplinary, long-term Coastal Ocean Research and Monitoring Program (CORMP) to understand the physical, biological, chemical and geological aspects of this region, as well as impacts of natural and anthropogenic changes on coastal–ocean resources and their management. One of the objectives within CORMP is the optical characterization of the waters of Onslow Bay, the Cape Fear River Plume and coastal southeastern North Carolina. Changes in the optical properties of water masses may be used to compare physicochemical processes within, and exchange between, the coastal system of Onslow Bay and the adjacent estuarine system of the Cape Fear River plume. Coastal and estuarine waters differ greatly in their inherent and apparent optical properties. In Onslow Bay, clear waters, low in particulate matter and phytoplankton pigment concentrations, reflect the optical characteristics of Case 1 waters, and may be found a few km offshore. In areas influenced by riverine inflow, water optical properties are characterized by high CDOM and sediment input as well as enhanced phytoplankton growth. These waters may be classified optically as Case 2 waters (Kowalczuk et al., 2006b). The Cape Fear River serves as a point source of water into the eastern SAB and the river end members of the study region are rich in CDOM (Kowalczuk et al., 2003). Reports on optical properties in the SAB are scarce (Nelson and Guarda, 1995) and the experimental data presented here should provide a valuable addition.

Sampling was undertaken between October 2001 and March 2005. River-plume samples were collected from a sample grid developed for CORMP (see Fig. 1). The grid was designed to study the spatial and temporal extent of the Cape Fear River plume and consists of 7 stations (initially labeled CFP1 through CFP7) located at the Cape Fear River mouth and adjacent inner continental shelf. The sampling grid was changed in September 2003. Stations CFP3 and CFP4 were omitted, but two additional sampling stations CFP8 and CFP9, located west of the previous sampling grid, were added. Onslow Bay samples were collected along a transect consisting of 14 stations (OB) starting at the sea buoy of Masonboro Inlet (station OBSB) and extending southeast across the continental shelf to its break at station OB63. The Cape Fear River plume area was sampled monthly (depending on weather) at ebb tide, for a maximum extent of the plume. The Onslow Bay transect was sampled monthly in the 2001, and every second month from April 2002. Surface water samples were collected at all stations in the plume grid and at selected stations across Onslow Bay. Initially, water samples were collected at stations OBSB, OB15, OB27, OB42, OB57, OB63. After preliminary data analysis of the Onslow Bay data, the sampling strategy was changed in April 2002, and samples were subsequently collected at every station from OBSB to OB27 to achieve better resolution of the near-shore spatial variability. Optical measurements and sample collection for inherent optical properties (IOP) in Onslow Bay ended in November 2003. All field operations were performed using small vessels: $\mathsf { R } / \mathsf { V }$ Sturgeon $( 9 \mathrm { m } )$ for the Cape Fear River plume and $\mathsf { R } / \mathsf { V }$ Cape Fear $( 2 1 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ for Onslow Bay.

During the period of field observations, the hydrological conditions in the Cape Fear River watershed changed dramatically. The spring and summer of 2002 was a period of severe drought in the Cape Fear watershed, which significantly limited riverine discharge into the coastal ocean (minimum flow was recorded between June and August $2 0 0 2 \mathrm { ~ - ~ } 2 7 . 9$ to $1 8 . 6 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ ). Precipitation increased in fall 2002 reaching a maximum in spring 2003 when the river discharge increased nearly 10 fold in April 2003 $( 2 0 2 3 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \ s ^ { - 1 }$ ). In August and

September 2003, high rainfall and the passage of Hurricane Isabel caused increased CFR discharge, ranging from approximately 300– $1 1 0 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } s ^ { - 1 }$ .

# 2.2. The optical properties of CDOM

Water samples for the determination of optical properties of CDOM were obtained using a pre-rinsed bucket, stored in HDPE bottles in ice coolers and processed in the laboratory the following day. HDPE bottles were flushed with a high volume of DI water, and then rinsed several times with the marine sample water from each site, prior to sample storage. Surface salinity was measured with a YSI handheld CT meter or a Seabird CTD.

Samples for spectroscopic analyses were first warmed to room temperature and filtered through ${ \sim } 0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ GF/F filters to remove large particles and phytoplankton cells then filtered again through $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , acid-washed and Milli-Q rinsed Gelman Supor $\textsuperscript { \textregistered }$ polysulfone filters. Absorbance scans from 240 to $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { \ n m }$ , $1 ~ \mathrm { \ n m }$ slit width, were undertaken using $1 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ Suprasil cuvettes and a Cary 1E dual-beam spectrophotometer connected to a micro-computer. Pre-filtered ${ \mathrm { ( } } 0 . 2 \ { \mu \mathrm { m } } { \mathrm { ) } }$ Milli-Q water was used in the reference cell. Absorbance measurements at each wavelength $( \lambda )$ were baseline corrected by subtracting the mean absorbance calculated in the spectral range from 650 to $7 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . The required corrections were generally less than 0.002. CDOM absorption coefficients $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( \lambda ) , \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ at each wavelength $( \lambda )$ were calculated according to Kirk (1994):

$$
a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( \lambda ) = 2 . 3 0 3 A ( \lambda ) / l
$$

where, $A ( \lambda )$ is the corrected spectrophotometer absorbance reading at wavelength $\lambda$ and $l$ is the optical path length in meters. A conservative detection limit of $0 . 0 4 6 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , corresponding to 0.002 absorbance with $1 0 \mathrm { c m }$ cells on the spectrophotometer, was estimated from repeated scans of Milli-Q water processed as a sample. Following the recommendations of recent studies (see: Stedmon et al., 2000; Blough and Del Vecchio, 2002), the spectral slope of the absorption spectrum S was calculated by applying a simple exponential model to fit the offset-corrected absorption spectrum at a spectral range from $3 0 0 { - } 5 0 0 \ \mathrm { ~ n m }$ . This method of spectral slope calculation is less dependent on the spectral range used. The spectral range was reduced $( 3 0 0 - 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m } )$ ) for the clearest samples collected at the shelf break, because of the very low absorption at longer wavelengths.

Samples for fluorescence were treated in the same manner as those for absorbance measurements. Highly-absorbing samples were diluted with Milli-Q water to the point where A(350) ( $1 \mathrm { \ c m }$ path length) was ${ \le } 0 . 0 2$ to minimize inner-filtering effects. Excitationemission matrix fluorescence properties were determined on a Jobin Yvon SPEX FluoroMax-3 scanning fluorometer equipped with a $1 5 0 \mathrm { W }$ Xe arc lamp and a R928P detector. EEMs were constructed using excitation wavelengths from 250 to $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ( $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals) and scanning emission wavelengths from 280 to $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (5 nm intervals). The instrument was configured to collect the signal in ratio mode with dark offset using a $5 \mathrm { n m }$ slit width on both the excitation and emission monochromators. Scans were corrected for instrument configuration using factory-supplied correction factors, which were determined essentially as described in Method 1 of Coble et al. (1993). Post processing of scans was performed using the FLToolbox 1.91 developed by Wade Sheldon (University of Georgia) for MATLAB $\textsuperscript { \textregistered }$ (Release 11) (Zepp et al., 2004). This software eliminates Rayleigh and Raman scattering peaks by excising portions $\ l \pm 1 0 { - } 1 5 \ \mathrm { n m } \ \mathrm { F W } ,$ ) of each scan centered on the respective scatter peak. The excised data were replaced using three-dimensional interpolation of the remaining data according to the Delaunay triangulation method and constraining the interpolation such that all non-excised data were retained. Following removal of scatter peaks, data were normalized to a daily-determined water Raman intensity (275ex/303em, $5 ~ \textrm { n m }$ band pass) and converted to Raman-normalized quinine sulfate equivalents (QSE) in ppb (Coble et al., 1998). For samples that required dilution, the scatter-corrected fluorescence of the diluent Milli-Q water was subtracted and the resultant fluorescence values were multiplied by the dilution factor to obtain the intensity for the original, undiluted sample. Replicate scans were generally within $5 \%$ agreement in terms of intensity and within slit width resolution in terms of peak location. The post-processing software did not adequately correct for secondorder Raman scattering. The tail of second-order Raman scattering was visible at low signal samples. That error was corrected by subtracting the EEM spectrum of the blank sample of freshly prepared Milli-Q water, measured separately for each data series.

# 2.3. The PARAFAC modeling

Initially, PARAFAC modeling was performed for two separate data batches. EEMs were combined into two 3-dimensional data arrays: (117 samples $\times 6 3$ excitation $\times 8 1$ emissions) and (291 samples $\times 5 1$ excitation $\times 8 1$ emissions) and then each subset was analyzed by PARAFAC separately according to the technique described by Bro (1997) and Stedmon et al. (2003). PARAFAC separates the data signal into a set of three linear terms and a residual array.

$$
x _ { i j k } = \sum _ { f = 1 } ^ { F } a _ { i f } b _ { j f } c _ { k f } + e _ { i j k } i = 1 . . . K j = 1 . . . J k = 1 . . . { K }
$$

In this application, $x _ { i j k }$ is the intensity of fluorescence for the ith sample at emission wavelength $j$ and excitation wavelength $k$ ; $a _ { i f }$ is directly proportional to the concentration (e.g., $\mathrm { { m M } ~ C }$ of the fth analyte in the ith sample. $b _ { j f }$ is linearly related to the fluorescence quantum efficiency (fraction of absorbed energy emitted as fluorescence) of the fth analyte at emission wavelength $j$ . Likewise, $c _ { k f }$ is linearly proportional to the specific absorption coefficient at excitation wavelength k. F defines the number of components in the model, and the residual matrix $e _ { i j k }$ represents variability not explained by the model. The model is calculated by minimizing the sum of squared residuals with an alternating least squares algorithm. With this technique, signals from a complex mixture of compounds (in this case, fluorescent DOM) can be separated, with no assumptions on their spectral shape or their number. The only assumptions in the PARAFAC algorithm are that the components differ from each other spectrally and do not have negative concentrations or spectra.

The analysis was carried out in MATLAB with the “Nway toolbox for MATLAB” (Andersson and Bro, 2000). Results of initial modeling revealed that, excitation and emission maxima of components derived for samples grouped in the second spectral format batch were red shifted relative to spectral characteristics of respective components derived for the samples grouped in the first batch. This effect could be explained by a much bigger proportion of high fluorescence intensity and low salinity samples present in the second batch of samples. Therefore, final PARAFAC modeling was performed on the combined data set that contained samples with matching excitation and emission spectra. Final dimensions of the data array were: 408 samples $\times 1 3$ excitations $\times 8 1$ emissions. Additionally, due to wide variability in fluorescence intensity range, data were weighted in a semi-logarithmic fashion: individual matrices were divided by 20, 10, or 5 according to intensity maximum over 120, 60, and 30 QSE, respectively. Samples with a maximum intensity lower than 30 QSE were not weighted. To define and validate the PARAFAC model, splithalf validation was applied according to Stedmon et al. (2003). The data array was divided into two halves and modeled separately. PARAFAC models ranging from 2 to 8 components were then derived for both data sets independently. Validation of the model was carried out by comparing the spectral shape of the components derived by the models.

The PARAFAC model returns only relative intensities of derived components (scores), because the specific absorption and quantum yield of fluorescence of the components remains unknown. The intensity of the nth component in a given sample, $I _ { n } ,$ was calculated as the fluorescence intensity at the peak excitation and emission maximum of the nth component using the following equation:

$$
I _ { n } = 5 \mathrm { c o r e } _ { n } { ^ * \mathrm { E x } _ { n } } ( \lambda _ { m a x } ) ^ { * } \mathrm { E m } _ { n } ( \lambda _ { m a x } )
$$

where: ${ \mathsf { S c o r e } } _ { n }$ is the relative intensity of the nth component, $\operatorname { E x } _ { n }$ $\left( \lambda _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } \right)$ is the maximum excitation loading of the nth component, $\operatorname { E m } _ { n }$ $\left( \lambda _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } \right)$ is the maximum emission loading of the nth component derived from the model. The total fluorescence intensity of a given sample was calculated as the sum of the components present in the sample:

$$
I _ { \mathrm { T O T } } = \sum _ { 1 } ^ { n } \ I _ { n }
$$

# 3. Results

# 3.1. PARAFAC model results

The combined data set was split into two random halves called “calibration” (CAL) and “validation” (VAL) to perform the split-half validation procedure. PARAFAC models were run for each CAL and VAL data group. The 6-component model was successfully validated in 3 independent data sets to ensure correctness of the modeled CDOM components. Individual components are shown on Fig. 3 as contour plots. Table 1 contains excitation and emission characteristics of the CDOM components we identified and examples of matching components identified by other researchers, who have modeled CDOM EEMs in various marine, oceanic and estuarine environments around the world. The PARAFAC model identified 3 humic-like substances of mainly terrestrial origin, two major ones: component 1 (C1), and component 2 (C2), and one secondary humic-like — component 4 (C4). Terrestrially-derived components C1 and C2 are common in various estuarine, marine and oceanic environments. Component 3 (C3) has been identified as representing marine humic substances and also as dissolved organic matter that has been altered by microbial reprocessing (Stedmon and Markager, 2005b). Component C4 represents fluorophores that have the longest excitation wavelength and broadest excitation band as well as the longest emission wavelength associated with a broad emission band. Such excitation and emission characteristics are associated with terrestrial organic matter that is composed of high molecular weight and aromatic organic compounds (McKnight et al., 2001; Stedmon et al., 2003). There were two components that have excitation/emission characteristics similar to fluorescent protein-like compounds and presumably a combination of fluorophores containing fluorescent proteins tryptophan and tyrosine: free amino acids or amino acids bound in protein molecular structures. Component 5 (C5) has excitation/emission characteristics close to tyrosine and component 6 (C6) has excitation/ emission characteristics close to tryptophan. Those components represent dissolved organic matter that contains a fraction of autochthonous DOM. The excitation/emission characteristics of both the tyrosine and tryptophan components are red shifted relative to the respective spectral characteristics of pure compounds. That may reflect the fact that both protein-like fluorescence components exhibit spectral properties of tyrosine and tryptophan bound into larger structures of organic molecules rather than pure compounds diluted in the seawater. Tryptophan and tyrosine bound together in the same protein can also undergo energy transfer with complex effects on the fluorescence (Moens et al., 2004). Another reason for decreased precision in estimation of excitation/emission characteristics of fluorescent protein-like compounds by the PARAFAC model may be associated with the low resolution at excitation in the common format batch of samples. There were only four matching excitation lines 250, 270, 290, and $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in the UVC region instead of 12 or 15 recorded in the original EEMs, where protein-like fluorophores are excited most.

The spectral characteristics of each of the identified components are shown as a function of excitation and emission wavelength and relative fluorescence in Raman units, and positioned next to the corresponding contour plot in Fig. 3. These spectra represent the results of the split-half validation procedure. Excitation spectra of the respective components are shown in the left part of the graph and the emission spectra of the modeled components are shown in the right. The relative fluorescence intensity of excitation and emission loadings is given in Raman units. The split-half validation had very good agreement between excitation and emission loading modeled for the “calibration” and “validation” part of the combined data set and the whole data set. There are some secondary emission peaks of very low intensity at wavelengths longer than 500. Those peaks could be associated with instrument artifacts and insufficient elimination of secondary Raman and Raleigh scatter in the EEMs during post processing. Murphy et al. (2008) observed similar effects for samples measured using a Jobin Yvon SPEX FluoroMax-3 scanning fluorometer.

Parallel Factor Analysis of fluorescence spectra revealed that the six identified fluorescence components occur in different proportions in different water masses. The fluorescence intensity of individual components identified in this study by a PARAFAC model, as well as their relative contribution to the total intensity were used to characterize CDOM composition in different water masses in the study area. In the Cape Fear River, the DOM fluorescence properties are presented at two sampling stations: CFP1, at the river mouth and CFP7, the most oceanic-influenced station of the plume sampling grid. Cape Fear River flow, salinity and basic descriptive statistics calculated to quantify variability of CDOM optical properties are given in Table 2 to characterize water masses at the selected sampling stations, and show changes of CDOM optical properties in the context of natural variability in environmental conditions.

In Onslow Bay, which acted as the reference area separated from direct impact of fresh water discharge by the Cape Fear River, we selected two stations for data presentation: the Sea Buoy — OBSB station located just offshore of Masonboro Inlet, connecting the Intracoastal Waterway with the coastal ocean at Wrightsville Beach, NC. The inlet links the coastal ocean with the coastal estuary located behind a barrier island system fringed with salt marshes. The second Onslow Bay station was located 27 miles offshore, OB27, and is influenced by very clear Gulf Stream waters and represents oceanic conditions.

Fig. 4 presents the average composition of CDOM fluorescence in the Cape Fear River estuary and adjacent coastal ocean. Differences in the magnitude and type of fluorescence characteristics of DOM in different water masses are apparent through visual analysis of Fig. 4 and descriptive statistics given in Table 2. The most noticeable differences in fluorescence characteristics can be seen by comparing the Cape Fear River to oceanic DOM. Two effects are apparent on the bar graphs: an overall decrease of fluorescence intensity of all components due to dilution and a gradual relative decrease of the main humic component C1 (note change in $y .$ -axis scales) and a relative increase in dominance of the protein-like component C5, with increased distance from the dominant source of the CDOM — the Cape Fear River. An average CDOM composition pattern of samples collected at station CFP1, closest to Cape Fear River mouth, is as follows: $\scriptstyle \mathbf { C } 1 > \mathbf { C } 3 > \mathbf { C } 2 > \mathbf { C } 4 > \mathbf { C } 5 > \mathbf { C } 6 .$ . In the most seaward plume station, CFP7, an average compositional pattern is: $\scriptstyle \mathbf { C } 1 > \mathbf { C } 3 > \mathbf { C } 2 > \mathbf { C } 5 > \mathbf { C } 4 > \mathbf { C } 6 .$ A subtle change in CDOM fluorescence properties relative to station CFP1 was observed: the relative increase of importance of the proteinlike component C5, which had a higher average intensity than the intensity of C4, the most aromatic and high molecular weight component.

3.2. Inter annual variability of CDOM absorption coefficient, and CDOM fluorescence intensity

Fig. 5 shows the temporal changes in CDOM absorption and fluorescence intensities recorded during the observation period at the selected sampling sites CFP1, CFP7, OBSB and OB27. In the Cape Fear River plume area (Fig. 5 upper panels), $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ and the total fluorescence intensity, ITOT, show an almost immediate response to changes in the magnitude of river flow after precipitation events. The temporal changes of $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ at CFP1 and CFP 7, are very similar to changes in river flow. The greatest decreases in CDOM absorption and fluorescence intensity were observed during drought conditions of the summer of 2002. The peak CDOM absorption and fluorescence intensities were observed during high flow in the spring of 2003. During 2004, absorption levels at both stations followed the pattern of the peaks and troughs of the Cape Fear River flow. There is very strong co-variance between absorption and fluorescence signals in the Cape Fear River plume area. The CDOM absorption coefficient $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ is very well correlated with CDOM fluorescence intensity (e.g. Kowalczuk et al., 2003). The range in temporal variation of $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ values at CFP1 was nearly two orders of magnitude (minimal value during the dry period: $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 ) = 0 . 7 7 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , maximal value during the wet period: $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 ) = 2 1 . 4 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ). The temporal variability of $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ and $I _ { \mathrm { T O T } }$ at CFP7 was much less than two orders of magnitude.

The lower panels of Fig. 5 represent the temporal variability of both optical parameters in Onslow Bay (OBSB and OB27, respectively). Similar trends of increased absorption and fluorescence intensity with increased amounts of fresh water discharge are clearly visible at the OB sites, even though they are not directly linked to the river acting as a point source. Maximum CDOM absorption and fluorescence in Onslow Bay, especially at the station OBSB, is phased off the main flow maximum compared to the CFR plume area, as observed earlier (March 2003). This maximum is synchronized with a local, very high flow peak at the beginning of the wet period (the flow was almost the same as after Hurricane Isabel landfall in September 2003), just before the CFR flow maximum in spring/summer 2003. Sampling in Onslow Bay in 2003 was synchronized with local troughs of the CFR flow, which may suggest that temporal variability in CDOM absorption and fluorescence is not driven by discharge of fresh water to the coastal ocean. The magnitude of temporal changes of optical quantities in Masonboro Inlet (Station OBSB) and in the coastal ocean (Station OB27) is an order of magnitude smaller than in the Cape Fear River plume area. The minimum levels of the CDOM absorption coefficient, $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ occurred during summer 2002 and maximum in spring 2003, which correspond to the peak of the drought and the beginning of the wet period. The seasonal range in $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ and ITOT variability decreased with the distance from the point source. Mixing and dilution of freshwater in the coastal ocean reduced variation in magnitude of $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ and $I _ { \mathrm { T O T } } ,$ which was within one order of magnitude at OBSB and OB27.

Temporal variation of CDOM fluorescence intensity is slightly distorted in the OBSB record. The maximum fluorescence corresponds well with the peak wet period, but an unexpected increase of $I _ { \mathrm { T O T } }$ was observed during the dry period, when $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ was at its minimum. One possible explanation for this departure from the general trend could be a change in quality of organic matter supplied by local creeks and salt marshes that surround Masonboro Inlet, thus causing changes in absorption fluorescence ratios. The salt marsh system may also act as a buffer, smoothing out the variability in precipitation and river flow. Additionally, the relative significance of this local diffuse source of organic matter increases during drought conditions. Tidally-controlled releases of CDOM from fringe salt marshes may be the main source of organic matter in Onslow Bay. This may explain the smallest variability in $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ and fluorescence (total and respective components) intensity relative to river flow, observed at OBSB. An increase in fluorescence intensity during the dry season in areas surrounded by mangrove forest in the Florida coastal Everglades has been reported by Maie et al. (2006).

Variability in CDOM optical properties between sampling sites is driven mostly by input and dilution of terrestrial CDOM with surrounding coastal ocean waters (Kowalczuk et al., 2003). The descriptive statistics presented in Table 2 support the observation that variability at the sampling sites in the plume area is presumably driven by variability of Cape Fear River flow: e.g. values of C.V. (the ratio between standard deviation to average in percent) calculated for $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ and fluorescence (total and respective components) intensity in the plume area did not differ significantly from the C.V. calculated for flow. At station OB27, which is the oceanic end member in our study, variability of the optical parameters was much greater than at OBSB, although smaller than the variability in river flow during the observation period and it was comparable to variability of optical parameters observed in the plume area. This result suggests that at least some of the changes in $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ and fluorescence may be introduced by discharge of terrestrial DOM by the CFR that mixes with Gulf Stream waters downstream in Long Bay and is then transported into the Onslow Bay.

Fluorescence intensities of the components derived by the PARAFAC model exhibited similar variation at all sampling sites except station OBSB, where the magnitude of the C.V. was smallest for all six derived components. In all stations, intensities of the principle humic component, C1, and the tryptophan-like component, C6, showed the highest variation. The least variable component was the tyrosine-like component C5. There were differences in the average decrease for each of the six CDOM components with increased salinity. The largest decrease in intensity was observed for the principle humic-like component, C1 — with a 35-fold decrease, and the smallest decrease of intensity was observed in the tyrosine-like component, C5 — only a 4-fold decrease. The average decrease of the secondary humic-like component, C2, and the tryptophan-like component, C6, was similar, ca. 10-fold. In addition, C3, marine and terrestrial humic-like components, and C4, another secondary humic-like component, decreased similarly with averaged intensities; ca. 20-fold smaller in the ocean than in the river mouth.

The spectral slope S was the only CDOM optical property that behaved distinctly different from other measured properties examined. Across sampling stations it was almost invariant, with exception of station OB27. The high C.V. calculated for the spectral slope S at this station may be explained by both changes in CDOM composition and by the low precision of the slope calculation at very low absorption signals.

# 3.3. Inter annual variability of CDOM composition

Table 3 presents descriptive statistics of percent contribution of intensities of the six components to total fluorescence at the four sampling sites. The dynamic range is constrained within a $0 { - } 1 0 0 \%$ range, because the contribution of the various components has been normalized to total fluorescence intensity, ITOT. The compositional changes in CDOM were much less variable than the variability of CDOM absorption and fluorescence. Compositional changes in CDOM at the sampling sites directly influenced by discharge of terrestrial DOM (CFP1 and CFP7) were relatively small (percent contribution of humic-like components C1, C2, C4 varied within a narrow range of 12– $1 8 \%$ ). The most variable fractions of the CDOM were the protein-like components, C5 and C6, with percent contribution of intensities to total fluorescence in the range of $3 0 { - } 8 5 \%$ . In the most oceanic sampling site, station OB27, changes in percent contribution of all components, except the tryptophan-like component, C6, to total fluorescence were almost equal (ca. $2 0 \%$ ). The CDOM composition at station OBSB was least variable. Two trends may be derived from these statistics: a significant decrease of average contribution of primary humic-like components C1, and a significant increase in the average contribution of the tyrosine-like component C5 across the transition from estuarine (fresh-water influenced) to oceanic water. This trend is in accord with CDOM compositional changes observed by Kowalczuk et al. (2003) using the “peak picking” technique.

Changes in CDOM composition were clearly distinguished between the dry and wet periods. To better illustrate compositional changes, Fig. 6 presents CDOM composition at the four sampling sites under the extreme hydrological conditions in the Cape Fear River watershed: the peak of the drought in the summer 2002 and the peak of the wet period in summer 2003. The sudden change from low-flow drought conditions to very high-flow high precipitation at station CFP1 resulted in an increase of humic-like compounds in the CDOM. During drought, the contribution of the primary humic-like component C1, dropped below $3 0 \%$ and the contribution of the protein-like component C5 reached $12 \%$ . The contribution of component C1 gradually increased with increased flow and during the peak of the wet season in summer 2003 reached $4 5 \%$ . At the same time, the contribution of C5 decreased to only $6 \%$ . A similar increase in humiclike material contribution to total fluorescence intensity was observed at station CFP1, again in summer 2004, during a smaller increase of

Cape Fear River flow (data not shown). During those two high-flow events, CDOM in the river was composed almost entirely of humic-like compounds. The compositional changes of CDOM at station CFP 7 were not as distinct as those in the river mouth, although the decrease of the contribution of component C5 was marked during the wet period (Fig. 6). Additionally, an increase in contribution of marine-like humic material, C3, was observed during the same time at CFP7.

Compositional changes in CDOM in Onslow Bay were quite different from those recorded in the Cape Fear River plume area. Composition of the CDOM samples collected at OBSB was relatively stable during the period of observation, and contribution of humiclike components C1, C2 and C4, which represent terrestrial material, varied little. Only a slight increase in the presence of the protein-like component C5 and decrease of primary humic component C1 was observed at the peak of the drought relative to CDOM composition during the wet period. All samples collected at OB27 had a very small input of humic-like fluorophores to the total fluorescence intensity (on average around $2 0 \%$ ). CDOM at OB27 was mostly composed of protein-like fluorophores C5 and C6. Their average contribution exceeded $4 0 \%$ (see data in Table 3). Similar to stations CFP1 and CFP7, changes in CDOM composition between dry and wet periods were well marked at OB27. During the peak drought period, CDOM was composed mainly of protein-like fluorophores, their contribution reaching almost $5 0 \%$ ; during the wet period their presence was reduced to $3 0 \%$ . In turn, the terrestrial humic-like material increased by 1/3 relative to the drought and exceeded $45 \%$ . The humic-like components were presumably transported from the plume area (Fig. 6). Similar to station CFP7, an increase in contribution of marine-like humic material (C3) was observed during the wet period in the coastal ocean station OB27.

# 3.4. Relationships of CDOM optical properties with salinity

The relationship of CDOM optical properties with salinity did not differ significantly from those reported earlier in the study area by Kowalczuk et al. (2003). Both CDOM absorption coefficient and total fluorescence were negatively correlated with salinity (Fig. 7). This result indicates that terrestrial discharge is the principal source of the CDOM in the Cape Fear River plume area and Long Bay and that mixing of fresh and saline waters is the main factor controlling the distribution of bulk optical properties of CDOM. Intensities of individual CDOM components derived from PARAFAC modeling were also negatively correlated with salinity (data not shown). Slopes and intercepts of linear regression equations between salinity and optical properties of CDOM fluorescence intensity of respective components are given in the Table 4. The highest coefficient of determination was calculated for the relationship between salinity and $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 3 6 )$ . The coefficients of determination between salinity and total fluorescence and fluorescence intensities of components C1, C2, C3 and C4 were similar $_ { ( > 0 . 6 ) }$ , but lower than in the case of $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ . The fluorescence intensity of components associated with protein-like fluorophores was least correlated with salinity. The fluorescence intensity of the tryptophan-like component, C6, was almost independent from the salinity. This component was present in all environments, however its distribution in the transition zone between fresh and oceanic water is not controlled by physical mixing. This component is presumably associated with autochthonous production of DOM in the aquatic environment or with DOM degradation through photobleaching and/or bacterial uptake.

# 4. Discussion

The number of components identified by PARAFAC modeling of DOM fluorescence spectroscopic data from different aquatic environments ranges between 3 and 13 and varies depending on the volume of data analyzed, sources of the DOM, and processes that control DOM variability (see references for details: Cory and McKnight, 2005; Stedmon and Markager, 2005a; Murphy et al., 2006; Wedborg et al., 2007; Murphy et al., 2008; Yamashita et al., 2008). Regardless of the number of components identified, the PARAFAC technique is capable of decomposing DOM EEMs into humic-like substances, which are usually associated with terrestrial sources, and protein-like compounds, usually associated with autochthonous production (e.g., Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003). Spectral characteristics of excitation and emission of terrestrial humic-like and marine components presented in this study fit well with components previously identified by other authors and reported in the literature, except the spectral characteristics of the protein-like fluorophores which had emission characteristics shifted toward longer wavelengths by ca. $2 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . The similar characteristics of excitation and emission spectra of matching components from various studies underlines the potential of PARAFAC modeling as a universal tool for studying optical properties of DOM in different aquatic environments and processes that lead to DOM variability, such as production, decomposition and compositional changes.

There was a noticeable discrepancy in excitation and emission characteristics of pure tyrosine and tryptophan and tyrosine-like (C5) and tryptophan-like (C6) fluorophores identified by PARAFAC model in EEMs samples collected from the South Atlantic Bight. Both amino acids are solvatochromic compounds, which means that their emission spectral characteristics change due to changes in solvent polarity. A hypsochromic shift (blue shift) corresponds to a decrease in solvent polarity and a bathochromic shift (red shift) corresponds to an increase in solvent polarity (Lakowicz, 1999). Marine water has lower polarity compared to deionized (distilled) water because of salt ion interactions with the dipoles of water molecules, therefore the blue shift in emission maximum of tyrosine and tryptophan dissolved in marine water would be expected. Furthermore, tyrosine and tryptophan fluorescence is influenced by the proximity of other residues, for example, metal ligands and changes in pH. The presence of metal ligands significantly quenches the fluorescence intensity. Recently, Yamashita and Jaffé (2008) have studied interactions between two metals of environmental concern: $\mathtt { C u ( I I ) }$ and $\mathrm { H g ( I I ) }$ and fluorescence properties of different components identified by the PARAFAC model in EEMs of samples of natural waters in the Florida Everglades. They observed significant quenching of fluorescence intensity of both humic-like and protein-like components. The emission spectral characteristics of protein-like components in their samples were also red shifted by approximately $1 5 \mathrm { n m }$ relative to pure compounds.

Trace metals are present in the Cape Fear River estuary. Willey and Atkinson (1982) reported that the iron concentration was too small to significantly impact the fluorescence properties of estuarine waters. However, Shank et al. (2004) reported significant concentrations of copper ligand complexes. Some other factors influencing fluorescence spectral properties of tryptophan and tyrosine have been studied in the Cape Fear River and its estuary. Willey and Atkinson (1982) observed that fluorescence intensity of natural riverine water samples increases with increasing pH. However, the pH values in riverine waters in North Carolina remained relatively stable around 6.6. Marine waters are slightly alkaline with pH ranging between 7.5 and 8.4. Marine waters exhibit higher pH compared to deionized water used in standard laboratory spectrofluorometric measurements (pH ca. 5). This may explain the observed red shift in the protein-like fluorophores emission characteristics. Furthermore, the energytransfer processes occurring between tyrosine and tryptophan bound in proteins are complex and may also result in a variety of fluorescence shifts (Moens et al., 2004).

Using the relative contributions of various component intensities to total fluorescence as markers of DOM properties, we characterized three distinct DOM fluorescence patterns in the different water masses in our study area. DOM fluorescence properties in the Cape Fear River plume area (CFP1 and CFP7) are dominated by the spectral characteristics of humic-like components with minor contributions by protein-like components. These properties closely match the DOM fluorescence composition of forest streams and wetlands that discharge fresh water into a temperate estuary in Denmark (Stedmon et al., 2003; Stedmon and Markager, 2005a). The fluorescence properties of oceanic DOM (OB27), which are dominated by the presence of protein-like components, match the fluorescent DOM composition of oceanic water reported by Murphy et al. (2008). The average intensity of the principle humic-like component, C1, the marker of terrestrial humic substances, decreased 35 fold at OB27 relative to its presence in the fresh water environment, which accounts for ca. $2 . 6 \%$ of the remaining terrestrial organic matter in the open ocean. This figure corresponds very well with the calculation by Opsahl and Benner (1997) of the concentration of lignin biomarkers in the ocean and the values of Murphy et al. (2008), which were based on fluorescence intensities of the humic-like component. The third group of distinct DOM fluorescence characteristics in our study region may be associated with the coastal salt marsh ecosystem. Although DOM at OBSB was composed mainly of humic-like components, there was a substantial contribution of components C5 and C6, which are related to tyrosine-like and tryptophan-like compounds. The composition of “salt marsh” DOM is temporally very stable and did not change significantly relative to other sampling sites, among seasons or during contrasting episodic events (severe drought followed by very high precipitation). Tzortziou et al. (2008) reported distinctly different optical properties of DOM produced by the salt marsh ecosystem than in adjacent estuarine waters, which is somewhat contradictory to our results. Tzortziou et al. (2008) reported that salt marsh DOM had a considerably stronger absorption, larger fluorescence signal, lower fluorescence per unit absorbance, and higher fluorescence at wavelengths ${ > } 4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , all of which optically characterize a relatively complex, high-molecular-weight, aromatic-rich DOM. Results presented in our study show that CDOM absorption and fluorescence in waters fringed by salt marshes are lower than in the Cape Fear River estuary, that the exponential slope of absorption spectrum is higher than in riverine waters, and the EEMs composition suggests lower molecular weight fractions of DOM.

Significant covariance of CDOM absorption with fresh water discharge from rivers has been observed in other coastal areas (e.g., in Narragansett Bay by Keith et al., 2002; in Tasmania by Clementson et al., 2004). High resolution time series observations by Clementson et al. (2004) revealed that large increases of the CDOM absorption coefficient $a _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 4 4 0 )$ were preceded by episodes of very high river flow. Both studies cited above, state that the magnitude and dependence of CDOM absorption coefficient from direct river discharge decreases with increased distance from the river mouth. This decrease is due to mixing of fresh water with oceanic waters and processes like autochthonous production and degradation that shape the effective bulk optical properties of CDOM. Evidence that riverine discharge is a major source of the colored fraction of DOM has been given in the results of field observations at larger spatial and temporal scales e.g., in the Mid Atlantic Bight (Vodacek et al., 1997; Del Vecchio and Blough, 2004), the West Florida Shelf (Del Castillo et al., 2000), and the Baltic Sea (Kowalczuk, 1999; Kowalczuk et al., 2006a).

This study presents a time series of CDOM composition among different water masses based on observations of fluorescence excitation/emission matrices and their deconstruction into distinct components through PARAFAC modeling. This approach to the study of temporal changes in CDOM composition has been applied by Stedmon and Markager (2005a) who described temporal compositional changes in a temperate estuary in Denmark. Their study showed strong covariance of identified components with precipitation rate (a sharp increase of respective component intensities after precipitation episodes) and temperature. They also observed that temporal changes in CDOM fluorescence composition in forest streams were different for different groups of components. The fluorescence intensities of some humic-like components varied similarly with two identified protein-like components, while fluorescence intensity of the remaining 4 humic-like components remained stable during their observation period. Our study has provided additional evidence that CDOM composition in coastal environments is highly variable. Similar to results presented by Stedmon and Markager (2005a), relative fluorescence intensity of some of the humic-like components (C2, C3, C4) and one protein-like component (C6) did not change significantly in the Cape Fear Estuary, while relative fluorescence of the principle humic-like component, C1, and the tyrosine-like component, C5, varied significantly. At all sampling stations, the contribution of humic material to CDOM fluorescence was higher during wet periods than dry period. The contribution of C5 to CDOM fluorescence increased significantly during the dry period. Changes in CDOM composition in various coastal environments of the Florida

Everglades have been studied by Maie et al. (2006), who used the Fluorescence Index value [ratio of fluorescence intensity at 450 to $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , excited at $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (McKnight et al., 2001)]. This study showed that CDOM composition in areas under direct influence of fresh water discharge changed under different hydrological conditions. During the wet season, CDOM was composed of a larger proportion of humic fraction than during the dry season. The temporal changes in CDOM composition at places fringed by mangrove forests were much smaller across wet and dry seasons. We observed a similar effect, presumably caused by salt marshes adjacent to station OBSB.

Seasonal averages of the respective component intensities in selected stations should be treated as preliminary indicators of seasonal variability, first because a relatively low number of samples were collected to calculate statistically significant averages, and second because of the unusual environmental conditions during the sampling period (severe drought followed by very high precipitation in the Cape Fear River watershed and the passage of Hurricane Isabel in September 2003). Taking this into account, seasonal variability is rather small when compared to the extremes of peak dry versus peak wet periods. In the Cape Fear River plume area, a slight seasonal trend was observed with maximum fluorescence intensity in the spring and minimum in winter. The sampling period in Onslow Bay was shorter than in the plume area, so any seasonal variability may be incidental. However, there was almost no seasonal change in either fluorescence intensity or composition at OBSB, except for a relatively small increase in peak intensities during the wet period compared to the dry period.

All statistical measures of variability in CDOM fluorescence components were higher than those calculated for the spectral slope coefficient, S. This suggests that spectral slope calculated over a broad spectral range is not a good approximation of CDOM composition. However, increased spectral slope values toward oceanic salinities (over 33–34) indicate an overall trend of a change in CDOM composition from domination of humic-like components to protein-like components. Helms et al. (2008) recently proposed that instead of calculating the spectral slope over a broad spectral range, a spectral slope ratio calculated in the UV and visible regions should be used to better approximate CDOM composition. They found that, the spectral slope ratio, $S _ { \mathrm { R } } ,$ correlated significantly with CDOM molecular weight.

Regression analysis of fluorescence intensities of the six identified components with salinity showed that in the South Atlantic Bight, the discharge of fresh water from the Cape Fear River is the main driving force that governs CDOM variability. All components, except the tryptophan-like component, C6, were significantly and negatively correlated with salinity. Similar results were presented by Yamashita et al. (2008), who observed significant co-variance of humic-like component intensities with salinity. They also reported that the distributional pattern of the two protein-like components was governed partially by riverine input and autochthonous production in Ise Bay, Japan and, changes in the intensity of the tyrosine-like component peak were almost independent from the salinity distribution. Stedmon and Markager (2005a) stated that there are significant non-linear relationships between fluorescence intensities of those components which distribution is govern by common factors. Based on non-linear regression analysis between log-transformed component intensities and ratios of component intensities, Murphy et al. (2008) proposed theoretical models to explain component distributions in the environment using conservative mixing and production/ decomposition scenarios. We performed a similar analysis to determine if the distributional patterns presented by Stedmon and Markager (2005a) and Murphy et al. (2008) were also present in our data set. Our regression analysis between the fluorescence intensity of the six identified CDOM components and salinity showed that mixing of fresh water rich in CDOM with CDOM-poor oceanic waters is the dominant factor controlling component distribution in the Cape Fear River plume area and adjacent coastal ocean. Pairs of log-transformed intensities of the various components were significantly correlated with each other, except with log-transformed fluorescence intensity of C6 (data not shown). Fig. 8 shows the co-variance of the logtransformed fluorescence intensities of components C1 and C5 (upper panel), and the ratio of fluorescence intensity of components C5 and C1 as a function of C5 fluorescence intensity (middle panel) and salinity (lower panel). Data presented in Fig. 8 suggest that conservative mixing could be regarded as a common factor controlling the distribution of components C1 and C5 in the coastal environment only to a certain degree. It is evident that at low intensities of both components (in the coastal ocean), their distribution in the environment is decoupled and not controlled by mixing. Comparing the C5 and C1 intensity ratio as a function of C5 fluorescence intensity to the theoretical distributions presented by Murphy et al. (2008), we conclude that to a certain point (until salinity reaches 30) the dominant factor that controls distribution of C1 and C5 is conservative mixing. At the zone of intensive mixing of fresh and oceanic waters (salinity range 30–35) the distribution of C1 and C5 is governed by the combined effects of conservative mixing and removal of C1 (faster than C5) and mutual production of C5 at an unknown rate. At oceanic salinities $( > 3 5 )$ the relative distribution of the protein-like component C5 is governed by its autochthonous production and the background level of humic-like components. Disappearance of humic fractions of CDOM may explain observations made by many researchers (De Souza-Sierra et al., 1994, 1997; Del Castillo et al., 1999) of the so called of “blue shift” of UV excited emissions of fluorescence maxima of water samples along the gradient from terrestrial to marine environments. Since the effective fluorescence emission spectrum at a given excitation is a superposition of emission spectra of the underlying components, a smaller contribution, or disappearance, of a component, which emits energy at a longer wavelength range, will cause a shift of maximum effective fluorescence toward shorter wavelengths. This effect is clearly visible in our data set.

# 5. Conclusions

The application of fluorescence spectroscopy through recording EEMs and PARAFAC modeling presented a unique opportunity to observe compositional changes in CDOM in the Cape Fear River estuary and adjacent coastal ocean. Regression analyses confirmed that the distribution of optical properties of CDOM in the study area is mostly regulated by the amount of precipitation in the Cape Fear River watershed, its flow, and the conservative mixing of fresh and oceanic waters. Comparison of the relative changes of fluorescence intensities of the identified components showed that their distribution in the study area was due to the combined effects of conservative mixing, mutual decomposition of major humic-like components with production of protein-like components, and autochthonous production of DOM in oceanic waters.

The PARAFAC model enabled identification of six distinct components in the CDOM fluorescence excitation emission spectra: three terrestrial humic-like components, one marine humic-like component and two protein-like components. CDOM fluorescence excitation emission spectra in estuarine, coastal ocean and Gulf Stream waters differ from each other. The EEMs composition of estuarine waters is dominated by humic-like components, while EEMs composition of Gulf Stream waters is dominated by protein-like components. The EEMs composition of coastal areas fringed by extensive salt marsh ecosystems is a mixture of lighter fraction humic-like components and protein-like components, indicating significant microbial reprocessing and local production of DOM.

The change in hydrologic regime from drought (low flow) to high precipitation (very high flow) that impacts the amount of terrestrial dissolved organic matter transported into the ocean from the land via the river was also reflected in the relative contributions of the modeled CDOM components to total fluorescence intensity. Low molecular weight dissolved organic matter and protein-like compounds present in the CDOM during the drought were replaced by high molecular weight DOM, mainly humic material, transported by river water to the estuary during maximum discharge.
