# Abstract

Fluorescence spectroscopy to evaluate the removal of dissolved organic matter (DOM) via biofiltration under varying operational and water quality conditions was investigated in this study. Three feedwaters (lake, river, and wastewater effluent) were examined at three temperatures (6, 22, and $2 8 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ) and three empty bed contact times (EBCTs; 5, 15, and $3 0 \mathrm { m i n } \mathrm { \ ; }$ ) to simulate seasonal and operational variations experienced by utilities. Parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis was used to characterize the fluorescing DOM and to determine the component removal. Three components were found in the lake and river feedwaters, and four components were found in the wastewater effluent. In natural source waters, protein-like components were removed most effectively via biofiltration. In the wastewater effluent, a humic-like component common in nutrient-rich waters was removed most effectively, and the protein-like component was removed second most effectively. Increased EBCT and temperature increased the removal efficiency of bulk DOM and the protein-like components.

# 1 | INTRODUCTION

Natural organic matter (NOM) is found in virtually all surface waters and consists of a complex, heterogeneous mixture of compounds. NOM in surface waters can be categorized based on its origin as follows: (a) NOM derived in the watershed from vegetative debris and then transported into the water body is often referred to as humiclike, terrestrial, or allochtonous material (Coble, 1996); (b) NOM derived from microbial processes within the water body is often referred to as protein-like, microbial, or autochtonous material (Coble, 1996); and (c) NOM derived from wastewater treatment plant effluent is referred to as effluent organic matter (EfOM) (Nam & Amy, 2008; Yang, Peleato, Legge, & Andrews, 2019) and can be synthetic organic chemicals from anthropogenic sources, recalcitrant NOM derived in the watershed, and soluble microbial products derived during wastewater treatment biological processes (Drewes & Fox, 1999). Dissolved organic matter (DOM), NOM that passes through a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter (Zsolnay, 2003), is often a concern for drinking water treatment utilities as DOM can react with disinfectants (e.g., chlorine) in drinking water systems to form toxic disinfection byproducts (DBPs), cause biological regrowth within the distribution system, or constitute a specific harmful or concerning pollutant (Terry & Summers, 2018). Biological regrowth within a system can lead to distribution pipe corrosion (Broo, Berghult, & Hedberg, 1999) and decrease the disinfectant residual throughout the distribution system (Jacangelo, Demarco, Owen, & Randtke, 1995).

Understanding the character of DOM is advantageous to understanding reactions occurring within the water treatment plant and the distribution system. DOM is often measured via bulk metrics such as dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and ultraviolet (UV) absorbance, yet these surrogate measurements are not capable of DOM characterization. Specific UV absorbance (SUVA), the ratio of UV absorbance to DOC, has been used to characterize DOM as it can indicate DOM aromaticity (Weishaar et al., 2003). DOM of terrestrial origin typically yields a higher SUVA value of ${ > } 4 \ \mathrm { L } \ \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , while DOM of microbial origin typically yields a lower SUVA value of $< 3 ~ \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g ^ { - 1 } ~ } \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Volk & LeChevallier, 2000; Volk & LeChevallier, 2002). In addition, fluorescence spectroscopy has become an increasingly popular method for analyzing and characterizing DOM (Murphy et al., 2011; Murphy, Stedmon, Graeber, & Bro, 2013; Peleato, Sidhu, Legge, & Andrews, 2017; Yamashita, Panton, Mahaffey, & Jaffé, 2011) as a small portion of DOM, which is typically correlated with aromaticity, fluoresces when excited by photons, that is, fluorescent DOM (fDOM) (Senesi, Miano, Provenzano, & Brunetti, 1991). The region in which the DOM fluoresces can indicate DOM composition and origin, and the fluorescence index (FI), the ratio of the emission intensities at wavelengths of 470 and $5 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ a t a constant excitation wavelength of $3 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Eq. 1), has been used by researchers to correlate DOM aromaticity and to indicate the origin of DOM compounds (Korak, Dotson, Summers, & Rosario-Ortiz, 2014; McKnight et al., 2001). DOM of terrestrial origin typically yields an FI value of 1.2–1.5, while DOM of microbial origin typically yields an FI value of 1.7–2.0 in natural waters (McKnight et al., 2001).

Fluorescence analysis is performed by collecting data in the form of three-dimensional excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) (Figure 1). Parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis, which statistically decomposes the EEMs into fluorescing components that represent groups of fluorophores, or groups of chemical compounds that fluoresce in a given region, and can be characterized by component origin is one method of analyzing EEMs (Bro, 1997; Murphy et al., 2011; Murphy et al., 2013; Stedmon & Bro, 2008). PARAFAC analysis allows for more detailed qualitative and semiquantitative analyses compared with other EEM analysis methods, such as peakpicking (Li, Jeon, Ryu, Santo Domingo, & Seo, 2020). Li et al. (2020) expands further upon the pros and cons of PARAFAC analysis. Humic-like material tends to fluoresce in regions denoted as Peak A $\mathrm { ( E x ~ 2 4 0 - 2 6 0 ~ n m }$ , Em $3 8 0 { - } 4 6 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and Peak C (Ex $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , Em $4 0 0 { - } 4 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m } )$ (Korak et al., 2014). Protein-like material tends to fluoresce in regions denoted as Peak B (Ex $2 7 0 { - } 2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , Em $3 0 0 { - } 3 1 0 ~ \mathrm { n m } ,$ ) and Peak T $\mathrm { E x } \ 2 7 0 { - } 2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , $\mathrm { E m } \ 3 3 0 { - } 3 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m } )$ (Korak et al., 2014). EfOM typically exhibits strong fluorescence in the proteinlike regions, but it can fluoresce in humic-like regions as well (Stedmon et al., 2011; Yang et al., 2019). EfOM is typically differentiated from natural source waters by a more defined protein-like peak and a higher FI value (Nam & Amy, 2008).

While DOC is removed during standard coagulation and flocculation practices (Sanchez, Skeriotis, & Miller, 2013; Vera et al., 2017; C. Volk et al., 2000), some utilities may require additional treatment to meet the US Environmental Protection Agency (USEPA) Stage 1 and 2 Disinfectants and Disinfection Byproducts Rule (USEPA, 2012). One promising treatment method of carbon removal is biological filtration (biofiltration), which employs biologically active media (e.g., granular activated carbon, sand, or anthracite) in a filter column to remove influent DOC and other contaminants through physical and biological processes (Urfer, Huck, Booth, & Coffey, 1997) and has been the subject of several recent studies (Bassett, Nieminski, & Stevens, 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Evans, Carter, Brown, & Lauderdale, 2019). One key parameter of biofilter operation is the hydraulic loading rate (HLR), which determines the empty bed contact time (EBCT) of the filter. EBCT is a measure of how long the influent water is in contact with the media and associated microbial community and is defined as the “filter media depth divided by the HLR” (Evans et al., 2013). Previous research has shown that longer EBCTs improve DOC removal efficiency in a biofilter as longer EBCTs provide more contact time for microbes to biodegrade the DOC (Evans et al., 2013; Terry & Summers, 2018; Urfer et al., 1997). In addition to EBCT, temperature affects biofilter performance (Moll,

Summers, Fonseca, & Matheis, 1999; Selbes, Amburgey, Peeler, Alansari, & Karanfil, 2016) as it is correlated to microbial growth and degradation rates (Urfer et al., 1997).

While several studies have evaluated fDOM removal through coagulation and flocculation (Korak, RosarioOrtiz, & Scott Summers, 2015; Sanchez et al., 2013; Vera et al., 2017; C. Volk et al., 2000), few have investigated fDOM removal through biofiltration. Researchers who have concomitantly examined biofiltration and fluorescence analysis tend to focus on a specific enhancing treatment, biofilm characterization, or DBP formation potential (Arnold, Batista, Dickenson, & Gerrity, 2018; Fu et al., 2017; Peleato et al., 2016; Xing, Wang, Hu, & Liu, 2018). Even fewer studies have characterized fractions of fDOM most effectively removed through biofiltration (Chen et al., 2016; Hooper et al., 2019). Therefore, the present work aims to characterize fractions of fDOM most effectively removed through biofiltration under multiple water quality conditions and operational parameters using PARAFAC analysis.

The objectives of this study are to (a) characterize fractions of fDOM present in three distinct water sources, (b) evaluate which fractions of fDOM are most effectively removed via biological filtration, (c) investigate the effect of EBCT and temperature on fDOM removal in a biofilter, and (d) evaluate the relationship between DOM aromaticity indicators, specifically SUVA and FI. Results from this work will elucidate which fDOM fractions are more amenable to biodegradation via biological filtration in a variety of source waters and operational conditions. Understanding, characterizing, and monitoring the fDOM fractions can help water utilities optimize treatment parameters for enhanced DOM removal.

# 2 | METHODS

# 2.1 | Source waters

Three local source waters were selected based on DOM matrix variation: Lake Tuscaloosa (Lake-OM), Black

Warrior River (River-OM), and a Tuscaloosa, AL (USA) wastewater treatment plant effluent (WWEff-OM). Lake Tuscaloosa (Lake-OM) serves as the water supply reservoir for Tuscaloosa and Northport, AL and is fed by upstream tributaries with little industrial or wastewater influence on the organic matter. Black Warrior River (River-OM) serves as the receiving water body for large industrial and municipal effluents and thus contains organic matter highly impacted by human activity. Wastewater effluent (WWEff-OM) was collected from a local wastewater treatment plant after tertiary treatment. Approximately $6 0 \mathrm { ~ L ~ }$ of water were collected from each source in three $2 0 { \cdot } \mathrm { L }$ plastic carboys. The water was then combined into a 55-gal barrel for homogeneity and stored in a walk-in refrigerator (KoldFront, Kolpak, Parsons, TN) at ${ } ^ { 7 } \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C }$ until use in the biofilter system. The water was collected 3 days prior to the beginning of testing and was refrigerated for up to 28 days before use. Each source water was tested for $\mathrm { p H }$ (sensION+ Benchtop Meter, HACH, Loveland, CO), alkalinity (Model AL-AP Alkalinity Test kit, HACH, Loveland, CO), bulk carbon metrics (see Section 2.4), and fDOM (see Section 2.6), as seen in Table 1.

# 2.3 | Sampling

The bench-scale biofilter setup was operated using the combined recirculating batch reactor and single-pass plug flow reactor experimental protocol developed by Terry et al. (2019). The source water was split into two batches for the recirculation batch mode and the single-pass mode. The recirculation batch mode consisted of a 5-day recirculation period to determine the biodegradable organic carbon (BDOC) content of the sample and to expose the biofilter to the specific water matrix. The single-pass mode consisted of a 4-hr single-pass operation followed by sampling to determine the biofilter performance at the targeted EBCTs. After the water samples were collected, the columns were drained, and biofilter media samples were collected from the top of each column for biological analysis.

# 2.4 | Bulk carbon measurements

DOC was measured in parts per million (ppm) using a TOC analyzer (Sievers 5310C TOC Analyzer, Suez, Boulder, CO). A vial of lab-grade 18.2 MΩ-cm deionized (DI) water (Synergy UV, Millipore, Molsheim, France) began and concluded each instrument run and was placed after every fourth sample vial to ensure proper flushing of the instrument and to prevent contamination. $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ absorbance $( \lambda = 2 5 4 \mathrm { { n m } }$ ) was measured with a spectrophotometer (DR 6000, HACH, Loveland, CO). The spectrophotometer was calibrated with DI water, and the cuvette was rinsed with DI prior to and postsample analysis. BDOC was measured using the 5-day bioacclimated biofilter column test described in Terry et al. (2019). BDOC was determined by subtracting the DOC remaining after the 5-day recirculation period from the initial DOC of the source water at room temperature. SUVA was calculated by normalizing $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ data to the influent DOC $\mathrm { ( S U V A = [ U V A _ { 2 5 4 } / D O C ] ^ { \ast } 1 0 0 ) }$ (Weishaar et al., 2003).

All vials and glassware were muffled at $5 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 \ \mathrm { h r }$ to prevent carbon contamination (Thermolyne Large Chamber Muffle Furnaces Type 30400, Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA). The samples were collected and immediately filtered through a $0 . 4 5 – \mu \mathrm { m }$ membrane filter (Whatman Cellulose Acetate Membrane Circles, VWR, Radnor, PA). The filters were first rinsed with $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of DI water prior to sample filtration to prevent carbon leaching from the filters.

# 2.5 | Biological activity

Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) was measured as an indicator of acclimated biofilter media as it is the primary energy carrier of all cells and has been correlated with microbial activity in the biofilter (Pharand, Van Dyke, Anderson, & Huck, 2014). Media samples were analyzed for ATP concentrations using a commercial test kit following the manufacturer's instructions (Deposit and Surface Analysis Kit, LuminULTRA Technologies, Fredericton, New Brunswick, Canada). To report biomass activity on a per-unit of media volume basis, the bed density $( 0 . 8 1 4 \mathrm { g / m l } )$ and dry: wet ratio $( 6 4 \% )$ of the media were used to calculate ATP concentrations as $\mathrm { n g A T P / m l }$ media. ATP results are reported on a per-unit of media volume basis so that comparison across studies can be made regardless of media type.

# 2.6 | Fluorescence

Fluorescence EEMs were collected using a spectrofluorometer (Aqualog, HORIBA, Kyoto, Japan). All samples were measured in duplicate, with a new aliquot for each EEM. Excitation wavelengths ranged from 240 to $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in $1 0 { \cdot } \mathrm { n m }$ increments (Korak et al., 2015). Emission scans were measured from approximately 240 to $8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in $4 . 6 7 – \mathrm { n m }$ increments per the capabilities of the instrument (Aqualog® Operation Manual Part number 5500810012 rev. K, 2019). A bandpass length of $5 \mathrm { n m }$ and an integration time of 0.5 s were used to follow standard scientific practice (Murphy et al., 2010) and to maximize the resolution of the EEMs. A blank sample of Milli- $\cdot Q ^ { \circledast }$ UltraPure water was collected on each day of sampling and subtracted from the samples to correct for background fluorescence. The samples were corrected for inner filter effects, Raman normalized, and corrected for first- and second-order Rayleigh masking using the Aqualog software. EEMs were generated from the corrected fluorescence data and were presented in Raman units. The Aqualog cuvette was rinsed with DI and the exterior was cleaned with Kim wipes (KIMWIPES Delicate Task Wipers, VWR, Radnor, PA) between samples to prevent fingerprint and other contamination.

The EEM data set for all samples was analyzed using PARAFAC software (Solo, Eigenvector Research Inc., Manson, WA). Nonnegativity constraints were set for the data, and the data were filtered through a $1 6 \mathrm { - n m }$ firstorder Rayleigh filter and a $3 2 \mathrm { - n m }$ second-order Rayleigh filter and normalized to 1.0 (Aqualog® Operation Manual Part number 5500810012 rev. K, 2019). The data set was edited to exclude any suspected noise below 240 and above $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The number of components for each water source was determined by comparing the core consistency, excitation and emission wavelengths, and split-half analyses of the generated models for different numbers of components. Each PARAFAC model was examined by its fit, such that greater than $9 8 \%$ of the variation within the data was explained by the model, and no pattern was evident in residuals. The spectral properties (excitation and emission wavelengths) were evaluated to ensure that the spectra were smooth, with only one peak for emission values and one to two peaks for excitation values. The split-half analysis split the data into two halves and compared the data sets against one other. A split-half value closer to $1 0 0 \%$ indicated a higher accuracy for model fit (Stedmon & Bro, 2008). Core consistency, a measure of the trilinearity of the model, is initially $1 0 0 \%$ for a onecomponent model and decreases with an increasing number of model components. Analysis was performed by starting with a one-component model and increasing the number of components until the model was no longer viable, ${ < } 5 0 \%$ core consistency (Bro & Kiers, 2003). The results were analyzed with the drEEM toolbox for MATLAB for confirmation (Murphy et al., 2013).

The PARAFAC software does not measure concentrations of fDOM within the samples, but it statistically analyzes the wavelengths at which the sample fluoresces to characterize components. The software then produces excitation and emission loading values for each component, which function similar to relative concentrations. The relative component loadings were used for analysis. The excitation and emission loadings and wavelengths were loaded into the OpenFluor database (Murphy, Stedmon, Wenig, & Bro, 2014) and were compared with components in other published papers. FI was calculated using Eq. (1) (Korak et al., 2014).

# 3 | RESULTS

# 3.1 | Bulk carbon metrics

Biofilter performance in terms of DOC removal was evaluated at 6, 22, and $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for each of the source waters at three EBCTs (5, 15, and $3 0 \mathrm { m i n } \AA$ ), with statistical evidence determined via analysis of variance testing. For all temperatures, the biofilter had the highest performance at the 30-min EBCT with DOC removals of $7 \%$ (avg, $n = 6$ ), $1 1 \%$ (avg, $n = 6$ ), and $1 0 \%$ (avg, $n = 6$ ) for Lake-OM $( p = . 0 7 6 )$ , River-OM $( p = . 0 0 7 )$ , and WWEff-OM $( p = . 0 0 5 )$ , respectively. At the $3 0 { \cdot } \mathrm { m i n }$ EBCT, the biofilter saw the highest performance at the warmest temperature condition $( 2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ , with DOC removals of $7 \%$ (avg, $n = 2 \mathrm { . }$ ), $1 7 \%$ (avg, $n = 2 ,$ ), and $1 4 \%$ (avg, $n = 2$ ) for Lake-OM $( p = . 0 7 2 )$ , RiverOM (0.011), and WWEff-OM (0.026), respectively. Overall, DOC removal increased with increasing filter depth and increased with higher temperatures for each source water (Figure S1), which is concurrent with other research studies (Terry & Summers, 2018; Urfer et al., 1997).

SUVA removal via biofiltration was also evaluated at 6, 22, and 28 for each of the source waters at the three EBCTs (5, 15, and $3 0 \mathrm { m i n } )$ ). For all temperatures, the 30-min EBCT had the greatest SUVA removal at $8 \%$ (avg, $n = 6$ , $9 \%$ (avg, $n = 6$ ), and $7 \%$ (avg, $n = 6$ ), respectively, for Lake-OM $( p = . 1 2 0 )$ , River-OM $( p = . 0 4 9 )$ , and WWEffOM $( p ~ = ~ . 0 2 4 )$ , which supports results found in Fu et al. (2017). At the 30-min EBCT, the warmest temperature condition $( 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ had the greatest SUVA removal at $9 \%$ (avg, $n = 2 \mathrm { \cdot }$ ), $1 1 \%$ (avg, $n = 2 ,$ ), and $9 \%$ (avg, $n = 2 \AA$ ), respectively, for Lake-OM $( p = . 0 1 7 )$ , River-OM $( p = . 1 8 8 )$ ), and WWEff-OM $( p = . 0 7 5 )$ . SUVA removal was found to be positively correlated with DOC removal $( R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 5 1 7 8$ , $\ n \ = \ 2 4 )$ , but the relationship was not one-to-one (Figure S2). SUVA values were found to be positively correlated with FI values $\textstyle \mathcal { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 6 2 4$ , $n = 3$ ), which is contrary to the negative correlation observed in Korak et al., 2015. However, only three source waters were examined; thus, no strong conclusions can be drawn regarding the relationship between SUVA and FI in this study without examining additional source waters encompassing a wider range of SUVA values.

# 3.2 | Adenosine triphosphate

Prior to the start of this experiment, ATP media samples and DOC removal samples were taken every 2 weeks for a period of 10 months and had reached stabilization. The anthracite biofilters were considered biologically active as the average biofilter $\mathrm { A T P _ { t o p } }$ value was within the range of $1 0 0 { - } 1 { , } 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { n g A T P / m l }$ , which was found by Pharand et al. (2014) to represent “active, acclimated biofilters for both GAC and anthracite biofilters.” The biofilters exhibited decreasing ATP values with increasing filter depth (Figure S3), as seen in other studies (Huck, Coffey, Amirtharajah, & Bouwer, 2000; Lauderdale, Alito, Hooper, Dowdell, & Wert, 2018; McKie, Bertoia, TaylorEdmonds, Andrews, & Andrews, 2019; Persson, Heinicke, Uhl, Hedberg, & Hermansson, 2006; Pharand et al., 2014; Rahman, 2013; Urfer & Huck, 2001; van der Aa, Magic-Knezev, Rietveld, & Van Dijk, 2006; Velten et al., 2011; Wang, 1995; Xiang et al., 2013). For all temperature conditions, Lake-OM-fed biofilter media ATP values averaged ( $\langle S D \rangle 1 0 9 \pm 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n g A T P / m l }$ $( n = 6 )$ ) at the top of the biofilter, $7 5 \pm 2 1$ ngATP/ml $( n = 6 )$ ) at 5-min EBCT, and $3 0 \pm 2 2 \mathrm { \ n g } \mathrm { A T P / m l }$ $( n = 6 )$ at 15-min EBCT. # 4 | CONCLUSION

The objectives of this study were to characterize fractions of fDOM present in a variety of water sources, assess which fractions of fDOM were most effectively removed via biological filtration across multiple EBCTs and temperatures, and evaluate the relationship between DOM aromaticity metrics. Increased temperature and EBCT had a positive effect on the removal efficiency of DOC, and these results are corroborated by previous studies (Moll et al., 1999; Selbes et al., 2016; Urfer et al., 1997). PARAFAC characterization of the fDOM in Lake-OM and the River-OM demonstrated that two fluorescing components were of a humic-like nature (A1, A2, B1, B2), and one component was of a protein-like nature (A3, B3). In the WWEff-OM, three of the components were of a humic-like nature (C1, C2, C3), and one component was of a protein-like nature (C4). While the WWEff-OM components fluoresced in similar regions as the natural source water components, the specific compounds are likely compositionally different.

In both the Lake-OM and River-OM samples, the protein-like component (A3, B3) was removed most effectively via biofiltration and consistently decreased with increasing EBCT for all temperature scenarios. In the WWEff-OM samples, the humic-like component found in areas of high nutrients (C1) was removed most effectively, and the protein-like component C4 was found to be removed second most efficiently, with the largest decrease in component loadings from 15- to $3 0 { \cdot } \mathrm { m i n }$ EBCT. Hence, biofiltration may be a targeted approach to remove protein-like and humic-like components found in areas of high nutrients in reuse scenarios to optimize treatment. Overall, the results across all source waters indicate that protein-like components are more amenable to removal through biofiltration than most terrestrial humic-like components, which is concurrent with other studies (Chen et al., 2016; Hallé, Huck, Peldszus, Haberkamp, & Jekel, 2009; Peleato et al., 2016). These results also indicate that EBCTs longer than 5 min may be beneficial for removing additional fractions of the fluorescing components as component loading removal efficiency of components A3, B3, and C1 increased with increasing EBCT and saw the largest decrease in the second half of the biofilter. Fluorescence spectroscopy can be a valuable tool to monitor fluctuations in DOM composition, but as only a small portion of DOM fluoresces, this analysis should be used in conjunction with bulk carbon monitoring (i.e., DOC removal).

SUVA and FI were found to exhibit a weak positive correlation in this study, yet there was no sufficiently wide range of values to draw any definitive conclusions about the relationship between these values. Future work should consider a wider range of source water aromaticity parameters to further develop the relationship between SUVA and FI and further investigate how fDOM can be used as a monitoring tool for surface water treatment plants through the correlation of fluorescence with other water quality parameters.
