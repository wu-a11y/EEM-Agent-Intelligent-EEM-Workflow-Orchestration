# Abstract

Despite considerable research on the linkages between dissolved organic matter (DOM) and bacteria, it is not yet clear how the abundance of the main aquatic clades relates to DOM composition in natural aquatic systems. We evaluated this relation using PARAFAC modeling of excitation–emission fluorescence spectroscopy and spectroscopic indexes to characterize DOM composition, and fluorescence in situ hybridization, to quantify the major bacterial groups in a subtropical lagoon. The DOM exhibited marked temporal variations in concentration, molecular weight, aromaticity, color, degree of humification, and freshness, and proportion of the three different fluorescent components identified. All major bacterial clades (Alphaproteobacteria, Betaproteobacteria, Gammaproteobacteria, and Cytophaga-Flavobacteria) were significantly linked to DOM concentration and/or composition, being those crucial factors for modeling their abundance in situ. The combination and significance of the factors was specific for each bacterial group, strongly indicating that they behave as coherent and distinctive units. Cytophaga-Flavobacteria and Betaproteobacteria were the groups which correlated with more DOM properties. Alphaproteobacteria and Gammaproteobacteria abundances were significantly explained by low or high dissolved organic carbon concentrations, respectively. The significant relationships between DOM properties and the main bacterial groups delineated a profile of each group regarding DOM preferences/dislikes, in agreement with evidence derived from genome analysis to single-cell substrate uptake. These results highlight the specificities of the main bacterial clades, providing support for a functional classification of the bacterioplankton regarding DOM processing at the level of bacterial classes.

Bacteria-dissolved organic matter (DOM) interactions are central in biogeochemical cycles. Elucidating the connections between bacterial community composition (BCC) and DOM composition is fundamental to understand the role and response of aquatic systems to global change. The liaison between BCC and DOM composition has been explored mainly focusing on the evolution of BCC during experimental incubations with varied DOM sources, through genome analysis of cultured and uncultured bacteria, and looking at in situ uptake of specific substrates by bacterial cells (Cottrell and Kirchman 2000; P-erez and Sommaruga 2006; Buck et al. 2009; Lauro et al. 2009; McCarren et al. 2010; Teeling et al. 2012).

Once this traditionally called “black box” was opened, it rapidly turned into a “Pandora’s box” for biogeochemical modeling, as casted evidence from both, generalist utilization of DOM and specialization in the usage of certain compounds (P-erez and Sommaruga 2006; Alonso-Saez and Gasol 2007; Mou et al. 2008; Poretsky et al. 2010). It appears that despite the utilization of environmentally relevant substrates can be quite widespread among different bacterial groups, they still show preferences linked to DOM structure, and/or concentration (Cottrell and Kirchman 2000; Alonso and Pernthaler 2006a). Furthermore, at a given location and time, few members of the bacterial assemblage might dominate the consumption of specific DOM components, even switching dominance through seasons (Alonso and Pernthaler 2006b; Alonso-Saez and Gasol 2007). While some features have been proven to hold at a comparably broad taxonomic level (e.g., alphaproteobacterial preference for monomers), sister species have been shown to exhibit dissimilar patterns of substrate utilization (Cottrell and Kirchman 2000; Alonso et al. 2009). Thus, finding an adequate level of resolution for modeling bacterial utilization of DOM remains a challenge.

Analogously, characterizing DOM composition of natural aquatic systems has been particularly difficult and is still not routinely included in many biogeochemical studies. Spectroscopic techniques—absorbance and fluorescence—are fast, precise, and relatively inexpensive means of characterizing DOM (Jaff-e et al. 2008), and thus particularly appealing when large numbers of samples need to be processed. For example, excitation Emission Matrices (EEMs) (Coble 1996) coupled with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) (Stedmon et al. 2003) have been successfully applied to characterize DOM in a wide range of environments (Stedmon et al. 2011; Graeber et al. 2012; Kowalczuk et al. 2013; Yamashita et al. 2013).

The recent combination of high resolution techniques for characterizing both DOM (FTICR-MS) and BCC (Pyrosequencing) in an experimental system have not found significant correlation patterns between them (Herlemann et al. 2014), suggesting the need to work at different levels of resolution. Thus, despite the considerable advances in disentangling bacterial-DOM interactions, so far there are no published examples of statistically significant relations between the abundance of specific bacterial groups and DOM composition in a natural aquatic system.

The aim of this work was to quantify and characterize DOM using a suite of spectral properties in a subtropical estuary, and test whether the main clades of aquatic bacteria were significantly correlated to those properties. We hypothesized that the abundance of the major bacterial clades (Alphaproteobacteria, Betaproteobacteria, Gammaproteobacteria and Cytophaga-Flavobacteria) should be significantly linked to DOM concentration and/or composition, and that they would exhibit different patterns in these linkages.

# Materials and procedures

# Study site and sampling

Laguna de Rocha is a large shallow coastal lagoon $( 7 2 \mathrm { ~ k m } ^ { 2 } .$ , $z _ { \mathrm { m e a n } } = 0 . 6 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ , located on the eastern coast of Uruguay $( 3 4 ^ { \circ } \ 3 3 ^ { \prime } \ \mathrm { S }$ , $5 4 ^ { \circ } \ 2 2 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { W } )$ , being a natural protected area, RAMSAR site, and MaB/UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. It is separated from the Atlantic Ocean by a sand bar which periodically opens, allowing for the interaction of both water masses, and having strong impact on dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentration and quality (Conde et al. 2000).

The sampling was conducted in two phases: In the first phase, four sample collections were completed from March 2013 to June 2013, when the lagoon was physically separated from the ocean by the sand bar. In the second phase, from November 2013 to April 2014, four additional samplings were performed when the lagoon was connected to the ocean.

Samples were collected at three different stations: North $( \mathrm { N } )$ , Centre (C), and South (S), where N is the station most influenced by freshwater discharge and S the station most influenced by the adjacent ocean (Supporting Information Fig. 1). Conductivity $( K )$ and temperature $( T )$ were measured in situ using a multiparameter probe (HORIBA). At each station, a 5-L surface water sample was collected and kept fresh in the dark until laboratory processing, conducted within $1 -$ $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after collection.

# Nutrient analysis

Nutrient analyses (ammonium, nitrate, nitrite, phosphate and total dissolved nitrogen, and phosphorus) were performed following standard colorimetric protocols (APHA 1995). Color development of the corresponding chemical reactions was measured using a Lambda 35 spectrophotometer (Perkin Elmer).

# DOM analysis

Samples for determination of DOC and for DOM spectral characterization were gravity filtered using precombusted GF/F filters $( 0 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { { m } }$ pore size, combusted for $4 \textrm { h }$ at $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } $ ). Even though these filters do not retain all bacterial cells, as compared to $0 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate filters, they have advantages that justified their application in this study, i.e., they can be cleaned by combustion at high temperatures, it is possible to filter an adequate sample volume without clogging, and they are suitable for gravity filtration instead of filtration under pressure, thus reducing potential sources of contamination (Coble et al. 2014).

Samples for DOC analysis were acidified to $\mathrm { p H } < 2$ with HCl and stored frozen $( - 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ in precombusted glass ampoules. DOC concentrations were determined by high temperature catalytic oxidation using a Shimadzu TOC-L analyzer. Three injections were made for each sample $( \mathrm { C V } < 1 . 5 \% )$ , MilliQ water was used as blank $( < \mathrm { ~ 0 . 0 7 ~ m g ~ C ~ }$ ${ \mathrm { ~ L ~ } } ^ { - 1 } )$ ), and measured after every seventh sample. Potassium phthalate was used as standard and four curves were used $( 0 { - } 2 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ , $2 { - } 1 0 \ \mathrm { ~ m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ , $1 0 { - } 2 0 \ \mathrm { m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ , $2 0 { - } 4 0 \ \mathrm { \ m g L } ^ { - 1 } ,$ with five concentrations each.

Samples for DOM spectral characterization were stored immediately at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in precombusted glass vials, for maximum 1 week. Absorbance and fluorescence measurements were conducted at room temperature.

Absorbance spectra $2 4 0 { - } 8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ) were measured using a Perkin Elmer Lambda 35 spectrophotometer, employing a $1 \mathrm { \ c m }$ quartz cuvette and Milli-Q water as blank. Absorbance was blank corrected (average absorbance between $7 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ) and converted to Napierian absorption coefficients: $a _ { \lambda }$ $( \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } ) = 2 . 3 0 3 A _ { \lambda } / r$ , where $A _ { \lambda }$ is the absorbance measured across pathlength $r$ at a wavelength of $\lambda$ (Helms et al. 2008).

Three indexes were derived from the absorption coefficients: (1) $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ $( \mathrm { L ~ m g ^ { - 1 } ~ m } ^ { - 1 } )$ , estimated as the absorption coefficient at $2 5 4  { \mathrm { ~ \textrm ~ { ~ ~ } ~ } }  { \mathrm { n m } }$ divided by the DOC concentration, which is a surrogate for DOM aromaticity (Weishaar et al. 2003), (2) Spectral slope coefficients, S $( \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 } )$ , were calculated using a linear fit of log-linearized absorption coefficients for $2 7 5 { - } 2 9 5 \quad \mathrm { n m }$ $( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ , 350– $4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ $( S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 } )$ , and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ $( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } / S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 } )$ , and used as proxies to DOM average molecular size (Helms et al. 2008), and (3) the absorption coefficient at $3 5 0 \ \mathrm { ~ n m } \ ( a _ { 3 5 0 } ) \ ( \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ which is indicative of CDOM content (Fichot and Benner 2012).

Fluorescence properties of DOM were measured on a Varian Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer at room temperature. EEMs were obtained for emission wavelengths from $2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ to $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ $2 \ \mathrm { n m }$ steps) and excitation wavelengths from $2 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ( $\mathrm { ~ 5 ~ n m }$ steps). The drEEM toolbox was used to analyze the EEMs (Murphy et al. 2013). Briefly, the spectra were corrected using the instrument correction factors; inner-filter correction was based on absorbance measurements; data were standardized and normalized to Raman units $( \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 } )$ based on measurements of Raman peak at $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ .

A data set of 118 EEMs including in situ and bioassays samples was compiled and characterized by PARAFAC employing the drEEMtoolbox (Murphy et al. 2013) for MATLAB (R2014b version, MathWorks, Central and South America). Three independent components (hereafter referred to as fluorophores C1–C3) were validated by split-half validation (Supporting Information Fig. 2), random-initialization analysis and by a thorough examination of the model’s residuals as described in Murphy et al. (2013). We further identified each PARAFAC component by comparison to published and reported components using the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al. 2014).

Three indexes were further derived from the EEMs: (1) humification index (Ohno 2002), which ranges from 0 to 1, with higher values indicating a higher degree of humification, (2) fluorescence index (FI) (McKnight et al. 2001), for which values $\sim 1 . 3$ suggest a dominant terrestrial, higherplant DOM source and values $\sim 1 . 8$ suggest a predominant microbial DOM source, and (3) freshness index (Parlanti et al. 2000), for which values $> 1$ correspond to freshly released DOM, whereas values $\sim \ 0 . 6 – 0 . 7$ correspond to a comparably higher terrestrial input.

# DOM characterization after microbial processing

Incubations of the in situ samples with an inoculum derived from the native bacterial community were conducted to evaluate the quality of the DOM consumed/produced by bacterial metabolism. At each sampling time and site, $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of water sample prefiltered ( $0 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size) was amended with $2 . 5 \mathrm { \ m L }$ of the water sample prefiltered $( 0 . 8 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size), acting as grazer-free bacterial inoculum. Incubations were conducted for 28 d in the dark at room temperature, to avoid photochemical alteration of the DOM. A spectral characterization (absorbance and fluorescence) of the DOM in the incubations was performed at the beginning and at the end of the assays, as detailed above.

# In situ abundance of main clades of aquatic bacteria

At each sampling time and site, $3 \ \mathrm { m L }$ of PFA-fixed water sample were filtered onto white $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size polycarbonate filters. The filters were rinsed with $1 \times$ phosphate buffered saline and with MQ water and stored at $- 2 0 \mathrm { { ^ \circ C } }$ until further processing. CARD-FISH was performed with probes targeting Alphaproteobacteria (ALF968), Betaproteobacteria (BET42a), Gammaproteobacteria (GAM42a), and Bacteroidetes (CF319a), as described previously for this system (Alonso et al. 2013). After hybridization, samples were counterstained with DAPI $( 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } \ : \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 } )$ ). The relative abundance of each targeted group was determined by epi-fluorescence microscopy, counting at least 1000 DAPI-stained objects per evaluation.

# Statistical analysis

All analysis was run in R (version 3.1.2; R Development Core Team, 2013).

DOM temporal and spatial differences between samples were analyzed by one-way ANOVA, and post hoc Tukey multiple comparisons of means $9 5 \%$ family-wise confidence level. We log-transformed not-normally distributed variables (visually checked with q–q plots and histograms and Shapiro–Wilk test). When data failed in normality or equal variance, even after simple transformations, Kruskal–Wallis (KW) tests were performed.

Spearman correlations (r) were used to explore (1) relationships among environmental variables and variables used to characterize DOM (i.e., spectroscopic indexes, PARAFAC components, and DOC concentration) (2) relationships among bacterial groups and DOM composition and concentration.

Multiple linear regression analysis (MLR) was performed for each bacterial group to find the best functional model relating bacterial abundance to environmental variables (in situ and nutrients) and DOM concentration and composition variables (i.e., DOC, spectroscopic indexes, and PARAFAC components). Before MLR, principal component analysis with all variables was performed to select and reduce the number of explanatory variables (not shown). We assessed the importance of each explanatory variable using the backward elimination procedure, to get the most parsimonious explanation of the data (Legendre and Legendre 2012). As selection criteria, the variable that contributed the least at each step, was excluded (highest $p$ contribution) until all explanatory variables had a significant $p$ $( < 0 . 0 5 )$ . We used the Akaike information criteria to select the best models, checked residuals for normality and variance homogeneity and outliers to maximize the coefficient of determination $( R ^ { 2 } )$ , and performed ANOVA between models to confirm that no information was lost with variables exclusion.

# Results

The sampling period covered an ample range of environmental conditions of this dynamic system (Table 1). In particular, salinity measurements ranged from oligohaline to polyhaline (3.2–23.2), and temperatures reflected the expected seasonal variation $( 9 . 9 \mathrm { - } 2 2 . 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ). Inorganic nutrients concentrations were also highly variable (Table 1).

# DOM concentration and quality

DOC concentrations were between $5 8 3 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ and $4 2 3 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ (Fig. 1), and no significant changes were found among sampling sites or dates. A weak but significant positive correlation was found between DOC and salinity $( r = 0 . 4 8 _ { \scriptscriptstyle \cdot }$ $p { < } 0 . 0 1 $ ).

The different spectroscopic indexes evidenced variations in DOM quality, rather linked to temporal than to spatial scale. Spectral slopes $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ (Fig. 2A) and $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ (Fig. 2B) exhibited significant temporal variability (ANOVA, $F =$ 32.66/24.53 $p { < } 0 . 0 0 0 1$ , respectively); $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ were significantly higher when the sandbar was closed (ANOVA, $F = 3 6 . 9 3 / 4 7 . 6 2$ , $p < 0 . 0 0 0 1 $ ). A strong negative correlation with temperature was found for both indicators $( r = - 0 . 8 1$ , $r = - 0 . 6 8$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 0 0 1$ , respectively). In addition, $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ displayed a negative correlation with DOC $( r = - 0 . 5 3$ , $\begin{array} { r } { p < 0 . 0 5 . } \end{array}$ ) suggesting an increase of high molecular weight (HMW) DOM with increasing carbon concentration. No significant correlations were found between spectral slopes and salinity. The Spectral ratio $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ did not exhibit any significant variation between samplings (data not shown).

The SUVA index varied between $0 . 1 9 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g ^ { - 1 } ~ m ^ { - 1 } }$ and $3 . 0 8 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g ^ { - 1 } ~ m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Fig. 2C); this range corresponds to aromatic carbon content of $7 . 3 \mathrm { - } 2 4 . 4 \%$ , according to the linear model developed by Weishaar et al. (2003). Although no spatial or temporal differences were found for this index, a significant negative correlation with salinity was found $( r = - 0 . 4 7$ , $p < 0 . 0 5 )$ ), indicating a higher aromatic content of the DOM at lower salinity. In contrast, the absorption coefficient $a _ { 3 5 0 }$ did show a temporal significant variation due to higher values in January 2014 (ANOVA, $F = 2 . 5 5 7$ , $p < 0 . 0 5$ ) (Fig. 2D). A positive significant correlation was further observed with $T$ $( r = 0 . 5 0$ , $\textstyle p < 0 . 0 5 ,$ ).

In regards to the indexes obtained from fluorescence measurements, the FI variations were not significant (Fig. 3A), with values in the range expected for DOM of mixed terrestrial and microbial origin (1.65–1.77). In contrast, significant temporal variations were observed for the Humification index (HIX) and the Freshness index (BIX). HIX generally exhibited relatively high values (around 0.9), with exceptions after December 2013 (KW, $p { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ , when it decreased, especially in the North and Centre sampling sites (Fig. 3B). It was negatively related to temperature and salinity $( r = - 0 . 6 3$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 0 5$ , and $r = - 0 . 4 3$ , $p < 0 . 0 5$ , respectively). Conversely, BIX followed an opposite pattern being generally low (around 0.6), with exceptions in December 2013 (KW, $p < 0 . 0 5 ,$ ), where values above 1 were found, particularly in the North and Centre sites (Fig. 3C). Moreover, this index was positively correlated to temperature $( r = 0 . 4 3$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ ) and salinity $( r = 0 . 6 6$ , $\begin{array} { r } { p < 0 . 0 0 5 , } \end{array}$ ), indicating the predominance of freshly produced DOM under higher temperatures and salinity values.

PARAFAC modeling allowed the identification and validation of three fluorescent components (C1, C2, and C3, Table 2, Supporting Information Fig. 2). Matching of the components with the Openfluor database showed that C1 has been described as humic of terrestrial origin (C1 in Stedmon et al. 2011), (C1 and C4 in Graeber et al. 2012) enriched with fulvic acids (C1 and C3 in Yamashita et al. 2010). This component is a mixture between peaks A and C according to Coble classification (Coble 1996; Yamashita et al. 2011). Peak A corresponds to terrestrial fulvic components, and peak C is associated with humic properties, and often linked to anthropogenic activities (i.e., agriculture) (Coble 1996), and positively related to percentage of wetland in the catchment (Graeber et al. 2012). C2 is a widespread component in aquatic environments (C2 in Kowalczuk et al. 2013), (C3 in Murphy et al. 2006), (C3 in Stedmon et al. 2007), and has been reported as terrestrial humic composed of high molecular weight and aromatic organic compounds (McKnight et al. 2001), but more recently also as product of microbial mineralization of algal exudates (Romera-Castillo et al. 2011). C3 component has been identified as protein-like, with tryptophan-derived fluorescence, derived from plankton, thus associated to autochtonous production (C4 in Stedmon et al. 2007), (C1 in Stedmon et al. 2011), and to peak T in Coble classification (Coble 1996).

# Changes in DOM spectral properties after microbial processing

The microbial transformations of the DOM involved changes in the FDOM composition, DOM molecular weight, aromaticity, and humification degree (Table 3). The most frequent changes for each variable were production of C1, production of C2, consumption of C3, increase in $a _ { 2 5 4 } ,$ increase in $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } ,$ , and increase in HIX. Thus, most incubations exhibited simultaneous variations in at least 3 variables indicative of more recalcitrant DOM as a result of microbial processing (Table 3).

# Dynamics of the main bacterial groups

Alphaproteobacteria was the dominant group, followed by Betaproteobacteria, Cytophaga-Flavobacteria and finally Gammaproteobacteria (Fig. 5). None of the groups exhibited significant variations in abundance between sampling sites.

Alphaproteobacteria neither displayed significant temporal differences in abundance (Fig. 5A). In contrast, Betaproteobacteria (Fig. 5B), Gammaproteobacteria (Fig. 5C) and Cytophaga-Flavobacteria (Fig. 5D) exhibited significant temporal variations $( F = 3 . 4$ , $p < 0 . 0 5$ ; $F = 3 . 8$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 1$ ; $F = 4 . 8$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 1$ , respectively). In all cases the maximal abundance of each group was found in summer samplings (Fig. 5). In addition, Gammaproteobacteria and Cytophaga-Flavobacteria abundances were higher when the lagoon was open to the ocean $( F = 4 . 4 , ~ p < 0 . 0 5 , ~ F = 5 . 0 ,$ $p { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ ).

A concur of different DOM sources in the system is indeed evident from the spectral characterization. In all samplings sites and times, FDOM was comprised of a mixture of components of terrestrial and in situ origin (Fig. 4) and DOM sources were not evident in simple DOC-salinity relations (Yamashita et al. 2013).

Negative DOC-salinity correlations and/or positive correlation between humic FDOM components and DOC have been interpreted as indication of a predominance of terrestrial sources of DOM (Cauwet 2002; Yamashita et al. 2011). Here, DOC concentration exhibited a significant positive correlation with salinity in contrast to what observed for marine waters (Fichot and Benner 2011), and was positively correlated with C3 (and not correlated with C1 and C2), and bacterial production (data not shown), indicating in situ production as main DOC driver. As C3 was in turn positively correlated with salinity, this might be the mechanism underlying the positive correlation between DOC and salinity found here.

Similar evidence might help understanding the absence of a positive correlation between spectral slopes and salinity (Helms et al. 2008), as in this study S were significantly lower (indicative of higher molecular weight) in the period when C3 increased its contribution to FDOM (Figs. 2, 4) and there was strong indication of dominance of freshly produced DOM (Fig. 3).

A marked change in the dominance of the different DOM sources was observed comparing the two sampling periods. From March 2013 to November 2013 the DOM was characterized by a relatively low molecular weight (Fig. 2A,B), high degree of humification (Fig. 3B) being relatively old (Fig. 3C) and mainly dominated by terrestrial-humic/fulvic components (Fig. 4) with an estimated aromatic content between $7 . 3 \%$ and $2 4 . 4 \%$ (Weishaar et al. 2003). All these indicators experienced notorious changes during the second phase of the study (November 2013–April 2014), with a transition towards predominance of freshly produced (Fig. 3C) HMW compounds (Fig. 2A,B), with much lower degree of humification (Fig. 3B), possibly explained by a notorious augment of protein-like components of microbial origin (Fig. 4), with an estimated aromatic content between $< 7 . 3 \%$ and $1 7 . 2 \%$ (Weishaar et al. 2003). These two periods were associated to the state of the connection to the sea and the water temperature (closed sandbar and comparably lower temperatures in the first phase vs. open sandbar and higher temperatures in the second phase). Modeling the effect of state of the sandbar and temperature on DOM quality, we found that the dynamics of the sand bar explained the variability observed in DOM molecular weight (var. exp. $6 6 \%$ , $\textstyle p < 0 . 0 0 0 5 ,$ ), while the effect of temperature was not significant for any DOM descriptor.

The observed transition in DOM quality apparently reflects a shift from terrestrially derived toward microbially derived DOM, but, a note of caution regarding the origin of the different fluorophores is provided by the biodegradations assays. The C1 and C2 components were both produced during bacterial processing of DOM, indicating that they can also be derived from microbial metabolism, as previously reported for C2 (Romera-Castillo et al. 2011; Guillemette and del Giorgio 2012). Furthermore, in the whole period the FI exhibited values closer to those expected for microbial origin vs. terrestrial origin.

Another note of caution while interpreting these results is that none of the most frequently used filter types prior to DOM analysis ensures a filtrate free from microbial contamination. On one hand, glass fiber filters (GF/F) have a nominal pore size of $0 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , which allows for the passage of small bacterial cells. On the other hand, filters of $0 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ are available only made from organic materials, and need filtration under pressure to render a suitable amount of sample, thus increasing the possibility of cell lysis (Coble et al. 2014). A recent study by Nimptsch et al. (2014) evidenced that calculation of several spectroscopic indexes and quantification of PARAFAC components is not substantially different between both types of filters, however this does not rule out the possibility that organic matter derived from microbial organisms could be quantified as truly dissolved.

Although is out of the scope of this study to evaluate the balance between different processes (e.g., allochtonous, photochemical, microbial), we can nevertheless conclude that microbial processes consume and produce all FDOM components identified in this study and that a substantial share of the FDOM in the system appears to be of microbial origin. Further work—including tributaries characterization—is needed to establish the share of each source into the concentration and composition of the DOM in the system.

# Strong linkages between main bacterial groups and DOM properties

A distinctive feature of this study is the observed linkages between DOM concentration and composition to BCC in a natural environment. Despite extensive research on bacteriaDOM interactions at multiple scales, DOM properties have been seldom taken into account when attempting to explain the distribution patterns of particular bacterial groups in natural aquatic systems (Teeling et al. 2012, Ruiz-Gonzalez et al. - 2015). In this system—presumably due to working in mesohaline conditions—we could go beyond the strong structuring effect of salinity (Lozupone and Knight 2007), and determine that the abundance of the main aquatic clades was significantly controlled by at least two parameters related to DOM concentration or composition (Eq. 1-4).

Alphaproteobacteria appeared as the group whose abundance was almost exclusively controlled by resources in the system, in agreement with previous studies (Alonso et al. 2013). DOM concentrations and properties were among the most important ones (Eq. 1). The Betaproteobacteria, Gammaproteobacteria and Cytophaga-Flavobacteria, had a higher degree of unexplained variation, presumably due to a stronger top–down control typically observed in members of these groups (Simek et al. 2001).

Interestingly, the combination and significance of the factors controlling bacterial abundance in this system was specific for each of the targeted bacterial groups, indicating that they do behave as coherent units, at least to some extent.

A closer look into the significant relationships between DOM properties and the main bacterial groups made it possible to delineate a profile of each group regarding DOM preferences/dislikes. These results agree with evidence derived from very different approaches, from genome analysis to single-cell substrate uptake. Thus, based on the MLR and Spearman correlations Alphaproteobacteria in this system would prefer low DOC concentrations and terrestrial humic DOM enriched in fulvic acids and characterized by low molecular weight, and low lignin content. This profile agrees with preferences by low substrate concentrations (Alonso and Pernthaler 2006a) and low molecular weight components reported for the main Alphaproteobacteria groups in different aquatic systems (Cottrell and Kirchman 2000; AlonsoSaez and Gasol 2007). These results thus concur with the oligotrophic life-style postulated for the most abundant members of this bacterial class and delineated from cultivation to genomic approaches (Rappe et al. 2002; Lauro et al. 2009).

In contrast, Gammaproteobacteria appeared as the only group with high DOC concentrations as the main DOMrelated factor controlling their abundance in the system. Also, they would prefer compounds of terrestrial origin but characterized by higher molecular weight. These results are in agreement with the copiotrophic style proposed for many members of this class, generalists from the point of view of DOM utilization, being particularly stimulated in incubations amended with lignin-derived, plant-derived material, or high-molecular-weight semi-labile compounds (Mou et al. 2008; Lauro et al. 2009; McCarren et al. 2010; Alonso et al. 2013). Also, their negative correlation with the Freshness index supports the hypothesis that this group might subsist on recycled and/or allochtonous DOM (Salcher 2014).

Although not directly controlled by DOC concentrations, Cytophaga-Flavobacteria members were positively correlated with DOC concentration, and with preferences for terrestrial humic DOM enriched in fulvic acids, with high molecular weight, low aromatic content and low degree of humification. Preference for high molecular weight compounds is a hallmark of this group, evidenced by polymer degradation by isolates, in situ uptake of polymers, and gene content ((Reichenbach 1992; Cottrell and Kirchman 2000; Teeling et al. 2012). The high number of DOM variables to which this group related to (but with comparatively low significance of each of them), together with the lowest percentage of explanation provided by the RLM model for their abundance, suggests that this group might be somehow less coherent in terms of DOM processing. This might be due to the observation that Flavobacteria are typically composed by several more or less equally abundant populations, which might differ in their eco-physiology and controlling factors (Gomez-Pereira et al. 2010).

Also, Betaproteobacteria displayed a pattern that could derive from the presence of equally abundant populations with different physiologies. This group appeared associated with protein-like DOM and low humification, but also with terrestrial and relatively old DOM. Abundant members of this cluster (Polynucleobacter sp.) have shown preferences for allochntonous humic-like DOM, and its photo-oxidation derivatives, while others (Limnohabitans sp.) appear as specialists for autochtonous low molecular weight fresh-DOM, exhibiting copiotrophic lifestyles (Burkert et al. 2003; P-erez and Sommaruga 2006; Buck et al. 2009). Both groups often exhibit equivalent abundances in natural environments, particularly in coastal lagoons (Alonso et al. 2009), so the observed pattern could arise from the particularities of these populations.

# Implications for modeling microbial DOM processing

Most frequently, microbial transformations of DOM resulted in the lowering of DOM molecular weight, increased humification degree, increased aromatic content, production of C1 and C2, and consumption of C3, indicating that the microbial processing led to a more recalcitrant DOM, consistent with the size-reactivity continuum model (Benner and Amon 2014). This evidence, together with the dominance of C1 and C2 in situ, and the high FI indicative of the important microbial contribution to the DOM in this system, suggest that the bacterial metabolism at this coastal environment is often transforming inputs of fresh-available DOM into more refractory compounds presumably resulting in carbon sequestration via the microbial carbon pump (Jiao et al. 2010). Further work is needed (e.g., coupling with field $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ capture and emission measurements) to assess the extent in which bacterial metabolism contributes to the sequestration and emission of carbon here.

In reference to the adequacy of the level of resolution utilized to analyze the links between bacterial community and DOM, on one hand, recent evidence has been provided indicating that the molecular families identified by application of FTICR-MS are significantly linked to DOM optical properties (Stubbins et al. 2014), thus supporting their use as adequate DOM descriptors.

On the bacterial side, our results provide strong support to the delineation of functional groups rather concordant with the phylogenetic definition provided by our tool of analysis, roughly corresponding to classes. In comparison to the published literature, this would agree with the evidence derived from studies on bacterial-DOM interaction, mainly looking at direct substrate uptake by individual cells that have shown coherent patterns of DOM utilization at this level of resolution (Cottrell and Kirchman 2000; Alonso et al. 2012; Alonso-Saez et al. 2012). In contrast, recent stud- - ies analyzing biodegradation experiments with increased resolution for BCC (high-throughput sequencing) have failed in finding correlation patterns between BCC and utilization of different DOM components, or bioavailable DOC concentration, or even with DOM components identified in a detailed characterization (FT-ICR-MS) (Mou et al. 2008; Dinasquet et al. 2013; Herlemann et al. 2014, Ruiz-Gonzalez - et al. 2015). These results contradicted our expectations, as higher resolution in one or both characterizations did not put in evidence meaningful patterns, further stirring the issue of which is the adequate level of resolution for describing these relationships. Here, using an intermediate level of resolution for both, BCC and DOM composition, we could find strong significant correlations. Our conclusion is that, without neglecting the enormous diversity within each clade, they could still act as functional groups in a context of biogeochemical modeling of the transformations of meaningful DOM components.
