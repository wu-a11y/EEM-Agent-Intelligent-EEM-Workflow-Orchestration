We have developed the package albatross in freely available R environment to perform parallel factor analysis of excitation-emission matrices (EEM/PARAFAC) analysis of seawaters. The key pre-processing steps for successful data analysis included intensity corrections for the instrument response function; inner filter efect correction; Raman and Rayleigh scattering handling. After implementing the necessary routines, we have focused on evaluation of their robustness for analysis of real data as well as on model validation and the assessment of adequacy of identified factors. The tolerance of $1 0 ^ { - 9 }$ in the absolute change of $R ^ { 2 }$ was found to be enough to prevent stopping before reaching the solution during the modeling. We have found that widely used $S _ { 4 } C _ { 6 } T _ { 3 }$ split-half procedure for validation cannot result in an unequivocal selection of the number of components. We have instead suggested a statistical evaluation of 100 randomly obtained splits to optimize number of factors ("randomized split-half). The median split-half distance allows finding the unique optimal decomposition of EEM (4-component model in our case)， and, therefore, it can serve as a criterion for model validation. # 331. Introduction

Three-dimensional fluorescence spectroscopy [1] combined with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) [2,3] of excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) is a powerful tool for studying fluorescence properties of a mixture of fluorophores [4]. It is used, for example, for investigation of the presence of fluorescence components and their transformation in drinking waters and wastewaters [5], food [6,7], oil spills [8], etc. Along with traditional chromatographic methods, this technique is proposed for quality control in production of sugars [9] and pharmaceuticals [10]. One of the most common fields of the use of EEMs decomposition by PARAFAC is the study of distribution, dynamics and transformation of natural dissolved organic matter (DOM). Indeed, DOM represents the largest pools of organic carbon in aquatic ecosystems and its dynamics have implications for carbon cycling on local to global scales [11,12]. Until 2003,the use of fluorescence in the study of composition of natural DOM was limited mostly to the qualitative identification of fluorophore groups [13], as well as the calculation of characteristic relationships, so-called optical indices, which allow estimation the bioavailability of organic matter, extent of humification, and determination of predominant DOM sources [14].   
Application of the trilinear decomposition techniques allowed a more detailed analysis of EEMs than traditional peak-picking methods. Thus， PARAFAC provide estimates of the relative contribution of each component to total DOM fluorescence, that opens up new opportunities for investigation of the optical properties of aquatic DOM[15]. At present EEM/PARAFAC analysis is widely used for study of seasonal and spatial variability in the magnitude, source and composition of DOM[16-18], binding the heavy metals with DOM[19-21], tracing water masses [22], evaluating an impact of permafrost thaw on DOM composition in the Arctic [23,24], and many others.

The IUPAC technical report stresses the need of calibration, correction and standardization of EEM data for the correct use of chemometric data analysis. The key issues that should be considered step by step include intensity corrections for both the variations in the instrument response function and the quantitative comparison between instruments; inner filter effects (IFE) correction for optically thick samples; data pre-processing, first of all Raman and Rayleigh scattering handling [25]. Suggested by Determann et al. [26] normalization of fluorescence intensity in waters by Raman scattering (Raman units) instead of well-known quinine sulfate have become universal technique for a broad range of fluorescence applications [27]. A special atention is needed for accurate Raman peak areas calculations to produce data in units that are comparable between instruments [28]. # 2.1. Sample collection

A total of 66 seawater samples with salinity ranging from O to 34 psu and DOC varying between 1.2 to $1 2 . 1 \ \mathrm { m g / l }$ [47-50] were taken during the 63th cruise of RV Akademik Mstislav Keldysh in August- September, 2015. A total of 17 samples from the delta regions of the Northern Dvina and Lena Rivers and desalinated waters of the Kara Sea were characterized by minimal values of salinity taken. # 2.4. PARAFAC modeling

All calculation code was writen in R language using the albatross package; which was recently published by us on CRAN [62]. The albatross package relies on the multiway R package for the actual PARAFAC computation [42]. All PARAFAC models in this work were built with non-negative constraints because the underlying physical model requires the concentrations and the excitation and emission spectra to be non-negative to produce positive intensity values. The fitting procedure from the multiway package uses the absolute change in $R ^ { 2 }$ to stop the optimization process. Since the procedure should ensure the robust handling of experimental EEM dataset, the influence of the stop criterion on a model needs to be thoroughly studied. Randomly initialized PARAFAC models of the dataset were repeatedly (10 times per each combination) fited for different stopping criterion value and number of components. Resulting components were then matched according to highest pairwise Tucker's congruence coefficient (TCC) between all repetitions (given 10 repetitions, one can compare each of them with 9 other resulting decompositions,which results in 45 different TCC values after accounting for comparison symmetry), and the resulting overall TCC values were analyzed. It should be noted that TCC values were sufficiently lower in case of EEMs with missing data, and, therefore this dataset was excluded from further consideration. In Figure 5 distributions of TCC values, medians and confidence bands are presented. The tolerance of $1 0 ^ { - 9 }$ in $R ^ { 2 }$ was found to be enough to reach the goal. It should be noted that the more rigorous tolerance criterion results in huge growth of calculation costs and even continuous process sometimes.

# 3. Results and discussions

To ensure stable solution and prevent overfiting, the number of components was selected using split-half procedure [2]. Often all obtained components are placed in the same plot, and the model with similar components in subsets is used. Such an approach is poorly algorithmized in general, since it intrinsically requires the visual expertise of “similarity". Bro et al [4] have suggested the $S _ { 4 } C _ { 6 } T _ { 3 }$ method which involves splitting the dataset into 4 equally-sized parts,combining them into ${ \frac { \binom { 4 } { 2 } } { 2 } } = 6$ different halves,then comparing the decompositions obtained from halves that do not have any samples in common (3 comparisons in total). This approach is easily integrated into EEM decomposition technique and requires moderate increase of calculations. Simultaneously, the correct handling of experimental data obtained for natural water samples can be complicated due to high variability of fluorophores, when some of them present in a few samples only. We suppose that statistical evaluation ofthe model using numerous split-halves provides more reliable validation and exclude accidental decisions based on a limited sampling. We have published the code as package named albatross on CRAN including detailed documentation for its subroutines [62]. The "albatross" package provides convenient tools for fluorescence excitation-emission matrix preprocessing (scattering signal handling, inner filter effect correction)， PARAFAC fitting and validation, including “randomized split-half’ and commonly used tests (jack-knifing and S4C3T6 algorithm). The conversion of the fluorescence intensity to Raman units and replacement of scattered signal with smooth interpolated surface were found to be optimal pre-processing steps. Although the Raman band of water excited at $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ is widely used for fluorescence signal correction, the relatively low intensity with regard to shift to fluorescence peak prevents the robust quantification of the Raman intensity. The main concern that gave raise to randomized split-half procedure instead of widely used $S _ { 4 } C _ { 6 } T _ { 3 }$ method is the diversity of the samples that are present in the dataset and may therefore affect one split but not the other. One of the main features of developed procedure is fully criterial test based on statistical evaluation of 100 randomly obtained splits to optimize number of factors. We have provided automatic assignment of components, calculation of pairwise distances, and estimation of their distributions for numeric description of the procedure that makes it possible compare results of stability analysis across research groups and PARAFAC implementations. Although the used number of splits explores a small part of the split space the suggested approach seems desirable than consideration of just 4 splits in case of $S _ { 4 } C _ { 6 } T _ { 3 }$ method. Moreover， the $S _ { 4 } C _ { 6 } T _ { 3 }$ can accidentally results in different number of recommended factors. Thus randomized split half is the good tool for model validation for now with intent to work more for a better solution based on a stratified bootstrap procedure.
