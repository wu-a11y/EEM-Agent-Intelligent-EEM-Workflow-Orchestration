Artificial barriers such as dams and weirs profoundly affect the hydrological conditions of aquatic ecosystems and organic carbon dynamics. This study examines the sources, characteristics, and spatial and seasonal variations in dissolved organic matter (DOM) in the Yeongsan River estuary, South Korea, impacted by an estuary dam. We explore how these barriers influence DOM concentrations and compositions seasonally. Observations from five sites (YR1 to YR5) revealed high levels of fluorescence index (FI, 1.7–1.9), biopolymer fraction $( 2 - 1 6 \% )$ , and percentage of dissolved organic sulfur $( \mathrm { D O S \% }$ , up to $4 2 \%$ ), indicating a dominant algal contribution. The dam’s installation disrupted the DOM continuum, distinguishing between upstream sites with terrestrial DOM and downstream sites influenced by marine sources. Compared to downstream sites, upstream sites consistently showed higher dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and absorption at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m } \ ( \mathrm { a } _ { 2 5 4 } )$ , with depleted $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC across all seasons. Seasonal differences included higher concentrations of humic substances and biopolymers in upstream sites during spring, with pronounced protein-like fluorescence. During the monsoon, these sites had enhanced humic substances, biopolymers, low molecular weight neutrals, and peak N fluorescence associated with chlorophyll intensity. The dam controlled freshwater discharge to downstream sites, especially during peak monsoon. Stable isotopic values $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC) confirmed distinct DOM sources between upstream and downstream sites. Notably, only the middle site (YR3) displayed unique characteristics in the pre-monsoon season, indicating the monsoon’s residual impact on DOM. The lower estuary likely experiences extended water residence times post-monsoon. This study underscores the significant influence of phytoplankton blooms and distinct spatiotemporal variations in DOM characteristics in an estuarine system disrupted by artificial barriers.

# 1. Introduction

Estuaries, situated at the interface between land and ocean, are critical, dynamic, and vulnerable zones where riverine freshwater converges with marine saltwater. During estuarine mixing, a range of complex geochemical transformations occur, including changes in pH, particle aggregation, colloidal binding of certain metals, and flocculation of dissolved organic matter (DOM). These changes are primarily driven by increased salinity and the presence of divalent cations (Mosley and Liss, 2020). With the escalating demand for water resources and flood control in urbanized regions, the construction of dams and weirs along rivers and within estuaries has become increasingly common worldwide. However, these structures significantly disrupt not only the freshwater flow and the river-estuary-ocean continuum, but also the biogeochemical cycling of sediments, nutrients, and organic carbon. The consequences of damming, which are compounded by wastewater discharge and surface runoff from agricultural, urban, and industrial sources upstream and within watershed basins, are multifaceted. They include the trapping of sediments and organic matter within dams, alteration of water residence time (WRT), water stratification, the development of hypoxic conditions in bottom waters, and the occurrence of algal blooms, among other effects (Hopkinson and Vallino, 1995; Kim et al., 2017a, 2017b; Chen et al., 2017; Park et al., 2020; Kim and Shin, 2021). These alterations can lead to significant changes in DOM characteristics and have profound water quality and environmental consequences.

The Yeongsan River estuary in South Korea serves as an exemplary site for examining the impacts of artificial barriers on aquatic ecosystems. Since the construction of the estuary dam approximately four decades ago, many studies have investigated changes in water quality, structure, trophic dynamics, nutrient levels, and algal bloom characteristics in this dam-affected temperate estuary (Cho et al., 2004; Kim et al., 2017a; Park et al., 2020; Kim and Shin, 2021; Yoon et al., 2023). These studies have revealed that anthropogenic interventions in the river-estuary continuum have significantly altered biogeochemical processes and water quality. Principal causes include increased sedimentation rates and organic matter deposition inside the dam, water stratification, hypoxia in bottom waters, and frequent algal blooms (Kim and Shin, 2021; Jargal et al., 2021). Further observations indicate higher concentrations of organic matter and nutrients upstream of the estuary dam. It was found that the vertical fluxes of nutrients and organic matter, as well as phytoplankton size structure, are predominantly governed by artificial regulation of freshwater discharge through the dam gates, rather than by natural monsoon meteorological events, with increased frequency during the summer monsoon season (Sin et al., 2013, 2015; Kim et al., 2017a; Yoon et al., 2023). Additionally, in both the upstream and downstream regions of the dam, algal blooms, dominated by different algal species according to season and location, occur during the summer monsoon and winter. These blooms lead to rapid increases in primary productivity in seawater following freshwater discharges (Sin et al., 2013, 2015). Notably, opening the estuary dam could potentially reduce algal blooms by up to $8 3 \%$ in the dam region (Kim and Shin, 2021). Despite the numerous studies carried out in this estuary, no studies have been found regarding the DOM characteristics and sources, which is a one of the key players of water quality, ecosystem structure and function, and climate change as well (Findlay and Sinsabaugh, 2003; Chen et al., 2010, 2017).

Various methods have been employed to elucidate the dynamics and biogeochemistry of DOM in natural and dam-impacted estuary (Medeiros, 2022; Kumar et al., 2022). For instance, the $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values of dissolved organic carbon $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { \mathrm { D O C } } )$ are widely used to trace the origins of DOM. In aquatic ecosystems, $8 ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C _ { D O C } }$ levels are influenced by allochthonous (external) and autochthonous (internal) organic matter sources. The majority of C3 plants typically exhibit lighter $8 ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C _ { D O C } }$ values ranging from $- 3 4 \text{‰}$ to $- 2 3 \text{‰}$ , while C4 plants, such as corn and sugarcane, display heavier values between $- 1 9 \text{‰}$ and $- 9 \text{‰}$ (Deines, 1980). Freshwater phytoplankton generally show $8 ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C _ { D O C } }$ values from $- 3 2 \text{‰}$ to $- 2 3 \text{‰}$ , contrasting with marine phytoplankton, which range from $- 2 2 \text{‰}$ to $- 1 8 \text{‰}$ (Kelleyl et al., 1998; Finlay and Kendall, 2007). Moreover, advanced techniques like fluorescence excitation-emission matrix coupled with parallel factor analysis (EEM-PARAFAC), size exclusion chromatography coupled with an organic carbon detector (SEC-OCD), and high-resolution Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FT-ICR MS) have been utilized. These methods are instrumental in studying the fluorescent composition, molecular size distributions, and molecular-level composition within bulk DOM (Chen et al., 2016a,b). Such approaches enable a more comprehensive understanding of DOM dynamics, shedding light on their origins, physical mixing processes, and biogeochemical alterations (Raymond and Bauer, 2001; Chen et al., 2010). This comprehensive study is important for providing a panoramic view of DOM characteristics and dynamics across seasons in a dam-affected temperate estuary, as very few similar studies have been conducted before. A prior study in India used very limited analytical methods (Kumar et al., 2022).

This study delved into the dynamics of DOM along a salinity gradient in a dam-disrupted temperate estuary, employing a range of DOM characterization methods. The main goal was to elucidate the impact of the dam installation on DOM biogeochemistry and water quality. Specifically, the study was designed with two primary objectives: 1) to examine the spatiotemporal patterns of DOM dynamics along the estuary, and 2) to trace the spatial variations in potential DOM sources within the estuary across seasons.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Site descriptions

The Yeongsan River, spanning approximately $1 4 0 \mathrm { k m }$ in length with a catchment area of around $3 4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , is one of South Korea’s major rivers (Park et al., 2020). It is a vital source of drinking water, irrigation, and industrial water for the southwest region of the country. An estuary dam, measuring $4 . 3 \mathrm { k m }$ in length and $1 9 . 5 \mathrm { m }$ in height, was constructed $7 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ from the river’s mouth in 1981 to improve water resource management for irrigation and land reclamation (Fig. 1) (Park et al., 2020).

In the estuary, the average width is approximately $2 \ \mathrm { k m }$ , and the mean water depth ranges between 10 and $2 0 \textrm { m }$ . The estuarine area experiences a semidiurnal tide, with tidal amplitudes of about $3 \textrm { m }$ during neap tides and $6 \mathrm { m }$ during spring tides (Park et al., 2020). South Korea’s average annual precipitation is around $1 3 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , with roughly two-thirds occurring during the summer monsoon season (Korea Meteorological Administration: https://www.kma.go.kr). This period coincides with approximately $8 0 \%$ of the annual freshwater discharge of $1 . 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 9 } \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ (from July to September) (Cho et al., 2004). The annual average water residence time (WRT) in the upstream Yeongsan Lake is about 33 days (Kim et al., 2017b). However, the WRT in the estuarine section of the Yeongsan River is relatively short, approximately 5–10 days, during the summer monsoon period, owing to the rapid flushing rate (Kim et al., 2017a). Previous studies reported a multi-layered water structure in the Yeongsan River estuary during summer, comprising a surface freshwater layer, a cold middle layer, and a warmer saline bottom layer, with vertical mixing occurring in spring (Cho et al., 2004). The major land uses in the Yeongsan River basin include agriculture, forest, and urban settlements (Fig. S4).

# 2.2. Sampling and basic water chemistry analyses

Surface water sampling was conducted at five distinct sites along the estuary, designated from upstream to downstream as YR1, YR2, YR3, YR4, and YR5, during January, April, July, September, and December of 2022, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Water samples were collected using an aspirator system that transferred water directly into high-density polyethylene carboys via Tygon tubing. All sampling tubes and bottles were cleaned with HCl and rinsed with Milli-Q water. In-situ measurements of temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen (DO), and salinity were recorded at each site using a multiparameter water quality sonde (YSI ProDSS). The collected water samples, with a volume of $4 \mathrm { L }$ each, were transported to the laboratory in an ice box and subsequently stored in a refrigerator until further analysis.

For DOM analyses, approximately $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ L ~ } }$ of each sample were filtered through pre-ashed $( 4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } , \mathrm { ~ } 5 ~ \mathrm { h } )$ $0 . 4 5 – \mu \mathrm { m }$ glass fiber filters. Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations were then measured using a hightemperature combustion total organic carbon analyzer (TOC-L CPH/ CPN, Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan).

# 2.3. Stable isotope analyses of $\delta ^ { 1 3 } C$ -DOC

The bulk $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC measurements were carried out using an isotope ratio mass spectrometer (isoprime visION; Elementar UK Ltd.,

Manchester, UK) in conjunction with a TOC analyzer (Vario TOC Cube; Elementar Analysensysteme GmbH). The δ values $( \\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\\ ) _ { 0 0 } )$ were determined by the formula:

$$
\delta ^ { 1 3 } C = [ \left( \mathrm { R } _ { \mathrm { s a m p l e } } \right) / \left( \mathrm { R } _ { \mathrm { s t a n d a r d } } \right) - 1 ] \times 1 0 0 0
$$

Where R represents the $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } / ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { C }$ ratio. For calibration, the internationally recognized reference standard, Vienna PeeDee Belemnite, obtained from the International Atomic Energy Agency in Vienna, Austria, was employed. The standardization of the δ13C values utilized the IAEA-CH6 sucrose standard $( - 1 0 . 4 4 9 \% 0 )$ from the same agency, achieving an analytical precision of $0 . 1 \\\text{‰}$ (Kim et al., 2023).

# 2.4. Analytical measurements of DOC and optical data

A TOC analyzer (Shimadzu TOC-VCPH) was employed for the measurement of DOC concentrations. EEMs were scanned using a Hitachi F7000 luminescence spectrometer (Hitachi Inc., Japan). Both the excitation and emission slits were set to $1 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , with excitation/emission wavelengths (ex/em) ranging from 200 to $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and $2 8 0 { - } 5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. Absorption spectra were recorded from 200 to $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ utilizing a Shimadzu 1800 ultraviolet–visible (UV–Vis) spectrophotometer (Shimadzu Inc., Japan). All corrected DOM EEMs $\mathbf { ( n = 7 5 ) }$ ) were utilized for modeling purposes. PARAFAC modeling was conducted using MATLAB (version 7.0.4) equipped with the DOMFluor toolbox (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). For comprehensive information regarding these measurement methodologies, post-acquisition correction procedures for EEM data, and normalization of fluorescence intensity to Raman Unit (RU), readers are referred to existing literature (Chen et al., 2010).

# 2.5. Analytical measurements of SEC-OCD

SEC coupled with an organic carbon detector (DOC Labor Dr. Huber, Karlsruhe, Germany) was utilized to assess the molecular weight (MW) distribution of the samples. The chromatographic column employed was a TSK HW $5 0 ~ { \cal { S } } ,$ , measuring $2 5 0 \times 2 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ (Toso, Japan). A phosphate buffer at $\mathrm { p H } ~ 6 . 8 5$ was used as the mobile phase, with a flow rate maintained at $1 . 1 \mathrm { \ m L / m i n }$ . The nominal average molecular weights (Mn) distributions were categorized into five fractions. A detailed methodological description is provided in the literature (Huber et al., 2011; Chen et al., 2016b).

# 2.6. Solid phase extraction pretreatment and measurements of FT-ICR MS

The solid phase extraction (SPE) process was employed to concentrate DOM prior to FT-ICR-MS analysis, as described in a previous literature (Dittmar et al., 2008). Bond Elut cartridges $_ { 1 \textbf { \ g g } }$ sorbent, Agilent Inc.) were used for this purpose. Before use, the PPL cartridges were conditioned by rinsing with methanol and thoroughly washed with acidified Milli-Q water. The water samples were acidified to $\mathrm { p H } 2$ using HPLC-grade HCl and subsequently passed through the pre-cleaned cartridge. After drying, the retained DOM was eluted from the cartridges with methanol. The FT-ICR MS measurements were conducted using a 15-T instrument, equipped with an Apollo II electrospray ionization source (ESI, Bruker Daltonik, Germany). The negative ion mode was selected for these measurements, with a signal-to-noise $( S / \mathbb { N } )$ ratio threshold set at $\geq 4$ . Spectral evaluation was conducted in the mass range of $m / z 2 0 0 { - } 6 5 0$ . The mass accuracy threshold was set at $| \Delta \mathbf { m } | \leq 1$ ppm. Elemental ratio criteria applied included a hydrogen to carbon (H/C) ratio between 0.3 and 2.2 and an oxygen to carbon (O/C) ratio of less than 1.2. The double bond equivalent (DBE) was calculated using the formula: $\mathrm { D B E } = 1 + 1 / 2$ $( 2 \mathsf { C } - \mathsf { H } + \mathsf { N } )$ (Koch and Dittmar, 2006, 2016). Further details of the methodology can be found in a previous report (Chen et al., 2016b).

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Seasonal variations of water chemistry and DOM along the estuary

In East Asia, seasonal patterns are distinctly marked by the monsoon (July to September) and non-monsoon seasons. Initially, data were grouped into these two seasons to facilitate comparisons. However, this classification did not reveal significant seasonality in many parameters (Fig. S7). Consequently, data were reanalyzed using three distinct seasons—April (spring), July and September (monsoon), and December and January (winter)—to better capture clear seasonal trends (Fig. 3).

Water temperatures in the estuary ranged from a low of $4 . 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in mid-January to a peak of $2 8 . 3 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in mid-July (Table S1). pH values varied from 6.3 at YR1 and YR2 in early December to 8.1 at YR2 in late September. Dissolved oxygen (DO) levels were generally lower in July and December compared to other months. Bulk dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations fluctuated between $0 . 8 9 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ and $5 . 2 1 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ , with spring and monsoon seasons generally recording significantly higher levels than in winter at both upstream and downstream sites (Fig. 2). These DOC levels are higher than those in the Pearl River Estuary in South China $( 0 . 9 { - } 2 . 3 \ \mathrm { m g / L } )$ (Zhang et al., 2023) but are comparable to those in the Godavari River Estuary in India (approximately $0 . 6 { - } 6 . 0 \mathrm { m g / L } \rangle$ ), which also features an estuary dam (Kumar et al., 2022).

For chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM), the absorption coefficient at a wavelength of $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m } ( \mathrm { a } _ { 2 5 4 } )$ varied significantly, ranging from approximately 1 to $2 3 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { \mathord { \sim } 1 }$ . Notably, $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were higher during the monsoon and winter seasons at downstream sites. The maximum $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ value of $2 3 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ was much higher than that observed in the Pearl River Estuary in China without estuary dams $( 1 4 \mathrm m ^ { - 1 } )$ (Zhang et al., 2023). Specific ultraviolet absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { \Delta S U V A _ { 2 5 4 } ) }$ , an indicator of CDOM aromaticity, ranged between 0.2 and $2 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { g }$ . The $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were same as the higher end observed in the Pearl River Estuary $( 2 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { g } )$ , but the lower end $( 0 . 2 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { g } )$ was below the 0.5 $\mathbf { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathbf { g }$ recorded there (Zhang et al., 2023). Notably, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were relatively low in December $( 0 . 2 { - } 0 . 6 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { g }$ across all sites), suggesting minimal contributions from higher plant or soil-derived DOM and significant contributions from algal DOM.

Four EEM-PARAFAC components were identified for fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM) (Fig. S3). The components $\mathrm { C } _ { 3 3 0 / 4 1 5 } ,$ C275/388, C(265)375/450, and $\mathrm { C _ { 2 9 0 / 3 0 5 } } ,$ where $\mathbf { X }$ and $\mathbf { y }$ represent the excitation and emission wavelength pairs corresponding to the highest peak in each component $\mathrm { ( C _ { x / y } ) }$ , were categorized as microbial humic-like, peak N (associated with chlorophyll intensity) (Coble et al., 1998), terrestrial humic-like, and protein-like components, respectively. These categorizations were based on comparisons with an open-access spectral database (http://www.openfluor.org, Table 3). Similar components have been previously reported in limnic, estuarine, and coastal environments. Overall, the first three components exhibited higher values during the monsoon season compared to the non-monsoon season across all sampling sites. Conversely, the protein-like component showed lower values during the monsoon season, with this trend being more pronounced at the upstream sites of the estuary dam (YR1 and YR2).

From the SEC-OCD measurements, five distinct size fractions were identified, ordered by increasing retention time: biopolymers $( \mathbf { M } _ { \mathrm { n } } > 1 0 \mathbf { k }$ Da), humic substances (HS, $\mathbf { M } _ { \mathrm { n } } \approx 1 \mathrm { ~ k ~ }$ Da), building blocks (BB, $\mathrm { { M _ { n } } = }$ 350–500 Da), low molecular weight acids (LMWA, $\mathrm { M } _ { \mathrm { n } } < 3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { D a }$ ), and low molecular weight neutrals (LMWN, $\mathrm { M _ { n } } < 3 5 0 \mathrm { D a }$ ) (Table 3, Fig. S5). Notably, the fraction of humic substances was significantly elevated during the monsoon season, indicative of increased terrestrial inputs from catchment runoff. The highest level of the biopolymers fraction $( 7 1 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ was recorded in April, a pre-monsoon period, at the upstream site YR2, highlighting a substantial algal contribution to DOM. Despite this, no marked differences were observed in the biopolymer fraction between monsoon and non-monsoon seasons. Similarly, clear trends were not evident for the other fractions. It is particularly noteworthy that the LMW acids fraction was very low $( 0 { - } 1 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ , whereas the LMW neutrals fraction was generally higher $( 8 4 1 - 1 7 5 2 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ in July, suggesting different DOM origins for these two low molecular weight fractions. Additionally, molecular formulas derived from FT-ICR MS analysis (Fig. 3, Table S3) exhibited higher weight-averaged double bond equivalent $\mathrm { ( D B E _ { w a } ) }$ , weight-averaged modified aromatic index $( \mathrm { { A I } _ { m , w a } ) }$ , and percentages of dissolved organic nitrogen (DON), polyphenols, peptides, and condensed aromatics during the monsoon season at upstream sites. Conversely, there was a lower percentage of aliphatics and highly unsaturated molecular formulas, underscoring a significant seasonal influence on the molecular composition of DOM at these locations.

# 3.2. Spatial variations of water chemistry and DOM along the estuary

The spatial variations between upstream (YR1, YR2) and downstream (YR3 to YR5) sites were pronounced (Fig. 3, Fig. S7). At upstream sites, the levels of DOC, $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , humic substances fraction, building blocks fraction, biopolymers fraction, and humic-like components were significantly higher, indicating mixed DOM sources from substantial inputs not only from terrestrial vascular plants and/or soils but also from algal sources. Previous studies have documented an increased presence of nutrients and chlorophyll $a$ (chl-a) upstream of the estuary dam (Sin et al., 2013, 2015). Conversely, the slope ratio $( S _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ , a proxy for CDOM molecular size, was lower upstream than downstream, suggesting a larger molecular size at the upstream sites (Helms et al., 2008). The salinity at upstream sites (specifically, YR1 and YR2) remained at low levels, ranging from 0.3 practical salinity units (psu) at YR1 in July to 1.9 psu at YR2 in December, indicating predominant freshwater influence (Table S1). In contrast, salinity at the lower estuary sites (YR4 and YR5) consistently remained high, fluctuating between 27.0 psu at YR4 in July and 32.9 psu at YR5 in April, reflecting a strong marine influence.

However, freshwater inputs during the peak of the monsoon season in July were substantial, as demonstrated at site YR3 in the middle estuary, where salinity exhibited significant seasonal fluctuations from 19.7 psu in July to 31.6 psu in April and December. The lower salinity in July at this site suggests a mix of freshwater and marine water inputs during the peak of the monsoon season. DO levels also varied significantly across different sites and times. The highest DO level was recorded at 12.7 $\scriptstyle { \mathrm { m g / L } }$ at YR1 in April. In stark contrast, DO levels at the lower estuary sites (YR4 and YR5) were markedly lower, with values ranging between 2.3 and $2 . 5 \mathrm { m g } / \mathrm { L }$ in July.

The bulk DOC levels varied significantly, ranging from 5.21 $( \pm 0 . 0 5 )$ $\mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ at YR1 in July to $0 . 8 9 \left( \pm 0 . 0 3 \right) \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ at YR3 in December. DOC concentrations were significantly higher at upstream sites than downstream $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ (Fig. 4), suggesting that terrestrial inputs, particularly influenced by the dam installation, substantially increase DOC levels in the estuary. The $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ranged from $2 2 \left( \pm 7 \right) \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ at the upstream site YR1 in July to $1 \ ( \pm 0 ) \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ at downstream site YR4 in April (Table 1). Upstream sites consistently showed higher $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ compared to downstream sites. The $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ was generally higher at upstream sites, with the exception being as high as $2 . 8 \left( \pm 1 . 2 \right) \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } \mathbf { g } ^ { - 1 }$ at site YR5 in September. This observation suggests a dominant contribution of terrestrial CDOM to the estuary in September (Fig. 4). For FDOM, the microbial humic-like component $\mathrm { C } _ { 3 3 0 / 4 1 5 }$ at upstream sites (i.e., YR1 and YR2) varied between 0.23 and 0.59 RU, approximately an order of magnitude higher than at downstream sites (i.e., YR3, YR4, and YR5), which varied between 0.02 and 0.05 RU (Fig. S3 and Table 1). An exception was observed at site YR3 in July, where a C330/415 value of 0.19 RU coincided with peak discharge of freshwater from the upstream reservoir during the monsoon season (Fig. S2). Similarly, the peak N component $\mathrm { C _ { 2 7 5 / 3 8 8 } }$ , associated with chlorophyll intensity, varied from 0.11 to 0.50 RU at upstream sites, roughly an order of magnitude higher than at downstream sites, where it fluctuated between 0.00 and 0.07 RU, except for site YR3 in July. The terrestrial humic-like component $\mathrm { C } _ { ( 2 6 5 ) }$ 375/450 varied between 0.11 and 0.31 RU at upstream sites and between 0.01 and 0.09 RU at downstream sites. This pattern suggests a primarily terrestrial origin of these FDOM components. In contrast, the proteinlike component $\mathrm { C _ { 2 9 0 / 3 0 5 } }$ did not exhibit a clear spatial pattern, ranging between 0.00 and 0.26 RU at upstream sites and between 0.02 and 0.17 RU at downstream sites. For SEC-OCD measurements, the biopolymer fraction varied between 241 and $7 1 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ at upstream sites and fluctuated between 29 and $3 8 8 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ at downstream sites (Fig. S5 and Table 3). The biopolymer concentrations, accounting for $2 { - } 1 6 \%$ of DOC for all samples, were significantly higher at upstream sites than downstream sites $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ . The humic substances fraction followed a similar trend, ranging from 606 to $4 0 6 9 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ at upstream sites and from 216 to $1 8 7 3 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ at downstream sites. Similarly, the building blocks fraction ranged from 263 to $9 9 8 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ at upstream sites and from 51 to $5 3 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ at downstream sites. However, no clear spatial trend was observed for the LMW acids fraction, which showed the lowest concentration levels among all the fractions, with the highest level at site YR5 in December and many samples displaying zero. The LMW neutrals fraction also did not show a clear spatial pattern, varying from 455 to $1 7 5 2 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ at upstream sites and spanning from 237 to $1 3 2 9 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ at downstream sites.

# 3.3. Variations in DOM sources/composition along the estuary and the dam-driven effects

The fluorescence index (FI) was consistently high across all samples, ranging from 1.7 to 1.9 (Table 1), indicating significant to dominant algal contributions to the DOM pool throughout the estuary, consistent with the observations of McKnight et al. (2001). The $8 ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C D O C }$ values were generally more depleted at upstream sites YR1 and YR2 (ranging from $- 2 4 . 5 \%$ to $- 2 7 . 8 \\\\text{ textperthousand}$ ) compared to downstream sites (ranging from $- 1 9 . 3 \%$ to $- 2 3 . 2 \\\\textmu _ { 0 0 } \textbar { \textmd { 1 } }$ ), as shown in Table S1 and Fig. 6b. These results suggest the dominance of terrestrial C3 plants, soil, and freshwater phytoplankton as DOC sources at upstream sites, in contrast to a dominance of marine phytoplankton at the downstream locations. The $8 ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C _ { D O C } }$ values at sites YR1 and YR2 are comparable to those reported for the Amazon River and Chesapeake Bay tributaries (Raymond and Bauer, 2001). However, these values are relatively lighter than those in the Godavari River estuary of India, which range from $- 2 8 . 3 \\\text{ textperthousand}$ to $- 1 4 . 0 \text{‰}$ (Kumar et al., 2022, Table 2), and include DOC inputs from C4 plants that possess heavier $8 ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C _ { D O C } }$ values. Two exceptions were noted: in January and July at downstream site YR3, more depleted $8 ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C _ { D O C } }$ values were observed (Table 1). The particularly light $8 ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C _ { D O C } }$ value of $- 2 5 . 4 \text{‰}$ at site YR3 in July is likely attributable to significant freshwater discharge during the peak of the monsoon season.

From the PCA plot, it is evident that upstream sites YR1 and YR2 are strongly influenced by terrestrial end-member DOM parameters, characterized by an enriched humic substances fraction, high humic-like components, and higher humification index (HIX). The downstream site YR3, close to the dam, generally displays DOM characteristics akin to the marine endmember, except in July during the peak of the wet season, when significant freshwater discharge through the estuary dam gate is expected. For upstream sites, July and September (monsoon season) samples are located on the positive (upper) side of the $\mathbf { y }$ -axis. Downstream sites, however, only show April and January samples at YR3 on the negative (lower) side. This pattern suggests that downstream sites might be influenced by upstream freshwater discharge, except in April for all sites and January for YR3 only, during the pre-monsoon season. The WRT in the downstream estuary is shorter in the monsoon season due to significant freshwater flushing. However, during the post-monsoon season in December and January, freshwater may reach the lower estuary months later, indicating a longer WRT, particularly for sites YR4 and YR5.

This suggests potential shared origins of these DOM parameters, with upstream sites displaying higher levels compared to downstream sites.

This result is consistent with a changing carbon cycle in coastal regions during post-industrial times (Bauer et al., 2013).

# 4. Conclusions

This study identified distinct spatiotemporal patterns in DOM characteristics along the Yeongsan River estuary, heavily influenced by an estuary dam. Upstream sites, YR1 and YR2, showed characteristics markedly different from those at downstream sites (YR3, YR4, and YR5). This difference was evident through box plots, clustering, and PCA of DOM parameters. At the upstream sites, DOM primarily originated from terrestrial sources like C3 plants, soils, and freshwater phytoplankton, while downstream sites were predominantly influenced by marine phytoplankton. Site YR3, located downstream near the dam, exhibited the greatest variability in DOM characteristics, particularly during significant freshwater discharges in the peak monsoon season (July), when it clustered with the upstream sites (YR1 and YR2) based on optical and SEC-OCD data of DOM. This observation highlights the impact of damming on DOM characteristics in the downstream section of the Yeongsan River estuary, resulting in decreased freshwater delivery and longer water residence times. Although the study could not quantitatively distinguish the effects of damming from those of sea water mixing at downstream sites, the transition of site YR3 from clustering with marine endmember DOM in January (pre-monsoon season) to predominantly terrestrial endmember DOM in July (monsoon season), during periods of increased freshwater discharge via dam gates, underscores the influence of the estuarine dam on downstream DOM characteristics. Seasonal trends also varied significantly between upstream and downstream sites. Upstream sites showed clear distinctions between the monsoon (July and September) and non-monsoon seasons (December, January, and April). Conversely, downstream sites displayed different patterns, with the pre-monsoon season distinctly separated from the combined monsoon and post-monsoon periods. This suggests prolonged effects of upstream freshwater discharges, assuming extended WRT during the post-monsoon compared to the monsoon season. Overall, high values of FI, biopolymers fraction, and $\mathrm { D O S \% }$ indicated dominant algal contributions to the DOM pool, derived from freshwater phytoplankton upstream and marine phytoplankton downstream. Additionally, compared to estuaries not affected by dams, DOC concentrations and a254 values were generally higher. These observations suggest that the estuary dam plays a crucial role in shaping DOM dynamics in the estuary. Traditionally, rivers and estuaries have been considered net carbon sources to the atmosphere. However, our results suggest that human-impacted estuary systems might transition from carbon sources to carbon sinks, driven by frequent algal blooms both upstream and downstream. Further research is necessary to develop quantitative carbon budgets for estuarine systems by dam operations. This potential shift in carbon dynamics could have profound implications for water quality, ecosystem function, and the carbon budget. For better ecosystem management affected by dams, opening gates more frequently to reduce WRT and nutrients accumulation could be a feasible strategy.
