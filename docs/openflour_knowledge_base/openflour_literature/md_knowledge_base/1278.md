Characterization of organic matter (OM) present in treated wastewater (TWW) after various treatment stages is important for optimizing wastewater recycling. The general aim of this research was to carry out a long-term examination of OM in wastewater along the treatment, by applying excitation-emission matrices (EEM) fluorescence spectroscopy and parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC). Fluorescent OM was examined in water samples obtained from four wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) in Israel for 20 months. The PARAFAC analysis of EEMs of water samples from the four WWTPs yielded six components. The fluorescent components included proteinaceous tryptophan-like matter (C1), three humic-like components (C2eC4), a component (C5) that was characterized by excitation and emission with a distinct vibrational structure similar to that of pyrene and a component (C6) that was characterized by the excitation and emission spectra demonstrating two peaks where the appearance of two emission peaks was suggested to reflect the formation of an intra-molecular exyplex. The biological treatment strongly reduced the concentration of component C1 thus increasing the overall fraction of humic-like OM over the proteinaceous OM in the treated water. The fluorescence of component C1 could therefore be used as an indicator of the biological treatment efficacy. The concentration of the humic-like component C2 characterized by excitation and emission maxima at $< 2 4 0 , 3 0 5 / 4 2 2 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively, was also sensitive to biological treatment. The soil aquifer treatment was not effective in completely eliminating the fingerprints of the initial wastewater. The concentrations of the fluorescent components in wastewater after the biological treatment were only slightly affected by filtration $( 0 . 4 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ of the samples. For water sampled prior to the biological treatment, the $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filtration had the most pronounced effect on concentrations of the proteinaceous matter and component C6. Strong positive correlations were found between concentrations of component C1 and total carbon (TC) in wastewater samples from the WWTPs thus suggesting the proteinaceous fluorescence in wastewater as an indicator for TC reduction. Chemical oxygen demand (COD) and the fluorescein diacetate hydrolyzing activity (a measure for the total microbial activity) were strongly positively correlated with the concentrations of components C1eC3 thus suggesting the fluorescence of these components as indicators for reduction in COD and the total microbial activity in wastewater.

# 1. Introduction

Excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectroscopy is considered a useful tool for characterizing organic matter (OM) in various natural and engineered aquatic systems (Hudson et al., 2007; Henderson et al., 2009; Ishii and Boyer, 2012). Its potential to discriminate among various types of OM is based on differences in light emission following excitation by illumination of fluorophores present in aquatic OM (e.g., in humic and fulvic acids, proteinaceous material, pigments and various anthropogenic chemicals). A significant progress when using EEMs is obtained by applying Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC; Stedmon et al., 2003; Stedmon and Bro, 2008) where the contribution of different fluorescent components to the total emission can be separated without additional assumptions regarding their excitation and emission spectra. Because of the physical nature of the excitationemission process, the mathematical tri-linear PARAFAC model may deconvolute EEMs into chemically meaningful components (Andersen and Bro, 2003; Smilde et al., 2004). The PARAFAC analysis of EEMs enables the identification of the number of fluorescent components present, their excitation and emission spectra and concentration scores, thus providing a basis for quantitative analysis of changes in the composition of fluorescent matter in water samples.

Therefore, coupling of the EEM fluorescence spectroscopy with PARAFAC analysis can be useful for characterizing aquatic OM in different water treatment facilities, such as those for drinking water or wastewater. For instance, the EEMþPARAFAC methodology was used by Baghoth et al. (2011) to examine water along the process stages in two drinking water treatment facilities. Bieroza et al. (2011) compared the EEMþPARAFAC methodology with other data mining methods for characterizing OM properties and its removal from drinking water at sixteen water treatment facilities. Recently, a 32 month long monitoring program (EEMþPARAFAC) of raw and treated water (after coagulation-filtration) in a drinking water treatment plant was carried out by Sanchez et al. (2013), who commented on the scarce use of EEM fluorescence spectroscopy and PARAFAC in long-term studies of drinking water treatment facilities.

As distinct from water intended for drinking, wastewater (i.e., non-potable water originating from municipal sewage and industrial effluents) is typically characterized by high concentrations of diverse organic matter. Little work involving systematic long-term studies has been done with regard to EEM PARAFAC analysis of wastewater along the different stages of water treatment processes. A detailed study of the distribution of fluorescent components in six municipal water recycling plants over 10e12 weeks has been carried out by Murphy et al. (2011). Yu et al. (2013) evaluated the removal efficacy of dissolved organic matter (DOM) at the wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) using the EEM PARAFAC methodology and second derivative synchronous fluorescence; however, this study was based on just a one-day sampling from the multiple sites at the examined WWTP. Recently, a one month-long monitoring of fluorescence of municipal wastewater at various stages of treatments was carried by Bridgeman et al. (2013), however in this study a peak-picking analysis of the EEMs rather than the PARAFAC-based methodology was used.

The quality of the wastewater treatment may be important when the final product, treated wastewater (TWW), is intended for use in agriculture, e.g., for irrigation in arid and semi-arid regions experiencing a shortage in freshwater. For example, in Israel $78 \%$ of the produced TWW is used for agricultural purposes (http://www.mekorot.co.il; accessed in 7.11.2013). Under such circumstances, the TWW-originating DOM may affect soil properties (e.g., enhance clay dispersivity, induce water repellency, decrease aggregate stability and hydraulic conductivity) as well as the transport and fate of soil contaminants and their potential to pollute ground and surface water (Levy and Assouline, 2011; Gerstl and Graber, 2011).

It seems that monitoring the various stages of the treatment of wastewater would assist in determining whether and how the composition of the wastewater with respect to OM is indeed affected by the various levels of treatment. This information could contribute to the optimization of the wastewater recycling process. Therefore, it was deemed important to perform a longer-term examination of OM composition in wastewater from different sources along the treatment stages, by applying EEM fluorescence spectroscopy and the accompanying PARAFAC analysis. For that, we have examined fluorescent OM from four WWTPs and pursued the following specific goals: (1) to determine the major fluorescent OM components in different types of wastewater; (2) to evaluate whether the EEM PARAFAC methodology may distinguish between TWWs from various WWTPs; (3) to examine the contribution of DOM components to the whole fluorescent OM in treated wastewater of varying quality, (4) to learn of the treatment efficacy and selectivity towards fluorescent OM components, and finally, (5) to examine possible relations between concentrations of fluorescent components in wastewater and common parameters used for characterizing the quality of water at different levels of purification.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. WWTPs and water sampling

Water samples were taken from four WWTPs in Israel between April 2010 and November 2011. The list of the WWTPs studied, the types of the treatments associated with each WWTP and the number of sampling events are listed in Table 1. In every sampling event, carried out once every 30e60 days, water was sampled from each stage of the treatment at a given WWTP. Water was collected in cleaned glass bottles and transported during one hour to the laboratory.

# 2.2. Water sample characterization

For all the sampling campaigns, the water collected was characterized after being filtered through a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ glass fiber (Whatman GF/F). In addition, the water samples from three WWTPs (i.e., from Ayalon, Shafdan and Raanana) have been characterized also prior to filtration. Thus, on the day of sampling both the filtered and non-filtered water samples were characterized for pH, absorbance spectra and EEMs of fluorescence. Absorbance spectra were measured in a standard $1 . 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cuvette using a Genesis 10UV (Thermo Scientific) at the $2 1 0 { - } 6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ range.

EEMs of fluorescence were obtained as described in detail by Borisover et al. (2009, 2011). Briefly, fluorescence emission was measured in a $1 . 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cell using a Shimadzu RF5301PC spectrofluorometer equipped with 150-W Xenon lamp (Ushio Inc., Japan). EEMs were determined at excitation and emission slit widths of $5 . 0 \mathrm { n m }$ band pass. The emission wavelengths changed between 220 and $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $_ { 2 \ n m }$ intervals, while the excitation wavelengths varied between 210 and $5 9 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ intervals, with the overall scanning time of $8 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ per one EEM. In order to minimize the inner filter effect (IFE) on the fluorescence measured, the samples were diluted, when necessary, with pure deionized water to guarantee that absorbance of a water sample did not exceed 0.05 at $2 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (Lakowicz, 1999). If necessary, the pH values of the water samples were adjusted with 0.01 M HCl and NaOH to the range of 7.8e8.2 for the filtered samples and $7 . 5 \mathrm { - } 8 . 5$ for the non-filtered samples. In addition, EEMs of pure deionized water were obtained for each day of measurements. The spectrofluorometer stability was controlled by monitoring the Raman scattering peak of water (at excitation/emission wavelengths of $2 7 0 / 2 9 8 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ; Borisover et al., 2009).

On the day of sampling, non-filtered water samples from the Ayalon and Raanana WWTPs were characterized for total microbial activity by measuring hydrolysis of fluorescein diacetate (FDA; Schnurer and Rosswall, 1982 } ). This test is typically used to quantify total microbial activity in the soil and litter, but has also been applied for aquatic systems, i.e., to activated sludge from a sewage treatment plant (Fontvieille et al., 1992). In brief, $1 0 { - } 4 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of a water sample were mixed with sodium phosphate buffer $\left( \mathrm { p H } 7 . 6 \right)$ up to a volume of $5 0 \mathrm { m l }$ , and spiked with $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of the $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ FDA (Sigma) solution in acetone. The mixture was then incubated for one hour at $3 0 ^ { \circ }$ in a Lab Line Environ-Shaker. Thereafter, it was centrifuged for $4 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $6 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ in Eppendorf centrifuge 5417R, and the absorbance in the supernatant was measured at $4 9 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ in Varian DMS-100 visible spectrophotometer. Assays were carried out in triplicates. Fluorescein concentrations representing microbial activity in a water sample were obtained by means of a calibration curve.

The filtered water samples from all the WWTPs were also immediately characterized for electric conductivity (EC) measured with El Hama Instrument TH 2400Ecmeter and for $\mathrm { H C O } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ concentrations by titration with 0.01N $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S O } _ { 4 }$ (Rhoades, 1982). In addition, in the course of a week after sampling, the filtered water samples were characterized for (a) dissolved total and organic carbon (TC and DOC, respectively) and total nitrogen (TN) concentrations measured with Shimadzu TOC- $\mathbf { \nabla \cdot v }$ , and (b) concentrations of $\mathsf { C a } ^ { + 2 }$ , $\mathbf { M } \mathbf { g } ^ { + 2 }$ with an Atomic Absorption Spectrometer Perkin Elmer Analyst 800, $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ and $\mathbb { K } ^ { + }$ with a flame photometer Corning 400, and ${ \bf C } { \bf l } ^ { - }$ with a Digital Chloridometer, Labconco, USA. Prior to measurements, the filtered water samples were stored at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Data on biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), chemical oxygen demand (COD) and total suspended solid (TSS) concentrations were provided by the laboratories from the WWTPs in Ayalon and Raanana.

# 2.3. PARAFAC analysis e pre-processing, modeling and validation

The pre-processing of the EEMs was carried out as described previously (Andersen and Bro, 2003; Ohno and Bro, 2006; Borisover et al., 2009). In the EEMs, zero emission intensity values were assigned when the emission wavelength was less than the excitation wavelength. To remove the Rayleigh scatter effects, the emission intensities in the corridors $\lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } \le \lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } \le \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } + 2 0$ and $2 \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } \ : - \ : 2 0 \ : \le \lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } \le 2 \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } \ : + \ : 2 0$ were considered as missing values. Any negative emission intensities, if found, were converted to missing values. The Raman scatter effect was minimized by subtracting EEMs of pure deionized water from the sample EEM; any negative intensity values produced by this subtraction were converted to missing values. Then, the emission intensity data were normalized by the intensity of the Raman scattering peak of water at excitation/emission wavelengths of $2 7 0 / 2 9 8 ~ \mathrm { { \ n m } }$ (Borisover et al., 2009, 2011). Because the IFE was controlled by forcing the absorbance of water samples not to exceed 0.05 at $2 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (see section 2.2), to further reduce its impact and that of the instrument’s noise, the emission data at the excitation wavelengths shorter than $2 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ were not considered.

The PARAFAC analysis was carried out on 11 various EEMs arrays (Table 2). Array $\# 1$ was built of the EEMs of filtered water samples from the Lachish WWTP. Then, for each of the other three WWTPs, three arrays were built: one composed of the EEMs of filtered water samples, the second composed of the EEMs of non-filtered samples, and the third combined the EEMs of the filtered and non-filtered water samples associated with different treatment levels and sampling dates for a given WWTP (arrays $\# 2 \cdot 1 0$ , Table 2). An additional “global” array (#11) included the EEMs of all the filtered water samples from the four WWTPs. Examination of the arrays containing EEMs of filtered samples provided information on the DOM in wastewater. Comparison of the components obtained in the “global” array with those determined for a given WWTP was informative regarding the confidence in the PARAFAC analysis. “Mixing” EEMs of filtered and non-filtered samples in the combined arrays was used in order to quantify the contributions of fluorescent DOM components to the whole fluorescing OM (i.e., suspended and dissolved) present in the tested water. Finally, the analysis of non-filtered water samples was used to examine the relations between the concentrations of the fluorescent components and some other parameters used for characterizing the water quality.

# 2.4. Correlation analysis

Pearson correlation analysis was used to investigate the strength of the linear relationships between the concentration scores of the fluorescent components obtained from the PARAFAC analysis and some water characteristics. The correlation analyses were performed using JMP version 7 (SAS 2007).

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. PARAFAC-derived fluorescent DOM components in wastewater from the WWTPs

The analysis of the global array (#11, Table 2) which includes 171 EEMs of all the filtered water samples (without outliers) from the four WWTPs yielded six components. The excitation and emission loadings of these fluorescent components, denoted further as C1eC6, are shown in Fig. 1.

It transpired that each separate WWTP was associated with only four components (of the six found in total). The distribution of the components among the WWTPs is shown in Table 3. Components C1eC3 were, however, identified in all the WWTPs. Component C4 was found in the water samples from the WWTPs in Ayalon and Raanana, while components C5 and C6 were determined in the samples of water from Lachish and Shafdan, respectively (Table 3). When the PARAFAC analysis was applied separately to the WWTP-specific sets of filtered water samples (i.e., to the arrays $\# 1 , 2 , 5 , 8$ Table 2), for each WWTP, the number of components obtained and their types were the same as determined when the global array (#11) was used. The positions of the maxima in the component’s spectral loadings determined with the global array and with the WWTP-specific arrays were similar. To illustrate the extent of this similarity, the wavelengths of the excitation and emission maxima of components determined with the global array are shown in Table 3 together with the largest deviations (in parentheses) observed from the analysis of the WWTP-specific EEMs arrays.

The excitation spectra of components C1 and C2 suggest the presence of two peaks, with one situated below $2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. 1, Table 3). Two excitation peaks are also clearly seen for components C3 and C4. Conversely, the emission spectra of all four components C1eC4 show a single peak (Fig. 1). Most probably, the presence of two excitation and one emission peaks reflects the excitation transitions to two different electronic states $( S _ { 0 } {  } S _ { 1 }$ and $S _ { 0 } {  } S _ { 2 } \rangle$ , but with only the emission from the first excited state to the ground state $( S _ { 1 } {  } S _ { 0 } )$ (Lakowicz, 1999). The C1eC4 components were found in various types of DOM in multiple former studies (Table 4). Based on the positions of the excitation and emission peaks, C1 was identified as a proteinaceous component, most likely associated with the amino acid tryptophan, and components C2eC4 were identified as humic-like substances.

The excitation spectrum of component C5 shows multiple excitations with a distinct vibrational structure of the excited electronic states (Fig. 1). The emission spectrum of component C5, with its vibrational structure, is seen as a mirror image of the longer-wavelength excitation, thus indicating the similarity in the spacing of the vibrational energy levels of the ground and first excited electronic states (Lakowicz, 1999). Hence, the excitation and emission spectra of component C5 appear to have more features compared with the typical spectra of natural components of DOM, such as humic-like substances. As noted above, component C5 was found only in the wastewater samples from Lachish WWTP (Table 3). It may be of importance to mention that about $50 \%$ of the influent water in the Lachish WWTP originates from the wastewater treatment facility at the nearby Intel factory (Cohen, 2013). Therefore, component C5 could be associated with a specific industrial pollutant. Identifying a spectrally well-structured component such as component C5 from both the global array (#11) and the Lachish-specific array (#1), with practically same positions of excitation and emission maxima (Table 3), seems to further support the validity of the PARAFAC-applied procedure for the analysis of the collected EEMs data.

There is a high degree of similarity in the structure of excitation and emission spectra between component C5 and polynuclear aromatic hydrocarbons. For example, pyrene (in an aqueous environment) is characterized by the excitation spectrum with maxima at $\sim 2 4 0$ , $\sim 2 6 5$ , 272, 320 and $3 3 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and a shoulder at $\sim 3 1 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (Wang et al., 2010) that resemble the excitation maxima of component C5 at 245, 270, 280, 350 and $3 7 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and a shoulder at $\sim 3 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (Table 3, Fig. 1). The emission spectrum of pyrene includes two distinct peaks at 372 and $3 9 2 \mathrm { n m }$ and a shoulder at $\sim 4 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Wang et al., 2010) that correlate well with two peaks at 384 and $4 0 2 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and a shoulder at $\sim 4 2 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ of component C5 (Table 3, Fig. 1). In addition to the positions of the maxima in the spectra of pyrene and component C5 that correlate, there is also a strong resemblance in the overall structures of the excitation and emission spectra of component C5 and those of pyrene (Wang et al., 2010). This spectral similarity suggests that the molecular structure of component C5 contains a “pyrene-like” moiety. The difference in the exact positions of the excitation and emission maxima between component C5 and pyrene demonstrates a bathochromic (red) shift which may be well explained by the presence of functional groups associated with a “pyrene-like” moiety in the component C5, in contrast to pyrene.

Finally, component C6 discovered in water samples from the Shafdan WWTP (Table 3) demonstrates an interesting finding: it is characterized by two peaks both in the excitation and the emission spectra (Fig. 1). The two excitation peaks of component C6 represent most probably the electron transitions from the ground state to two different energetic states, S1 and S2. The emission spectrum with two peaks is a rare phenomenon in fluorescent DOM. Stedmon et al. (2003) concluded that the existence of multiple emission bands within a component implies the presence of greatly differing fluorophores. Murphy et al. (2011) found a second emission peak in the spectrum of a tyrosine-like PARAFAC-derived fluorescent component (present in wastewater) and considered it spurious. However, the presence of two maxima in the emission spectrum of component C6 in our study is not believed to be a result of mistreatment of the fluorescence data. Component C6 was obtained in the separate PARAFAC analyses, i.e., when examining the global array (#11) which includes the filtered samples from all the WWTPs, and Shafdan-specific arrays #5 and #6 (Table 2).

The presence of two peaks in the emission spectrum of component C6 might reflect various excited-state processes (Lakowicz, 1999). A possible explanation for the bimodal nature of the emission spectrum of component C6 is to consider the intra-molecular formation of charge-transfer complexes in the excited state. Examples of emission spectra with two maxima, due to the formation of excimers (i.e., the excitedstate dimers) or exciplexes (i.e., the excited-state complexes) are known (Lakowicz, 1999). The intra-molecular electron donor e electron acceptor charge-transfer complexes in the ground and excited states were also suggested to explain the absorbance and emission spectra of humic substances (Del Vecchio and Blough, 2004). In such a case, the bimodal emission spectrum of component C6 could represent the relative population of the excited state by the light-emitting complexed and non-complexed species.

# 3.2. Removal of fluorescent DOM components along the wastewater treatment stages

Changes in the fluorescent DOM along the wastewater treatment stages at the four WWTPs examined were characterized by using the concentration scores of fluorescent components. The concentration scores were obtained from the separate analyses of the WWTP-specific arrays #1, #2, #5 and #8, respectively (Table 2). For a certain treatment stage in a given WWTP, the concentration scores of a component were averaged over the entire sampling period. Thereafter, the averaged concentration score of a given component was normalized by its value as determined in the samples at the end of the first treatment stage, except for Lachish WWTP where data normalization was by the original influent water which is of secondary biological treatment quality. Therefore, this normalized concentration score can be considered as a relative concentration of a component quantifying its possible removal along the wastewater treatment stages. The normalized concentration scores are presented for all the four WWTPs in Fig. 2; the relative changes of the normalized concentration scores along the wastewater treatment stages allow comparisons between different components.

As can be seen in Fig. 2, the treatments in the Lachish facility were unsuccessful in reducing the concentrations of all the four components C1eC3 and C5 in the wastewater influent (which was of secondary biological treatment quality). The biological treatment in the other three WWTPs (i.e., Ayalon, Raanana and Shafdan) strongly reduced the concentrations of the proteinaceous component C1. The sensitivity of component C1 to the biological treatment is expected due to the recognized biodegradability of a proteinaceous matter (Marschner and Kalbitz, 2003; Stedmon et al., 2003; Saadi et al., 2006; Fellman et al., 2008; Hudson et al., 2008; Henderson et al., 2009; Mostofa et al., 2010; Gue et al., 2012; Yu et al., 2013). Neither the primary treatment preceding the biological treatment in the Ayalon WWTP nor the pond and tertiary treatments in the Raanana WWTP affected its concentration. However, the SAT in the Shafdan’s WWTP caused a further dramatic decrease in the concentration of C1.

The biological treatment reduced the concentration of the humic-like component C2 in all the WWTPs where such a step exists. The sensitivity of the humic-like component C2 to microbial activity (the essence of the biological treatment) was also reported in the literature (Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Gue’guen et al., 2011; Murphy et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2011). The SAT in the Shafdan WWTP was very effective in reducing the concentration of this humic-like component; the pond in the Raanana’s WWTP was not effective but the tertiary treatment involving the filtration through the sand-carbonbasalt granular medium succeeded to reduce its concentration by about $20 \%$ . The dynamics of this component in the Raanana’s WWTP due to the pond treatment and filtration agrees partially with that reported by Ishii and Boyer (2012) who suggested that this fluorescent component is unaffected by both reservoir storage and sand filtration.

The effect of the biological treatment on the concentrations of the humic-like components C3 and C4 varied from insignificant to minor, thus demonstrating their resistance to biodegradation in this wastewater treatment process; this observation was in agreement with the findings of Murphy et al. (2011). However, both the SAT in the Shafdan’s WWTP and the tertiary treatment in the Raanana facility reduced the concentrations of these humic-like components. The little response of components C3 and C4 to the pond treatment and the notable reduction in their concentration following the tertiary treatment in the Raanana WWTP seem to be opposite to the expected response to reservoir storage and sand filtration as reported by Ishii and Boyer (2012). However, any comparisons of the sensitivity of the fluorescent components to wastewater treatment should be made with a great caution due to possible multiple differences in the treatment systems. Finally, component C6, observed only in the Shafdan WWTP, demonstrated sensitivity to the biological treatment which was intermediate between the sensitivity of component C1 and that of component C2. The SAT was found effective in reducing the presence of component C6 (Fig. 2).

Thus, among the various wastewater treatment stages in the WWTPs, the biological treatment increased the overall fraction of humic-like organic matter in DOM over that of the proteinaceous matter. The additional treatments, such as SAT (in the Shafdan’s WWTP) or the tertiary one (in the Raanana’s WWTP), reversed the situation and increased the removal of humic-like components relative to the proteinaceous matter.

It should be noted that the observed fluorescence-based trends in the removal of various DOM fractions (Fig. 2) might be affected by presence of some quenchers, i.e., metals, which could result in an apparent decrease of the concentration scores of the fluorescent components. However, in recycled water systems clear indications for metal quenching effects are yet under question, due, most probably, to the fact that metals, if present, are associated with particulate or colloidal organic matter (Henderson et al., 2009). In addition, in Israel the presence of metals in TWW is limited, as it is mostly used for irrigation in arable lands. For example, copper concentrations in TWW should not exceed $0 . 5 \mathrm { m g / L }$ (Health regulations e quality standards for TWW and guidelines for treating sewage, http://www.sviva.gov.il/InfoServices/ReservoirInfo/ DocLib/water-and-streams/maim43.pdf; accessed at 17, February, 2014); earlier, it was demonstrated that the presence of $1 \mathrm { m g / L }$ of copper would result only in a $5 \%$ decrease of the fluorescence of DOM sampled from a WWTP (Saadi et al., 2006). Therefore, the trends shown in Fig. 2 are not considered to be strongly affected by the possible presence of quenching metals in the wastewater.

# 3.3. Contribution of DOM components to the whole fluorescent OM in wastewater

Fluorescence spectroscopy suggests the possibility for online and real-time monitoring of various types of water treatment (Hambly et al., 2010; Murphy et al., 2011). Fluorescence of the whole water samples may include the contributions from DOM and the suspended OM, e.g., the cells and bacteria (Determann et al., 1998; Baker et al., 2007). Therefore, for the fluorescence-based characterization of the whole wastewater along the treatment stages it may be useful to know of the possible contribution of the DOM components and that of the suspended OM to the fluorescence measured. Specifically, this knowledge on DOM contribution is of interest when TWW is used for field irrigation, due to possible various effects of DOM on soil properties (e.g., Levy et al., 2013).

Therefore, in order to quantify the contribution of the DOM components to the fluorescence of the OM present in the wastewater studied, the arrays that included the EEMs of the samples before the $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filtration and after filtration were PARAFAC-analyzed separately for the WWTPs of Ayalon, Shafdan and Raanana (i.e., arrays $\# 4 , 7$ and 10, respectively; Table 2). In the wastewater from the Shafdan and Raanana WWTPs four components were determined, similarly to the results of the PARAFAC analysis of the filtered only wastewater samples (i.e., arrays $\# 5 , 8$ and 11; Table 2). When analyzing the combined array $\# 4$ associated with the Ayalon’s WWTP, only three components were found, as compared with the four components determined in the array #2 containing EEMs of only filtered wastewater samples from this WWTP (Table 2). Two of the three fluorescent components obtained from the combined array $\# 4$ corresponded well to C1 and C2 in Table 3 (with the slight changes in the wavelengths of the spectral maxima up to $1 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ). The third one (C3-4), a humic-like component derived from the array $\# 4$ and characterized by the excitation maxima at 275 and $3 7 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and one emission maximum at $4 5 2 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ appeared as a combination of components C3 and C4 that were detected in the same water samples but have first passed a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter and thus analyzed in array #2.

Thereafter, regardless of the sampling date and the specific WWTP (i.e., Ayalon, Raanana or Shafdan), all the wastewater samples examined in the combined arrays $^ { ( \# 4 , 7 }$ and 10, Table 2) were divided into two subsets: “prior to biological treatment” and “post biological treatment”. The concentration score of a component in a wastewater sample that passed a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter was divided by the concentration score in the same sample but prior to filtration, with accounting for dilutions (if made during the EEMs determination). For a given component, the calculated concentration score ratios were averaged over all the samples in a subset covering various sampling dates and different treatments in all the three WWTPs. These averaged ratios reveal the effect of the $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filtration of the wastewater samples from two subsets on the concentrations of the fluorescent components determined (Fig. 3).

The effect of the $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filtration of the wastewater samples on the concentrations of detectable fluorescent components differed between the two subsets and between different components in the “prior to biological treatment” subset. In the “prior to biological treatment” subset, filtration resulted in a decrease in the concentrations of the various components. A minor decrease was noted in the averaged concentrations of components C2 and C4 (by $5 \%$ and $12 \%$ respectively), whereas the averaged concentrations of components C3 and C3-4 decreased moderately, by $3 3 \%$ and $1 9 \%$ , respectively. Filtration had the most pronounced effect on the proteinaceous matter (C1) and component C6, decreasing their averaged concentrations in water to almost half (by $4 6 \%$ and $52 \%$ , respectively). It is worth mentioning that the observed response of component C6 to the wastewater filtration, as well as the consistent decrease in its concentration along the water treatment process (Fig. 2, section 3.2), support our view that this component is not an artifact of the EEMþPARAFAC analysis but reflects a certain chemical entity.

The observed effects of wastewater filtration on the determined concentrations of fluorescent components in the samples from the “prior to biological treatment” subset correlate with the observations by Baker et al. (2007) who examined the effect of filtration of samples from six different freshwaters via the 1.2 and $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filters. It was found that use of these filters had little effect on the fluorescence of humiclike compounds, thus suggesting that the humic-like components are dissolved; however, the fluorescence of proteinaceous material demonstrated a sever reduction after water filtration via the $1 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter, and an additional small decrease when further using the $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter.

In the “post biological treatment” subset including also the wastewater samples after the SAT in the Shafdan’s WWTP and from the pond and tertiary treatment in the Raanana’s WWTP, the concentrations of humic-like components C2eC4, C3-4 and component C6 demonstrated a weak response (if any) to the wastewater filtration (Fig. 3). This small effect of filtration on the concentration of component C1 in the samples of the “post biological treatment” subset agreed with a recent observation by Bridgeman et al. (2013), who noted that most of tryptophan-like fluorescence in wastewater samples in final settlement and humus tanks was derived from the fraction that passed a $0 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter. Bridgeman et al. (2013) extended this conclusion also to other fluorophores present in wastewater samples at different treatment stages.

Thus, the data shown in Fig. 3 suggest that when characterizing the advanced stages of wastewater treatment in terms of aquatic OM fluorescent components, chromophoric DOM is the major contributor to the fluorescent properties of TWW. When examining water samples of heavier OM-loading, i.e., prior to biological treatment, a meaningful contribution of the OM constituents (not limited only to proteinaceous matter) associated with a particle size greater than $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ should be expected in the overall fluorescence of wastewater.

# 3.4. Relations between concentrations of fluorescent components and the parameters characterizing water quality

Examination of the relations between the concentrations of fluorescent components in wastewater samples and the parameters commonly used for characterizing water quality in treatment processes could be utilized for predicting purposes and may also contribute to a better understanding of the transformation of OM in WWTP. In general, multiple relations between fluorescence of certain organic components in water and water characteristics were reported in the literature (e.g., Baker, 2005; Hur and Cho, 2012; Holbrook et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2007; Wu et al., 2007; Bridgeman et al., 2013). More specifically, measurement of the proteinaceous fluorescence in water was considered a surrogate for the time-consuming BOD determination in water quality assessment (Hudson et al., 2008; Bridgeman et al., 2013). However, much less data is available on relations between the PARAFAC-resolved fluorescent components and the wastewater quality characteristics examined along the treatment track.

Therefore, we examined the relations between the concentration scores of the fluorescent components in water samples from the Ayalon, Raanana and Shafdan WWTPs (collected in various dates and from various treatment stages) and the following water quality parameters: TC, DOC, TN, TSS, $\mathsf { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ , $\mathsf { M g } ^ { 2 + }$ , $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ , $\mathbb { K } ^ { + }$ , ${ \bf C } { \bf l } ^ { - }$ and $\mathrm { H C O } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ concentrations, absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ( $\mathsf { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ; widely used to characterize the content of aromatic organic compounds in water), EC, BOD, COD and the FDA hydrolyzing activity. The relevant concentration scores of the fluorescent components in the wastewater samples were extracted from the EEM $^ +$ PARAFAC analysis of the arrays including only non-filtered samples (arrays #3,6,9; Table 2); the PARAFAC analysis of these WWTP-specific arrays yielded four components for each WWTP. The components obtained corresponded to those described in Table 3. Only statistically significant correlations characterized by coefficient $/ r / > 0 . 7$ (and entitled further as “strong”) were selected and listed for each of the three above-mentioned WWTPs (Table 5). This correlation analysis was not carried out for the wastewater from Lachish WWTP since non-filtered water samples were not examined in this facility.

# r e f e r e n c e s

# 4. Conclusions

The results of our study clearly indicate that the EEM PARAFAC methodology allows differentiating between water samples from different WWTPs, based on the type of fluorescent components. Observation of a “pyrene-like” component with the expressed vibrational structure in its fluorescence spectra at the background of the commonly found proteinaceous and humic-like materials demonstrates the efficacy of the applied methodology in identifying unique components in wastewater. Our results also suggest that in analyzing EEMs of water samples, the appearance of fluorescent components with multiple emission peaks should not be ruled out.

Monitoring the wastewater along the various treatment stages revealed the selectivity and efficacy of the treatment in terms of the removal of certain fluorescent components. Even a long-term soil-aquifer treatment may not remove completely the fluorescent fingerprints of the initial wastewater. Different humic-like components seemed to respond differently to the biological treatment. The concentration of the proteinaceous tryptophan-like fluorescent component was found to be sensitive to the biological treatment.

The observation that chromophoric DOM is the major contributor to the fluorescent properties of biologically treated wastewater suggests that the characterization of DOM at the advanced treatment stages may be possible, based on measuring the fluorescence of the whole, non-filtered water samples.

Finally, fluorescence of the proteinaceous tryptophan-like component can be used as an indicator of the biological treatment efficacy and may serve as a marker for decrease in the TC content at the various stages of the wastewater treatment. Fluorescence of both the proteinaceous tryptophan-like and certain humic-like components provides indications for reduction in COD and the total microbial activity in wastewater.
