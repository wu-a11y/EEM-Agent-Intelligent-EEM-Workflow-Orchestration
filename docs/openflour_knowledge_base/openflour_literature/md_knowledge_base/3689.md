# ABSTRACT

Concentration and chemical composition of disolved organic mater (DOM) play a major role in formation and speciation of disinfection by-products,such as trihalomethanes (THMs),in water treatment plants (WTPs)during disinfection.This study characterized DOM across the process trains of WTPs using fluorescence excitation emission matrices (EEMs) together with paralel factor analysis (PARAFAC).The PARAFAC model was developed from 216 EEMs of bimonthly water samples from three WTPs in Khon Kaen,Thailand,from May 2018 to Mar 2019.Three PARAFAC components identified were humic-like DOMof terrestrial,and microbial oragricultural origin,while the one protein-like component was previously defined as tryptophan-like fluorophore.The relationships between water quality parameters,including the maximum fluorescent intensities $( F _ { m a x } )$ of PARAFAC components and THM formation potential(THM-FP)were investigated using Spearman's rank correlation.The $F _ { m a x }$ of PARAFAC components, $\mathrm { { U V A } } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ，DOC,and THM-FP were greater in dry season.Chloroform was the primary THM formed at two sites using surface water as their water source,while the site using surface water with saline groundwater intrusion had higher concentration of brominated THMs.Results indicated that $F _ { m a x }$ of humic-like components extracted by PARAFAC analysis were the most accurate THM-FP surogate parameter assessed for the water samples tested and the correlations between $F _ { m a x }$ and THM-FP were site specific $( \rho =$ 0.81-0.85).The result demonstrates that fluorescence spectroscopy analysis has yielded insights into relationships between the DOM optical characteristics and their total THM-FP even at sites with diferent speciation of THMs.

# 1．Introduction

Chemical disinfection is typically the essential final step for water treatment plants (WTPs) to inactivate pathogenic microorganisms and reduce the overall risk from waterborne diseases.Free and combined chlorine,such as chlorine gas and hypochlorite salts,are widely used as chemical disinfectants due to their oxidative power,low cost,and ability to produce a residual in the distribution system (Gilca et al.,2020). However,chlorination introduces other concerns in finished drinking water as the disinfectants themselves can react with dissolved organic matter (DOM),anthropogenic contaminants,and bromide/iodine in source water,resulting in the formation of carcinogenic disinfection by-products (DBPs) (Richardson et al., 2007).

More than 6Oo DBPs of different genotoxicity and carcinogenicity have previously been reported in the literature (Krasner et al., 2006). The most prevalent DBPs formed during chlorination are trihalomethanes (THMs) and haloacetic acids (HAAs) (Hao et al., 2017; Park et al.，2019；Singer and Bilyk，2002). In 1998，the U.S.

Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) promulgated the Stage 1 Disinfectants and Disinfection Byproducts Rule which established maximum contaminant levels(MCLs） for total trihalomethanes (TTHMs)and five HAAs (HAA5)as 80 and $6 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ ,respectively (US EPA, 2001).

In Thailand,water quality standards of the Provincial Waterworks Authority (PWA) follow World Health Organization (WHO) guidelines for drinking water quality,which set the MCL for individual THMs as follows: $6 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ for bromodichloromethane (BDCM), $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ for bromoform (BF) and dibromochloromethane (DBCM),and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ for chloroform (CF) (Ratpukdi et al.， 2019; World Health Organization, 2011).The PWA also specifies that the sum of ratios of each THM concentration to its standard value may not exceed one.Thus far,other DBPs in drinking water have not been regulated in Thailand.Previous studies conducted in Thailand have reported that formation potential (FP) of THMs in raw water and their occurrence in finished tap water were greater than the FP and occurrence of other DBPs like HAAs (Kanokkantapong et al.，2Oo6)，haloacetonitriles (HANs) (Ratpukdi et al., 2019), $N _ { ☉ }$ -nitrosodimethylamine (NDMA) (Na-Phatthalung et al., 2016),and other emerging DBPs (Na Phatthalung and Musikavong, 2019).Therefore,characterizing the precursors and THM formation potential (THM-FP) can be more comprehensive and more practical than trying to quantify the occurrence of other DBPs in water treatment trains.

Factors affecting DBP formation potential (DBP-FP) during chlorination include the concentration and composition of DOM in source water,disinfectant types,operational conditions,and contact time (Krasner, 2009;Mian et al., 2018). DOM is a heterogeneous mixture with a wide range of hydrocarbon structures and functional groups (Leenheer and Croué, 2oo3) that can originate from terrestrial, microbial,and anthropogenic sources like wastewater and agricultural activities.Thus,understanding the nature of DOM is needed for the assessment of its reactivity and treatability in WTPs to achieve better drinking water quality.Recently,DOMhas increasingly been studied and characterized using three-dimensional fluorescent excitation-emission matrix (EEM), which is advantageous due to its rapid and sensitive analysis,minimum sample volume and preparation needed,and potential for online monitoring (Bridgeman et al., 2011).

Compared to UVabsorbance measurement,fluorescent EEManalysis can extract more comprehensive data regarding DOM chemical composition and,thus,better differentiate its origin.However,EEM contains complex,three-dimensional data that is difficult to analyze, interpret,and identify patterns in the dataset based on visual inspection of peak positions (Cory etal., 2Ol1).Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) is a multivariate data analysis technique that is most widely used to decompose EEM data for quantitative comparison of different DOM fractions (Ishii and Boyer，20l2； Zhu et al.,2017). The resulting PAFARAC model contains underlying independent fluorophores with specific excitation and emission spectra that are separated mathematically and chemically,thus enabling the tracking of changes in DOM across diverse aquatic environments.In the drinking water treatment train,significant correlations have been found between the maximum fluorescent intensities $( F _ { m a x } )$ of humic-like components and dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations and also between protein-like components and biopolymer fraction (Baghoth et al.，2Ol1).The application of PARAFAC also allows researchers to better understand the relationship between DOM fractions and DBP-FP.For example, THM-FP was found to be significantly correlated with fluorescent intensity of humic-like DOM components identified by PARAFAC (Hao et al.,2012; Hua et al., 2010; Jutaporn et al., 2020; Pifer and Fairey, 2014).

Fluorescent EEMhas been used as a tool to measure DOM removal at WTPs, either by a traditional peak picking method (Bieroza et al., 2010) or by PARAFAC modeling (Baghoth et al., 201l; Shutova et al., 2014). Comprehensive,long-term studies including PARAFAC analysis of full-scale WTPs that are able to track seasonal changes of DOM characteristics and to link fluorescent characteristics of DOM to its reactivity with disinfectants are limited (Pifer and Fairey, 2O14).Moreover,previous work was conducted in North America, Europe,and Australia, where meteorological conditions and aquatic environments are very different from tropical countries like Thailand.Thus, there is a need for improved source water characterization and monitoring in tropical countries,especially in watersheds where anthropologic discharges occurupstream of WTP intake.The main objective of this study was to characterize DOMin samples from treatment trains of WTPs in Thailand over a long-term period using EEM together with PARAFAC modeling. A further objective was to investigate the relationships between water quality parameters,including the extracted PARAFAC components and THM-FP.We aimed to use PARAFAC to track problematic DOM fractions in order to optimize WTPs operation to improve removal of THM precursors.To date,this study is the first PARAFAC model ever conducted on water samples collected from WTPs in Thailand.

# 2．Materials and methods

# 2.1．Site description and water sample collection

Water samples were collected from three WTPs in Khon Kaen municipality,Thailand,including Kotha (KT-WTP),Tapra (TP-WTP),and Khon Kaen University (KKU-WTP).The treatment capacities of KT-WTP, TP-WTP and KKU-WTP are 139,200；48,000；and $1 4 , 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { 3 } / \mathrm { d }$ respectively.Khon Kaen is a rapidly developing city in northeastern Thailand,with a growing population and water demand.KT-WTP obtains its raw water from the Pong River,while TP-WTP uses the Chi River south of the city as its raw water source.The raw water of KKUWTP is a mixture of water from the Pong River and a reservoir collecting rainwater on-site.Upstream of the raw water intakes,the rivers receive both direct and indirect effluent discharge from industries,as well as urban and agricultural runoff.Moreover, river basins in northeastern Thailand are underlain by rock salts (Pholkern et al., 2018); thus, the concentration of dissolved inorganic matter in the rivers can be elevated due to groundwater and soil salinity distribution.All these conditions have major effects on the concentration of THM precursors in water sources and their removal by WTPs.Locations of the three WTPs and the two rivers are shown in Figure S1 of the Supporting Information. The disinfectants used in each WTP are also different.KT-WTP and TP-WTP use chorine gas $\left( \mathrm { C l } _ { 2 } \right)$ $( 2 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ as $\mathrm { C l } _ { 2 } )$ ,while KKU-WTP uses chlorine dioxide $\mathrm { ( C l O _ { 2 } ) }$ $( 2 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m g / L } )$ (Ratpukdi et al., 2019).

Grab sampling was performed once a month between May 2018 and March 2O19.The entire treatment steps of the water treatment plants include(1) coagulation & sedimentation,(2) sand filtration,(3) disinfection.Thus,samples were collected from four locations: raw water (RW),water after coagulation and sedimentation (CA),water after sand filtration (SA),and finished tap water after chlorination (TW).During analysis,samples were categorized into seasons according to amount of average daily rainfall,namely wet (May-Sep 2018, $2 . 5 1 { - } 1 0 . 2 8 ~ \mathrm { m m } /$ day) and dry (Nov 2018-Mar 2019,0-1.19 mm/day). Samples were collected in 2-L polyethylene bottles and transported immediately to the nearby laboratory.Upon arrival,pH and turbidity were measured immediately. Then, suspended solids were removed using $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ glass fiber filter (GF/F,Whatman) and the filtered sample was filled in head space-free bottes and refrigerated in the dark at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for up to 14 days until chlorination experiments and DOM characterization.

# 2.2.THM formation potential (THM-FP)

Tap water from each WTP was extracted for THM analysis directly, while other filtered water samples were chlorinated using sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl) solution $( 4 \mathrm { - } 6 \%$ ,Loba Chemie Pvt.Ltd.） following the uniform formation conditions (UFC) method,which simulates conditions of tap water flow in pipe line systems.The UFC is the chlorination condition that results in residual chlorine of $1 \pm 0 . 2 \mathrm { m g / L }$ after $2 4 \mathrm { - h }$ incubation at $\mathtt { p H } 8$ in the dark at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Summers et al., 1996). Residual chlorine was measured according to DPD $( N , N \mathrm { \Omega }$ -diethyl-p-phenylenediamine) method using a Portable Colorimeter II (HACH DR300,USA). The chlorinated samples were extracted and analyzed for THMs, including CF,BDCM,DBCM,and BF,following U.S.EPA method 551.1 with slight modification (Ratpukdi et al.,2O19),including extraction with Methyl tert butyl ether (MtBE) and analysis with gas chromatography with electron capture detector (GC-ECD,Agilent 7890 B,USA) equipped with HP-5 column.The retention times for CF,DCBM,DBCM, and BF were 1.61,2.11,3.27,and $5 . 5 4 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ ,respectively.All samples were extracted in duplicates and analyzed by GC-ECD in replicates. Bromide $\mathsf { ( B r ^ { - } ) }$ concentration was obtained using Ion Chromatography (IC) (882 Compact IC plus,Metrohm).

# 2.3.DOM characterization

DOC was measured using multi $\mathrm { N / C { \mathfrak { R } } }$ 2100 TOC Analyzer (Analytik Jena AG,Germany).UVabsorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { ( U V A _ { 2 5 4 } ) }$ measurement was made using 1-cm quartz cell and DR6ooO spectrophotometry (Hach, USA).Then, specific UVabsorbance (SUVA) was calculated as a ratio of $\mathrm { U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and the product of the DOC concentration and UV cell path length.Fluorescent EEM spectra was collected using FP-820o Spectrofluorometer (Jasco).Details about instrument setting,data collection, inner-filtering effect correction,blank subtract,and normalization were reported in a previous study (Poojamnong et al.,2O2O).Fluorescent intensity is reported in Raman Units (RU, $\mathbf { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ ).All sampleswere analyzed in triplicate and average values were reported.

PARAFAC analysis was applied to decompose 216 EEMs of water samples from the three WTPs using Matlab (R2016a,MathWorks) and DOMFluor Toolbox following the procedure in a previous study (Stedmon and Bro,2Oo8).Residual monitoring and sum-of-squared error (SSE)of 2-7 component models were visually inspected. Split-half analysis and random initialization (Stedmon et al.,2Oo3) was performed during model validation to determine the proper number of fluorescent components.A five-component model was selected according to its randomness of residuals,smaller SSE value,and similarity of the component profiles compared to OpenFluor database (Murphy et al., 2014).

Additionally,the major difference among the water quality of the three WTPs was that the conductivity at TP-WTP was much higher compared to the other two WTPs.The average conductivity of TP-RW was $8 1 9 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m / m } }$ and $1 3 2 7 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m / m } }$ in wet and dry seasons,respectively, while the conductivity of KT-RW and KKU-RW never exceeded $6 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m } /$ m during sampling period.TP-WTP obtains its raw water from the Chi River,while the water source of KT-WTP and KKU-WTP is the Pong River.The increased conductivity of RW from the Chi River during the dry season was likely due to saline groundwater inflow during low precipitation (Ratpukdi et al., 2O19). # 3.2.PARAFAC components

PARAFAC analysis with 2-7 components was performed on the 216 EEMs of water samples from the water treatment trains of the three WTPs.Afteran initial exploratory analysis and outlier removal,only 3- and 5-component models could be validated by split-half method (Stedmon and Bro, 20o8). Only the 5-component model had component EEM output with excitation and emission loadings that could be compared to similar spectra in published literature in the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al.，2O14） with the corresponding Tucker's congruence coefficients (TCC) greater than 0.95.Thus,the 5-component model was selected.Table S1 in the Supporting Information shows the excitation/emission wavelength (λex/λem） of the five components decomposed by PARAFAC analysis with the descriptions and source assignments compared to previous studies in the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al.,2Ol4).However,no fluorescent emission peak was observed for C5 (Fig S2).This component presented in the EEM of all water samples and the blank EEM of Milli-Q water at low intensity (0.01-0.18 RU). Previous studies (Murphy et al., 2Oo8; Yamashita et al., 2010a) also identified a component of similar profile using either SPEX FluoroLog-2 and F-45oo fluorometer (Hitachi).Its origin was uncertain, and it was likely an artifact of the fluorometer.Thus,component C5 was excluded from further analyses and not discussed in this study.

Fig.l shows the contour plots of the four components identified from the PARAFAC model with split-half validation results.All four components had single emission peaks that overlapped slightly with the excitation spectra, thus exhibiting properties of a true fluorophore (Stedmon and Bro,2Oo8).The high degree of similarity between the complete dataset (solid) and one of the independent halves (dotted） with congruent coefficient ${ > } 0 . 9 5$ indicated that five is the proper number of components to represent the fluorescent signature of this EEM dataset. The components were named with sequential numbering Cl to C4 in order of decreasing variation explained by each component.

Component C1 had excitation and emission maxima at $3 1 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and $4 1 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ,respectively. The component at this λex/λem coordinate was previously associated with humic-like DOM (Chen et al., 2O18) and also correlated to anthropogenic fluorophore originating from agricultural activities(Stedmon andMarkager， 2Oo5） ormicrobial-derived humic-like DOM (Podgorski et al., 2018). Component C2 was composed of two excitation maxima at $< 2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and $4 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and emission maxima at $4 9 8 \mathrm { n m }$ .This component also corresponds to humic-like DOM but of a terrestrial origin (Stedmon et al., 2011; Yamashita et al., 201Ob) and is considered a ubiquitous component in fulvic and humic acid present in soils.Compared to C2,C1 represents DOM with more aliphatic and lower in molecular weight character as decreasing molecular size was often resulted in blue-shift of the humic fluorescence peak (Mostofa et al., 2013).

Component C3 had two excitation peaks at ${ < } 2 7 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 7 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ with $4 3 4 \mathrm { n m }$ excitation peak. Components with similar spectral features to C3 in this study have been reported as a microbial reduced quinonelike component (Cory and McKnight, 2005; Yamashita et al., 201Ob) and also has been reported in freshwaters impacted by agricultural discharge (Stedmon et al., 2O11). Component C4 had spectra resembling autochthonous protein-like or tryptophan-like DOMoriginating from microbial activities.Component C4 had an λex/^em maximumat $2 8 5 \mathrm { n m } / 3 4 8 \mathrm { n m }$ This component has been previously reported in coastal ecosystems (Yamashita et al., 2Ol3) and also wastewater treatment plants (Murphy et al., 2008).

The relative concentration of each PARAFAC component at different treatment stages is represented by the $F _ { m a x }$ of the component.The average $F _ { m a x }$ of the four PARAFAC components in RW,CA,and TW of the three WTPs in wet and dry season is shown in Fig.2.For all the water samples analyzed, $F _ { m a x }$ of microbial humic-like components (C1） was greater than that of the terrestrial humic-like component (C2) and microbial reduced quinone-like component (C3),while the protein-like component (C4) had the lowest $F _ { m a x }$ values.Moreover, $\% { \bf C 1 }$ ， $\% \mathrm { C 2 }$ $\%$ C3,and $\% { \bf C 4 }$ or percentage contribution of each component to the total fluorescence can be used as indices to characterize DOM.In RW throughout the year, the $\% { \bf C 1 }$ was highest for all samples $( 3 5 - 4 4 \% )$ _， followed by $\% \mathrm { C 2 }$ 0 $3 8 \mathrm { - } 2 8 \%$ )， $\% { \bf C 3 }$ $( 1 6 - 2 9 \% )$ ，and $\% { \bf C 4 }$ $( 9 - 2 4 \% )$ .C1 represents low molecular weight humic-like DOM,which may originate from wastewater and agricultural catchments (Stedmon and Markager, 2005)or microbial activities (Podgorski et al., 2O18). The Pong river is widely utilized for industrial and agriculture and also received wastewater discharge upstream,which could release DOM into the river. Agricultural activities can also export DOM into the rivers due to the spreading of fertilizer and runoff.The high C1 $F _ { m a x }$ observed in this study concurs well with the a recent study (Prasert et al., 2O21) reported that The Pong River is enrich with humic substances.However,a direct comparison between concentration of different components is not possible as each fluorophore is different in its quantum efficiencies (Baghoth et al., 2O11). Standard deviation of the $F _ { m a x }$ of each component validated by the model from triplicate measurements ranged between 0.01 and $8 . 2 9 \%$ with an average of $0 . 6 1 \%$ .Thus,analytical reproducibility was verified.

In comparison to available data from WTPs in other countries, the composition of DOM in the RW samples of this study was different.

Specifically, the $F _ { m a x }$ of microbial humic-like DOM C1 was greater than terrestrial humic-like DOM C2.Surface water used for raw water in WTPs is generally enriched in terrestrial humic-like DOM.For example, nine WTPs across South Carolina,USA,had water sources comprised of ${ > } 8 0 \%$ of terrestrial humic-like component and $2 { - } 1 3 \%$ of protein-like component (Yang et al.,2O15). This result,on the other hand,indicated that the water samples were dominated by anthropogenic DOM. Anthropogenic and reprocess DOM components with a profile similar to Cl and C3 were previously found in the raw water of WTPs in Amsterdam and Australia (Baghoth et al., 2011; Shutova et al., 2014), but not in North Carolina and South Carolina (Jutaporn et al., 2O2O; Yang et al., 2015).Caution is necessary, though, since the relative concentrations of the four components cannot be determined,as there is insuficient information regarding respective quantum yield of each component. However,since concentration fluorophore is proportionate to its fluorescence intensity (Baghoth et al., 2011; Murphy et al., 2006), the differences in the relative $F _ { m a x }$ of a specific component can be used to evaluate the differences in concentrations in different seasons and also itsremoval in WTP process train.Notably,this study is the first PARAFAC model conducted in Thailand; therefore,comparison of the fluorescent signature of DOM in this study to other regions in Thailand is not possible.

For the three WTPs studied,the total $F _ { m a x }$ (summation of the $F _ { m a x }$ of four components) was greater in dry season,which is consistent with DOC results (Figure S2).For the RW,the total $F _ { m a x }$ in the dry season was greater than that in the wet season by $3 3 \%$ $^ { 1 5 \% }$ and $3 4 \%$ for KT,KKU and TP-WTPs,respectively.KT-RW and KKU-RWhad similar fluorescent characteristic as they both receive their RW from The Pong River.The total $F _ { m a x }$ of the RW was in the range of 1.04-1.08 RU and1.1O-1.26 RU in wet and dry season,respectively.The two RW samples had greater $F _ { m a x }$ of C1,C2,and C3 in dry season but no significance difference observed on $F _ { m a x }$ of C4 between the two seasons. Changes in composition of DOM,as computed by $\%$ component, were also relatively minor atKT-RW and KKU-RW.

In comparison, the total $F _ { m a x }$ of TP-RW was lower than that of the other two WTPs, with a total $F _ { m a x }$ of 0.84 RU and 1.00 RU in wet and dry season,respectively.The low $F _ { m a x }$ of TP-RW can be explained by the lower DOC and SUVA of TW-RW compare to the other two WTPs (Table 1). TP-RW had greater $F _ { m a x }$ of C1,C2,and C4 in dry season. The largest change observed was an increase in $\mathrm { C } 4 F _ { m a x }$ from 0.12RU in wet season to O.19 RU in dry season,which resulted in $\% { \mathrm { C 4 } }$ increasing from $1 4 \%$ to $1 9 \%$ .Hence,low precipitation and ground water infiltration not only increased DOC concentration and conductivity but also had impact on microbial activities in the water,and consequently increased content of microbially protein-like DOM in the river.Previously,a study on the characterization of Pong River DOM (Prasert et al., 2O21) also reported that surface-runoff and domestic wastewater discharges caused an increase in lipid-like molecules in dry season.

# 3.3. PARAFAC components by WTP treatment stage

To evaluate the changes in fluorescent characteristics of DOM by treatment stages,the average percentage reduction of $F _ { m a x }$ of PARAFAC components across the three water treatment trains were shown in Figure S4 in the Supporting Information. $F _ { m a x }$ reduction can occur by two mechanisms—physical removal and chemical transformation.DOM can be physically removed by precipitation during coagulation and also by adsorption onto particles and subsequently retained in the sand filtration media. Chemical disinfectants, $\mathrm { C l O } _ { 2 }$ and HOCl,can accept electrons from phenolic and hydroquinone moieties of DOM and subsequently cause chemical transformation of DOM and decrease in SUVA (Wenk et al., 2013).

# 3.3.1.Coagulation

Coagulation was effective in removal of fluorescent components: C1 $( 1 2 \mathrm { - } 1 6 \% )$ ，(C2 $( 9 - 2 8 \% )$ ，C3 $( 1 0 - 2 3 \% )$ ,and C4 $( 1 7 - 2 8 \% )$ but did not show any trend in preferential removal of any component specifically. Within the large variation in performance of coagulation during this long-term study, the average removal of C2 $( 1 7 \pm 8 \% )$ was slightly higher than that of C1 $( 1 4 \pm 2 \% )$ .This indicates that,among the two humic-like components,terrestrially-derived DOM (C2） had higher treatability by coagulation relative to microbial DOM (C1).Previous studies also reported that coagulation was effective for the removal of hydrophobicorganiccompounds(Zhuetal., 2014)，and terrestrially-delivered DOM(Shutova et al., 2Ol4).Notably, coagulation in this study refers to the process at full-scale WTPs without pH adjustment.The real coagulant dose applied might be different to the optimum doses applied in previous lab-scale studies.Coagulation preferentially removed humic substances and DOM of larger molecular weight fractions from 300 to 500 Da (Na Phatthalung et al., 2021). Thus, the effective removal of C2 and C4 can be explained by the humic-like properties of C2 and also the larger molecular weight properties of C4, as protein-like fluorophores were strongly correlated with biopolymer fraction of the DOM pool (Baghoth et al., 2011).

# 3.3.2.Sand filtration

Sand filtration resulted in $F _ { m a x }$ reduction of C1,C2 and C3 in the range of $8 . 5 \mathrm { - } 2 2 \%$ with no preferential removal.However, sand filtration also caused an increase in C4 $F _ { m a x }$ in all three WTPs $( 4 . 6 - 2 4 \% )$ ,which indicates a potential of inadequate backwash with potential of DOM accumulation and bacteria regrowth in filter media.Sand filtration typically reduced $F _ { m a x }$ of all components by less than ${ \bf 1 0 \% }$ (Baghoth et al., 2O11) and an increase in $F _ { m a x }$ of components after sand filtration stage has been previously reported (Shutova et al., 2Ol4).Thus,water utilities can use this information to adjust the operation of the sand filter to prevent accumulation of microbial derived DOM.

# 3.3.3.Chlorination

Chlorination, either with $\mathrm { C l } _ { 2 }$ at KT and TP WTPs or with $\mathrm { C l O } _ { 2 }$ at KKUWTP,showed preferential reduction of C4 $( 1 8 - 3 3 \% )$ ,while the reduction of $F _ { m a x }$ of other components were in a wide range from increase of fluorescence intensities (mainly C2) to $1 8 \%$ reduction. The initial and moderate reduction in fluorescence intensity of humic-like component by chlorination is consistent with a previous study (Ishii and Boyer, 2012). Previous studies (Jutaporn et al., 2021; Sgroi et al., 2020) also reported that chlorination had preferential reduction in fluorescence intensity of microbial protein-like fluorophores over humic-like DOM. The reduction in fluorescence intensity after chlorination can be explained by the oxidation reaction that breakdown carbon double bonds in aromatic molecules.Halogen substitution reaction can also cause fluorescent loss (Beggs and Summers, 2O11). Thus, the preferential reduction in fluorescent intensity of protein-like component indicates that the aromatic moieties of protein-like DOM can be more effectively broken down by oxidation reaction. Overall, the application of EEM with PARAFAC at these sites allows an identification of anthropogenic derived DOM (C1) in the water sources and that C1 was not effectively remove by coagulation.

# 3.4.Formation potential and speciation of THMs

The occurrence of THMs formed in these sites using different disinfectants has been previously reported (Ratpukdi et al., 2Ol9); thus, this study focused primarily on the characteristics of DOMand its THM yield when reacted with NaOCl disinfectant. For all three WTPs,TTHM formation potential(TTHM-FP）was greater in the dry season,consistent with DOC and $F _ { m a x }$ results. The higher FP in dry season also agrees well with the occurrence of THMs reported in the water distribution networks in a previous study (Ratpukdi etal., 2O19).Raw waterat KT and KKU-WTPs had similar THM speciation,in which CF was the major THM species formed during the FP study.During the time of investigation,FP of CF at the KT-RW and KKU-RW was in the range of $1 3 8 { \mathrm { - } } 2 6 2 \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ ,which represented $6 6 { - } 9 9 \%$ of total THMs formed.FP of BDCM and DBCM were in the range of $7 . 8 \mathrm { - } 6 5 \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ and $2 . 3 { - } 2 1 \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ ,respectively.BF was found to form only in a low concentration at $0 . 5 { - } 2 . 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ in KT-RW and $0 . 1 { - } 1 . 0 ~ \mu \mathbf { g } / \mathrm { L }$ in KKU-RW.After coagulation and sand filtration,TTHM-FP was reduced by $3 3 \mathrm { - } 3 7 \%$ at KT-WTP and $3 5 { - } 3 7 \%$ at KKU-WTP.Irrespective of the season and the initial DOM concentration,the results suggested these treatment stages had consistent performance in terms of THM precursor removal.FP of all THM species in the SA water samples met the water quality standards in Thailand.However,compared to other studies, TTHM-FP reduction at these sites was lower than that in the US ( $1 5 \mathrm { - } 7 3 \%$ by alum) (Singer and Bilyk,2OO2） and in the Southern Thailand $( 3 9 - 5 7 \%$ by PACl) (Musikavong et al., 2013), which could be explained by a different dose of coagulant used the full-scale WTPs and optimum dose employed inlaboratory studies.Thelow SUVA values of thewater sources could also affect efficacy of coagulation as it removes DOM more effectively in water with high SUVA (Archer and Singer, 2006).

At TP-WTP,FP of brominated THMs,including BDCM,DBCM,and BF,were found to be the same order of magnitude as CF.THM species and concentrations $\left( \mu \ g / \mathrm { L } \right)$ at TP-RW were as follows:CF (50-222), BDCM (32-82),DBCM (51-126)，and BF (10-52)．Thus,CF only accounted for $3 2 \%$ and $4 0 \%$ of TTHM-FP in wet season and in dry season,respectively.During the dry season,concentration of brominated THMs increased significantly.After being treated by coagulation and sand filtration $( 2 4 - 2 5 \%$ removal of TTHM-FP),FP of CF,BDCM,and DBCM were in the range of 28-178,16-50,and $5 3 \mathrm { - } 1 0 3 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / L } ,$ respectively.FP of DBCM in TP-SA samples Was found to exceed the regulated value of $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ in Sep 2018 $( 1 0 3 \mu \mathrm { g / L } )$ and Mar 2019 $( 1 0 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / L } )$ .FP of BF in TP-SA samples was in the range of $1 3 { \mathrm { - } } 3 6 ~ { \mu } 8 / \mathrm { L }$ ,which is much higher than the mean concentration of BF in WTPs in Canada (World Health Organization, 2005).

These findings on brominated THMs combined together with the higher conductivity of the water source at TP-WTP in the dry season suggest the concentration of inorganic precursors to brominated THM formation,including $\mathtt { B r } ^ { - }$ and $\Gamma ^ { - }$ ,atTP-WTP were higher in dry season due to the saline groundwater infiltration.Free chlorine can oxidize $\mathrm { B r } ^ { - }$ to hypobromous acid (HOBr),which is also a strong oxidant that can react with DOM to form brominated DBPs (Hsu and Singer, 2010). Increased $\mathtt { B r } ^ { - }$ incorporation in THMs can result in an increase of TTHMs, as molecular weight of $\mathtt { B r } ^ { - }$ is greater than chlorine (Mctigue et al., 2014).

Incorporation of brominated species into THMs may also result in increased toxicity (Liu et al.,2O18),as BF and DBCM have greater cytotoxicity than CF (Richardson et al., 2Oo7). The bromine incorporation factor (BIF) indicates the molar contribution of all brominated species into DBPs and the formation of Br-DBPs (Liu et al.,2O18). The calculation of BIF was explained in a previous study (Tian et al., 2013). The BIF values are in the range between 1 and 3 (Mctigue et al., 2014). Figure S5 in the Supporting Information illustrates BIF values in RW SA samples during wet and dry seasons.BIF values of all samples from KT-WTP and KKU-WTP was in the range of $0 . 0 4 \mathrm { - } 0 . 6 9$ ，while samples from TP-WTP had greater BIF values in the range of 0.73-1.94.For the same sample types,there was no significant difference observed between the BIF values in wet and dry season.The coagulation and sand filtration treatments combined did not significantly change BIF values of the water samples at these threeWTPs.The high BIF values observed at TP-WTP was greater than that of the raw water source in China with concentration of bromide ion of $1 6 0 \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ (Tian et al., 2013) but was in the same range with WTPs in the US locating downstream froma wet scrubber power plant's discharge location (Mctigue etal.,2O14).Despite high FP of brominated THMs found at TP-WTP,the concentration of $\mathrm { B r } ^ { - }$ in the water source as analyzed by IC in this study was lower than the detection limit of $0 . 1 \ \mathrm { m g / L }$ . The halide ions detected by IC in TP-RW samples were chloride $\mathrm { \Omega C l ^ { - } }$ $1 6 9 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ in wet season and $5 0 1 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ in dry season) and fluoride $\mathrm { ( F ^ { - } }$ $, 0 . 1 6 \mathrm { m g / L }$ in wet season and $0 . 1 9 \mathrm { m g / L }$ in dry season).Hence,future study is needed to evaluate effects of $\mathrm { B r } ^ { - }$ concentration on formation and speciation of THMs,as well as removal of their precusors.

# 3.5.Relating DOM characteristics and THM yield

The relationships between water quality parameters,including the extracted PARAFAC components and THM-FP were further investigated using Spearman's rank correlation coeficients (p).In a previous study (Shutova et al., 2Ol4), the relationships between PARAFC component scores, $\mathrm { U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ absorbance and DOC concentrations were site specific. During data exploratoration,we found that the dataset from KT-WTP and KKU-WTP could be grouped together while TP-WTP dataset had to be investigated separately (Table 2):the THM yield and characteristics of DOM in Chi River were different from those in The Pong River. The dataset used for the statistical analysis in Table 2 included RW, CA, and SA water samples.TW samples chlorinated at the sites were not included for FP analysis.

# 3.5.1.Pong River (KT-WTP and KKU-WTP)

Among the KT-WTP and KKU-WTPs,TTHM-FP had the highest correlation with $F _ { m a x }$ of C1 $( \rho = 0 . 8 1 )$ _， $\operatorname { F } _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C2 $( \rho = 0 . 7 4 )$ ，DOC $( \rho =$ 0.73),and $\mathrm { U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } \left( \rho = 0 . 7 3 \right)$ (Table 2 and Fig. 4a-4c).In these sites, the relationships between CF and humic-like components (C1 and C2) were stronger( ${ \mathrm { \Delta } } \cdot { \mathrm { \Delta } } \mathrm { 0 } . 8 3$ and 0.79,respectively) than those between CF-FP and other surrogate parameters $\mathrm { ( \rho = 0 . 4 0 \mathrm { - } 0 . 7 5 ) }$ ).As CF was the major form of THM at these sites,the high correlation coefficients between the PARAFAC components and CF also resulted in strong correlations between the components and TTHM-FP.The strong correlations between $F _ { m a x }$ and TTHM-FP should be expected as fluorescence measurement is more sensitive to changes in DOM characteristics than $\mathrm { U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Bridgeman et al., 2011) and also more selective than DOC analysis (Pifer and Fairey, 2014).Previously, surrogate parameters that were good indicators of DBP-FP included $\mathrm { U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ，and DOC of hydrophobic DOM fractions (Golea et al., 2Ol7). Our results agree well with linear correlations between TTHM-FP and fluorescentintensities of humic-like DOM previously reported in reclaimed water(Hao etal.,2Ol2),raw and coagulated water (Pifer and Fairey,2Ol4),raw and magnetic ion exchange (MIEX) resin-treated water (Jutaporn et al., 2020).

# 3.5.2.Chi River (TP-WTP)

For TP-WTP,the humic-like component C1 and C2 showed better predictions of TTHM-FP ${ \bf \tilde { \rho } } ( { \bf { \rho } } ) = 0 . 7 8$ and 0.85) than did DOC and $\mathrm { U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ $( \rho = 0 . 6 5$ and 0.66)(Table 2 and Fig.4d-4f).At this site, Cl and C2 were still strongly correlated to CF and DCBM $( \rho = 0 . 6 7 – 0 . 8 5 )$ ,but not with DBCM and BF.Thus,as more $\mathrm { B r } ^ { - }$ was substituted into THM species, the predictive power of organic surrogate parameters decreased.These results confirm that fluorescent DOM fractions could be related to the chlorine reactive fraction and the TTHM forming fraction of DOM. However,the correlations should be used with caution if $\mathrm { B r ^ { - } }$ is present in the water source,because the distribution of the individual species was influenced by $\mathrm { B r } ^ { - }$ concentration rather than the concentration of organic precursors (Chang et al.,2OoO).The presence of brominated THMs at TP-WTP makes it more dificult to associate FP to organic precursor surrogates.Boyer (2O15) investigated predictive capability of THM formation models and concluded that, even $\mathrm { B r } ^ { - }$ concentration was included as the model variable,the existing models were not sensitive to changing $\mathrm { B r } ^ { - }$ levels and tended to underpredict THM formation.For TP-WTP,the results suggested that not only the concentration of organic precursors in its source water but also concentration of $\mathrm { B r } ^ { - }$ should also be monitored. Options of adjusting treatment conditions (coagulation optimization),alternative treatment process,and changing of disinfectant type may be considered when brominated THMs are of concern. Noted that this study chose to focus on THMs based on the regulated dominant DBPs recorded in past studies.In the future,the toxicity of otherDBPs may become a public health concern.Hence,DBPs of interest for future research should be selected based on their toxicity.

Of all organic surrogate parameters investigated using samples from three sampling sites over a period of11 months, $F _ { m a x }$ values of humiclike components C1 and C2 were most strongly correlated to TTHMFP.This result suggests the robustness of fluorescent measurement as THM surrogates across raw water quality,watershed and soil conditions, treatment conditions,and THM speciation.EEM analysis also allows the identification of microbial humic-like DOM,which increased in the dry season,was not effectively removed by coagulation,and was strongly correlated to TTHM-FP.The implication of the results of this study is that,within this dataset collecting from the three WTPs in Khon Kaen municipality,monitoring specific excitation and emission spectra of humic-like DOM could be an alternative tool to rapidly determine characteristic of DOM related to THM precursors in the source waters in different season.In practice,non-fluorescent DOM could also be punctually present in the water sources and subsequently cause problems with the water treatment system.Accordingly,water utilities should closely monitor quality of their raw water to optimize the treatment processes to achieve high quality tap water.

# 4. Conclusions

Based on THM yield and data on DOM characterization using fluorescent EEMs and PARAFAC analysis, $\mathrm { U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ,SUVA,and DOC at three water utilities in Khon Kaen, Thailand,over 11 months,the following

conclusions were drawn:

·Four common fluorescence PARAFAC components were identified for the characterization of DOM fractions and their reactivity with chlorine-based disinfectant to form THMs.Three components are humic-like DOM of terrestrial,anthropogenic,and microbial or agricultural origin,while the one protein-like component had fluorescence spectra similar to tryptophan-like fluorophore. ·Application of fluorescent measurement allows the identification of anthropogenic humic-like DOM,which was one of the strong predictors of THM formation.The abundance of this anthropogenic DOM increased in dry season,and it is recalcitrant to coagulation. ·Of all organic surrogate parameters investigated, $F _ { m a x }$ values of anthropogenic and terrestrial humic-like components were found to be strongly correlated to overall and long-term THM formation.The result showed that fluorescent measurement is a promising tool in predicting THM formation during chlorination across raw water quality and THM speciation.

·At the site where brominated THMs presented in relatively high concentration compared to chloroform, even the fluorescent surrogates showed stronger predictive power to THM formation than DOC and $\mathrm { U V A } _ { 2 5 4 ; \ l }$ $\mathrm { B r } ^ { - }$ concentration should also be monitored because the distribution of THM species was influenced by $\mathrm { B r ^ { - } }$ concentration rather than the concentration of organic precursors.
