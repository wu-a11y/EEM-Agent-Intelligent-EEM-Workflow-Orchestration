The Transpolar Drift (TPD)is a mechanism for the rapid transport of shelf-modified Siberian river waters across the Arctic Ocean and out through Fram Strait.We present results from three trans-Arctic research cruises in August-September 2014-2O16 to investigate the distributionand dynamics of dissolved organic mater (DOM) in the upper waters across the North Pole,from Svalbard to the Mackenzie River mouth,and in two important outflow gateways of the Arctic Ocean (Fram Strait and Lancaster Sound).Fluorescence measurements of DOM obtained using in situ sensor and laboratory-based spectroscopic analyses,coupled with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) were used to map components of DOM at high spatial resolution.The optical properties showed a completely diffrent composition with a much stronger humic-like signal observed in surface waters in the Makarov Basin than in the adjacent Canada Basin,likely due to the substantial amount of Siberian runoff transported by the TPD.The high levels of humic-like in TPD-influenced surface waters were traced from southern Makarov shelves up to the Lomonosov Ridge near the North Pole.Lower DOMlevels were observed in theeastern Amundsen basin waters,likely the result of mixing with terrestrial-poor DOM Atlantic waters.The annual flux of humic-like C2 transported to the Atlantic through Fram Strait was estimated at $1 8 1 6 \pm 9 2 6$ r.u. $\mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 }$ This study reveals the importance of shelf processes and TPD in supplying humic-like material to the central Arctic Ocean to Fram Strait.

# 1．Introduction

The Arctic Ocean is the smallest ocean and accounts for $4 . 3 \%$ of the world's ocean area, $1 1 \%$ of global riverine discharge (Opsahl et al., 1999;and references therein),and $\sim 1 5 \%$ of the global land surface and organic carbon inflow (Dai et al., 2012; Feng et al., 2021). The Arctic Ocean is warming twice as fast as the lower latitudes.The river flux to the Arctic Ocean has significantly increased during the last 3O years (Peterson et al., 2002; Shiklomanov et al., 2002) with a pan-Arctic water export acceleration of $1 1 . 6 \ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 } \ \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ (Feng et al., 202l). The annual riverine input of the five largest rivers (i.e.,Mackenzie,Yukon,Ob,Lena, and Yenisey rivers）has been estimated as $2 5 \mathrm { - } 3 6 \mathrm { ~ T } \mathrm { g } \mathrm { ~ C ~ }$ during the 1936-2015 period (Raymond et al.，2007). The increased river discharge combined with the acceleration of permafrost thawing (Parmentier et al., 2017;Vonk and Gustafsson, 2013) will release a significant amount of terrigenous material including dissolved organic matter (DOM) to coastal ecosystems (Frey and Smith, 2005; Juhls et al., 2020; Pokrovsky et al.,2O2O). Riverine-derived DOM is exported across the

Arctic continental shelves and into the surface waters of the Arctic Ocean basins (Guéguen et al., 2005; 2007; Charette et al., 2020). From there it eventually flows to the North Atlantic (Granskog et al., 2012) which further influences the thermohaline circulation and the global climate (Rennermalm et al., 2006).

The Arctic Ocean basin circulation is influenced by two major currents, the anticyclonic Beaufort Gyre (BG; Armitage etal., 2Ol7) and the Transpolar Drift (TPD; Armitage etal., 2O17). The Beaufort Gyre stores approximately $2 0 , 0 0 0 { \mathrm { k m } } ^ { 3 }$ of liquid freshwater and approximately 800 $\mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 }$ of solid (sea ice) freshwater (Proshutinsky et al.,2Oo9). The TPD is a surface current that carries shelf-modified river waters from the Laptev and East Siberian Seas across the central Arctic Ocean and the North Pole to the North Atlantic Ocean via Fram Strait (Mysack, 2OOl; Rudels, 2015).The transit for the trans-Arctic crossing current is estimated at ${ \bf < } 1 { \bf - } 3$ years (Rutgers van der Loeff et al., 1995). The position of the TDP is determined by the large-scale Arctic Oscillation (AO) (Macdonald et al.,2Oo5) which is highly correlated with the North Atlantic Oscillation (Mysack,2Ool).A positive AO leads to an expansion of the

Atlantic-derived water and a retreat of the Pacific waters whereas a negative AO produces a strong Arctic High, resulting in the expansion of the Beaufort Gyre.The TPD waters showed elevated concentrations of shelf-derived materials including metals,halogenated compounds, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons,and dissolved organic matter (DOM) (e.g.,Charette et al.， 2020; Gerringa et al.，2021; Goncalves-Araujo et al., 2018; Jonsson et al., 2022; Karlsson et al., 2013; Lin et al., 2022; Slagter et al., 2017, 2019; Williford et al., 2021,2022),and sea ice (Krumpen et al., 2019).

DOM is a heterogeneous mixture that requires complementary techniques like ultraviolet/visible absorbance and fluorescence to better assess its nature,source,and fate in the ocean.The absorbance analyses of the colored DOM (CDOM) can provide valuable information on the molecular size (Helms et al.，2Oo8) and the aromaticity of DOM (Weishaar et al., 2003).A fraction of DOM (FDOM) also emits a fluorescent signal,which can provide not only quantitative information on DOM (Stedmon et al.,2Oo3) but also qualitative information on the source and composition of DOM (Coble,2Oo7). The decomposition of the excitation-emission fluorescence matrix (EEM) using parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC; Murphy et al.,2O13) has been used to determine the main fluorescent components in Arctic Ocean waters (e.g. Dainard et al., 2019;DeFrancesco et Guéguen,2021; Fichot et al., 2016; Goncalves-Araujo and al., 2015; Shi and al., 2016; Shen et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2017).The vertical profiles provide an opportunity to link the FDOM composition and levels to water masses,which could provide a powerful tool to guide water sampling of other parameters such as iron in the ocean (Laglera et al.，2019； Slagter et al.，2019). In addition to laboratory-based analyses, in situ fluorometers are capable of measuring FDOM at fixed emisson and excitation pairs along the cruise track (Guay et al.,1999; Guéguen et al., 2015) or mounted on CTD and moorings (Dmitrenko et al.， 2021; Guéguen et al.， 2012; Laney et al.， 2014; Stedmon et al., 2O21). Earlier studies using discrete and in situ fluorescence at ${ \sim } 7 { - } 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ depth in the Amerasian basin showed drastic changes in FDOM levels which were attributed to the mixing of two dominant sources originating in the Eurasian and central Canada basins (Guéguen et al.，20l2,2Ol5).Recently,in situ fluorometer was mounted on ice-tethered profilers highlighted frontal regions on either side of TPD in the central Arctic Ocean (Stedmon et al.,2021).

In this study we aim to 1)assess the spatial distribution of DOM in the four main basins of the Arctic n,i.e. Canada,Makarov,Amundsen,and Nansen Basins,and across Fram Strait as part of the 2014-20l6 summer UNCLOS cruises,and 2)provide a better understanding of the TPD pathway in the central Arctic Ocean to Fram Strait.An EEM-PARAFAC model is developed for the first time to include DOM samples collected in the major Arctic Ocean basins (i.e.Canada,Makarov,

Amundsen,and Nansen basins.

# 2．Materials and methods

# 2.1．Sample collection

In situ collection of high-frequency DOM fluorescence associated (depth ${ \sim } 1 0 \mathrm { m } )$ with opportunistic bottle sampling was conducted during the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (Canadian UNCLOS) cruises in the summers of 2014-2016 (Fig.1).In these 3 years, the Canadian Coast Guard icebreaker Louis S.St-Laurent traveled from Fram Strait across the NorthPole to the Beaufort Sea.Seven surface transects were defined:Makarov Basin (transects A-C),Canada Basin (transect D),Amundsen Basin (transect E),and Nansen Basin (transect F),and western and eastern Fram Strait waters (transects G and H, respectively).The Alpha Mendeleev,Lomonosov,and Gakkel Ridges separate each of the four basins from west to east.The ECO-FLD Wetlabs in situ FDOM sensor (FDOMwETLABs; excitation and emission Wavelengths of 370 and $4 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ,respectively)was used in flow-through mode via the ship's seawater pumping system (sampling depth $\sim ~ 1 0 \mathrm { m } )$ equipped with a thermosalinometer.Discrete surface water samples (depth ${ \sim } 1 0 \ \mathrm { ~ m } )$ ）were collected at 235 sites along the cruise track. Additional samples were collected using the conductivity-temperaturedepth (CTD)/rosette in the top $1 0 0 0 \mathrm { ~ - m }$ depth (Fig.1B).All discrete DOM were collected into acid-washed and pre-combusted amber glass vials and refrigerated until analyses. Expendable CTD (XCTD) surveys were also conducted to monitor the water mass distribution along the cruise track.

# 2.2.Optical properties

The absorbance of DOM was measured on a UV-Visible spectrophotometer (Shimadzu 2550) with a $_ { 1 0 - \mathrm { c m } }$ quartz cuvette over a wavelength range of $2 5 0 { - } 7 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ 、Milli-Q blanks were acquired between each sample to avoid cross-contamination and subtracted from the CDOM spectrum.DOM absorbance values were only available in 2014-2o15.The measured absorbance was converted to an absorption coefficient $( \mathbf { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ according to the equation: $\mathbf { a } _ { \lambda } = 2 . 3 0 3 ( \mathrm { A } _ { \lambda } / 1 )$ ; where $\mathbf { A } _ { \lambda }$ is the absorbance and L the path length of the optical cell in meters. The absorption coefficient at $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (a350) was used as a proxy for CDOM of terrestrial origin (Hoge and Anthony, 1993; Nguyen et al., 2022). The spectral slope $\mathsf { S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } }$ ,a proxy of CDOM molecular weight (Helms et al., 2008),was determined over a wavelength range of 275-295 nm using a nonlinear least square fitting method in Matlab R2021b.

Fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs） measurements were made using a Fluoromax-4 Jobin Yvon spectrofluorometer (excitation wavelengths $\operatorname { E x } = 2 5 0 \mathrm { - } 5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ；emission wavelengths $\mathrm { E m } =$ $2 6 0 { - } 6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ，slit widths and wavelength interval $\operatorname { E x } = 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ .Milli-Q was also used to check for cuvette cleanliness between each sample at an excitation wavelength of $2 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ .No absorbance correction was carried outas the absorbance was lower than O.4 (ina 1O-cm cuvette) in all samples and wavelengths (Stedmon and Bro, 2Oo8). Despite no absorbance scans being available in 2016,the 20l6 cruise track was furthest from coasts suggesting little inner filter effect.Acalibration scan of the water Raman peak at the excitation of $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ was performed daily to ensure optimal instrument performance and for normalization of EEMs to Raman units (r.u.) (Lawaetz and Stedmon,2Oo9). The normalization to the Milli-Q Raman peak enables a direct comparison of fluorescence intensities with other studies.

# 2.3． Statistical analyses

A total of 477 EEMs(10-1000m deep) were decomposed by the PARAFAC model using the DOMFluor toolbox (0.6.3) (Murphy and al. 2013) in MATLAB 2O2la.The model was constrained to nonnegative values and run for 4 to 6 components.The spectra of the five validated PARAFAC components and the split-half validation are shown in the Supplemental Information (Supplemental Fig. S1).All components have been previously reported in the OpenFluor database at $9 5 \%$ similarity and assessed through Tucker's congruence coefficient.

Student's t-tests were used to compare the DOM averages found between two basins and carried out using OriginLab 2O21.The significance threshold was set at $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 5$

# 3.3.Surface DOM characteristics

The surface waters (depth ${ \sim } 1 0 \mathrm { m } )$ showed significant spatial differences in DOM properties (Fig.3). The Canada Basin surface waters showed lower a350 $[ 0 . 2 0 \pm 0 . 0 5 \mathrm { { m } ^ { - 1 } }$ ,FDOMWETLABS $( 0 . 0 8 1 \pm 0 . 0 0 5 \mathrm { V } )$ _， and Cl-C5 values,but higher S275-295 $( 0 . 0 3 3 \pm 0 . 0 0 1 \ \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 } )$ compared to the Eurasian basin waters $( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ .Similar DOM characteristics were reported in previous studies (DeFrancesco and Guéguen, 2021; Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; Shen et al., 2016). The presence of lower molecular weight and less aromatic DOM in the Canada Basin surface waters is expected to primarily be the result of the reduced influence of allochthonous DOM (Granskog et al., 2012; Guéguen et al., 2015). The AMSR2 passive microwave sea ice concentration satellite products (Spreen et al., 20o8) (Supplemental Fig. S3) showed substantially lower sea ice coverage in the Canada Basin compared to the other regions.A smaller sea ice cover suggests that more surface water is exposed to solar radiation which can lead to a decrease in CDOM/FDOM (Dainard et al., 2019; DeFrancesco and Guéguen, 2021; Du et al., 2021). Meteoric water inventories were ${ \sim } 9$ and ${ \sim } 1 2 \mathrm { m }$ in the Canada and Makarov Basins during the 20o5 Beringia expedition (Jones et al., 2Oo8),which had a similar cruise track as our UNCLOS 2O16 cruise.Similar meteoric fractions were measured during the 2015 Arctic Geotraces expeditions (Williford et al., 2022). The greater river water fraction in the Makarov basin was consistent with higher CDOM/FDOM levels than on the North American side of the Arctic Ocean.

# 3.4.Vertical characteristics of DOM

The vertical profiles of salinity and C1-C5 intensities at four representative sites in the Canada,Makarov,and Amundsen Basins,and in eastern Fram Strait (Fig.1B) showed a very distinct pattern, especially within the upper $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ 、All the depth profiles of C1-C5 are shown in Supplemental Fig. S5.The fluorescence vertical characteristics in the Canada Basin concur with previous studies (Guéguen et al., 2Oo7; Jung et al.,2O2l).Indeed, the very high Cl-C4 fluorescence at $\sim 5 0 { \mathrm { - } } 1 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ coincided with the chlorophyll maximum (up to $0 . 5 1 ~ \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 }$ ; Supplemental Fig. S6),marking the core of the Pacific summer waters (Timmermans et al.，2Ol4). It can be noted that the two protein-like fluorescence profiles were decoupled,confirming again the factors thatcontrolled their levels were different in the water column.A second humic-like peak at $1 5 0 { - } 2 0 0 \ \mathrm { m }$ was associated with cooler and saltier Pacific winter water which has its properties set in the Chukchi shelves in winter when sea ice brine rejection helps mobilize organic sediments before its advection into the Canada Basin (Cooper et al., 2OO5; Guo et al., 2007; Jones and Anderson, 1986).

In the Makarov basin,the humic-like Cl-C2 and C4 intensities peaked at $5 0 { - } 6 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ due to contact of brine with shelf sediments (Guéguen et al., 2012; Stedmon et al., 202l). The Pacific winter halocline characterized by high humic-like and salinity 33-34 sees its thickness decrease when moving eastward toward the Lomonosov ridge. In the eastern Arctic Ocean, the Pacific waters were absent (Korhonen et al., 2013; Williford et al., 2022) and the Atlantic waters were separated from the surface bya cold layerat $3 0 \mathrm { m }$ in the Amundsen basin as confirmed with the XCTD surveys (Supplemental Fig. S2). Below $4 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ comparable salinity and Cl-C5 levels were found in all four basins highlighting the common Atlantic origin.

# 3.5.DOMcharacteristics in Fram Strait

In the eastern Fram Strait, the low surface fluorescence intensities (Figs.3-4) associated with the high salinity are in agreement with the absence of major freshwater sources on the west North European coast. FDOM-poor waters were observed in previous eastern Fram Strait studies (Goncalves-Araujo et al., 2016). Samples in deep eastern Fram Strait $\left( > 4 0 0 \mathrm { m } \right)$ have similar properties to those from the deep Arctic Basins, consistent with the Atlantic origin of the deep Arctic Basin waters.The export of terrigenous DOM was estimated using humic-like (C2)and a35o. Charette et al. (202O) estimated the transport of dissolved organic carbon associated with the TPD at $( 3 . 7 \pm 1 . { \overset { \mathrm { . } } { 9 } } ) \mathbf { x } 1 0 ^ { 1 5 } \mathbf { m o l }$ $\mathbf { y } \mathbf { r } ^ { - 1 }$ .However, to the best of our knowledge,there are no estimates of the CDOM and FDOM transport associated with the TPD.To do make their calculation, Charette et al. (2O2O) first estimated the volume transport of the of TPD as $0 . 9 \pm 0 . 4 \mathrm { S v }$ by multiplying prior estimates of TPD velocity by an estimate of the depth $\left( 2 5 \mathrm { m } \right)$ and width $( 6 0 0 \mathrm { k m } )$ of the TPD region in their data.With their TPD salinity of approximately 28 (corresponding to $2 0 \%$ freshwater),this implies a freshwater flux of $5 7 0 0 \pm$ $2 5 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ ,and Charette et al. (202O) noted that the low end of this estimate compares well with recent estimates of total Eurasian river runoff (Rudels, 2015).Following Charette et al. # 4. Conclusions

This study provides a comparison in chromophoric and fluorescent DOM composition in the four main Arctic Ocean basins to follow the distribution and degradation processes of DOM as controlled by transport in the TPD and storage in the Beaufort Gyre.The important difference between the Canada and Makarov Basin surface waters is the levels of terrestrially derived DOM.In the Canada Basin,the Beaufort gyre facilitates storage of surface waters and their alteration over time whereas,in the Makarov basin,the DOM-rich Siberian shelf waters were rapidly transported by TPD via the central Arctic Ocean to Fram Strait and the North Atlantic Ocean. The depth profiles showed a maximum FDOM peak in the Pacific waters in the Canada Basin,and in the PML in the Makarov and Amundsen basins.The deep waters $ 4 0 0 \ \mathrm { m }$ showed comparable humic- and protein-like composition across Arctic basins and with deep Fram Strait waters,congruent with their Atlantic origin. Fram Strait represents one of the major pathways of Arctic Ocean exchange with the Atlantic Ocean carrying up to 1816 r.u. $\mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 } \ : \mathrm { y e a r } ^ { - 1 }$ of fluorescent terrestrial humic matter and 36557 $\mathrm { { m } } ^ { - 1 } \ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 }$ year $^ { - 1 }$ of colored dissolved organic matter.
