# a b s t r a c t

Fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM) samples collected from water masses of Beaufort Sea $\mathrm { \dot { n } } = 1 2$ ) and the North Atlantic Subtropical Gyre (NASG; $\mathrm { n } = 6$ ) were assessed based on susceptibilities of its parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) modeled fluorescent components to photobleaching over 72 hours of simulated solar irradiation. 315 excitation–emission matrix spectra (EEMs) were PARAFAC modeled yielding 4 humic-like (C1–3 and C5) and 1 protein-like (C4) fluorescent components. Protein-like C4 and humic-like C5 did not adhere to first order kinetics and did not yield decay rate constant $( k )$ or half-life $\left( t _ { 1 / 2 } \right)$ values as part of this study. Humiclike C1 was found to be most resilient to photodegradation with lowest $k$ values. Principal component analysis (PCA) illustrated a shift towards $C 1 \%$ with photo-exposure in addition to distinguishing compositions of $C 1 - 5 \%$ in Beaufort Sea halocline and Atlantic layer as well as NASG deep water masses. When Beaufort Sea water masses were considered, $k$ of C2 was found to be lower in upper (UH) and mid (MH) halocline layers when compared to lower halocline (LH) and underlying Atlantic layer (AL; $\mathbf { p } < 0 . 1 0 $ ). This suggested that C2 in Pacific-derived seawater was less photoreactive than C2 in Atlantic-derived seawater in Beaufort Sea, likely as a result of distinct formation pathways of these water masses. Similarly, C2 in upper Labrador Seawater (ULSW) and ‘classical’ Labrador seawater (LSW) was less photolabile than in deeper NASG layers (Iceland-Scotland and Denmark Strait overflow waters; ISOW and DSOW). Interestingly, despite similar formation pathways of AL in Beaufort Sea and deeper waters sampled from NASG (ISOW $^ +$ DSOW), lower $k$ for C1 was found in NASG.

# 1. Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is comprised of aliphatic and aromatic organic compounds including amino acids, lignin phenols and humic/fulvic acids that are ubiquitous in marine environments (Thurman, 1985; Nelson and Siegel, 2013). DOM contains chromophores (CDOM) and fluorophores (FDOM) responsible for absorbing and emitting light, respectively, at ultraviolet (UV) and visible wavelengths. CDOM largely controls optical properties of seawater (Kitidis et al., 2006) influencing the extent of solar radiation penetration in the water column and consequently the marine ecosystem structure (Zepp, 2002; Foden et al., 2008). Photochemical degradation and transformation of DOM in marine environments influence the distribution of DOM in seawater along with autochthonous production and mixing processes (Yamashita et al., 2013). Recently, there have been advances in describing the photo-induced response of CDOM using absorption coefficients $\left( \mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } \right)$ and spectral slopes (Helms et al., 2008, 2013; Spencer et al., 2009; Shank and Evans, 2011; Stubbins et al., 2012; Swan et al.,

2012; Yamashita et al., 2013) while photobleaching of FDOM fluorescence characteristics has been studied much less extensively.

Photodegradation of fluorescent components modeled from excitation–emission matrix spectra (EEMs) using parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC; Stedmon et al., 2003) has been recently assessed in freshwaters (Laurion and Mladenov, 2013; Osburn et al., 2011; McEnroe et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2013) as well as coastal and open ocean waters (Boyd et al., 2010; Helms et al., 2013; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Stedmon et al., 2007). Helms et al. (2013) reported a higher ratio of microbially-derived/marine humic-like to terrestrially-derived visible humic-like after photobleaching indicating that visible humic-like component was particularly photo-labile, which has been shown in other works (Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Stedmon et al., 2007). Terrigenous humic-like DOM is generally considered highly photoreactive due to abundance of aromatic carbon (Obernosterer and Benner, 2004). Protein-like fluorescent components (i.e. tryptophan and tyrosine-like) have also been shown to behave distinctly during photo-exposure, both from one another, as well as other humic-like components (Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Stedmon et al., 2007; Boyd et al., 2010; Zhang et al., 2013). For example, protein-like components were photoaltered in the Baltic Sea (Stedmon et al., 2007) and

Arctic ponds (Laurion and Mladenov, 2013), but not in Lake Taihu, China (Zhang et al., 2013).

In this study, 72-hour photobleaching experiments were conducted for 18 independent FDOM samples collected from Pacific-derived halocline and Atlantic layer in Beaufort Sea $\langle \boldsymbol { \mathrm { n } } = 1 2 $ ) and from 6 mid-deep water masses in the North Atlantic Subtropical Gyre (NASG), namely Upper Labrador Sea Water (ULSW), classical Labrador Sea Water (LSW), Iceland-Scotland Overflow Water (ISOW) and Denmark Strait Overflow Water (DSOW). These mid-deep layer water masses are relatively stable, aged within 10 to 40 years (Nelson et al., 2007; Carlson et al., 2010; Smethie et al., 2000) and contain FDOM that has not been significantly photo-processed thus reducing biases that would be expected to be apparent in surface waters. This study therefore provided a unique opportunity to assess photoreactivity of PARAFAC modeled FDOM components associated with water masses in Beaufort Sea and NASG.

# 2. Methods

# 2.1. Sampling

A total of eighteen samples were collected: twelve in the Beaufort Sea between Aug. 4–30, 2013 as part of the Canada/U.S. Joint Oceans Ice Study (JOIS) Program and six in NASG in spring 2013 (May 15–18; June 15–18) onboard a research cruise led by the Bermuda Atlantic Time-series Study (BATS) (Fig. 1). Beaufort Sea samples were taken at 3 stations (BL8, CB19 and CB51) from upper (UH, $\mathrm { n } = 3$ ; practical salinity or $S _ { \mathrm { p } }$ [Millero and Huang, 2009] centered around 32.4), middle (MH, $\mathrm { n } = 3$ ; $S _ { \mathrm { p } } \sim 3 3 . 1$ ; Coachman and Barnes, 1961) and lower (LH, $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { n } } = 3 ; \boldsymbol { \mathrm { S _ { p } } }$ $\sim 3 4 . 4$ ; Woodgate et al., 2005) layers of the Pacific-derived halocline $( 5 0 - 2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ as well as the underlying Atlantic layer (AL, $\mathrm { n } = 3 ; > 3 5 0$ m and $\mathrm { S _ { p } } 3 4 . 7 5 \mathrm { - } 3 4 . 8 5$ ; Rudels et al., 1996). Samples of the NASG were collected at 1 sampling station during BATS cruises from 4 layers of the North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW). Neutral density isopycnals $( \gamma ^ { \mathrm { n } } )$ have been used to characterize NADW layers (Joyce et al., 2001). Samples were taken at 1100 and $1 3 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ from Upper Labrador Sea Water (ULSW, $\mathrm { n } = 2$ ; $\gamma ^ { \mathrm { n } } 2 7 . 8 \mathrm { - } 2 7 . 8 7 5 ~ \mathrm { k g ~ m } ^ { - 3 } )$ , at 2000 and $2 2 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ from classical Labrador Sea Water (LSW, $\mathrm { n } = 2$ ; $\gamma ^ { \mathrm { n } } 2 7 . 8 7 5  – 2 7 . 9 7 5 \mathrm { k g }$ g $\mathfrak { m } ^ { - 3 } .$ ), at $3 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ from Iceland-Scotland Overflow Water (ISOW, $\mathrm { n } =$ 1; $\gamma ^ { \mathrm { n } } 2 7 . 9 7 5 \mathrm { - } 2 8 . 0 5 \mathrm { k g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 } ,$ ) and at $4 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ from Denmark Strait Overflow Water (DSOW, $\mathrm { n } = 1$ ; $\gamma ^ { \mathrm { n } } 2 8 . 0 5  – 2 8 . 1 4 \mathrm { k g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 3 } )$ . LSW involves convective winter mixing in Labrador Sea (Lazier, 1973) and south-west Irminger Sea (Pickart et al., 2003), with less dense ULSW forming in southern Labrador Sea (Pickart et al., 1996). ISOW and DSOW are formed by waters cascading over the Iceland-Scotland Ridge and the Denmark Strait, respectively (Swift, 1984; Mauritzen, 1996). During JOIS and BATS cruises seawater was collected in Niskin bottles during casts of a rosette equipped with a conductivity-temperature-depth profiler. DOM samples collected from NASG were filtered through a $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate membrane (Whatman, Polycap AS), whereas those from Beaufort Sea were unfiltered as particulate organic matter is a very small fraction of DOM (Mathis et al., 2007). DOM samples were collected in precombusted ( $4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 4 \mathrm { h } }$ ) amber glass bottles and were immediately stored in the dark at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } .$ The Beaufort Sea and NASG samples were stored for 1–3 and 3–6 months, respectively. The regrowth of bacteria during storage was not checked, but since dark control samples showed limited changes in fluorescence loadings $( < 5 \% )$ microbial degradation was considered minimal during the $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ exposure.

# 2.2. Photobleaching Experiment

Quartz tubes $6 0 \mathrm { m L }$ ) were soaked in $1 0 \% \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S O } _ { 4 }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ followed by Milli-Q water rinsing and combustion at $4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 \mathrm { h }$ . DOM samples were dispensed into the cleaned tubes using precombusted Pasteur pipettes and were then sealed with polyethylene caps. The tubes were positioned vertically in a test tube holder which was placed in an aircooled Atlas SUNTEST ${ \tt X L S + }$ solar simulator equipped with a xenon arc lamp. Irradiation dose was set to 700 W $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 }$ , which was similar to that used in previous studies (Shank et al., 2009; Shank and Evans, 2011). Duplicate samples were taken at 0, 3, 6, 9, 12, 18, 24, 48 and $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of exposure. Duplicate dark control samples were thoroughly wrapped in aluminum foil and collected after 0, 3, 12 and $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of irradiation.

# 2.3. FDOM fluorescence

DOM excitation–emission matrix spectra (EEM) were acquired following experimental protocol outlined in Dainard and Guéguen (2013). Briefly, fluorescence measurements were made on a Horiba Jobin Yvon Fluoromax-4 using a 1-cm quartz cuvette with excitation and emission slit widths set at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . Excitation emission matrix (EEM) scans for each sample were obtained by collecting a series of emission wavelengths ranging from 300 to $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at excitation wavelengths ranging from 250 to $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ Fluorescence scans were corrected with emission and excitation correction files generated by the manufacturer and an EEM of de-ionized water (Millipore, Milli-Q) acquired daily. Finally, the fluorescence spectra were Raman calibrated by normalizing the data to the area under the Raman scatter peak (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009). A relative standard deviation (RSD) of $3 . 6 2 \%$ was associated with Raman areas determined over the 4 months of analysis. Absorbance at $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ measured on a Shimadzu UV-2550 spectrophotometer was less than 0.02 on pre irradiated samples $( 0 \mathrm { h } )$ so that reabsorption and inner filter were minimized (Miller et al., 2010).

# 2.4. PARAFAC modeling

Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was used to decompose 324 EEMs $\mathrm { { n } } = 2 4$ and 192 dark control and exposed Beaufort Sea, respectively; n $= 1 0$ and 96 dark control and exposed NASG, respectively) into independent fluorescent components. Duplicate control samples at one NASG site were immediately removed due to obvious contamination and not included in the PARAFAC model. Only two dark control samples (at $0 \mathrm { h r } \dot { }$ ) were included in the PARAFAC models as they may give problems with the split half validation. The PARAFAC model was later applied to the dark control samples collected at 3, 12 and $7 2 \mathrm { h }$ to assess for microbial degradation.

$1 . 4 \%$ and $3 . 9 \%$ of the EEMs from Beaufort Sea and NASG, respectively, were considered outliers based on their high leverages and were omitted. The analysis was carried out in Matlab R2013a (MathWorks) using drEEM (Murphy et al., 2013) and DOMFluor (Stedmon and Bro, 2008) toolboxes. The model was constrained to non-negative values and run for three to ten components. The appropriate number of components was determined by split-half and Tucker congruence coefficient analyses (Stedmon and Bro, 2008).

Proportions of fluorescent components were determined by dividing each component by total fluorescence (e.g. ${ \mathsf { C } } 1 \% = { \mathsf { C } } 1 / ( { \mathsf { C } } 1 + { \mathsf { C } } 2 +$ ${ \mathsf { C } } 3 + { \mathsf { C } } 4 + { \mathsf { C } } 5 ) ^ { * } 1 0 0 )$ . For statistical tests of variance (t-tests) and principal component analyses, the distribution of proportion data was normalized by taking the arcsine of the square root of the proportion.

# 2.5. Kinetic parameters

Rate constant $( k )$ and half-life $\left( t _ { 1 / 2 } \right)$ were calculated for each fluorescent component (C1–3) during photobleaching experiments for DOM samples following first order reaction kinetics (Shank et al., 2009; Shank and Evans, 2011). Rate constant $k$ was determined as the slope of the natural logarithm of fluorescent component intensity in Raman units (r.u.) plotted against irradiation time (0 to $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ ) (average $r ^ { 2 } =$ 0.82 and $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 5$ ). Based on the maximum excitation–emission peak position, 4 humiclike (C1-C3, C5) and 1 protein-like components (C4) were found. Component C1 (Fig. 2A) showed a primary excitation maxima at ${ < } 2 6 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ followed by a low intensity secondary peak at $3 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and an emission maximum at $4 4 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , whereas C2 (Fig. 2B) displayed a primary excitation maxima at ${ < } 2 6 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , a broad secondary peak at $3 7 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and an emission maximum at $4 7 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . Both components were described as UV/visible terrestrial humic-like (Coble, 1996) and have been reported in Arctic (Murphy et al., 2008; Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; Guéguen et al., 2014) and Atlantic waters (Jørgensen et al., 2011; Kowalczuk et al., 2009, 2013). These components appeared to have elevated degrees of aromaticity given their particularly long emission maxima (Stedmon et al., 2003).

Component C3 (Fig. 2C) showed an excitation maximum at $3 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and an emission maximum at $3 9 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . Previous studies have associated this peak to marine humic-like (Coble, 1996) and more recently to microbially derived humic-like (Ishii and Boyer, 2006; Yamashita et al., 2008). The excitation and emission spectra of C3 (Fig. 2C) matched those previously reported in the Arctic Ocean (C2 in Guéguen et al., 2012; Fig. S2). Similarly, C5 (Fig. 2E) had an excitation maximum at $2 8 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and an emission maximum at $3 9 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , which has been associated with recent biological activity (Shimotori et al., 2012; Yamashita et al., 2010) including potential microbial reprocessing (Stedmon et al., 2007) and/or phytoplankton degradation (McIntyre and Guéguen, 2012; Romera-Castillo et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2011). Similar components have been found previously in oceanic waters (Murphy et al., 2008; Jørgensen et al., 2011; Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; Kowalczuk et al., 2009, 2013).

Component C4 (Fig. 2D) showed an excitation maximum at $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and an emission maximum at $3 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , similar to protein-like component (Coble, 1996). Fluorescence of proteinaceous material has been linked with recent biological production (Coble et al., 1998) and has been commonly found in both freshwater and marine environments (Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; Fellman et al., 2009; Guéguen et al., 2012, 2014; Kowalczuk et al., 2009; Murphy et al., 2006; Murphy et al., 2008; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Walker et al., 2009; Yamashita et al., 2011).

Conversely, C3:C1 ratio decreased from 0.81 to 0.47 and 0.79 to 0.46 for Beaufort Sea and NASG samples from pre to post irradiation. Together these results suggest that C3 was more photo resistant than C2, but less than C1.

# 3.5. Principal component analysis

A principal component analysis was conducted to assess the behavior of FDOM composition upon photoexposure (Fig. 5). The first two principal components (F1–F2) accounted for $8 5 . 1 7 \%$ of the total variance (Fig. 5A). Significant ${ \bf \langle p < 0 . 0 5 \rangle }$ covariance between humic-like $C 1 { - } C 3 \%$ was found, suggesting similar behavior. Protein-like $C 4 \%$ was more strongly negatively correlated with $C 1 \%$ $r = 0 . 7 5$ ), $C 2 \%$ $r = 0 . 7 5$ and $C 3 \%$ $r = 0 . 8 6$ ) compared to $C 5 \%$ $r = 0 . 2 8$ ). In general, F1 can be described as a source axis with negative F1 representing terrestrial humic-like and positive F1 values representing marine/biologically influenced sources (Fig. 5A). Three clusters were found based on the proportion in humic-like (F1 axis; Fig. 5B): Pacific-derived halocline (UH and MH) was clustered in the left portion of the graph (negative F1) whereas Atlantic-derived halocline and Atlantic layer (LH and AL) and upper NASG (ULSW and LSW) showed higher protein-like abundance (more positive F1 scores; $\mathsf { p } < 1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 7 } .$ ). This clustering is consistent with the enrichment in humic-like fluorescence in the Pacific-derived halocline in the Arctic (Guéguen et al., 2012). # 4. Conclusions

This study examines the behavior of FDOM components during photobleaching experiments performed on samples collected from water masses of the Beaufort Sea and mid-deep layer water masses of the North Atlantic Subtropical Gyre (NASG). Humic-like C1 was found to be most resilient to photobleaching with lowest rate constant $k$ values. Beaufort Sea Pacific-derived middle halocline was found to have less readily photobleached C2 than underlying Atlantic-derived lower halocline. It was also found that C2 and C3 in Iceland-Scotland and Denmark Strait overflow waters (ISOW and DSOW) were more easily photodegraded when compared to upper and classical Labrador Sea waters (ULSW and LSW).
