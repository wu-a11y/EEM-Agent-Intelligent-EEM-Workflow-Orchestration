# Relationship between the characterization of natural colloids and metal elements in surface waters

# Abstract

Natural colloids (NCs) are ubiquities in aquatic environments, which play an important role in the fate and transport of metal elements. Combined with a multi-method analytical approach, this study investigates the spectral characteristics and the contamination of metals of NCs from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City. Results showed that NCs in river samples were characteristic by the smaller molecular weight, lower chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM) concentration, higher aromaticity, and higher CDOM contribution to the organic carbon than those in lake samples. Based on the parallel factor analysis model, three fluorophores were identified, including two humic-like components (C1 and C2) and a protein-like component (C3). NCs in river and lake waters were dominant by the humic-like substance (C1) and the protein-like substance (C3), respectively, with the relatively high fluorescence intensity for all the fluorophores in lake samples. Furthermore, NCs from the river samples were primarily terrestrial NCs with a high degree of humification. The average detection frequency of metal elements was nearly $50 \%$ for both river and lake samples, whereas the concentrations of the metal elements were higher in lake samples. Principal component analysis (PCA) results showed that the contamination of the detected metals could divide into three categories, with relatively high concentrations of Ba, Pb, Zn, Al, Sr, and Fe in lake samples. Moreover, PCA results showed that NCs in lakes with higher values of the absorbance and fluorescence parameters were associated with the higher concentration of metal elements, revealing that the spectral characteristic could be the proxy indicator of the contamination of metal elements of NCs.

# Introduction

Natural colloids (NCs) are particles with at least onedimensional size between 1 and $1 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , which are ubiquities in aquatic environments and are heterogeneous mixed particles composed of inorganic and organic matter (Lead and Wilkinson 2006). Inorganic colloids are dominated by iron/ manganese oxides and hydroxides, clay minerals, sulfides, and carbonates, while organic colloids are usually composed of small organic compounds such as fulvic and humic acids, and biopolymers such as extracellular polymers (Doucet et al. 2005; Worms et al. 2010). In addition, organic colloids are considered being the largest pool of organic matter in natural water, with up to $90 \%$ of the total organic matter (Benner et al. 1997; Song et al. 2019). The complex material composition and structural characteristics show the diverse sources and the complex environmental processes of NCs. As the important sink of contaminants in the aquatic environment, the migration and transformation of NCs are of great significance to global organic carbon dynamics and carbon cycle (Prairie 2008; Wünsch et al. 2018). Besides, because of the large specific surface area, the high contaminant reactivity, and mobility implications, it is easy for NCs to react strongly with the nutrients, trace metals, and pollutants in the aquatic environment (Li et al. 2018a; Yan et al. 2015; Yuan et al. 2018). Moreover, NCs affect the migration of elements between particles and the dissolved phase, which can be taken as an expression of “colloidal pumping” (Honeyman and Santschi 1989). On one hand, NCs can adsorb pollutants, accumulate and settle, and thus purify the water quality; on the other hand, NCs can disperse from the agglomerated particles to the dissolved phase, and then enhance the activity of the pollutants (Santschi et al. 1995). Therefore, NCs pose an important role in the migration and transformation of pollutants such as metals in aquatic environments (Wu et al. 2011; Zhang et al. 2016b).

Chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM) is the lightabsorbing component of organic colloids, which strongly absorbs ultraviolet light and significantly affect carbon dynamics, phytoplankton activity, and microbial growth in aquatic ecosystems (Zhang et al. 2009). Fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM) is CDOM emitting the fluorescence photons after radiation absorption. According to the changes in the relative proportion of different compounds or the oxidation state of FDOM, it can identify the sources of CDOM (Urban-Rich et al. 2006). Because of the numerous ionizable functional groups (e.g., carboxyl and phenolic groups) on the surface of FDOM, it exhibits strong affinities towards metal ions (Hernández et al. 2006; Wu et al. 2011). Based on this, the current studies on the quenching of FDOM by heavy metals and the migration behavior of heavy metals affecting by FDOM have been sufficient investigated (Chen et al. 2015; He et al. 2017; Zhang et al. 2017b). For example, it reported that the increase of humic acid could effectively decrease the available of Cu and $\mathrm { P b }$ , but slightly influence Cd; also, the rise of pH could obviously decline the available of all the three metals; in addition, the removal efficiency for these metals is much higher in the contaminated solution compared with the contaminated soil (Wu et al. 2017). These results revealed that different metals showed different sorption behavior towards the same FDOM, and the interaction between metals and FDOM was more complex in the natural environment. Therefore, more attention needs to be paid to the interaction between FDOM in NCs and multiple metals. Furthermore, due to the difference in the source of NCs, the combination of FDOM and metal ions still has regional uncertainty (Jiang et al. 2017; Kikuchi et al. 2017). Thus, it is necessary to carry out studies on the relationship between the physicochemical properties of NCs and metal elements in more regional natural waters.

Poyang Lake is the largest freshwater lake in China. In recent years, the water quality of Poyang Lake suffered serious pollution under the influence of human activities such as the rapid growth of the population and the rapid development of industrial and agricultural products from the surrounding area (Duan et al. 2016; Xie et al. 2013). As the major contributors of wastewater to the lake, the water quality of the upstream five tributaries received growing attention (Zhang et al. 2017a). It has been shown that the Gan River contributed over $50 \%$ of heavy metals into Poyang Lake sediments during the past five decades, which is the highest among the five rivers (Yuan et al. 2011). Nanchang City is the largest city in the downstream of the Gan River before flowing into Poyang Lake. Therefore, the heavy metal pollution in the downstream of Gan River is heavily impacted by the industrial influents and domestic wastewater from Nanchang City (Zhang et al. 2016a). There are numerous lakes in Nanchang City, which however suffer different degrees of pollution in recent years (Kuang et al. 2016). Additionally, few studies pay attention to the metal pollution of the lake waters in Nanchang City at present (Liang et al. 2014), especially for the distribution of metal elements in NCs and their correlation with the physicochemical properties of NCs.

Therefore, using cross-flow ultrafiltration (CFUF) and multiple nano-technologies, the water samples were selected along the downstream of five rivers to Poyang Lake and in the lakes of Nanchang City. The main objective of this study is to systematically assess the relationships among colloidal properties such as organic carbon, absorbance, fluorescence, and metal elements. The results provided important data for understanding the fate of NCs in the aquatic environment and for pollution control of metals.

# Materials and methods

# Sample collection and treatment

Twenty-three surface water samples from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and eight surface water samples from the lakes in Nanchang City were collected in April 2018 (Fig. 1). For samples from the rivers, R1–R5, R6–R12, R13–R17, R19–R20, and R21–R23 were collected from Xiu, Gan, Fu, Xin, and Rao River, respectively. R18 was collected from the sub-catchment of Gan, Fu, and Xin River. For samples from the lakes, L1–L6 were collected from Xiang, Qian, Huangjia, Dong, Qingshan, and Aixi Lake, respectively; Sample L7 and L8 were collected from Yao Lake. A multi-parameter water quality probe (YSI6600, USA) was used to systematically measure the general hydro-chemical parameters during sampling. Once transported to the laboratory, all surface water samples were filtered immediately through $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filters (PVDF, Millipore, USA) within 3 days. The filtrates were stored at $4 ~ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in dark for further ultrafiltration.

NCs, within the size range from $1 \ \mathrm { k D a }$ to $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ , were isolated from the filtrate by CFUF (Vivaflow50, PES membrane, $5 \times 1 0 ^ { 3 } \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ Sartorius Stadium, Germany) according to our previous study (Yan et al. 2018). To obtain enough NCs, a concentration factor $( c f )$ of 20 was chosen for further analysis of metal elements. The retentate flow was directed back to the feed container as the colloidal phase, while the permeate flow was collected separately as the soluble phase. All water samples were stored in a $4 \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ refrigerator and diluted when necessary during the spectral analysis.

# Organic carbon analysis

Organic carbon concentrations in filtrates, retentates, and permeates were determined using a total organic carbon analyzer (TOC-L, Shimadzu Corporation, Japan), which defined as $F _ { \mathrm { O C } } , R _ { \mathrm { O C } } , P _ { \mathrm { O C } } ,$ respectively. The recovery of the CFUF operation was assessed by the organic carbon recovery rate (R, $\%$ ). The calculation is shown in Eq. (1). The recoveries of the ultrafiltration process in this study were $9 4 . 1 \pm 1 6 . 3 \%$ .

$$
R = 1 0 0 \times [ ( c f - 1 ) \times P O C + R O C ] / ( c f \times F O C )
$$

where $c f$ is the concentration factor, here is 20.

# Spectrometric determination and analysis

# UV-visible absorption

UV-visible absorbance of NCs in retentates was measured using a UV-visible spectrophotometer (Shimadzu Corporation, UV-2600) in the region $2 0 0 { - } 8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (at a resolution of $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ) with a 1-cm path-length quartz cell. All samples were ultrasonicated before detection. The absorbance of Milli-Q water was taken as the baseline which was subtracted from all the samples (Batchelli et al. 2009).

The measured absorbance at wavelength $\lambda$ was converted to absorption coefficient $\alpha ( \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ according to Eq. (2).

$$
\alpha \lambda = 2 . 3 0 3 \times A \lambda / L
$$

where $\mathbf { A } _ { \lambda }$ is the absorbance at wavelength $\lambda$ , and $\mathrm { L }$ is the pathlength of the optical cell (here $0 . 0 1 \mathrm { m }$ ).

Absorption coefficient at $3 5 5 \ \mathrm { n m } \ ( \alpha _ { 3 5 5 } )$ was often chosen to represent the CDOM concentration (Helms et al. 2008). $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ $( \mathrm { L } \ : \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ is defined as the ratio of the absorption coefficient at a wavelength of $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and its dissolved organic carbon (here $R _ { O C } )$ (Eq. (3)), which is strongly related to the aromaticity of NCs (Weishaar et al. 2003).

$$
S U V A 2 5 4 = \alpha 2 5 4 / R O C
$$

In addition to the above parameters, the spectral slope (S, $\mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) provides further insights into the average characteristics of CDOM such as the ratio of fulvic acid and humic acid and molecular size (Fichot and Benner 2012; Helms et al. 2008). $S$ was calculated using a non-linear exponential model according to Eq. (4) in SigmaPlot 12.3. The spectral slope ratio $( S _ { R } )$ was determined by the spectral slope $S$ over two narrow wavelength ranges of 275–295 nm and $3 5 0 { \ - } 4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ according to Eq. (5):

$$
\begin{array} { l } { \alpha _ { \lambda } = \alpha _ { \lambda _ { 0 } } \mathrm { e } ^ { - \mathrm { S } ( \lambda - \lambda _ { 0 } ) } } \\ { S R = S 2 7 5  – 2 9 5 / S 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 } \end{array}
$$

where $\alpha _ { \lambda _ { 0 } }$ is the absorption coefficient at the reference wavelength $\lambda _ { 0 } ,$ and $S$ is the spectral slope. $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ are the absorption spectral slopes over the 275–295 nm and $3 5 0 { \ - } 4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ range, respectively.

# Fluorescence analysis

Fluorescence measurements were processed using a Hitachi F-7100 fluorescence spectrophotometer (Tokyo, Japan) to obtain the three-dimensional excitation-emission matrix fluorescence spectroscopy (3D-EEM). Excitation $\left( \operatorname { E x } \right)$ and emission (Em) wavelengths were ranged from 200 to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and from 250 to $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively, with the stepwise increments of $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ for $\operatorname { E x }$ and $2 \ \mathrm { n m }$ for $\mathrm { E m }$ . Spectrophotometer PMT voltage was adjusted to $7 0 0 \mathrm { ~ V ~ }$ . Scan speed was set at $1 2 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m \ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ , and $\operatorname { E x }$ and Em slit widths were fixed at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . Colloidal samples were diluted with Milli-Q water to avoid inner filter effect by detecting the absorbance lower than 0.3 at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ (Ohno 2002).

Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) model was performed based on a dataset of all 3D-EEMs for analyzing the fluorescence composition of NCs. The standard model was introduced and processed in Matlab 2012a with the DOMFluor toolbox (Stedmon and Bro 2008). Before modeling, the majority of Raman and Rayleigh scattering effects were trimmed using the method described in the previous study (Zepp et al. 2004). Moreover, the fluorescence intensity of all samples was normalized to Raman units (r.u.) using the area of the Raman scattering peak of Milli-Q water at $\mathrm { E x } = 3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Lawaetz and Stedmon 2009). Due to the bad signal noise ratio of $\mathrm { E x } < 2 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , the relevant raw data was deleted before modeling. The appropriate number components were determined by residual analysis, split-half analysis, and visual inspection (Stedmon et al. 2003). The quantitative and qualitative variations in NCs were estimated by the coordinate of maximum intensity value $\left( \operatorname { F } _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } \right)$ (Stedmon and Markager 2005).

The fluorescence index (FI), the humification index (HIX), and the biological index (BIX) are often used to identify the sources, age, and freshness of NCs. Equations (6) to (8) show their calculations:

$$
\begin{array} { r l } & { \mathrm { F I } = \frac { \mathrm { I n t . E m = 4 5 0 n m , E x = 3 7 0 n m } } { \mathrm { I n t . E m = 5 0 0 n m , E x = 3 7 0 n m } } } \\ & { \mathrm { H I X } = \frac { { \mathrm { A r e a } } _ { \mathrm { E m } } = 4 3 5 - 4 8 0 \mathrm { n m , E x = 2 5 4 n m } } { { \mathrm { A r e a } } _ { \mathrm { E m } } = 3 0 0 - 3 4 5 \mathrm { n m , E x = 2 5 4 n m } } } \\ & { \mathrm { B I X } = \frac { \mathrm { I n t . E m = 3 8 0 n m , E x = 3 1 0 n m } } { \mathrm { I n t . E m = 4 3 0 n m , E x = 3 1 0 n m } } } \end{array}
$$

where $\mathrm { \Delta } [ \mathrm { n t . } _ { \mathrm { E m = } x , \mathrm { E x = } y }$ is the fluorescence intensity at the corresponding wavelength of Ex and Em; $\mathrm { A r e a } _ { \mathrm { E m } = x , \mathrm { E x } = y }$ is the spectral region area at the specified Em range fixed at $\mathrm { E x } = 2 5 4$ nm.

FI describes the source of NCs with values of 1.4 indicating terrestrial source and of 1.9 indicating microbial source (Jaffé et al. 2004). HIX indicates the degree of maturation and humification of NCs with higher values (10–16) for the strongly humified organic matter (OM) and with lower values $( < 4 )$ for the autochthonous OM (Zsolnay et al. 1999). BIX indicates the autotrophic productivity with high values $( > 1 )$ associated with a predominantly autochthonous origin and low values (0.6–0.7) indicated a low OM production (Huguet et al. 2009).

# Metal element determination

All samples were digested using a Microwave Digestion System (MDS-8G Digestion System, Shanghai Sineo Microwave Chemistry Technology Co., Ltd., China). Figure S1 shows the treatment process of metal elements in retentates. Firstly, aliquots of $1 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ colloidal samples were added into a Teflon reaction container with a mixed acid solution comprising $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 } ( 6 0 \% )$ , HCl $( 3 0 \% )$ , and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ( 1 0 \% )$ . Then, all digestion vessels were digested in MDS using the gradient temperature program. After cooling to room temperature, the digested samples were transferred to a $3 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ polytetrafluoroethylene crucible and heated at $1 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ on the heating plate (EH20A plus, LabTech) until nearly dry (less than $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ ). Finally, $5 \%$ nitric acid was used to dilute the remaining solution, which was transferred into a 5-mL volumetric tube before analysis. In this study, fifteen metal elements including Al, As, Ba, Cd, Co, Cr, Cu, Mn, Mo, Ni, Pb, Se, Sr, Zn, and Fe in the colloidal samples were analyzed by inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES, 5110, Agilent Technologies Inc, USA). The detection limits of the 14 metals in this study ranged from 1 to $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ . The recoveries of the target metals spiked to the Milli-Q water ranged from 68.7 to $1 0 5 . 3 \%$ . The linear calibration curves were constructed by analyzing the target metals in $5 \%$ nitric acid ranged from 0.5 to $5 0 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ $( R ^ { 2 } > 0 . 9 9 )$ .

# Principal component analysis

PCA is a common multivariate statistical method that groups interrelated variables, explains the variance between samples over a range of parameters, and effectively reduces the dimensionality of the data (Guéguen and Cuss 2011). In this study, PCA was carried out using the SPSS 19.0 when exploring (1) the contamination of metals in rivers and lakes, and (2) the relationship between the physicochemical parameters and the metals. The data of PCA were as follows:

1. The concentration of the detected metals in river and lake samples, i.e., the concentration of Al, Ba, Co, Cr, Cu, Mn, Mo, Ni, Pb, Zn, and Fe;   
2. Three general hydro-chemical parameters (Cond, Chl_a, and $R _ { \mathrm { O C } } )$ , three absorbance parameters ( $\alpha _ { 3 5 5 }$ , $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , and $S _ { R }$ ), three fluorescence components (C1, C2, and C3), three fluorescence indices (FI, HIX, and BIX), and six typical metal elements obtained from PCA of the contamination of metals in rivers and lakes (Al, Ba, Cr, Mn, Pb, and Fe).

Bartlett sphericity test (Sig. $< 0 . 0 0 1$ ) and Kaiser-MeyerOlkin $( \mathrm { K M O } > 0 . 5 )$ ) were used to examine the validity of PCA.

# Results and discussion

# General hydro-chemical parameters in filtrates

The general hydro-chemical parameters in filtrates of waters from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City are shown in Figure S2. As the samples were collected all in April, the temperature (T) of all the water samples is similar and ranged from 17 to $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . The pH values for all the samples were circumneutral (6.91–8.06), with the relatively high and low $\mathsf { p H }$ values found in Xiu and Gan River, respectively. According to the correlation analysis, dissolved oxygen (DO) was significantly negatively correlated with T $( R = - ~ 0 . 5 8 2$ , $P = 0 . 0 0 4 _ { , }$ ), which indicated that the higher the temperature, the lower the DO value. In addition, a significant positive correlation was found between conductivity (Cond) and the total dissolved solids (TDS) for the samples from the five rivers $\textstyle R = 0 . 9 8 7$ , $P { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ ), indicating that TDS concentration played a vital role on Cond value. This result was consistent with many previous studies (Atekwana et al. 2004; Rhoades 1996). Previous studies had shown that Cond and TDS were the indicators of contaminated water with hydrocarbon and undergoing intrinsic biodegradation (Atekwana et al. 2004), which suggested that the lakes in Nanchang City with relatively high Cond and TDS values were more contaminated than those of the five rivers. This result was also verified by the higher concentration of chlorophyll-a (Chl_a) and dissolved organic carbon in waters from the lakes in Nanchang City.

# UV-visible absorbance

The absorbance parameters of NCs from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City are shown in Fig. 2. The CDOM concentrations of rivers and lakes were in the range of $1 . 4 { - } 8 . 5 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ $( 3 . 8 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , average) and $4 . 4 { - } 1 6 . 6 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ $( 9 . 1 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , average), respectively. The CDOM concentration of NCs in lakes was significantly higher than that of rivers. Compared with the results reported from other waters, the CDOM concentration in this study was within the range of that in the water level fluctuation zone of the Three Gorges Reservoir area $( 0 . 1 1 { - } 1 2 . 8 7 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) (Jiang et al. 2018) and that in the Pearl River Estuary $( 0 . 1 8 - 5 . 5 5 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) (Lei et al. 2018), whereas slightly higher than that in Poyang Lake $( 2 . 7 7 \pm$ $1 . 7 3 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ) $\mathrm { X u }$ et al. 2017).

Furthermore, the contribution of CDOM to the dissolved organic carbon and the light absorption capacity of OM can be described by dividing the CDOM concentration by the corresponding organic carbon (i.e., $\alpha _ { 3 5 5 } / R _ { O C } )$ . The results showed that the largest CDOM contribution and the largest variation among the sampling sites were both found in Gan and Xin River, while the smallest counterparts were found in Fu River. Compared with the values in lakes $( 0 . 3 2 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { ~ m g } ^ { - 1 }$ average), the overall CDOM contribution of NCs was greater in rivers $( 0 . 4 1 ~ \mathrm { L ~ m } ^ { - 1 } ~ \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 }$ , average). Combined with the results of CDOM concentration in rivers and lakes, it showed that although the absolute CDOM concentration was higher in lakes than that in rivers, the CDOM contribution to the organic carbon was lower in lakes than that in rivers. This phenomenon may be attributed to the faster degradation of CDOM in lakes which were resulted from strong microbial activity (Obernosterer and Benner 2004). Additionally, the correlation analysis results showed that the CDOM concentration was significantly positively correlated with the corresponding organic carbon concentration $R = 0 . 7 1 7$ , $P < 0 . 0 0 1$ ), which were consistent with the previous studies (Matsuoka et al. 2012, 2013). This result also revealed that the CDOM concentration in this study could be used to estimate the corresponding organic carbon concentration.

The $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values of NCs in rivers and lakes were $0 . 5 8 -$ $6 . 3 5 \mathrm { L } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ( $1 . 5 8 \mathrm { L } \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , average) and $0 . 3 5 { - } 3 . 2 8 \operatorname { L }$ $\mathrm { m g ^ { - 1 } m ^ { - 1 } } ( 1 . 0 4 \mathrm { L } \mathrm { m g ^ { - 1 } m ^ { - 1 } }$ , average), respectively, indicating the relatively high aromaticity of NCs in rivers than that in lakes. In addition, a good correlation was found between $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ value and $\alpha _ { 3 5 5 } / R _ { O C }$ $\begin{array} { r } { R = 0 . 6 8 2 } \end{array}$ , $P < 0 . 0 0 1$ ), suggesting that the CDOM contribution to the organic carbon was positively correlated with its aromaticity, that is, the greater the CDOM contribution, the higher the aromaticity.

In this study, the values of $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ in rivers ranged from 0.006 to 3.813 (0.201, average) and from 0.003 to 0.715 (0.076, average), respectively, while the counterparts in lakes were ranged from 0.011 to 0.015 (0.012, average) and from 0.005 to 0.013 (0.008, average), respectively. The $S _ { R }$ values of the samples in rivers and lakes were in the range of 0.868–3.200 (1.601, average) and 0.937–2.500 (1.673, average), respectively. $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ and the spectral slope $S _ { R }$ were usually inversely and positively correlated to the molecular weight and the aromatization degree of NCs, respectively (Helms et al. 2008). Therefore, the higher $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ , $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ , and $S _ { R }$ values in samples from rivers suggested the smaller molecular weight and the higher degree aromatization of NCs in rivers than those in lakes, which were agreed with the results of $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ value. Correlation analysis showed that $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ values were positively correlated with $S _ { R }$ values, with $R$ values of 0.817 and 0.816, respectively $( P < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ ). Studies showed that the small molecular weight of NCs was mainly freshly produced and terrestrial source or strongly photodegraded (Fichot and Benner 2012), indicating that NCs in the samples from the rivers was mainly terrestrial source or strongly photodegraded.

# 3D-EEM fluorescence analysis

# PARAFAC component characteristics

Three fluorescent components C1–C3 were extracted by the PARAFAC model on the 3D-EEMs of NCs from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City (Fig. 3). The position of Ex and Em maxima for the three components was 240 (325)/410 nm of C1, 245 (360)/ $4 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ of C2, and 230 (280)/340 nm of C3, respectively. All three PARAFAC components were well matched with the previously published PARAFAC models in the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al. 2014), with the Tucker congruence coefficient (TCC) up to 0.97 (Table S1). With the assumption that OpenFluor matching inferred the identity of the components, C1 represents humic-like material derived from biological/microbial activity, commonly in marine environments, wastewater, wetland, and agricultural environments (Garcia et al. 2018; Garcia et al. 2015; Graeber et al. 2012). C2, a terrestrial humic-like component, resembled plantderived material with more conjugated fluorescing molecules (Coble et al. 1998; Peleato et al. 2017). C3 can be characterized as a protein-like or tryptophan-like compound (Murphy et al. 2011; Shutova et al. 2014).

# Characterization of PARAFAC components in retentates

The distribution of PARAFAC components in retentates from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City is shown in Figure S3. All three fluorescence components were all detected in all sampling sites, indicating that both humic-like and protein-like fluorophores were contained in all water samples. Moreover, the fluorescence intensity of NCs in lakes was much higher than that in rivers; i.e., the average fluorescence intensity of C1, C2, and C3 in lake samples was 2.64, 2.63, and 8.38 times of that in river samples. It obviously showed that the protein-like components in lakes were much higher than those in rivers, which indicated the more serious pollution of water in lakes in Nanchang City than that in five rivers from Poyang Lake. It had been verified in the previous studies that high levels of protein-like fluorescence were usually related to anthropogenic NCs such as sewage treatment outfall, sewerage overflows, or farm wastes (Baker 2001, 2002).

Fig. 3 3D-EEMs of PARAFAC components in retentates from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City

The fluorescence intensity of the three fluorescence components (C1–C3) in rivers was 0.49–1.52 r.u., 0.31–1.02 r.u., and $0 . 4 6 { - } 1 . 2 9 \mathrm { r . u . }$ ., respectively (Figure S3). Overall, most of the samples in rivers were characterized by higher fluorescence intensity with the C1 component. Figure 4 describes the distribution of the ratios of the three fluorescence components, which also shows the highest proportion of C1 in samples from the five rivers. In detail, the proportions of C1–C3 were $3 5 \mathrm { - } 4 6 \%$ , $2 2 - 3 2 \%$ , and $2 3 \%$ , respectively. In addition, the proportion of C1 and C3 in the samples from Xiu, Fu, and Rao River increased and decreased with the direction of water flow, respectively. This suggested that there were more protein-like substance and less humic-like substance of NCs in the waters from the upstream rivers, while NCs from the downstream showed contrary to those from the upstream rivers. This differed from the situation where most cities were located in the downstream of rivers (Yuan et al. 2019). Because the sections near the estuary of the five tributaries are the wetland of Poyang Lake, most of the populations and cities are distributed in the relative upstream river, which resulted in the more serious pollution and higher protein-like substance of the waters from the upstream rivers. However, this result was not found in the Gan River; i.e., the highest proportions of C3 were shown in water samples at R11 and R12 (in the southern branch of Gan River) among all the sampling sites along Gan River. It was consistent with the chemical feature in the Nanchang section of Gan River (Li et al. 2018b), which could be attributed to the serious industrial pollution and numerous domestic sewage discharge outlets around the southern branch of Gan River (Hu et al. 2010; Yu et al. 2010).

The fluorescence intensity of the three fluorescence components (C1–C3) in lakes was 1.06–8.45 r.u., 0.60–6.67 r.u., and 1.15–23.65 r.u., respectively (Figure S3). Most of the samples in lakes were characterized by higher fluorescence intensity with C3 component. Figure 4 shows that the proportions of C1–C3 in samples from lakes in Nanchang City were $1 5 \mathrm { - } 4 0 \%$ , $7- 2 6 \%$ , and $3 5 \mathrm { - } 7 8 \%$ , respectively, with the highest proportion found for C3. The intensity of all fluorescence components from L7 was highest among all the samples from lakes, especially for C3 component. It indicated the relatively serious pollution at this sampling site, which may be related to the sampling point near the sewage outlet.

# Fluorescence indices

Figure S4 shows the values of the fluorescence indices (i.e., FI, HIX, and BIX) of NCs from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City. FI values in rivers and lakes ranged from 1.31 to 1.43 and from 1.24 to 1.42, indicating that all the NCs in this study were mainly terrestrial sources. In general, the FI values in rivers were slightly lower than those in lakes, suggesting that NCs from rivers were dominated by terrestrial sources, which was consistent with the results from the analysis of absorbance (i.e., $S _ { R }$ ) . Moreover, the lower FI value also characterized the higher aromaticity of NCs (Batchelli et al. 2009), demonstrating the higher aromaticity of NCs in rivers than that in lakes. In contrast to the FI values, the HIX values of NCs in rivers were generally higher than those in lakes. In detail, the HIX values of NCs in rivers were all higher than 4 (4.7–10.9), indicating the higher degree humification of NCs in rivers, while the counterparts in lakes were in the range of 0.4–12.0, showing the larger variation but a generally lower degree of humification. The BIX values of NCs in rivers and lakes were $0 . 6 { - } 0 . 7$ (all values lower than 0.8) and 0.7–1.1, respectively, suggesting the primarily autochthonous origin of NCs in lakes.

# Characterization of metal elements in retentates

Of the selected fifteen metal elements, only three metals (As, Cd, and Se) were not detected in any water samples from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City. The detection frequencies of Al, Ba, Cr, Mn, Ni, Sr, Zn, and Fe were all greater than $80 \%$ , while the detection frequencies of Co, Cu, Mo, and $\mathrm { P b }$ were lower than $20 \%$ (only found in one or two samples). The average detection frequency between the river samples and the lake samples showed little difference, with an average detection frequency of $5 5 \%$ .

The concentration of metal elements of NCs in this study showed great differences. Among the twelve detected metal elements, the concentration of Fe was the highest with the average value of $7 3 . 4 9 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ , followed by $Z \mathrm { n }$ with the average value of $3 7 . 8 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g } } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ , and the lowest concentration was found for Mo with the average value of $0 . 0 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g } } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ (Fig. 5). The metal concentrations of NCs in most of the samples were lower than the Environmental Quality Standards for Surface Water (GB3838-2002). Only the concentration of Cr in R13, R17, and L7 sample exceeded the level of class V ( $1 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g } }$ ${ \mathrm { L } } ^ { - 1 }$ ), and the concentration of $Z \mathrm { n }$ in a few samples exceeded the level of class I $( 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ . Besides $Z \mathrm { n }$ , the metal concentrations of NCs in river samples were generally lower than those in lake samples. The concentration of Al, Ba, Co, Cr, Cu, Mn, Ni, Sr, Zn, and Fe in lake samples was 1.5, 1.3, 1.6, 1.7, 2.0, 2.1, 1.5, 2.0, 0.5, and 2.0 times of the corresponding metal concentrations in river samples, which demonstrate the more serious pollution of metals in NCs from the urban lake waters. The higher average concentration of $Z \mathrm { n }$ in river samples could be attributed to the higher concentration of $Z \mathrm { n }$ at R13 and R14 sampling site in Fu River (about $4 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ).

In addition, PCA was introduced to explore the contamination characteristics of the twelve detected metal elements and the distribution of metal elements in rivers and lakes $( \mathrm { K M O } = 0 . 7 1$ , Sig. $< 0 . 0 0 1$ ). Three principal components (PC1–PC3) were sufficient to explain the characteristics contamination of the metals, accounting for $4 7 . 1 \%$ , $2 0 . 2 \%$ , and $1 6 . 2 \%$ of the total variance, respectively. Rotated factor matrix (Fig. 6a) showed that Co, Cr, Cu, Mn, Mo, and Ni had the higher positive weightings on PC1 $( > 0 . 9 )$ , indicating that PC1 represented the pollution of these six metal elements. While Ba, Pb, and $Z \mathrm { n }$ had the highest positive loadings on PC2, revealing that PC2 represented the pollution of these three metal elements, the other three metal elements (i.e. Al, Sr, and Fe) showed high positive loadings on PC3, revealing that PC3 represented the pollution of these metal elements. The scores plotting for the river and lake samples showed (Fig. 6b) that both the scores of the river and lake samples on PC1 were not high, indicating the low-level concentration of these six metal elements for NCs in rivers and lakes with little difference between them. However, the lake samples contributed higher positively scores to PC2 and PC3 compared with the river samples, which suggested that the concentration of Ba, Pb, Zn, Al, Sr, and Fe was higher in lake samples.

# Correlation analysis between physicochemical properties and metal elements

Pearson correlation analysis was conducted between the fluorescence components (C1–C3) and the fluorescence indices (FI, HIX, and BIX) (Table S2). Results showed that the three fluorescence components were significantly interpersonally correlated to each other $( R > 0 . 9$ , $P < 0 . 0 1$ ), indicating the homology sources of humic-like and protein-like materials of NCs in this study. Additionally, all the three fluorescence components were significantly negatively and positively correlated with the HIX value and BIX value, respectively, suggesting that the higher concentration of fluorescence substance in NCs was associated with the high autochthonous origin and low degree humification of OM. This result was agreed with the above analysis that the high fluorescence intensity of NCs in samples from the lakes was characterized by the high primarily autochthonous origin and low degree humification.

PCA was also processed in order to investigate the relationship between the colloidal physicochemical properties and the metal elements with $\mathrm { K M O } = 0 . 6 5$ (Sig. $< 0 . 0 0 1$ ). Based on PCA, six PCs were obtained, accounting for $2 5 . 6 \%$ (PC1), $1 3 . 5 \%$ (PC2), $12 . 2 \%$ (PC3), $1 1 . 3 \%$ (PC4), $9 . 9 \%$ (PC5), and $7 . 9 \%$ (PC6) of the total variance, respectively. Figure 7a displays the loading values of each variable after rotation on each PC: (1) PC1 represented the fluorescence parameters with the fluorescence components (C1, C2, and C3) and the fluorescence indices (FI and BIX) having higher positive weighting on PC1 (loadings $> 0 . 6$ ); (2) PC2 represented the colloidal organic carbon and the concentration of Fe (loadings $>$ 0.75); (3) PC3 and PC4 represented the concentration of $\mathrm { C r }$ and Mn and Ba and $\mathrm { P b }$ , respectively, which were consistent with the results from PCA of the contamination of metals; (4) PC5 represented the absorbance parameters with the absorbance parameters ( $\alpha _ { 3 5 5 }$ and $S _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ and Chl_a having relatively high positive loadings on PC5 (loadings $> 0 . 6$ ); (5) PC6 represented the concentration of Al (loadings $= \ 0 . 8 7$ ). The similar results were found for PC5 and PC6, indicating that the value of absorbance parameters and the concentration of Al were higher in samples from lakes than those in the five rivers. But PC3 scores of rivers were higher than those of lakes, which may be related to the higher concentration of $\mathrm { C r }$ and Mn in Fu River. The previous study showed that different species and portions of functional groups (such as carboxylic, phenolic, and hydroxyl groups) on the fluorophores can result to the changes in affinity for the metals (Huang et al. 2018; Liu et al. 2019). That is, the absorbance and fluorescence characteristic could be the proxy indicator of the contamination of metal elements of NCs. Furthermore, as the crucial effective carriers, Fe, Al, and Mn oxyhydroxides and/or oxides often play the preponderant role of the trace metal sorption in the aquatic environment (Baalousha et al. 2019; Gandois et al. 2020). However, no significant relationship was found between them in this study. For example, NCs in lakes have higher scores on PC2 and lower scores on PC3 and PC4, i.e., no relationship exited between Fe and the other trace metals such as $\mathrm { C r }$ and Mn. This might be because these metals mainly form of inorganicorganic complex and their pristine traits are replaced by the complex, which is conducive to stable interactions with the trace metals (Shaker et al. 2012; Xiong et al. 2015). However, it is worth noting that the different metals could show different sorption behavior even for the same fluorophore (e.g., the number of sorption sites) (Yang et al. 2019), while the same metal also could show different sorption behavior to the different fluorophores (e.g., the sorption order) (Huang et al. 2018). Therefore, the indicator of the spectral properties of NCs to the contamination of metals should be investigated much further to know the deeper mechanism.

# Conclusion

Characterization of NCs from the five tributaries of Poyang Lake and the lakes in Nanchang City was conducted in this study by applying multiple technologies, aiming to explore the relationship between the physicochemical properties (i.e., absorbance and fluorescence) of NCs and the contamination of metals. Compared with the samples in lakes, NCs in river samples showed the low molecular weight, the high degree of aromaticity, and low CDOM concentration, while high CDOM contribution to the organic carbon. In addition, NCs in rivers were dominated by the more humified OM derived from the terrestrial source. Based on 3D-EEMs and PARAFAC model, two humic-like components (C1 and C2) and a protein-like component (C3) were identified. The fluorescence intensity of all the fluorophores in lake samples was much higher than that in river samples. But the primary fluorescence component of NCs was quite different with the higher proportion of C1 and C3 in river and lake samples, respectively. The concentration of metal elements in lakes was generally higher than that in rivers, especially for Ba, Pb, Zn, Al, Sr, and Fe. Relationship between the physicochemical properties and the contamination of metals from PCA showed that the higher values of the absorbance and fluorescence parameters were usually associated with the high concentration of most of the metal elements. These results in this study could have the potential for wider applications in extrapolating to the natural aquatic environments when considering the impact of colloids on the fate and transport of pollutants such as the metals.
