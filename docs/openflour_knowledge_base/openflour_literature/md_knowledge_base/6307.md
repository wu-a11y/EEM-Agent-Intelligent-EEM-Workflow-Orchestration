Abstract Peatland open-Water pools can be net carbon (C) emiters within heterogeneous peatland ecosystems that are generally net C sinks. However,the intra- and inter-regional paterns and drivers of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ production,as well as their link with dissolved organic mater (DOM) quality and quantity, remain poorly understood. We analyzed a range of optical characteristics and chemical variables controlling DOM and $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ concentrations in peatland pools across two regions with contrasting geographical properties (i.e., climate,topography, morphometry,and vegetation cover) of eastern Canada and Chilean Patagonia. We found inter-regional patterns in $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ and DOM concentrations and composition that were coherent with patterns in mean annual temperature and precipitation,and vegetation cover. Cross-regional patterns of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ were driven by morphometry,vegetation cover,and protein-like DOM composition,a proxy of high biological activity,whereas temporal variations of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ concentrations were further influenced by seasonal changes in humic-like DOM composition,dissolved organic carbon and nutrients (i.e., total phosphorus and total nitrogen) concentrations,as well as $\mathsf { p H }$ and oxygen levels. Our results suggest that geophysical constraints associated with local peat and pool characteristics as well as climate paterns are major drivers of DOM and greenhouse gases concentrations and the links between them in broadly distributed peatland pools.

# 1. Introduction

Peatlands are important global carbon (C) reservoir as net primary production is higher than peat decomposition over millnnial time scales (Frolking etal.,2O01;Waddington& Roulet,2OO0).Nevertheles,peatland open-water pools play a particular role in the C dynamics of heterogeneous C-rich peatland ecosystems,as they are often net C sources to the atmosphere (McEnroe etal.,2009; Peletier et al.,2014; Repo et al.,2007; Turner et al.,2016; Waddington& Roulet,2Oo0).This can bedue to peat decomposition at thebotomof pools,photochemical and microbial degradation of dissolved organic matter (DOM),as wellas low atmospheric C uptake by the limited aquatic vegetation (Pelletier et al.,2014).Decomposition at the bottom of peatland pools is higher than in other freshwater systems due to morphological characteristics of pools (smaller size and shallwer depth) that favor higher sediment temperature through the direct uptake of energy from the environment (Holgerson & Raymond, 2016; Wik et al.,2014,2016).The heterogeneityof Caccumulation and emissionbetween terrestrial and aquatic parts of peatlands has seldom been included in the contemporary C budget for these ecosystems (Pelletier et al.,2O15)and there is limited information on the paterns and drivers of Ccycling within peatland pools compared to their terrestrial areas as well as other small freshwater ecosystems.

Multiple properties (i.e.,climate,hydrology,morphology,vegetation cover,and water chemistry）dictate the spatial and temporal paterns in theCcycling of smallakes,ponds and wetlands (Arsenault etal.,2O18; DelSontro et al.,2016; Holgerson,2015; Kuhnetal.,2021; Turner etal.,2016; Wik etal.,2016).Yet,itisunclear howthese processes drive C cycling in peatland pools (Arsenault etal., 2022). In small waterbodies, climate (i.e., temperature and precipitation) and hydrological connectivity influence the production of greenhouse gases (GHG) by affecting internal biogeochemical processes (i.e.,photo-chemical and microbial degradation,primary production,and water chemistry)(Serikovaet al.,2019; Weyhenmeyeretal.,2015; Wik etal.,2016; Zhong etal.,2020). For example, warming temperatures increase methane $\mathrm { ( C H _ { 4 } ) }$ production in ponds and small peatland lakes during summer by stimulating methanogenic activity in sediments (Holgerson, 2015; Kuhn et al.,2021). Temperature also increases the activities of heterotrophic microbial respiration and hence increases carbon dioxide $( \mathbf { C O } _ { 2 } )$ production in water columns and sediments (Kosten et al.,2010; Sobek et al.,2017). Depending on catchment characteristics (e.g., vegetation cover,soil characteristics,geomorphology),increased precipitation can lead to elevated concentrations of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ (Einola et al., 2011; Roehm et al., 2009) and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ (Natchimuthu et al., 2014) through the input of terrigenous dissolved organic carbon (DOC) to small waterbodies.On the contrary,precipitation and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ concentrations can be inversely related due to the dilution of DOM and nutrients and an increase in dissolved oxygen in smallponds after precipitation events (Holgerson,2O15). During wet periods,a positive hydrological gradient driven by higher water levels in peat than in pools can enable the transport of terrestrial DOM and nutrients from peat to pools via groundwater flow (Arsenault et al.,2O19).Lateral exchanges with the surrounding peat may also transport $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ produced in the organic soil (e.g., Weyhenmeyer et al.,2015). Peatland pools are generally nutrient-poor,and nutrients typically do not show a significant influence on $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ production (Turner et al., 2016).

DOM is an important player in the global Ccycle as it acts as an indirect modulator of multiple aquatic biogeochemical processes (Prairie,2Oo8),including the availability of substrates for $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ production (Coble,2O0O7). In peatland pools,DOM quality is influenced by the peat characteristics and vegetation cover.For example,Arsenault et al. (2O18) found higher specific UV absorbance (SUVA), which is an indicator of DOM aromaticity,in pools surrounded by trees and shrubs than those surrounded by mosses.SUVA and $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration were positively correlated in artificial urban ponds (Peacock etal.,2O19)and peatland pools (Arsenault et al.,2018; Peletier et al.,2014; Turner et al.,2O16).As for morphology, pool surface area and depth have a significant inverse relationship with $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ fluxes (McEnroe et al.,20O9). Together, this suggests that spatial and temporal paterns in climate, peat characteristics,and water environmental properties may lead to predictable paterns in DOM quality and quantityas wellas GHG concentrations in peatland pools,but there is a lack of empirical data supporting this hypothesis.

Long-term averages of climate (e.g., mean annual temperature (MAT) or precipitation (MAP)) have been shown to correlate with broad-scale patterns of geographic and ecosystem properties in aquatic environments (e.g,land cover,water clarity, nutrients and chlorophyll $^ { \cdot a }$ concentrations) because such measurements are highly structured in space (Lapierre et al., 2O18). Most climate variables used to predict aquatic ecosystem functioning, however, have relatively low temporal (yearly to decadal scales)compared to spatial variation (Soranno et al.,2O2O). Given theirimpact on aquatic Ccycling,climatic conditions may thus induce broad-scale paterns in the Cbiogeochemistry of peatland pools.Indeed,cross-regional variabilityof peatland pool DOC and GHG concentrations as well as other water chemistry has been shown to be higher than within regions due to variability in geophysical properties along a climate gradient in threeregions (northern Ireland,northern Scotland and south-west Scotland)of the UK(Turner etal.,2016).Landscape position (i.e.,distance from the sea)affects the internal biogeochemical characteristics of peatland pools (Turner etal.,2O016)and freshwater wetland ponds (Vizza et al.,2017)by influencing the precipitation chemistry,and by enhancing the atmospheric deposition of sea-borne cations (Haraguchi et al.,2003).Smaller scale patterns in DOM and GHG in ponds and peatland pools could thus be more strongly related to locally structured geographic and morphological properties such as vegetation or pond depth and area (e.g.,Arsenault etal.,2018). However,the relative effects of climate and geomorphological properties on multiplefacets of peatland pools Ccycling have rarely,if ever,been tested in asingle study.Moreover,while numerous studies have reported empirical relationships between DOM concentrations and properties with GHG in different aquatic ecosystems (e.g.,Amaral et al.,2021; D'Amario& Xenopoulos,2015; Zhou etal.,2018),fundamental differences in peatland pools functioning may limit the transposition of knowledge derived from lakes to derive understanding in peatland ponds (Arsenault et al.,2O22).Therefore,the potential inks between DOM and GHG in peatland pools found across wide geographical gradients remain largely unknown.

In this context, our goal was to assess and quantify patterns and drivers of DOM and dissolved $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ in peatland pools with changing geographic properties. We did so by collecting data from eight peatland sites located acrosstwo regions in eastern Canada and southern Chilean Patagonia. Our specific objectives were to: (a)determine the influence of intra-and inter-regional paterns of climate,topography,morphology,vegetation cover and water environmental properties on DOM composition and GHG concentrations in peatland pools, (b)assess the efects of DOM composition on GHG concentrations,and (c) evaluate the temporal variability of climatic conditions and water environmental properties,and subsequent impacts on DOM composition and GHG concentrations within a region.

UV-visible absorbance measurements were recorded between 190 and $9 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ using a $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ -path quartz cell on a Shimadzu UV-1800 spectrophotometer (Shimadzu Scientific Instruments, USA). The ratio of absorbance at $4 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ to absorbance at $6 6 5 \mathrm { n m }$ (E4/E6) was calculated as a proxy of terrestrially loaded humic DOM in pools, with higher values indicating lower aromaticity of humic substances and lower degree of humification.This proxy can inform on the humification condition of the peatland area adjacent to the pool (Turner et al.,2O16).DOMfluorescence spectra were scanned using a Varian Cary Eclipse Fluorescence Spectrophotometer(Agilent Technologies, USA) with a $1 0 \mathrm { m m }$ -path quartz cuvette at room temperature by measuring fluorescence intensity across $2 2 0 { - } 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $5 \mathrm { - n m }$ increments) excitation wavelengths and $2 3 0 { - } 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (2-nm increments) emission wavelengths at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ slit widths.

# 2.5. PARAFAC Modeling and DOM Components Identification and Interpretation

Fluorescence spectra were used to conduct Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC)on inner-filter effect corrected 156 samples, which decompose the excitation and emision matrices (EEMs) by grouping fluorescent compounds with a similar molecular structure into different components (Stedmon et al.,2O03)using MATLAB with drEEM toolbox (Murphy et al., 2013). We validated a 3-component PARAFAC model that explained $9 9 . 6 \%$ of the variation among EEMs data (Table S1 in Supporting Information S2 and Figure S1 in Supporting Information S1).

The online repository OpenFluor database was used for the comparison of the PARAFAC components with published studies (Murphy et al.,2O14; Table S1 in Supporting Information S2).There were two humic-like components (C1 and C2) that have previously been associated to a terrstrial origin.In particular,these two humic-like fluorophores associated with high molecular weight and aromatic compounds have similar spectral features and are most common in freshwater environments (Lambert et al., 2O16; Walker et al.,2013; Wauthy et al.,2018; Yamashita etal.,2O08).A protein-like component (C3) has been associated toan autochthonous origin from production in the water column or to recent production in surrounding environments (Li et al.,2016; Stedmon etal.,2003; Walker etal.,2013).It is typically associated to high biological activity in environments where it is abundant (Li et al.,2O16).In this study,SUVA,E4/E6 ratioand relative abundance of PARAFAC components $( \% { \bf C } 1 { - } \% { \bf C } 3 )$ ） were used to characterize the DOM quality.

# 2.6. Statistical Analyses

Kruskal-Wallis rank tests with Dunn's post hoc tests $( p < 0 . 0 5 ) $ ）were carried out to identify the regional variability of geographical parameters and the spatial and temporal distribution of DOM and GHG. Variance partitioning analysis was conducted to explain how different sets of geographical predictors contribute to DOM and GHG variables. Partial least squares regressons (PLSR) were performed using“pls"r package to identify how morphology,topography,vegetation cover, water chemistry,and DOM composition and concentration influenced the levels of GHG to explore spatial C dynamics among regions and temporal C dynamics within GPB.Non-normalized data of response and explanatory variables were standardized through log transformation. We selected this predictive PLS regression method due to multicollinearity among our variables.We also calculated variable importance in projection (VIP) to identify which variables strongly influenced the GHG concentrations in pools. The R software (R 4.2.1—R Core Team,2022)was used to perform al statistical analyses and graphs.

# 4. Discussion

In this study,we found spatial patterns in DOM and GHG (i.e.,concentrations of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 } )$ in peatland pools that were associated with inter-regional paterns in climate and pool morphometry,vegetation cover,and the predominance of protein-like DOM in the water. Temporal patterns in DOM and GHG were less variable than among pool patterns at the local peat scale,suggesting that inter-pool differences in water chemistry (Figure 4),likely driven by differences in morphometry(Arsenault et al.,2O18),were relatively more important than the seasonal changes in temperature and hydrology that may be driving seasonal patterns. These results highlight scale-dependent controls on peatland pools carbon (C) cycling,where morphometric and climate factors appear to affect DOM and GHG,as well as the links among these,in the water,but the main drivers varied from local to inter-regional scale.

At the inter-regional scale,the effect of climate on DOC concentration and composition was significant but relatively weak compared to that of morphometry (Figure la): DOC appeared generally higher in warmer regions,although this effect was not linear (Figure S2 in Supporting Information S1),while DOC appeared to be negatively related to pool specific variables area and depth (Figure 2a). This could be explained by higher production and leaching of DOC in and from the peat at higher temperatures (Freeman et al., 2001). These differential inputs and processing rates appeared to impact DOM composition,as $\% { \bf C } 1$ tended to increase as a function of MAT and decrease as a function of MAP,a pattern that was inverse compared to $\% \mathbf { C } 2$ (Figure S2 in Supporting Information S1).The similar pattern between DOC and $\% { \bf C 1 }$ suggests that most of the DOC is composed of C1 and that C1 predominately originates from the peat.The weak relationship between the percent of protein-like C3 and climate variables suggests that it may follow a more local behavior tied to rapid in-pool production and uptake,as has been shown in other types of aquatic ecosystems (Lapierre & del Giorgio,2014).The DOC concentrations (Table 2) in this study are within the range of those found in peatland pools in the UK and Canada (Billett & Moore,2OO8;Hamilton et al.,1994; Pelletier et al., 2014; Turner et al., 2016),in small peatland lakes in boreal western Canada (Kuhn et al., 2O21) and in streams draining peatland sites in the UK (Billett et al.,2006,2007; Clark et al.,2008)and Finland (Dinsmore et al., 2O11). The effects of climate and landscape position on inter-regional patterns in DOM could thus be mediated by their influences on the floristic composition and peat characteristics of peatlands.

Pool area was the most important morphological driver of GHG patterns, with smaller pools having higher levels of both $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ (Figure 2). Reasons behind the high GHG concentrations in small pools compared to larger pools could include high terrigenous C loading relative to water volume,high perimeter to area ratio,and low water depth (Holgerson, 2015; Holgerson & Raymond,2016;Wik et al., 2016). These factors would favor higher direct inputs of peat-derived GHG and stimulate in situ production of GHG through increased DOM inputs.At the inter-regional scale,depth did not show any relationship with GHG concentrations,a pattern opposed to the findings of Arsenault et al.(2018) within the GPB peatland pools. Our results thus suggest that the high inter-regional variation in climate, vegetation and pool area in the sampled pools overwhelms the local variation in depth in driving peatland pools C biogeochemistry over broad spatial extents.

At the regional scale,DOM quality may primarily depend on the type of surounding vegetation, while its concentration depends on the morphometry of pools.The higher absolute concentrations and relative abundance of humiclike DOM(C1 and C2)that we typically observed across regions (Table S2 in Supporting Information S2; Table 2) indicate high terrestrial inputs from the surrounding peat at allsites.A strong positive relationship was found between humic-like DOM composition and SUVA (Figure 2a),suggestive of high molecular weight and aromaticity (Helms et al.,2008).At GPB,Arsenault et al. (2O18) previously measured higher SUVA in peatland pools surounded by trees and shrub plants (trees $>$ shrubs $>$ herbs $>$ mosses). Here, we found relatively homogeneous values among the regions $( p = 0 . 8 8$ ； Table S2 in Supporting Information S2) that could be explained by a similar dominance of mosses across regions (Table 1). The protein-like DOM composition $( \% C 3 )$ had a positive relationship with relative trees and mosses cover and peat humification index (E4/E6ratio),suggesting that this component may also originate fromfreshly produced terrestriallyderivedDOM(Amaral etal.,2O21; Lapierre etal.,2013; Yamashita et al.,2013).

The regional variability of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ concentrations (Figures 1b and 1c) could be explained by direct and indirect climate effects.Warmer temperatures can enhance GHG production (especially $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { , }$ in the bottom anoxic sediments by stimulating the activities of methanogens (Kuhn et al.,2021). However, we found no strong relationship between long-term averages in MAT and MAP (Figure 2; Figure S3 in Supporting Information S1),suggesting thatif there is a climate effect,itis more related to short-term variations in weather,orit is mediated by more proximal factors directly associated to gas production and loading mechanisms in or around pools. An alternative pathway for GHG paterns in peatland pools is the input of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ produced in the peat (Billet et al.,2O04). The strong positive relationship between peat characteristics (E4/E6 ratio)and GHG levels suggests that peat-produced GHG could have been imported into peatland pools along with peat-derived DOC.Low humification of surrounding peat and small pool size in GPB compared to other studied peatland sites suggest that there may be higher hydraulic connectivity between peat and pools in this site (Billett et al.,2012),influencing peat-produced GHG transport to the pools. Our findings of increasing $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ concentrations with higher tree abundance suggest that root exudate andlitter deposition may stimulate the inputs of freshly produced and bio-labile DOM from peat to water (Hoyos-Santillan et al.,2O16),increasing biological activity in pools. Inter-regional patterns in climate and in peat characteristics may thus drive spatial patterns in the inputs of DOM and GHG from peat to water, as wellas in situ production of DOM and GHG within pools.However, further study is required to identify how peat properties (i.e., peat structure and biogeochemical characteristics)and hydrological connectivity influence the pool C biogeochemistry by transporting DOM and peat-produced GHG using molecular analysis.

We found that GHG most strongly correlated with autochthonous or freshly produced allochthonous DOM $( \% { \bf C } 3 )$ across the studied sites (Figure 2). Our findings of decreasing $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ concentrations in peatland pools with higher humic-like DOM composition contrast previous studies in lake,stream, river,and estuary (Amaral et al., 2021;D'Amario & Xenopoulos,2015; Zhou et al.,2O18),where high inputs of terrestrial DOM may correlate with hydrological inputs of terrestrially produced GHG,and where terrestrial DOM itself may serve as an important substrate for mineralization to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ within the aquatic environment.We suggest that this discrepancy may have occurred because of different hydrological settings,morphometry,loadings of terrestrial DOM and nutrient limitation in peatland pools compared to these previously studied systems.A recent study conducted on peatland lakes found an inverse relationship between DOC and $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentrations (Kuhn et al.,2O21),which may be explained by the fact that increasing terrestrial-derived DOM may not significantly influence microbial respiration compared to its stimulating effect on aquatic primary production.This reasoning is coherent with the fact that protein-like DOM composition $( \% { \bf C } 3 )$ appeared to positively influence the cross-regional spatial distribution of dissolved $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ concentrations in peatland pools. This protein-like DOM may originate from high phototrophic and heterotrophic activity in the water or at the water-peat interface,or be loaded from the surrounding peat under conditions of high production or mobilization of DOM from peat to water.A previous study has found that presumed high input of freshly produced DOM (in the water or on land) in wetlands may sustain high proportions of protein-like DOM (Lapierre & del Giorgio,2014),although this DOM may differ from Sphagnum-produced DOM. Aquatic microbial communities then preferentially consume and respire this protein-like DOM due to its high accessibility and nutrient quality (Guillemette et al.,2016; Kritzberg et al., 2004), potentiallyleading to high levels of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ production. On the other hand,methanogenesis in anoxic C-rich sediment is the main pathway of biological $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ production in aquatic environments (Michaud et al.,2O17). Intrinsic algal-derived DOM can also be preferentially bio-labile for the microbial community in an anoxic environment (Zhou et al.,2018)and likely enhance methanogenesis (Bogard et al.,2014). Our findings thus suggest that higher abundance of protein-like freshly produced DOM in peatland pools leads to higher biological activity,hence to higher GHG production and concentrations.

Inter-pool pattrns of DOM were stronger than temporal patterns in GPB (Figure 4),suggesting that pool morphometry may be more important than seasonal variation in temperature and hydrology in driving DOM paterns in a peatland where climatic and other geographic factors are constrained.DOC and $\% { \bf C 1 }$ tended to increase with progressing growing seasons (Figures 3a and 3b), but only the month of September was significantly different from other months due to large pool-to-pool variation.Protein-like DOM $( \% C 3 )$ showed significant temporal changes with the highest values during mid-summer (July-August). Primary production (algae and cyanobacteria) may be higher in the limnetic botom peatof pools due to the availabilityoflight,oxygen,and nutrients (Hamilton et al.,1994),resulting in higher production of autochthonous DOM,but this has not been measured.Moreover,terrestrial DOMand nutrients are known to be transported from surrounding peat to pools throughout the summerdue to higher water table in peatcompared to pool water level (Arsenault et al.,2O19).This lateral input, combined with evaporation in the pool may be responsible for higher loading and concentration of peat-derived protein-like C3 during mid-summer. $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ tended to increase over months and reached a maximum in September, similar to DOC and $\% { \bf C } 1$ ,but $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ followed a pattern that more closely matched temporal variation in protein-like $\% \mathbf { C } 3$ DOM.Unlike C3, however,this patern was not significant because variation among pools at any moment is higher than variation within asingle pool over time. At the intra-regional scale,higher inputs ofhumic-like DOM $( \% { \bf C 1 } )$ and nutrients (TN and TP) from peat to water are a stronger driver of GHG patterns than the proportion of protein-like DOM( $\% \mathbf { C } 3$ ; Figure 4), suggesting different controls on GHG dynamics across spatial and temporal scales.It appears that increasing water temperature, nutrients,and high loading of DOM from surounding peat during the growing season may create an environment for elevated biological and photochemical production of GHG in the pools,as wellas be a proxyof the input of GHG from the surounding peat.Moreover,high peat-loading DOM and degradation processes in water create a favorable anoxic environment for methanogens by driving $\mathsf { p H }$ and DO levels down and leading to high in situ $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ production in pools (Figure 4b). Strong relation between total nutrients (TN and TP) and GHG as well as the weak relation between inorganic nutrients $( \mathrm { P O } _ { 4 } ^ { 3 - }$ $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ ,and $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + } ,$ and GHG (Figure 4b)suggest that organic nutrients may be an important driver of GHG production n peatland pools.Among pools, however, humic-like DOM composition explained a larger proportion of $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ temporal patern than the protein-like DOM.Recent studies found that high content of terrestrial-derived DOM contributes to methanogenesis along with algal-derived autochthonous DOM(Araujo et al.,2018). Moreover,peat-produced GHG may be imported via runof(Paytan et al.,2015; Weyhenmeyer et al.,2O15)throughout the growing season explaining in part the elevated $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ levels that were observed in this study,but evidence remains rare for hydrological gas input from peat to pools.

# 5.Conclusion

Peatlands are important players in the global carbon budget (Frolking et al.,2O01; Waddington & Roulet,2000; Yu et al.,2O11),and the C dynamics in pools can,to some level,counter-balance the net role of peatlands as sinks (Pelletier et al.,2015).While there is rich literature on the aquatic C cycling in lakes and rivers,this knowledge cannot be readily transposed to peatland pools because they occupy a very diferent biogeochemical niche compared to other lentic ecosystems (Arsenault et al.2022). We found that local scale drivers such as pool morphometry predominantly control intra-regional, temporal paterns in DOM and GHG (both $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 } )$ ， as well as,with vegetation cover,the inter-regional patterns in DOM and $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ ；this suggests that these drivers of peatland pools C cycling are structured over broad spatial extents,with an effect on DOM and $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ that is independent of climate.Autochthonous protein-like DOM appears as an indicator of biological activity and within pool GHG production across regions,a pattrn that is opposed to most other aquatic ecosystems where a positive correlation between terrestrial humic-like DOM and GHG concentrations has been observed. This result is coherent with the relatively stronger relationship of $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ with protein-like DOM (Figure 2b),as well as with long-term climate averages (Figure lc).These results show how DOMand GHG paterns,as well as the relationship among them,vary depending on geophysical constraints associated with climate as wellas peat and pool characteristics that influence the C dynamics in the water,emphasizing the importance of pools in peatland ecosystems and in the global C cycle under rapid environmental changes.
