A fraction of dissolved organic matter (DOM) is able to fluoresce. This ability has been used in the present study to investigate the characteristics and distribution of different DOM fractions. A unique global dataset revealed seven different fluorescent fractions of DOM: two humic-like, four amino acid-like and one chemically uncharacterized. The spectral characteristics of the amino acid-like fractions resemble those of pure tryptophan, tyrosine and phenylalanine. The fluorescence intensities of these amino acid-like fractions are highest in the surface waters, rapidly decreasing with depth and at a constant low level in the bathypelagic layer. Such profiles indicate that amino acid-like DOM is linked to surface water production. From calibration curves we estimated the mean surface water concentrations of tryptophan and tyrosine to be 12.5 and $\sim 1 5 . 9 \ \mathrm { n m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively, while mean deep water concentrations are 5.1 and ${ \sim } 6 . 5 \mathrm { n m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ . The fluorescence intensities of the humic-like DOM fractions were low in the surface layer, rapidly increasing in the mesopelagic layer and at constant high level in the bathypelagic layer. The low fluorescence intensities observed in the surface layer indicate the quantitative importance of photochemical degradation as a sink of the humic-like compounds. In the dark ocean (below $2 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ ), significant linear relationships between humic-like DOM fluorescence and microbial activity (apparent oxygen utilization, ${ \mathrm { N O } } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ and $\mathsf { P O } _ { 4 } ^ { 3 - }$ ) were found. These observations imply a link to dark ocean microbial remineralization and indicate that the major source of humic-like compounds is microbial turnover of organic matter. The results of the present study show that the distribution of the humic-like DOM fractions is a balance between supply from continental run off, net microbial production and photochemical removal in surface waters.

# 1. Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is present in all aquatic environments and constitutes one of the largest pools of reduced carbon on Earth's surface (Hedges and Keil, 1995). DOM plays an important role in the global biogeochemical cycles of C, N and P, since it serves as a substrate for heterotrophic microbial growth and degradation of DOM leads to formation of inorganic substrates such as ${ \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ , $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ and $\mathsf { P O } _ { 4 } ^ { 3 - }$ (Francko and Heath, 1982; Bushaw et al., 1996; Carlson, 2002). Despite its importance very little is known about the individual compounds of DOM.

Since the chemical nature of DOM defines its optical properties, DOM fluorescence measurements have been used to identify and trace different DOM fractions. Between 20 and $7 0 \%$ of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) is estimated to be colored (CDOM) (Lanne and

Koole, 1982). A subfraction of this material fluoresces (FDOM) and to date, two major types of DOM fluorescence have been indentified: amino acid-like compounds $\mathrm { ( F D O M _ { A } ) }$ and humic-like compounds $( \mathrm { F D O M _ { H } ) }$ (Coble, 2007). Generally, the fluorescence signal of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ displays broad fluorescence peaks with emission wavelengths typically above $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ whereas $\mathrm { F D O M _ { A } }$ displays narrower peaks with wavelengths typically below $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Coble, 2007).

Tryptophan, tyrosine and phenylalanine are fluorescent amino acids common in living organisms and therefore also found in natural waters. The $\mathrm { F D O M _ { A } }$ observed in seawater fluorescence until now are tryptophan-like and tyrosine-like, i.e. DOM fractions with fluorescence spectra almost similar to those of pure tryptophan and tyrosine (e.g. Coble, 1996, 2007; Nieto-Cid et al., 2005; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003a; Stedmon et al., 2007a). Yamashita and Tanoue (2003a) found a strong correlation between these amino acid-like fluorescence signals of DOM and concentrations of pure tryptophan and tyrosine, and it is therefore believed that tryptophan and tyrosine are responsible for the observed amino acid-like fluorescence. Phenylalanine fluorescence, however, has never been observed, even though this amino acid is generally present in much higher concentration than tryptophan and tyrosine in seawater (Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003a). The vertical distribution of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { A } }$ has been found to be high in the surface layer $( 0 - 2 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ ), decreasing in the mesopelagic layer $( 2 0 0 - 1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ ) and at a constant low level in the bathypelagic layer $\left( > 1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m } \right)$ (Mopper and Schultz, 1993; Determann et al., 1996; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003a). $\mathrm { F D O M _ { A } }$ is therefore associated with biological activity. It is thought to be produced by phytoplankton and bacteria (Determann et al., 1994; 1998; Yamashita and Tanoue, $2 0 0 3 \mathrm { a }$ ; Cammack et al., 2004) and to be bound in proteins in DOM and in bacteria (Determann et al., 1996, 1998; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003a). Studies have further suggested that $\mathrm { F D O M _ { A } }$ is a significant bioavailable substrate for bacteria, but that net consumption only takes place when more labile substrates are depleted (Cammack et al., 2004; Stedmon and Markager, 2005). Different fractions of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { A } }$ are, however, not equally bioavailable, since some fractions are consumed more readily than others (Stedmon and Markager, 2005).

$\mathsf { F D O M } _ { \mathrm { H } }$ is widely distributed in the ocean, and has been used for several decades to trace terrestrial organic matter in the ocean (e.g. Coble, 1996; Walker et al., 2009); to study carbon fluxes associated with ocean currents (Amon et al., 2003); and to investigate the production and removal mechanisms of humic-like DOM (e.g. Chen and Bada, 1992; Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Rochelle-Newall and Fisher, 2002; Shimotori et al., 2009). The vertical distribution of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ has been found to be low in the surface layer and increasing with depth (Chen and Bada, 1992; Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Determann et al., 1996; Yamashita et al., 2007). This depth profile has been explained in part by the high sensitivity of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ to photochemical degradation by UV and blue light (Mopper et al., 1991; Chen and Bada, 1992; Stedmon and Markager, 2005). Additionally, the production of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ is thought to be linked to microbial oxidation and degradation of organic material in the mesopelagic and bathypelagic layers. The fluorescence intensity of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ has been found to correlate with apparent oxygen utilization (AOU), $\mathsf { N O } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ and $\mathsf { P O } _ { 4 } ^ { 3 - }$ , which are all products of microbial remineralization (Chen and Bada, 1992; Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Nieto-Cid et al., 2005, 2006; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2008; Yamashita et al., 2010). $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ has also been shown to increase over time in degradation studies and in some cases to correlate with a buildup of bacterial biomass (Rochelle-Newall et al., 1999; Rochelle-Newall and Fisher, 2002; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Shimotori et al., 2009).

Studies on $\mathrm { F D O M _ { A } }$ and $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ dynamics (distribution, production and removal) have so far been limited to laboratory studies (e.g. Determann et al., 1998; Cammack et al., 2004; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Shimotori et al., 2009) and field observations limited to single wavelength measurements (e.g. Chen and Bada, 1992; Determann et al., 1994; 1996; Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2008) or specific geographical areas (e.g. Nieto-Cid et al., 2005, 2006; Stedmon et al., 2007a; Yamashita et al., 2010). In order to understand the dynamics of the entire FDOM pool on a larger spatial scale, global ocean studies measuring DOM fluorescence at a wide range of excitation and emission wavelengths are still needed. An important tool in such studies is fluorescence Excitation Emission Matrix (EEM) spectroscopy, which has been used over the past decades to trace different fractions of DOM (e.g. Coble, 1996; Stedmon et al., 2003). EEMs are obtained by combining the emission spectra from different excitation wavelengths into a three dimensional structure. When combined with the multivariate data analysis technique Parallel Factor (PARAFAC) Analysis, which can separate the complex fluorescence signal into its underlying components (Stedmon and Bro, 2008), fluorescence EEM spectroscopy offers an important tool in studying the underlying dynamics of different fluorescent organic fractions (e.g. Murphy et al., 2008; Walker et al., 2009; Yamashita et al., 2010).

The overall aim of this study is to characterize DOM fluorescence across a range of oceanic provinces by using fluorescence EEM spectroscopy in combination with PARAFAC. More specifically, the aim is to investigate if current knowledge on FDOM dynamics holds on a global scale using data for FDOM distribution, vertically and geographically, along the cruise track. The third Danish Galathea expedition circumnavigated the world and presented a unique opportunity to collect and analyze a large number of oceanic samples using a uniform protocol and avoiding possible storage artifacts. Our hypothesis is that despite the wide geographic range covered by the cruise, DOM fluorescence can be characterized into a limited number of ubiquitous fractions, including amino acid-like and humic-like DOM, and that the relative abundance of these fractions varies depending on the balance between oceanic production and removal processes.

# 2. Methods

# 2.1. Sampling and measurements

Sampling was carried out as part of the third Danish Galathea Expedition, which ran from August 2006 to April 2007. Water samples were collected from 170 stations along the cruise track (Fig. 1). Typically 7–9 depths were sampled at each station using a Niskin sampler rosette with a Seabird CTD, which measured profiles of temperature, conductivity, oxygen and chlorophyll fluorescence. Apparent oxygen utilization (AOU) was computed from salinity, temperature and oxygen according to Weiss (1970).

Water samples were taken for measurements of inorganic nutrients (nitrate, nitrite, ammonium, phosphate and silicate), chlorophyll a and DOM fluorescence. Nutrient samples were frozen on board and measured on a Scalar auto-analyser after the cruise according to the techniques in Hansen and Koroleff (1999). Chlorophyll a samples were taken in triplicate by filtering between 200 and $5 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of seawater with a GF/F (approximately $0 . 7 5 \mu \mathrm { m } \dot { }$ ) filter. After filtration, filters were stored in $5 \mathrm { m L } 9 6 \%$ ethanol at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } C .$ . Prior to analysis, the samples were extracted for between 6 and $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ in the dark at room temperature and measured on a TD-700 fluorometer (Turner Designs) calibrated with a pure chlorophyll a standard (Arar and Collins, 1997). FDOM samples were filtered through pre-combusted GF/F filters into acid washed amber glass bottles, allowed to acclimate to room temperature and subsequently analyzed in a Varian Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer in the laboratory onboard the ship within $^ { 1 - 2 \mathrm { h } }$ after sampling. Excitation and emission slit widths were set to $5 \mathrm { n m }$ . Emission scans were recorded from 300 to $5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , every $2 \mathrm { n m }$ , with sequentially excitation every $5 \mathrm { n m }$ between 240 and $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The EEMs were spectrally corrected using the instruments correction factors and Raman calibrated according to Lawaetz and Stedmon (2009) using daily spectra of MilliQ water freshly generated on board.

A total of 686 samples for DOM fluorescence were collected, 487 of which were collected in the surface layer $( 0 - 2 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ , 148 in the mesopelagic layer $( 2 0 0 - 1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ ) and 51 in the bathypelagic layer $\left( > 1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m } \right)$ . The EEMs obtained were analyzed by PARAFAC in MATLAB using the DOMFluor Toolbox (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Using split half analysis, seven components were validated. Three samples were removed from the dataset, because several of the components in those samples exhibited exceptionally high fluorescence intensities and therefore biased the PARAFAC characterization.

# 2.2. Correction of AOU in the Atlantic Ocean

Atlantic values of AOU were recalculated to take into account that water originating from the North originally contains $2 8 8 \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ $0 _ { 2 }$ $\mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ , while water from the South contains $2 5 7 \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ ${ { 0 } _ { 2 } }$ $\mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ (Broecker et al., 1991; Sarmiento and Gruber, 2006). Recalculation was done for dark ocean $( > 2 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ AOU values from the North Atlantic, the Equatorial and South Atlantic and the Sargasso Sea (Fig. 1). A water parcel in the Atlantic receives a fraction of water from the North $\left( \mathrm { f _ { n } } \right)$ and a fraction from the South $( 1 - \mathrm { f _ { n } } )$ , and it therefore has an initial oxygen concentration of $\mathrm { f _ { n } }$ times $2 8 8 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ plus $( 1 - \mathrm { f _ { n } } )$ times $2 5 7 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ . A phosphate tracer, $\mathrm { P O } _ { 4 } ^ { * }$ , was used to distinguish between water from the north and water from the south (Broecker et al., 1991). It is defined as:

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Spectral characteristics

The PARAFAC modeling revealed seven different fluorescent fractions of DOM referred to henceforth as components 1 to 7. Their spectral characteristics are shown in Fig. 2 and components 1 to 6 are compared to components identified in earlier studies and pure fluorophores in Fig. 3. Components 1 and 4 are characterized as humic-like DOM, since they display broad emission spectra around and above $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . In addition, they also have relatively broader excitation spectra in comparison to the other components. Components 2, 3, 5 and 7 are characterized as amino acid-like DOM, since they display more narrow emission spectra with maxima below $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . These four spectra are similar to those of the three amino acids: phenylalanine, tryptophan and tyrosine (the spectra of components 5 in particular and to a lesser extent 7, overlap that of tyrosine). It is more difficult to classify component 6 as it appears to have characteristics similar to both groups. It has a broad emission spectrum as seen for humic-like material, but its emission maximum is around $3 5 0 { - } 3 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , which is more like that of tryptophan. The excitation spectrum has two maxima; one below $2 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , which is common for many organic fluorophores, and a second at $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ which is greater than that normally seen for amino acids, which have their secondary excitation maxima between 270 and $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ Hence, component 6 cannot solely be classified as amino acidlike or humic-like DOM.

# 3.2. Vertical distribution

Two distinct vertical distribution patterns for the fluorescence of the components were indentified. The humic-like components typically exhibited low fluorescence in the surface waters $[ 0 -$ $2 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ ) (Fig. 4, C1 and C4). Approximately constant levels were reached from around 1000 and $2 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ for components 1 and 4, respectively. Samples from the surface waters of the North Atlantic and upwelling regions (Equatorial and South Atlantic and Eastern South Pacific) deviated from the aforementioned depth profile, exhibiting high fluorescence intensities $_ { ( > 0 . 0 1 1 }$ R.U.) in the surface layer (C1 and C4 in Fig. 4, orange circles). If surface water data from these three areas are omitted and the data binned into depth intervals, the trend with depth for these two components becomes even clearer (Fig. 5). The data in these depth intervals were tested and the fluorescence intensities were found to significantly increase from the surface to $1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ and $5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ (Tukey HSD, $\mathsf { p } { < } 0 . 0 5$ ) for components 1 and 4, respectively.

The depth profiles of the remaining components also follow a consistent pattern, however, different from that of the humic-like components (Fig. 4, C2, C3 and C5–C7). The depth profiles of components 2, 3, 5, 6 and 7 display rapidly decreasing fluorescence intensities from the high surface water values to approximately constant low levels below $\sim 5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ . The fluorescence intensities of components 2, 6 and 7 decrease significantly from the surface to $5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ , while the fluorescence intensities of components 2, 3, 5, 6 and 7 do not change significantly below $5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ (Tukey HSD, $\mathsf { p } { < } 0 . 0 5$ ). The apparent increase in mean fluorescence intensity of component 6 towards the bottom visible in Fig. 4, is therefore not statistically significant. For a small number of samples, components 3 and 5 display relatively high fluorescence intensities in the dark ocean $( > 2 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ . These samples are primarily from the North Atlantic and Equatorial and South Atlantic. The fluorescence intensities of components 3 and 5 are significantly decreasing from the surface to $5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ (Tukey HSD, $\mathsf { p } { < } 0 . 0 5$ ) if dark ocean data from the North Atlantic and Equatorial and South Atlantic are omitted.

# 3.3. Correlations to physical and biological parameters

In order to further understand the processes driving the observed patterns in DOM fluorescence, data were analyzed for correlations to salinity, inorganic nutrient concentrations, chlorophyll $a$ concentrations (as an indicator of phytoplankton biomass) and AOU. The fluorescence intensities of the humic-like components are positively correlated with AOU in the dark ocean $( > 2 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ (Fig. 6a and b). Only data from the North Atlantic (black circles) deviate from this general trend. With North Atlantic data omitted, the correlations are significant $\scriptstyle ( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ with $\mathrm { r } ^ { 2 }$ values of 0.72 for both components. Swan et al. (2009) have recalculated AOU in the Pacific Ocean by using potential temperature as a tracer instead of $\mathsf { P O } _ { 4 } ^ { 3 - }$ . Using this approach to recalculate our Atlantic AOU values, we find the trends to be similar to those seen in Fig. 6 (the relationships are significant $\scriptstyle ( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ with $\mathsf { R } ^ { 2 }$ values of 0.49 and 0.57 for components 1 and 4, respectively). So irrespective of how AOU values are adjusted to take into account mixing of bottom waters, it is clear that as AOU increases in the meso- and bathypelagic layers, the fluorescence intensities of the humic-like components also increases. Significant, but weak, correlations were also found between humic-like fluorescence and the concentrations of nitrate $\scriptstyle \cdot \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5$ , $\mathrm { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 9$ and 0.14 for components 1 and 4, respectively) and phosphate $\cdot { \mathrm { p } } { < } 0 . 0 5$ , $\mathrm { r } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 2 5$ and 0.21 for components 1 and 4, respectively), although it is unclear to what extent these are due to the correlation between AOU and nutrient concentrations in dark ocean waters. In the North Atlantic significant negative relationships between the fluorescence of components 1 and 4 and salinity exist (Fig. 7).

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Fluorescence characterization

The majorities of the components identified in this study have either been reported earlier or have spectral characteristics very similar to those of pure amino acids (Fig. 3). Two humic-like fluorescent components almost identical to components 1 and 4 have recently been reported by Yamashita et al. (2010). They were observed in oceanic samples from the northwestern Pacific, an area not covered by our study. Fluorescence at the wavelengths covered by these two humic-like components has been known to exist since some of the earliest fluorescence measurements (Kalle, 1949; Traganza, 1969). Initial studies reported vertical distributions similar to our results (Chen and Bada, 1992) and these two regions of fluorescence have also been visually identified from EEMs and found to vary independently across a range of environments (Coble, 1996). Fluorescence in these two regions is often referred to as the C and M humic-like peaks for components 1 and 4 respectively. The results of the present study, supported by the independent study from the northwestern Pacific (Yamashita et al., 2010), for the first time reveal the spectral characteristics of these two humic-like fractions, which represent a ubiquitous oceanic humic-like signal.

Fluorescence in the region often referred to as amino acid-like (emission below $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ) was found to be made up of four different components. Three of these components (2, 3 and 5) have fluorescence characteristics almost identical to those of the three natural fluorescent amino acids; tryptophan, tyrosine and phenylalanine (Fig. 3). Component 7 has spectral characteristics also resembling those of tyrosine. However, its excitation maximum is at slightly higher wavelengths. Tryptophan and tyrosine fluorescence have been reported in earlier PARAFAC models of oceanic DOM (e.g. Murphy et al., 2006, 2008) and DOM fluorescence at these wavelengths is correlated to seawater concentrations of tryptophan and tyrosine (Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003a). We therefore believe that tryptophan and tyrosine are responsible for the fluorescence of components 2 and 5, respectively. The fluorescence of phenylalanine is primarily at wavelengths below $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003a), which is the lower limit measured in the present study, so one can question the validity of the assignment of component 3 since there is no full spectrum available. The fluorescence maximum of component 7 overlaps considerably with the region of water Raman Scatter, which makes the fluorescence measurements much less reliable. Signals at these wavelengths are greatly influenced by artifacts from the blank subtraction, arising primarily from differences in the scattering properties of seawater and MilliQ. However, the vertical profile of component 7 indicates a systematic trend similar to that seen for the other amino acid-like components (Fig. 4), which suggests that this signal is not purely a scattering artifact. We therefore suggest that the fluorescence of component 7 primarily is resulting from tyrosine.

Since the fluorescence characteristics of amino acids are known to be dependent on whether they are freely dissolved or bound in proteins (Lakowicz, 2006), it is not surprising that two different tyrosine-like DOM fractions are observed in the present study. It is quite remarkable, however, that fluorescence signals from tryptophan, tyrosine and possibly phenylalanine are observed in the same study, since phenylalanine fluorescence can be quenched by tyrosine, and tyrosine fluorescence can be quenched by tryptophan if they are bound in the same protein (Lakowicz, 2006). In addition, the results here are quite remarkable in the sense that the tryptophan-like and tyrosine-like spectra are very similar to those of pure free dissolved tryptophan and tyrosine (Fig. 3). The majority of earlier studies have shown components with fluorescence characteristics resembling either a combination of these spectra or amino acid-like spectra shifted to shorter wavelengths (e.g. Determann et al., 1994, 1998; Stedmon and Markager, 2005). This shift has often been assumed to be due to the fact that these amino acids are associated with protein material rather than being free dissolved.

# 4.2. Humic-like DOM fluorescence

The vertical distributions of the humic-like components observed in the present study (Fig. 5) are the result of a balance between production and removal mechanisms. The low fluorescence intensities observed in the surface layer are probably due to photochemical degradation, which is thought to be the major removal mechanism of humic-like DOM (Mopper et al., 1991; Chen and Bada, 1992; Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Nieto-Cid et al., 2005). Microbial degradation is an important sink of organic matter in surface waters, however, it is questionable how bioavailable $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ is. Several studies have shown humic-like fluorescence to either remain unchanged or increase during microbial degradation incubations (Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Shimotori et al., 2009), and microbial degradation is therefore not thought to be a major sink of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ .

The fluorescence intensities of components 1 and 4 are relatively high in the surface waters of three regions: the upwelling regions (Equatorialand South Atlantic (indicated as red circles in Fig. 1) and Eastern South Pacific (pink circles)) and the North Atlantic (black circles). The upwelling regions are very productive in response to nutrient fertilization by deep waters, which have been previously shown to result in a net production of humic-like fluorescence (Nieto-Cid et al., 2005). The combination of intense biological activity in the surface waters and a net upward flux of water with high values of humic-like DOM from deeper layers, make the surface waters of coastal upwelling areas rich in fluorescent humic-like material (Siegel et al., 2002; Nieto-Cid et al., 2005). Additionally, there is a lesser penetration of UV light in these waters compared to the open ocean (Fichot and Miller, 2010), which potentially limits photochemical degradation, adding to the net accumulation of humic-like fluorescence in the upwelling areas.

The North Atlantic also displays high surface water fluorescence intensities. The relatively high productivity of this region may in part maintain these high levels, as is the case for the upwelling regions. However, the North Atlantic is not as productive as coastal upwelling areas (Hansell and Carlson, 1998), and the high surface water fluorescence intensities are therefore not likely only resulting from autochthonous production. We hypothesize that supply of terrestrial organic matter plays a major role, since the North Atlantic receives considerable amounts of terrestrial organic matter both directly from surrounding continents and from the Arctic Ocean (Opsahl and Benner, 1997; Amon et al., 2003; Benner et al., 2005) and since fluorescence similar to component 1 has been found to be correlated to lignin (Walker et al., 2009), a biomarker of terrestrial plant material. It is likely that the high fluorescence signals in the North Atlantic may represent degradation products of terrestrial organic matter, which appear to have the same spectral fluorescence characteristics as the products of oceanic humic-like DOM presumably devoid of terrestrial material. This hypothesis is supported by the fact that the spectral characteristics of components 1 and 4 are very similar to those of terrestrial humic-like DOM fractions found in earlier studies (Stedmon et al., 2007a,b; Walker et al., 2009) (Fig. 3a and d). In addition, a negative linear relationship between fluorescence intensity and salinity exists in the North Atlantic (Fig. 7): the higher the salinity, the lower the fluorescence intensities of components 1 and 4.

The North Atlantic not only differs from the other regions because of its high surface water fluorescence intensities. This region is also displaying relatively high fluorescence intensities in the dark ocean compared to the AOU values of this region (black circles in Fig. 6a and b). This pattern has also been observed in an earlier study by Nelson et al. (2010). As stated for CDOM absorption measurements by Nelson et al. (2010), the high surface water fluorescence intensities are preserved and entrained during North Atlantic Deep Water formation, leading to the relatively high fluorescence signals in the dark ocean. As this sinking surface water has been in contact with the atmosphere quite recently, the water is oxygenated and hence display low AOU values.

The positive linear regression between fluorescence intensity and AOU in the dark ocean (Fig. 6a and b) suggests that net production of humic-like DOM is linked to microbial activity. This trend has been observed earlier (Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2008; Nelson et al., 2010; Yamashita et al., 2010). At shallow depths $( < 4 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ , remineralization of DOC can be responsible for up to $7 0 \%$ of oxygen consumption (Hansell et al., 2009), although typically between 20 and $5 0 \%$ (Doval and Hansell, 2000). The relative importance of sinking organic particles increases with depth and the remineralization of particles is responsible for approximately $9 0 \%$ of the oxygen consumption in the dark ocean (Arístegui et al., 2002). Due to the clear correlations between humic-like DOM fluorescence and AOU observed in the present study, it is therefore likely that most of the humic-like signals identified in the deep ocean are derived from turnover of organic matter supplied by sinking particles. However, it is not possible to tell from Fig. 6 if the humic-like DOM is produced directly by the microbes or if it is produced indirectly. That is, if the microbes directly release humic-like DOM themselves or if they release precursor materials which outside the cell becomes humic. The chemical structures responsible for humic-like fluorescence signatures are thought to be complex and large in size. For example Boehme and Wells (2006) have shown that while amino acid like fluorescence is associated with the $1 { - } 5 \mathrm { k D a }$ fraction, humic fluorescence originates from larger size material $( 1 3 - 1 5 0 \mathrm { k D a } )$ . Bacterial membranes are permeable to molecules in the size range of $0 . 5 \mathrm { - } 1 \mathrm { k D a }$ and we therefore hypothesize that it is unlikely that humic fluorescent material is released directly by cells. We speculate that microbial (heterotrophic and autotrophic) activity in the ocean, including among other processes passive release, viral lysis, grazing, and excretion, produces the precursor materials necessary for these compounds to form extracelluarly by abiotic processes. An example of the occurrence of such a process is the abiotic formation of marine gels (Chin et al., 1998). In support of this, an earlier study has shown that broad humic-like fluorescence signals, overlapping with the spectra of components 1 and 4, can be produced abiotically via the extracellular autoxidative cross linking of marine lipids (Harvey et al., 1984).

# 4.3. Amino acid-like DOM fluorescence

The vertical distributions of the amino acid-like components display the highest fluorescence intensities in the surface waters and decreasing values with depth (Fig. 4), which is the same general oceanic pattern as seen for DOC (Carlson, 2002), dissolved total amino acids (Lee and Bada, 1977) and total hydrolyzable amino acids (Kaiser and Benner, 2009). The fluorescence intensities vary considerably more in the surface layer than in the bathypelagic layer. These results are consistent with earlier findings from the Pacific Ocean and the Atlantic Ocean (Mopper and Schultz, 1993; Determann et al., 1996; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003b) and suggest that the components are labile or semilabile and linked to primary production in the surface layer, despite the fact that no clear positive correlations between amino acid-like fluorescence and chlorophyll a could be found. Only in the Eastern South Pacific did we find a positive correlation, but it is worth noting that the $\mathrm { r } ^ { 2 }$ values are not very high, ranging from 0.06 to 0.51. There are no clear systematic differences between the surface water fluorescence intensities of the different oceanographic provinces covered by the cruise, indicating that these fluorescence signals are mainly produced autochthonously in surface waters. Determann et al. (1994, 1998) have shown that both phytoplankton and bacteria are sources of tryptophan and tyrosine. Similarly, incubation experiments have proved that grazing and excretion by zooplankton can release material with an amino acid-like fluorescence signal (Urban-Rich et al., 2006).

Components 3 and 5 (phenylalanine- and tyrosine-like) display relatively high fluorescence intensities in the mesopelagic and bathypelagic layers of the North Atlantic and Equatorial and South Atlantic (Fig. 4). This pattern was also evident in a previous study but could not be explained (Determann et al., 1996). It may be a result of deepwater formation in the North Atlantic, which causes surface waters that are rich in components 3 and 5 to sink to deeper layers. From here North Atlantic Bottom Water is transported southwards at depths between 1000 and $3 5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ and this may explain the elevated values observed in the Equatorial and South Atlantic at these depths (Fig. 4). No similar increase in fluorescence intensities of components 2 and 7 are observed in the deep waters of these regions. This indicates some fundamental difference in the properties of these components such as their lability, despite the fact that they are all produced in surface waters. Difference in lability of amino acid-like DOM has been observed in a previous study (Stedmon and Markager, 2005), but it is clear that further work is required before any substantial conclusions can be made in this regard.

Yamashita and Tanoue (2003a) observed a linear correlation between the fluorescence intensities of DOM in the regions of tyrosine and tryptophan and their concentrations measured by high performance liquid chromatogtraphy (HPLC). In our study, the concentrations of tryptophan and tyrosine were not measured, however, they can be inferred by second order calibration as the spectra of the components are almost identical to those of standard solutions of tryptophan and tyrosine measured in the laboratory. In this sense PARAFAC can be likened to physical chromatography, as a form of mathematical chromatography where substances are separated as a result of their independent variability within a dataset, rather than physical characteristics (as in LC), and thereafter identified by comparing the UV–visible spectroscopic properties with standards. Calibration curves for tyrosine and tryptophan were measured in the laboratory and used to convert the fluorescence signals into estimates of concentrations (Fig. 8). These concentration estimates seem reasonable when comparing to the concentration of total amino acids, since tryptophan and tyrosine normally only constitute a small fraction of total amino acids (Table 1). Previous measurements of tryptophan and tyrosine concentrations are ranging 0.3–9.5 and 0– $3 9 . 5 \mathrm { n m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively (Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003b; Kaiser and Benner, 2009). These values correspond well to the mean values in Table 1, however, the range in concentrations we found are up to five times higher. A major issue in the transformation of amino acidlike fluorescence into concentration is the fact that amino acid fluorescence spectra and intensities are dependent on the location and chemical form of the amino acids. These estimates should therefore be regarded as a first step towards understanding the variable and diverse DOM protein-like fluorescence and future work combining HPLC and fluorescence PARAFAC analysis is required.

# 4.4. Component 6

This DOM fraction cannot solely be characterized as humic-like or amino acid-like. Its fluorescence spectrum resembles to some extent that of humic-like DOM, since it displays a broad emission spectrum as typically seen for humic-like fluorescence (Fig. 2). However, the emission maximum of component 6 is at $3 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , which is less than the typically $4 0 0 { - } 5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ found for humic compounds (Coble, 2007). The vertical distribution of component 6 resembles that of the amino acid-like DOM fractions (Fig. 4) and, like the amino acid-like fractions, it is not correlated to AOU and chlorophyll a.

In general, the more complex chemical structure a molecule has, the higher emission maximum and the wider emission spectrum it displays. Simple monomeric compounds such as amino acids, and several benzoic acid derivatives have a relatively narrow emission spectrum and small stokes shift (Stedmon, unpublished data). The larger the molecular structure the greater the stokes shift and typically broader the emission spectrum. Compared to the humiclike and the amino acid-like DOM fractions observed in the present study, component 6 has an intermediate fluorescence spectrum. This suggests that component 6 represents organic material, that is more complex than simple monomers, but not as complex as the structures responsible for humic-like fluorescence. Like the amino acid-like DOM fractions, the vertical distribution of component 6 suggests that the formation of this component is linked to surface water productivity (Fig. 4). However, no clear correlation to chlorophyll $a$ is evident. Earlier work has attributed this type of fluorescence to anthropogenic activity, since its fluorescence signature was claimed to be similar to pyrene (Murphy et al., 2008).

# 5. Conclusions

Although some of the trends and findings of this study have been reported earlier from either more local regional studies or using a less specific DOM tracer (such as absorption or in situ fluorescence), this study is unique with respect to the coverage and the fact that samples were measured immediately and are therefore devoid of any possible storage artifacts. It is clear from the present study that several humicand amino acid-like DOM fractions are ubiquitous and exhibit a characteristic vertical distribution in the global ocean, something that has never been shown before. Two humic-like, four amino acid-like and one uncharacterized component were identified.

The vertical distribution of amino acid-like DOM was found to be high in the surface layer, decreasing in the mesopelagic layer and at a constant low level in the bathypelagic layer. Hence, the generally accepted notion on $\mathrm { F D O M _ { A } }$ distribution can be extended to a more global level. The same is true for humic-like DOM, which was found in the present study to be low in the surface layer, rapidly increasing in the mesopelagic layer and at a constant high level in the deeper layers. Only upwelling areas and areas with high input of terrestrial organic matter deviate from this general trend, as they show high surface water values of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ .

Net production of $\mathsf { F D O M } _ { \mathrm { H } }$ is linked to microbial activity in the dark ocean with the major substrate probably being particulate organic matter. We speculate that microbial activity in the ocean, including among other processes passive release, viral lysis, grazing, and excretion, produces the precursor materials necessary for $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ to form extracelluarly by abiotic processes. However, further research into the processes responsible for the formation of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ is clearly needed.

The present study has like other studies proved the applicability of fluorescence EEM spectroscopy in combination with PARAFAC for characterizing DOM dynamics. This approach can be used to further study the sequestration of terrestrial and marine organic matter into the deep ocean and the role of the oceans in the global carbon cycle. The structures responsible for $\mathrm { F D O M _ { A } }$ and $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ remain unknown, and it is clear that the real utility of ocean DOM fluorescence measurements can only be fully realized once we have begun to link these characteristics to chemical composition and bioreactivity.
