# Abstract

Background and aims Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is a vital bioactive component in soil nutrient cycling. However, the dynamics of its characteristics and composition along the soil profle during desertifcation reversal are poorly understood.

Methods Soil samples were gathered from shrub and arbor forests that have been established for 25, 35, 46, and 56  years on semifxed sand of Mu Us Sandy Land. The evolution of DOM composition and chemical properties were explored by ultraviolet-visible and fuorescence spectrophotometry.

Results After 56 revegetation years, DOM increased signifcantly. In arbor lands, DOM was impacted mainly by dissolved organic carbon, with mean concentrations increasing from $5 8 . 8 9 ~ \mathrm { \ m g { \cdot } k g { - } } 1$ to

131.95 mg·kg-1 in $0 { \cdot } 1 0 0 \mathrm { c m }$ soil profle. In the shrubland, changes in dissolved organic nitrogen were more remarkable, with mean concentrations increasing from $3 . 4 3 ~ \mathrm { m g { \cdot k g } ^ { - 1 } }$ to $6 . 8 2 ~ \mathrm { m g { \cdot k g } ^ { - 1 } }$ in $0 { \cdot } 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ soil profle. Concurrently, as soil depth increased, noticeably decreased in the proportion of humic-like components within DOM, while protein-like components displayed an upward trend. The chemical properties of DOM in arbor lands display a higher proportion of hydrophobic substances along with an elevated aromaticity and humifcation degree. Conversely, in shrubland, the DOM composition exhibits a higher proportion of tryptophan components that are more available for microbial metabolism. Correlation analysis showed that DOM content and specifc environmental factors (including soil depth, vegetation type, and aforestation age) profoundly afected soil DOM properties.

Conclusions The DOM in arbor land and surface soil layer were more humifed and relatively stable. Conversely, the DOM in shrubland and deeper soil layers contains more bioactive substances for microbial utilization.

Keywords Dissolved organic matter $\cdot$ Soil profle $\cdot$ EEM-PARAFAC $\cdot$ Vegetation restoration $\cdot$ Mu Us Sandy Land

# Introduction

Desertifcation is a serious environmental issue that reduces the soil nutrient pool and causes loss of soil resources worldwide. Aforestation in desertifed land has been found to control desertifcation efectively (Rong et al. 2022). Since the 1980s, global vegetation coverage has increased, and China has made the most substantial contribution, accounting for $2 5 \%$ of the new green areas between 2000 and 2017, according to National Aeronautics and Space Administration satellite data (Chen et  al. 2019; Piao et  al. 2020). Nonetheless, some researchers have observed that many revegetated areas have begun to experience vegetation degradation after several decades, mainly because most sand-fxing forests have grown in hostile environments (Song et  al. 2016). Therefore, to maintain the ecological functions of sand-fxing forests, and improve sandy soil properties, there is an urgent need for more ecological research on the processes and mechanisms responding to aforestation in the soil.

Vegetation restoration signifcantly infuences the composition and quantity of soil organic matter (SOM) and the soil carbon pool (Cotrufo et al. 2013). The response of dissolved organic matter (DOM) to vegetation restoration has become an increasingly important area of research due to its role as the biologically active and mobile component of SOM (Gmach et  al. 2019; Kalbitz et  al. 2003). The DOM pool consists of a complex mixture of organic compounds, primarily dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and a minor fraction of dissolved organic nitrogen (DON). DON plays a pivotal role in soil organic nitrogen mineralization and inorganic nitrogen fxation (Blesh 2019; Braun et al. 2018). It regulates the supply of soil ammonium nitrogen while governing the biotransformation of nitrogen and is an available source of soil nutrients. A correlation exists between alterations in the relative abundance of DOC, DON, and changes in DOM properties (Verstraeten et  al. 2016). However, previous research commonly neglecting the impact of DON on DOM in diferent soil fractions closely linked to plant-soil-microbe interactions (Chen et al. 2020). Therefore, the simultaneous study of the composition and content of DOC and DON at diferent depths and soil DOM properties is necessary.

Investigating the vertical distribution and variation of DOM in soils across diferent land use types is paramount in comprehending the dynamics of soil ecosystems. Recent studies have examined the DOM dynamics in various soil ecosystems, revealing a signifcant decrease in DOM concentrations in the order of forestland $>$ grassland $>$ cropland $>$ sand land (Cerdán et  al. 2016; Liang et  al. 2023; Zhang et  al. 2022). Factors such as aboveground vegetation composition and the quantity of litter reintegrated into the soil infuence the vertical variations of DOM. In the forest and grassland soils, the hydrophilic compounds are predominant in the surface soil DOM, while the subsoil predominantly contains highly sorptive, hydrophobic compounds (Scott and Rothstein 2014). Furthermore, Li et  al. (2019) found that the relative abundance of tryptophan-like components increased with soil depth, while the proportion of humic-like compounds decreased in diferent land-use patterns on the Loess Plateau. Diferences in DOM content between plant and microbial sources have been explored in the soil profle, which may determine the DOM properties at various depths (Li et  al. 2022a, b, c, d; Ye et al. 2020). Microbial processing is vital in transforming soil DOM by degrading and synthesizing microbial metabolites (Kaiser and Kalbitz 2012). A recent investigation established that microorganisms display a predilection for decomposing and transforming the low-molecular-weight fraction derived from plant substrates into larger molecules of microbial origin (Roth et al. 2019). Plant litter that is difcult for microbes to degrade may retain more compounds of plant origin, promoting C sequestration by mineralization processes (Cotrufo et al. 2013). Therefore, understanding the complexity of DOM characteristics in soil profles can provide insights into how aforestation afects diferent soil depths and DOM properties.

Over the last few decades, China has developed several restoration initiatives for the revegetation of Mu Us Sandy land. As a result of the combined eforts at the national and local levels, the desertifcation degree in this area has signifcantly reduced over the last 20 years (Wang et al. 2022). The revegetation of the Mu Us Sandy Land has substantially reduced the degree of soil erosion and increased SOM, DOC, and DON content in the soil (Li et  al. 2021; Zhang et  al. 2022). However, the efects of vegetation restoration on the vertical distribution of DOM and soil characteristics in this region require further understanding. Ultraviolet (UV)-visible spectroscopy and fuorescence spectrophotometry are highly sensitive analytical techniques instrumental in unraveling the complex nature of DOM in various soil ecosystems (e.g., agricultural, forest, grassland, and wetland environments) (Belenguer-Manzanedo et al. 2023; Boukra et  al. 2023). Additionally, fuorescence properties have proven efective in diferentiating DOM into biologically active, protein-like fractions and more recalcitrant, humifed fractions (Cuss and Guéguen 2015; Stedmon and Bro 2008). This study aims to examine the evolving characteristics of DOM in soil profles during long-term vegetation restoration in the Mu Us Sandy Land, with a particular focus on soil DOM leachate solutions. We hypothesized that the composition and structure of soil DOM at varying depths are closely associated with vegetation conditions and determine the and DOM fuorescence characteristics in soil profles. The primary goals of this research are (i) to quantify the variation of soil DOM (including DOC and DON) in soil profles under various revegetation types during long-term vegetation restoration; (ii) to elucidate the chemical properties and DOM sources under discrete revegetation types in diferent soil layers; and (iii) to understand the relationship between DOM concentration, specifc environmental factors (including soil depth, vegetation type, and aforestation age), and the DOM chemistry properties at the study sites.

# Materials and methods

Study site

![](images/28fd40405d79dfa0a4e35774470bdcf164e25c2ff69d38f5dea743102dce7bd2.jpg)  
Fig. 1 The study area (Mu Us Sandy Land) and the location of the experimental station

This study was conducted at the Hongshixia Desert Aforestation Experimental Station $( 3 8 ^ { \circ } 2 0 ^ { \prime }$ N, $1 0 9 ^ { \circ } 4 4 ^ { \prime }$ E), located in the northwestern inner land zone of China, approximately $8 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ north of Yulin city, Shaanxi province (Fig.  1). The climate in the research area is temperate semi-arid continental monsoon, with an average annual temperature of $7 . 9 - 1 1 . 3 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . During summer, the average temperature ranges between 22 and $2 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , whereas it plummets from $- 1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $- 3 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in winter (Wang et  al. 2021). This region has predominant southeast winds in summer and northwest winds in winter. The average annual wind speed ranges from 2.9 to $3 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m \cdot s ^ { - 1 } }$ , and 20 to 40 days of exceptionally high winds often occur in the winter (Fu et  al. 2019). Annual precipitation averages $4 0 9 . 7 \ \mathrm { m m }$ , concentrated mainly in summer. Based on the World Reference Base for Soil Resources, the soil in this region falls under the “Arenosols” category before revegetation, which is characterized by having weak soil horizon development and susceptible to wind erosion because of its low organic matter and poor nutrient content (Schad 2023).

The Hongshixia Desert Aforestation Experimental Station was established in 1958 to reverse ecological degradation and counter desertifcation. Initially, windbreaks were established, followed by the installation of checkerboard barriers on the surface of shifting sand. During the following years, natural pioneer species infltrated the area and transformed the shifting sand into SF (semifxed) sand. To further enhance sand fxation, xerophytic shrubs (Salix psammophila, Psammochloa villosa (Trin.) Bor, Artemisia ordosica), or arbors seedlings (Pinus sylvestris) were planted within the checkerboard barriers (Zhang et al. 2022). After consistent sand control techniques for years, vegetation coverage in the sandy land increased, and harm caused by wind-sand was reduced (Sun et  al. 2021). The dominant vegetation species include Hedysarum fruticosum and Caragana korshinskii in shrubland and Pinus sylvestris in arbor lands, resulting from the natural succession.

Experimental design and sample collection

This study adopted the spatial sequence method to assess the infuence of diferent aforestation types and ages on restoration. Utilizing increment-borer methods and local aforestation survey, we selected two types of restoration plots (including shrub and arbor plots which originally converted from SF sand and have been restored for 25, 35, 46, and 56 years) within the study area. The SF sand, which has not experienced reforestation and is classifed as a 0-yearold site, was designated as the control group. Therefore, the selected stands were under comparable site conditions, exhibiting relatively consistent soil and topographic characteristics prior to revegetation (more detailed information on the sample plots is presented in Table S1).

In July 2020, we selected three sample sites for each afforestation type, with a minimum of $5 0 \textrm { m }$ and a maximum of $5 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ separating any two sites.

One plot $( 2 0 ~ \mathrm { m } \times 2 0 ~ \mathrm { m } )$ was randomly set up for each sample site used for vegetation investigations and soil sampling. At each plot, a soil auger was employed to retrieve 12 soil samples distributed along an $\mathbf { \bar { s } } \mathbf { \bar { s } }$ shape and all soils in one plot from the same depth (0-10, 10-20, 20-40, 40-60, 60-80, and $8 0 - 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ ) were mixed to obtain composite samples respectively. Three $1 \ \mathrm { ~ m ~ } \times 1 \ \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ subplots were randomly chosen within each plot to measure above-ground litter biomass. Before analysis, all roots, stones, and plant litter were carefully removed and discarded. Half of the soil samples were preserved with their original moisture content and kept at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until DOM extraction; the remaining soil samples were air-dried at room temperature and sieved through a $0 . 2 5 \mathrm { \ m m }$ sieve to determine SOC and the total nitrogen (TN) content.

# Water-soluble DOM extraction and sample analysis

The DOM solution was prepared from unsifted soil samples to avoid potential interference in the results caused by drying and sieving (Akagi et  al. 2007; Christ and David 1996). Soil DOM was extracted using the water-leaching method. Briefy, $1 0 \mathrm { ~ g ~ }$ of soil was mixed with $5 0 \ \mathrm { m l }$ of deionized water in centrifuge tubes. The mixture was shaken at room temperature $( 2 0 - 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ and $1 8 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ for 2  hours. Subsequently, the suspension was centrifuged at $8 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for 6 minutes and fltered using a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ nitrocellulose membrane. The fltered DOM solution was stored at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until further analysis.

The SOC and plant litter carbon content was determined using the $\mathrm { K } _ { 2 } \mathrm { C r } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 7 } \mathrm { - H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S O } _ { 4 }$ oxidation wet combustion method (Nelson 1996), and the TN content was determined using the Kjeldahl method (CleverChem Anna, Dechem Technologies, Germany). The concentrations of extracted DOC and total dissolved nitrogen (TDN) extracted from soil samples were determined using the method of combustion catalytic oxidation (TOC-L CPN, Shimadzu, Japan). Furthermore, inorganic nitrogen levels $\mathrm { ( N H ^ { 4 + } { - } N }$ and $\mathrm { N O } ^ { 3 - } { \displaystyle - \mathrm { N } ) }$ were measured using a continuous-fow automated analyzer (Analytical Auto Analyzer AA3, Bran Luebbe, Germany). The concentration of DON was calculated as the diference between TDN and inorganic nitrogen.

Spectroscopic characterization

To minimize the potential interference caused by the ‘inner flter efect,’ the samples were diluted to ensure UV-vis absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ was below 0.3 (Korak et  al. 2014). Following dilution, the samples were evaluated within a $1 0 \mathrm { m m }$ quartz cuvette for their UVvis absorbance and fuorescence properties. The UVvis absorbance measurements were conducted using a UV spectrometer (UV-1780, Shimadzu, Japan) with a scanning range of 250 to $4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and a $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ interval. Milli-Q water was employed as a blank reference at the same time. Fluorescence landscapes were obtained using a fuorescence spectrophotometer (RF6000, Shimadzu, Japan) with excitation wavelengths ranging from 240 to $4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and emission wavelengths extending from 250 to $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . The scanning intervals were set at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ for excitation and $2 \ \mathrm { n m }$ for emission, with a $1 2 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m \ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ scanning speed. In this way, an excitation-emission matrix (EEM) can be established. Before analysis, an EEM was collected from the Milli-Q water baseline to remove Raman scatter efects, and setting the fuorescence intensity values of triangle-shaped sections where the emission wavelengths are more than the excitation wavelengths to zero values in this region eliminated Rayleigh scatter efects (Ohno and Bro 2006).

DOMFluor v.1.7 toolbox in MATLAB 7.0 (MathWorks, Natick, MA, USA) was employed to perform parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis. This analysis incorporated established techniques, including residual analysis, split-half analysis, and random initialization (Stedmon and Bro 2008). The N-way toolbox was employed for data normalization, ensuring the preservation of quantitative information within the model scores. The relative abundance of each component in the DOMFluor results was determined by identifying the component with the highest fuorescence intensity. All the PARAFAC components demonstrated a high degree of congruence with previously published PARAFAC models contained within the OpenFluor database (https://openf​l uor.lablicate. com/) (Murphy et al. 2013; Murphy et al. 2014).

Data analysis

SPSS version 23.0 (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA) was employed for statistical analyses. Two-way ANOVA tests were performed to examine the efects of revegetation type (shrub or arbor) and stand age (25, 35, 46, or 56 years) on plant litter characteristics. The signifcance of the diferences was determined using the least-signifcant diference (LSD) tests test $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ . Additionally, three-way ANOVA analysis $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ was employed to assess the combined infuences of revegetation type, aforestation age, and soil depth on SOM content, DOM concentration, and the proportions of fuorescence components. Before analysis, make sure all data met the normal distribution (Shapiro-Wilk test, $p { > } 0 . 0 5 $ ) and homogeneity of the variances (Levene test, $p { > } 0 . 0 5 )$ . Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed on SPSS version 23.0 using a matrix of correlation coefcients. The CANOCO v.5.0 software package was used to execute the redundancy analysis (RDA). Microsoft Excel 2016 (Microsoft, Cascadia, WA, USA) and Origin 2020 (OriginLab, Northampton, MA, USA) were used for data management and visualization.

# Results

Dynamic variations of underground litter properties, SOM contents, and DOM contents

Shrub aforestation showed signifcantly diferent aboveground litter characteristics than arbor aforestation for diferent aforestation periods. As expected, arbor lands had signifcantly higher amounts of litter C and litter $\mathbf { N }$ than shrubland. The litter C: N ratio across the various aforestation types also varied substantially, with arbor litter showing a higher average ratio and shrub litter displaying a lower ratio (Table 1, $p { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ . The vertical distribution of SOC and TN in shrubland and arbor lands under diferent aforestation durations is depicted in Fig.  2. The SOC and TN content increased with increasing aforestation age while decreasing with soil depth. After 56 years of revegetation, arbor lands accumulated more SOC compared to shrublands, increasing by 8.23, 5.25, 4.09, 3.96, 3.70, and 3.53 times in arbor lands compared to 4.79, 2.72, 2.51, 2.36, 2.20 and 2.29 times in shrublands, respectively in 0-10, 10-20, 20-40, 40-60, 60-80, and $8 0 { - } 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ soil depths compared to SF sand (Fig. 2b and c). Conversely, after 56 years of revegetation on SF sand, shrubland accumulated more TN than arbor lands, which increased 2.55, 1.11, 1.02, 0.89, 0.87, and 0.76 times in shrublands, compared to 2.31, 0.40, 0.37, 0.33, 0.30 and 0.27 times in arbor lands (Fig. 2e and f).

These components consisted of two protein-like components (C2 and C3) and a humic acid-like component (C1) (Fig.  S1 and Table  S5). # Discussion

Efects of revegetation on SOM and DOM content on Mu Us Sandy Land

The content of SOC and TN is primarily infuenced by factors such as surface litter input, underground root system decomposition, and mineralization processes (Liu et  al. 2020; Wu et  al. 2020). The vegetation restoration process increases SOC and TN content due to the improved quantity and quality of surface vegetation, attributed to the enhanced accumulation of litter and roots facilitated by vegetation restoration eforts (He et  al. 2009; Li et  al. 2018,  2021). Consistent with previous studies, the SOC and TN content declined along the soil profle; this trend is concentrated in the surface soil (Deng and Shangguan 2016; Wang et al. 2004). Additionally, the investigation of SOC and TN indicates that arbor lands exhibit a signifcantly higher C: N ratio than shrubland, refecting the variations in litter C: N ratio, arguing that plant litter signifcantly afect SOC and TN in the soil profle.

Interestingly, according to our studies, aforestation with shrubs and arbors on sandy soils could signifcantly increase the concentration of DOM, with a higher increasing rate observed on arbor lands. Previous studies have shown that factors such as litter decomposition, root distribution, sorption, and biodegradation play signifcant roles in infuencing the concentration of DOM in soils (Liu et al. 2023). Over the course of years of revegetation, the increased presence of plant leachates (Miao et al. 2015), microbial degradation products (Gmach et al. 2019; Li et al. 2022d), and root exudates (Uselman et  al. 2012), resulted in a gradual enrichment of DOM content in the soil. In contrast to shrubland, arbor aforestation results in deadfall with higher lignin content (Kiikkilä et al. 2013), diminished rates of microbial decomposition (Li et al. 2022d; Zhang et al. 2018), deeper and more extensive root systems (Rasse et al. 2005), and a greater volume of root secretions released (Bi et al. 2022; Li et  al. 2022c) during revegetation. These combined factors collectively promote the accumulation of DOM within the soil profle of arbor lands. In the process of revegetation, a consistent trend across various vegetation types is decreased DOM content with increasing soil depth (Kalbitz 2001; Wang et al. 2015). This decline in DOM content with soil depth can be attributed to the sources of DOM release from litter and roots decrease, as well as processes such as adsorption by soil constituents (which can be mainly infuenced by soil mineral composition, DOM characteristics, clay adsorption, and organic matter content in our research) and biodegradation that restrict the vertical movement of DOM through the soil profle (Angst et al. 2018; Avneri-Katz et al. 2017; Xi et al. 2018).

During the aforestation period from 25 to 56  years, the main factor infuencing DOM dynamics in arbor lands was DOC, while in shrubland, DON played a more prominent role. Fresh organic materials (e.g., plant residues, roots, and exudates) releasing various compounds, including organic acids, sugars, phenols, and amino acids, are recognized as crucial sources of DOM and signifcantly afect the rate of decomposition and the composition of the resulting DOM. Litter decomposition and root exudates release various organic compounds and promote microbial growth, which determines the increasing rate of DOC and DON (Chen et  al. 2021; Cotrufo et  al. 2013; Smreczak and Ukalska-Jaruga 2021). Moore (Moore et  al. 2006, 2011) reported that arbor litter has a higher tendency to release DOC compared to shrub litter, resulting in a higher DOC: DON ratio. On the other hand, shrub litter, characterized by a lower C: N ratio, contributes more DON, leading to a decrease in DOC: DON ratio. The variations in litter composition play a crucial role in shaping the relative proportions of DOM in the soil ecosystem. Therefore, a comprehensive analysis of both DOC and DON is essential for understanding the DOM dynamics during the revegetation process.

# Efects of revegetation on DOM quality and DOM chemistry properties on Mu Us Sandy Land

Based on PARAFAC modeling, this study identifed three DOM fuorescent components. Litter decomposition and root exudates are primary sources of DOM at the soil surface layer, while a series of microbial processes (e.g., organic matter decomposition, microbial respiration, and DOM compounds transformation) play a leading role in the subsoil, which elevates the proportion of protein-like substances, such as amino acids and peptides (Antony et al. 2017; Kramer et al. 2013; Lorenz and Lal 2005). Our investigation also highlights the impact of organic inputs originating from plant sources on the molecular attributes of DOM across the soil profle. Compared to shrubland, aforestation with arbor species on sandy soil led to an increased C1 component of DOM, while the C2 fraction decreased. The C: N ratio of soil microbial products typically falls within the range of 4-10 (Myrold 1999). Consequently, the microbial turnover of organic matter results in a DOM fraction with a lower C: N ratio compared to plant-derived DOM, while an increase in the DOC: DON ratio might suggest that the additional carbon contributed by litter is not fully balanced by additional nitrogen, or that the added carbon is in forms that are difcult for microbes to decompose (like aromatic compounds) (Hagedorn et al. 2012). It seems that the humic substances with hydrophobic acid fractions was closely associated with these aromatic and condensed compounds, further suggesting that the difcult-to-decompose plant litter fractions could signifcantly infuence DOM composition (Chen et al. 2023; Sun et al. 2021; Zhang et al. 2022).

Vegetation restoration processes profoundly impact the accumulation of organic material in the soil profle, primarily through the changes they induce in aboveground plant litter and root exudates. Studies conducted on sandy soils demonstrate that revegetation increases molecular weight, aromaticity, hydrophobicity, and DOM humifcation degree. Conversely, these characteristics decrease as soil depth increases (Romero et al. 2017). Changes in the composition of plant-derived organic materials during succession drive notable variations in FI, HIX, and BIX, with more pronounced efects observed in the topsoil compared to the subsoil (Ho and Chambers 2019; Li et  al. 2022b). Klotzbuecher et  al. (2013) reported that aforestation could increase the complexity of DOM molecules due to the substantial increase in litter and humus, which promotes the generation of lignin-derived aromatic compounds, humic substances, and condensed materials within DOM. The root characteristics of Pinus sylvestris (arbors) and shrubs, including their structure, density, and volume, substantially afect the composition of DOM. Specifcally, the deep-rooted nature of arbors might make a more substantial contribution to the DOM pool in deeper soil layers compared to shrubs.

Additionally, arbors with their denser and more expansive root system, tend to enhance the humifcation of soil DOM. Studies suggest that the faster root growth and shorter life cycle typically associated with shrubs lead to an increased production of root exudates (Guyonnet et al. 2018; Wen et al. 2022). These exudates likely promote a rich and dynamic microbial community within the soil (Yang et  al. 2023). The resultant microbial activity could expedite litter decomposition, subsequently infuencing the FI and BIX values (Suseela and Tharayil 2018). Thus, the distinct root attributes of arbors and shrubs contribute uniquely to the formation, stability, and bioactivity of DOM.

The analysis of FI, BIX, and HIX revealed that BIX was elevated and HIX was reduced in shrubland, demonstrating that microbial-derived DOM contributes more to shrubland. At the same time, DOM with more signifcant plant characteristics was observed in arbor land. According to the result above, we assume that arbor lands tend to increase DOM humifcation degree during aboveground vegetation succession while decreasing microbial decomposition than shrubland (Kiikkilä et  al. 2013; Zhang et  al. 2022). Marschner and Kalbitz (2003) observed that high contents of organic acids and proteins generally enhance DOM biodegradability; conversely, the existence of recalcitrant aromatic and hydrophobic structures can inhibit enzyme activity and thus reduce DOM biodegradability. Throughout the 56-year aforestation period, changes in FI and BIX suggest a steady rise in the share of DOM originating from microbial metabolic sources under shrubland. This shift resulted in the production of more autochthonous source products in the soil profle. Meanwhile, the prominence of plant characteristics in DOM within arbor forests progressively amplifed, coinciding with an increase in the proportion of humic substances in the DOM (Han et al. 2023). Shrub litter, characterized by lower lignin and cellulose content, a decreased C: N ratio, and a higher proportion of free amino acids with simpler molecular structures compared to Pinus sylvestris litter, proved to be more susceptible to microbial degradation (Gupta and Singh 1981; Lai et al. 2016; Liang and Zhu 2021). These characteristics lead to a notable outcome: during vegetation succession, the shrub aforestation soil profles are characterized by a relatively lower C: N ratio, lower molecular weight compounds, and reduced aromaticity content (Hodgkins et  al. 2014; Jílková et  al. 2020), increasing its availability to microorganisms (Moorhead and Sinsabaugh 2006).

In summary, the DOM of arbor restoration has higher molecular complexity, whereas the DOM of shrub restoration has more free amino acid fraction with a simple molecular structure and is more conducive to microbial decomposition (Don and Kalbitz 2005; Kalbitz et  al. 2006). At the same time, DOM can also infuence soil nutrient availability and indirectly afect the development of aboveground vegetation through various mechanisms (e.g., transport and fxation, mineralization and sequestration, absorption and desorption) (Gunina and Kuzyakov 2015). Furthermore, the C1 content linked with humiclike substances demonstrated a lower abundance in the shrubland relative to the arbor lands. Previous research has demonstrated that DOC and DON are prone to accumulate in separate fractions of DOM, which are hydrophobic and hydrophilic, respectively (Petrone et al. 2008). Therefore, compared to shrubland, in arbor lands, the DOM is relatively hydrophobic, complex, difcult to degrade by microorganisms, and limits the release of soil nutrients, making the rate of input of litter much higher than the rate of degradation by microorganisms (Franklin et al. 2020; Liu et  al. 2020). The PCA and RDA plots indicated a strong correlation between soil depth, which signifcantly infuences DOM turnover and the spectral characteristics of DOM. Changes in soil chemical properties associated with soil depth have a more signifcant impact on DOM characteristics than vegetation type and restoration age.

# Conclusions

During the 56 years of revegetation on semifxed sand with arbor and shrub species, increasing organic carbon, nitrogen, and DOM (including DOC and DON) content in the $0 { \cdot } 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ soil profle. However, dissimilar evolutions of DOM composition and chemistry were observed following revegetation. For plant species, the DOM increase in arbor forest soil was mainly from DOC and tended to be highly humifed and stable, while its increase in shrubland soil was more dependent on DON and became relatively more readily available for microbial utilization. In the soil profle of both forest lands, the DOM in the surface soil layer accumulated higher contents of hydrophobic, humic-like materials and tended to aromaticity and degree of humifcation, which was benefcial for carbon sequestration. The DOM in the deeper layer contained more tryptophan-like components, which were more conducive to microbial use and transformation of nutrients. They were closely related in that vegetation sources (encompassing contributions from aboveground biomass, root exudates, and other plant-derived materials) dominated soil DOM in the surface layer, while microbial sources dominated the deeper layer of both forest lands. Obviously, the interaction between vegetation types and soil depth greatly afected the distribution and evolution of DOM composition and chemical properties during revegetation on desertifed land.
