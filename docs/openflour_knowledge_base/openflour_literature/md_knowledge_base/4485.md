# Dissolved organic matter spectroscopy reveals a hot spot of organic matter changes at the river–reservoir boundary

Rafael Marcé $^ { 1 , 2 } \textcircled { \vert \overrightarrow { \vert } }$ · Laia Verdura1,2 · Nick Leung1,2,3

Received: 23 March 2021 / Accepted: 27 July 2021

# Abstract

Reservoirs show contrasting chemical and biological spatial gradients due to the strong efect of the infowing rivers on the whole ecosystem. However, the fate of dissolved organic matter (DOM) input from rivers along the reservoir water body is still unclear. Here, we characterized the composition of DOM in the Ter River–Sau Reservoir system (Spain), located in a watershed under anthropogenic pressures (urban wastewater efuents, intensive farming, agriculture). Water samples were collected in the river several kilometers upstream of the reservoir and during its travel throughout the water body until the dam. Absorbance and fuorescence spectroscopy with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was used to characterize DOM. Based on three-dimensional excitation-emission matrices (EEMs), PARAFAC produced a six-component model, most of them related to human-derived DOM and showing signifcant spatial trends. A component showing major infuence on the overall spectroscopy signal was associated with wastewater efuents and signifcantly correlated with environmental variables usually linked to DOM lability. Components trends showed a major discontinuity in the vicinity of the river plunge point (river–reservoir boundary), where the composition of DOM showed noticeable changes. Upstream from the plunge point, components related to human activity dominated the spectroscopy signal, while components related to autochthonous planktonic activity appeared downstream of the plunge point. This coincided with strong heterotrophic activity as revealed by oxygen undersaturation and nutrient concentration changes. Fluorescence indices also supported a strong signature of human-derived DOM in the system and pointed to the plunge point as a likely hot spot for DOM processing.

# Introduction

The relative high water load in reservoirs defnes these systems as important sites for organic carbon (OC) processing at local (Ulseth and Hall 2015) and global scales (Tranvik et al. 2018). Once in the reservoir, OC may be merely exported downstream, but can also undergo several biogeochemical transformations, including incorporation into organisms and subsequent sedimentation or export downstream, redox transformations, or re-mineralization and outgassing as carbon-containing gases. Unraveling the dynamics of these processes is necessary to understand how these systems contribute to regional carbon budgets and their role in the qualitative transformations of the organic pool as it is transported by rivers to the sea.

Ecologists have long recognized that reservoir ecosystems are open systems, best refected in the infuence of river infows on reservoir dynamics (Fischer et al. 1979). Manmade mainstream reservoirs show a relatively high drainage basin area to water body area ratio (Hayes et al. 2017), implying a strong advective fux from the river tributaries even during the low-fow summer conditions. This infow promotes chemical and biological gradients associated with advection (Šimek et al. 2011), and the recurrent presence of the river water as a distinctive layer in the reservoir (Vidal et al. 2012). Indeed, microbial communities in reservoirs have been a main subject of research in the feld of reservoir limnology during the last decades (Pradeep Ram et al. 2019;

Qin et al. 2021), and their role in the processing of allochthonous organic matter is occasionally invoked (Comerma et al. 2003). In fact, the recurrent observation that microbial biomass and activity accumulates in the riverine section of reservoirs suggests that this section should be relevant in terms of allochthonous organic matter processing. However, most research on microbial communities in reservoirs has focused on gradients associated with advection (Šimek et al. 2011; Huang et al. 2016), and the role of the autochthonous organic matter on the microbial and carbon dynamics have been rarely directly tested (but see Weissenberger et al. 2010).

An alternative approach to the role of allochthonous organic matter in the carbon processing and other limnological features of reservoirs has come from empirical load-response modeling (Marcé et al. 2008b). There we argued that the oxygen content of a particular reservoir, a variable closely related to carbon processing and the metabolic balance of the system, strongly depends on the dissolved organic carbon (DOC) content of the tributaries. The empirical approach supported the efect of allochthonous DOC content on hypolimnetic anoxia extent in a small set of reservoirs, but all proxies of autochthonous production remained non-signifcant even in highly eutrophic reservoirs showing high nutrient loading and chlorophyll content. This efect was particularly strong in reservoirs under anthropogenic pressures. However, the statistical relationship between DOC in tributaries and reservoir anoxia was system specifc, suggesting that qualitative proprieties of DOC may explain the varying role of allochthonous DOC on the oxygen content in rapidly fushed systems like reservoirs. Supporting this reasoning, most empirical equations worked as well (or even better) with chloride concentration, a compound with no capacity to consume oxygen but closely related to the C/N ratio of the river organic matter (Marcé et al. 2008b). Although the C/N ratio is not a direct measure of bioavailability, it is frequently related to labile organic carbon in soils and rivers (Hunt et al. 2000), suggesting that chloride can also be related to lability in watersheds under anthropogenic pressures.

Although the use of high-resolution mass spectrometry is the gold standard for studies on the composition of DOM, fuorescence spectroscopy is still used due to its relatively high sensitivity to certain features of the dissolved pool like source and biological reactivity (Coble et al. 2014). This technique is also rapid, precise, and relatively inexpensive, which allows for extensive sampling programs necessary to trace DOM dynamics in aquatic ecosystems. Computation of fuorescence indices and analysis of excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) are among the most common procedures to extract information from DOM fuorescence data (Fellman et al. 2010). The use of parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis of EEMs as an alternative to manual peak-picking is a customary approach (Murphy et al. 2013), and a great amount of information on particular components and their relationship with broad DOM categories and moieties is now available (e.g., Murphy et al. 2014).

In this study, we characterized sources and changes of DOM spectroscopic signals in a river–reservoir system located in a highly anthropized watershed, sampling DOM in the river several kilometers before entering the reservoir and during its travel throughout the lentic ambient until reaching the dam. Our main aim was to evaluate the hypothesis that the diferent fractions of DOM entering a humanimpacted reservoir will be diferentially processed along the river–reservoir system. This may include decaying gradients of allochthonous DOM probably overlapped with increasing patterns of autochthonous DOM. An accompanying expectation was the existence of a relationship between humanderived DOM and chloride concentration, a variable previously related to oxygen consumption in human-impacted reservoirs, but with no capacity to consume oxygen. To achieve these objectives, we characterized DOM using fuorescence spectroscopy EEMs analyzed using diverse indices and PARAFAC analysis.

# Materials and methods

# Study site

The Sau Reservoir and its tributary the Ter River (Spain, Fig. 1) were chosen because (1) it is a system located in a highly anthropized watershed, receiving nutrient and organic carbon enriched waters from several waste water treatment plants (WWTP’s) located just upstream from the reservoir (Fig. 1), and also impacted by non-point source pollution coming from extensive felds fertilized with slurries (Marcé et al. 2008a); (2) it is a canyon-shaped reservoir, implying that one single tributary is responsible for most part of the total infow, which facilitates the establishment of chemical and biological gradients along the reservoir; (3) the river water has repeatedly been observed as a density current in the reservoir, usually as an intermediate fow (Vidal et al. 2012); and fnally (4) it is a well studied system from several points of view, including bacterial communities and activity (Gasol et al. 2002; Šimek et al. 2011), carbon budgets (López et al. 2011), and oxygen dynamics (Marcé et al. 2010). The Sau Reservoir is a recreational and water supply system for Barcelona metropolitan area (ca. 3 million people), with a maximum capacity of $1 6 5 \mathrm { h m } ^ { 3 }$ and median infow of $7 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } ~ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ , although these fgures show huge seasonal variation. Main land covers in the watershed are woodland $( 7 8 \% )$ in headwaters, and non-irrigated agricultural land $( 1 6 \% )$ and large urban areas $( \sim 1 0 0 , 0 0 0$ inhabitants) in the vicinity of the reservoir. Industrial and farming activity is also important (ca 1 million pigs) upstream the reservoir, and manure application on the felds is a common practice. Extended limnological descriptions and detailed processes in the reservoir are found in references above and those therein.

As a river enters a reservoir it pushes ambient lake water ahead of itself until buoyancy forces arrest the fow. At this point (i.e., the plunge point), if the river water is denser than reservoir water (as it was in our case) the stream will plunge beneath the surface, fowing downward along the bottom as a gravity-driven density current until it reaches the level of neutral buoyancy (Rueda et al. 2007). Therefore, the plunge point can be considered as the boundary between the river and reservoir ecosystems (Assireu et al, 2011). In the Sau Reservoir the plunge point was located between Stations 23 and 24 during the sampling for this study (see below). However, the location of the plunge point at the Sau Reservoir changes with water temperature, streamfow, and reservoir water level variations, and it has been observed as downstream as Station 44 (Šimek et al. 2011).

# Experimental approach and sampling

We characterized spatial changes in the composition of DOM in the Ter River–Sau Reservoir system tracking the fate of DOM in the river several kilometers before entering the reservoir and during its travel throughout the reservoir until reaching the dam. Considering that one of the objectives of the study was to assess the efect of river plunging in the processing of DOM carried by the infow, we performed the study during autumn, because the river water frequently enters as an interfow with an insertion depth very close to half the maximum depth of the reservoir during this period (Marcé et al. 2008b). In this way, we minimized the probability of intrusions of surface water or deep sediment materials in the river interfow, which would have been at maximum during overfow or underfow situations. The study was carried out from October $1 3 \mathrm { t h }$ to 14th 2009, to sample the entire system as close as possible to an ideal stationary state. For the same reason, we took special care to start sampling after a period of stable fow for at least two entire weeks $( 3 . 4 8 \pm 0 . 8 0 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ . The last storm recorded 97 days before sampling only produced a small infow event reaching $3 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ (strong infow events frequently exceed $2 0 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ in this reservoir). Therefore, the assumption that the river–reservoir system was close to a steady state for such a short sampling period seemed reasonable.

We defned 62 sampling stations along the main axis of the river–reservoir system every $4 0 0 \ \mathrm { m }$ , which were visited starting upstream. Stations were visited by boat where possible (stations 23 to 62, Fig. 1), and by foot in those sections too shallow to navigate without disturbing the bottom sediments (1 to 22, Fig. 1). Due to the difcult access to some reaches of the river and to the fact that we did not use an anchor in the reservoir, stations were seldom separated by exactly $4 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ . We recorded the exact position of sampling stations (Fig. 1) with a global positioning system (GPS). Additionally, we sampled four sites with substantial anthropogenic infuence in the DOM pool which discharged either in the Ter River upstream from the reservoir or directly in the Sau Reservoir (Fig. 1, full triangles, from now on referred to as “Efuents and impaired streams”): the efuent of the WWTP at Manlleu (discharged in the Ter River); the Gurri River just before joining the Ter River, which collects the largest efuent in the area corresponding to the WWTP at Vic; the efuent of the WWTP at Roda de Ter, which discharges in the Ter River; and the Riera de Sant Martí, a small tributary of the reservoir that collects several industrial point sources. Finally, we also sampled a ffth site, Riera Major, a small tributary of the reservoir mostly draining a pristine forested catchment, although impacted by a small WWTP $\sim 2 0 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ upstream the confuence with the reservoir.

The sampling protocol was diferent upstream and downstream from the plunge point. Upstream from the plunge point (Stations 1 to 23) and in the efuents and streams we collected a single grab water sample for DOM spectroscopy and DOC analysis, and single values for water temperature, conductivity, and dissolved oxygen using calibrated feld probes. Downstream from the plunge point (Stations 23 to 62), we collected water from the river along its way through the reservoir, which implied several measures to locate the river water below the surface (see below) and the use of a 5-L hydrographic bottle (UWITEC). Additionally, we collected a surface water sample every two sampling stations for DOM characterization and DOC analysis, and profled temperature, conductivity, dissolved oxygen, and chlorophyll-a with a SeaBird 19plus SEACAT CTD Profler equipped with a Turner Designs’ Cyclops 7 fuorometer calibrated against extracts of freshwater chlorophyll-a. The amount of light at the surface layer (0 to $\sim 5 ~ \mathrm { m }$ ) and at the level of the river water was assessed by means of a Li-COR spherical Quantum Sensor LI-193SA. Finally, we collected additional water samples from both surface and the intruded river in stations 2, 7, 16, 20, 24, 31, 37, 43, 51, 59, and 62 to perform a more complete chemical characterization (see below).

We used the conductivity profles and an Acoustic Doppler Current Profiler (ADCP, $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k H z }$ Workhorse Rio Grande, Teledyne RD Instruments) to track the insertion depth of the river water along the reservoir. The instrument was mounted facing downwards on a floating platform attached to one side of the boat and was set to acquire data every 1 s using a bin size of $0 . 5 \mathrm { m }$ . Data acquisition started immediately after arriving at each sampling point as indicated by the GPS and lasted for $\sim 3 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ . Data were visualized with the manufacturer's software with bottom tracking to remove the ship’s velocity from the measured velocity. We determined the insertion depth of the river water paying special attention to conductivity profles obtained with the CTD and the back-scatter information collected by the ADCP, which is the strength of the refected signal from particles in suspension. River water carries inorganic particles and is easily detected as a strong back-scattering layer, usually coinciding with a conductivity peak. We preferred working with the back-scatter signal instead of velocity because the sampling time to obtain reliable velocity estimates would have been prohibitively long in the lacustrine section of the reservoir where the velocity of the intruded river water is minimal. We later confrmed that the back-scatter signal and the velocity profles gave the same information at least in the riverine section of the reservoir, where river water velocity was high enough to detect consistent vertical velocity patterns during $\sim 3 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ . Conductivity profles were also used to roughly estimate the amount of mixing between river and reservoir water in the plunge point and along the reservoir, assuming a simple linear mixing model between two end-members: the conductivity of the river water before the plunge point (Station 23, $5 3 0 \mu \mathrm { S } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ), and the conductivity of the surface water in Station 62 $( 3 9 5 \mu \mathrm { S } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 } .$ ).

# DOM characterization

DOC concentration was quantified by combustion in a Shimadzu TOC-5000 Analyzer after fltration through a precombusted GF/F flter (nominal pore size $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ and acidifcation with HCl to $\mathrm { p H } < 2$ to remove inorganic carbon. DOM fuorescence spectroscopy and UV–visible absorbance spectra were also measured on GF/F fltered samples, that were stored in the dark at $4 \mathrm { { ^ \circ C } }$ in acid-washed glass vials until analysis, within 2–8 weeks of sampling.

UV–visible absorbance spectra were measured between 200 and $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ (Shimadzu UV1800 spectrophotometer). The specifc UV absorbance (SUVA) was calculated as the absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m } ( \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ divided by the DOC concentration $( \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ and was intended to represent a surrogate for DOM aromaticity (Weishaar et al. 2003). We did not consider iron absorption efects on SUVA fgures since iron concentration in the Sau Reservoir and the Ter River were always below $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ during 2009 (RM, unpublished data from a monthly monitoring program).

Fluorescence EEMs were built using a Hitachi F-7000 fuorescence spectrophotometer with spectra corrected using Rhodamine B solution (Wako Pure Chemical Industries, Ltd.) and the light difuser and flter set provided by the manufacturer. The instrument was set to $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ bandwidths for excitation and emission, and scans were made over a range of $2 0 0 { - } 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at 3-nm increments, at $1 2 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ and $7 0 0 \mathrm { V } .$ . EEMs were corrected for inner flter efects with the corresponding absorbance spectra (Kothawala et al. 2013, maximum absorbance at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m } = 0 . 0 5 4 { \cdot }$ ) and converted to Raman Units (RU) using the area under the Milli-Q Raman scatter peak at excitation $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . The Raman scatter was removed subtracting an EEM for Milli-Q water, while

Rayleigh scatter efects were removed using a 3D interpolation approach (Murphy et al. 2010).

Several indices were calculated from discrete regions of the corrected EEMs. The humifcation index (HIX) was calculated from excitation at $2 5 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ as the ratio of the peak area under emissions $4 3 4 { \ - } 4 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 6 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (Zsolnay et al. 1999), and it is supposed to be proportional to the humic content of DOM, with values above 10 for fulvic acid extracts (Williams et al. 2010). The fuorescence index (FI), calculated from excitation at $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ as the ratio of emission intensities at $4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ (McKnight et al. 2001), is inversely related to the lignin content of DOM. Finally, the $\beta { : } \alpha$ ratio was calculated at excitation $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ from emission intensity at $3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ divided by the emission intensity maximum observed between 420 and $4 3 5 \mathrm { n m }$ (Parlanti et al. 2000; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009). The $\beta { : } \alpha$ ratio is used to assess the relative contribution of recently produced DOM to highly degraded DOM (Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009).

EEM data for the 105 samples were reduced into discrete components through PARAFAC analysis (Murphy et al. 2013) using MATLAB routines (Mathworks, Inc.). We validated the model using the split-half and random initialization methods. Prior to PARAFAC analysis, EEM wavelength ranges were reduced to $2 2 7 { - } 4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , to avoid the region between 200 and $2 2 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , where high absorbance was detected due to the presence of high concentrations of nitrates. We performed the analysis removing samples corresponding to WWTP efuents, Gurri River, and Riera de Sant Martí. These samples showed very strong DOM signatures and DOC concentration, badly infuencing the diagnosis statistics of PARAFAC performance even after substantial numerical dilution, and efects on some ftted components were conspicuous as well. However, we used some of the results of a model solved including the outliers just to highlight the most probable origin of some components. Results coming from this poorly ftted model were used only scarcely and have been clearly identifed in tables and text. The results of the PARAFAC model were queried (Tucker’s congruence coefcient $= 9 5 \%$ ) to search for quantitative matches with previously validated PARAFAC using the OpenFluor database (https://openf​l uor.lablicate. $\mathrm { c o m } /$ ; accessed March 2021), to compare the match of our components with previous PARAFAC models in the literature (Murphy et al. 2014).

# Chemical analyses

Samples collected for chemical characterization were fltered through precombusted GF/F flters and analyzed for nitrate and chloride by liquid chromatography in a Konik KNK 500-A, and for soluble reactive phosphorus (SRP) (Murphy and Riley 1962) and ammonium (Solórzano 1969) by a colorimetric method. Particulate organic carbon (POC)

and particulate inorganic carbon (PIC) were determined by elemental analysis in a Carlo Erba EA 1108 CHNS-O, using particulate material collected in precombusted GF/F flters with and without subsequent exposure to HCl fumes. DOC:DON elemental ratios were calculated after estimating the dissolved organic nitrogen (DON) concentration by subtracting the dissolved inorganic fractions to the total dissolved nitrogen concentration measured by alkaline persulfate digestion of a GF/F fltered sample. DOC:DON ratio is recognized as an important driver of DOM bioavailability, and an inverse relationship between lability and DOC:DON should be found (Fellman et al. 2008).

# Statistical analyses

Associations between variables corresponding to DOM spectroscopy analyses (components and indices) and water chemistry variables were assessed using Pearson correlation. All $p$ values were adjusted using the Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons. SUVA and DOC were not considered independent variables and therefore correlations were not calculated. Comparisons of group means among diferent environments (Ter River, Plunged Ter River, Reservoir surface layer, Riera Major, and Efuents and impaired streams) were assessed using one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). Trends along the Ter River–Sau Reservoir System were assessed using Kendall’s thau.

# Results

# Fate of the river water in the reservoir

Back-scatter signal and conductivity profles clearly showed that the Ter River behaved as a density current (interfow) during the sampling period (Fig. 2). From these data, we located the plunge point between stations 23 and 24, also suggested by conspicuous changes in the amount of suspended solids at the surface, and the accumulation of foating debris in the fow convergence typical of the plunge point. Visual inspection during sampling suggested that river fow started slowing down well before arriving at the plunge point around station 19, which roughly coincides with the limits of the reservoir when it is at full capacity (Fig. 1). The river water fowed near the bottom from stations 24 to 40. At that point, the density current separated the bottom and inserted into the water body at $\sim 1 8 \mathrm { m }$ depth. This should be regarded as the average situation since plunge point location and insertion depth are dynamic features that show variability even at sub-daily scale (Vidal et al. 2012). However, the shape of the conductivity profles and the strong backscatter signal suggested that most river water was confned in a discernible layer, especially at the infow section of the reservoir. The mixing model solved with conductivity also suggested little mixing just after the plunge point $\sim 1 4 \%$ of water at the bottom layer in Station 24 would be reservoir water), followed by a section of almost no further mixing between river and reservoir water until station 33. From there on, the river jet gradually entrained reservoir water at an almost constant rate of $\sim 6 \%$ per kilometer. At the dam, the river layer included as much as $\sim 8 0 \%$ of water from the reservoir.

# DOM characterization

FI and HIX indices showed variability along the river–reservoir transect and point sources (Fig. 3 and Table 1). In contrast, SUVA and $\beta { : } \alpha$ ratio showed little variability (Fig. 3) except for the WWTP efuents and impaired streams sampled (Table 1). SUVA mean values measured in the diferent environments sampled (Ter River, plunged river, surface water in the reservoir, Riera Major, and efuents and impaired streams) showed no signifcant diferences and hardly deviated from $\sim 2 . 5$ (Table 1). These values are in the lower end of fgures previously reported in other systems where variability is observed (Jafé et al. 2008; Han et al. 2021), and suggested low aromaticity $( \sim 2 0 \%$ , Weishaar et al. 2003). The $\beta { : } \alpha$ ratio was homogeneous in the reservoir and in the Ter River (Fig. 3) with values around 0.76 and showed slightly lower values in Riera Major, efuents, and efuents and impaired streams (Table 1). In general terms, these values are near the top of the range found in other studies (Parlanti et al. 2000; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009; Sepp et al. 2019; Kadjeski et al. 2020), suggesting that microbialderived carbon should play a major role in the DOM pool. The sampling coming from the most pristine system in our sampling design (Riera Major) showed the lowest $\beta { : } \alpha$ ratio (Table 1).

We found lowest FI values in Riera Major and highest in the efuents and impaired streams (Table 1), defning a range that covers most situations found in cross-sectional studies in freshwaters (Jafé et al. 2008; Wilson and Xenopoulos 2009; Sepp et al. 2019; Kadjeski et al. 2020). FI increased downstream the major efuent point source (Station 14, Fig. 1), and peaked after the last efuent input upstream the reservoir (Station 18, Fig. 1). From this peak to just downstream from the plunge point (Station 25, Fig. 1) FI progressively dropped to values around 1.3. FI remained around 1.3 until the dam, and diferences with surface water were non-signifcant (Table 1). In contrast to FI, HIX did not increase downstream the major efuent point source but increased downstream the plunge point (Table 1 and Fig. 3). In spite of mixing, HIX values were lower in the surface layer as we approached the dam (Table 1 and Fig. 3). HIX was highest in Riera Major and highly variable in the efuents and impaired streams (Table 1), although in the stream collecting the main efuent (Gurri River) was 3.07. In general, the values reported for HIX (always far below 10) suggested a minor role of humic substances on the DOM pool.

The PARAFAC analysis validated a six-component model (C1–C6, Fig. 4). C5 and C6 were identifed as protein-like with fuorescence signatures almost identical to tyrosine and tryptophan, respectively (Table 2). C5 may be related to the activity of organisms, and fuorescence signatures like C6 are usually found in WWTP efuents and associated with recent biological activity (Murphy et al. 2011; Derrien et al. 2020). C1 and C2 are humic-like components well described in the literature (Table 2), and they have been related with WWTP efuents and land uses receiving substantial nutrient and organic matter inputs. C3 is a microbial humic-like component relatively common to a wide range of freshwater environments, and C4 was identifed as a UVA humic-like component frequently found in lentic freshwater.

The sum of the fuorescence maximum score for each component (Total $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ , in RU) showed a signifcant relationship with DOC concentration $r ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 2 6$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ , $n = 8 5$ ; Fig. 5A). Total $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ peaked at Station 20 while DOC reached maximum values at Station 25. Neither DOC nor Total $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ showed signifcant diferences between plunged river and surface water (Table 1 and Fig. 5A), efuents and impaired streams showed the highest values for both variables, and Riera Major the lowest ones as was expected (Table 1). The relative weight for each component in the fuorescence signal was expressed as the $\%$ $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ relative to Total $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ , and it showed major changes along the transect (Fig. 5B). Whereas C1 and C2 did not show any signifcant trend or peak, the other components showed conspicuous spatial patterns. C3 showed a minor but signifcant increasing trend along the transect (Kendall’s $t = 0 . 3 1$ , $\scriptstyle { p = 0 . 0 0 0 4 }$ , $n = 6 2$ ). C4 before Station 19 was the lowest and suddenly increased when the river entered the zone of infuence of the plunge point (Stations 19–26), to remain stable thereafter. $\% F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ for C5 undergone a signifcant decreasing trend (Kendall’s $t = - 0 . 6 9$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 0 0 1$ , $n { = } 6 2$ ), and evident peaks and minima mirroring those of C6, at least in Stations 1 to 26 $\scriptstyle \gamma ^ { 2 } = 0 . 6 6$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 0 0 1$ , $n = 2 6$ ) but no from Stations 27 to 62 $\scriptstyle { r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 2 }$ , $\scriptstyle p = 0 . 0 4 1$ , $n = 3 6$ ). Finally, C6 signifcantly decreased along the reservoir (Kendall’s $t = - 0 . 4 5$ , $\scriptstyle { p = 0 . 0 0 0 3 }$ , $n { = } 6 2$ ), overlaid with an increase after the confuence with Gurri River, and a decrease when the river entered the zone of infuence of the plunge point. Concentration of PARAFAC components did not show diferences between surface water and plunged river water in the reservoir and, as expected, components’ $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ were lowest at Riera Major and highest at the efuents and impaired streams (Table 1).

# Discussion

# The river–reservoir boundary as a hot spot for DOM changes

Our main hypothesis that the diferent fractions of DOM are diferentially processed along the river–reservoir system was supported by the distinct behavior showed by PARAFAC components, FI, and HIX. We found that humic-like components related to human land uses (C1 and C2) were hardly variable along the system, suggesting that they include mainly non-labile compounds, at least at our study-site and during the time scale of our sampling. But the rest of PARAFAC components showed conspicuous spatial patterns, particularly C6 and C4 between stations 17 and 24. The region before and after the plunge point (from Station 19 to 25) seemed to be a hot spot for DOM changes and, presumably, organic matter processing. This may be promoted by both the input of efuents rich in organic matter and nutrients and the sudden increase of residence time (Šimek et al. 2011), which can make planktonic heterotrophic communities bloom. Unfortunately, we did not measure bacterial biomass or production during the experiment, but the recurrent observation of high bacterial biomass and activity in the upstream section of this reservoir supports this interpretation (Šimek et al. 2011). This is also supported by other observations such as the variability of PARAFAC components, the strong peak of ammonium and DOC:DON ratio after the plunge point (maximum value of 15.22), the presence of oxygen undersaturation in both in surface and river water, and the rapid decrease of nitrate and SRP concentration after the last point source between stations 17 and 18. This evidence points towards a rapid uptake/use of microbially derived labile DOM in the vicinity of the plunge point.

C6 is associated with WWTP efuents, and it sharply decreased when C4 (associated with bacterial planktonic activity) increased between stations 14 and 24 $\scriptstyle { r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 4 6 }$ , $p { = } 0 . 0 2$ , $n = 1 1$ ). This would be coherent with C6-related carbon being the source for bacterial planktonic production which would release the DOM identifed as C4 in our analysis. However, this interpretation should be regarded with caution, and not only because PARAFAC components are not entirely quantitative in terms of carbon. DOC erratically peaked and POC increased between stations 17 and 23, suggesting that POC should also be considered as an important direct or indirect source for bacterial growth in this section of the reservoir. This argues against a simplistic interpretation of C6 and C4 as a source-product couple. Also, the erratic behavior of DOC may be the consequence of the overlap of diferent pools including hardly labile substances that dominate the spectroscopy signal, and highly dynamic autochthonous DOM that may cycle very fast (del Giorgio and Pace 2008), decisively contributing to the carbon dynamics of the system but leaving no statistical discernible fngerprint in the fuorescence patterns.

# Spectroscopy indices and DOM

SUVA values $( \sim 2 . 5 )$ and the $\beta { : } \alpha$ ratio $( \sim 0 . 8 )$ strongly suggested that DOM pool in the Ter River–Sau Reservoir system is mainly constituted by recently produced, low aromatic organic compounds. In fact, these two variables showed limited variability along the system, probably because recalcitrant DOM rich in aromatic rings was never high enough to compensate for the strong anthropogenic signal. DOC:DON values found in forested headwater streams on this area can be as high $\mathrm { a s } \sim 4 0$ (Vazquez et al. 2011; Butturini 2021), while we found maximum values around 15. This also suggests that the DOM pool is not dominated by materials coming from terrestrial ecosystems, or at least not from undisturbed forested catchments. However, this interpretation is at odds with FI values around 1.3 in the lacustrine section of the reservoir, because values around 1.3 are frequently associated with terrestrial derived DOM (McKnight et al. 2001; Sepp et al. 2019). When variability in FI is related to the relative strength of the terrestrial derived DOM signal, a strong association with SUVA and the DOC:DON elemental ratio is usually found (Fellman et al. 2008; Yates et al. 2019), which was not the case in our study (Table 3). In fact, low FI values were closely related to C4 increases (Table 3), and it seems unreasonable to relate the raise of this component to a sudden increase of terrestrial sources at the plunge point. Nevertheless, FI was sensitive to the qualitative changes of the DOM pool as represented by the variability of the concentration of PARAFAC components, and FI peaked at Station 17 roughly following C6. Finally, HIX increased near the dam and was sensitive to C5 variability (Table 3), probably refecting the consumption of allochthonous (from the reservoir perspective) tyrosine-containing materials. We did not consider photodegradation as a major process generating the observed spatial patterns in PARAFAC components and indices. In spite of the fact that light reaching the plunged Ter River inside the Sau Reservoir was negligible while the surface layer received plenty of solar radiation, we did not fnd any signifcant diference between the value of PARAFAC components and indices in these two layers (except for HIX, Table 1). This was particularly relevant for SUVA and FI, which are supposed to decrease under UV light efects.

# Chloride concentration as a proxy for labile DOM

One of our expectations was to fnd a relationship between human-derived DOM and river chloride concentration, a variable previously related to the oxygen consumption in au Reservoir, but with no capacity to consume oxygen (Marcé et al. 2008b, 2010). Our reasoning was that river chloride concentration co-varied with some labile, human-derived DOM fraction in databases used to build the empirical relationships included in the aforementioned works. Indeed, chloride concentration did correlate with C6, a PARAFAC component related to human efuents. This result supports the use of river chloride concentration as a proxy for allochthonous labile DOC inputs in empirical models predicting anoxia in this reservoir. This is not at odds with the fact that reservoirs usually have short water residence times, because only materials that decay in a time scale equal or shorter than that of the water retention time will be relevant for carbon and oxygen cycling. However, it is unlikely that the association between chloride concentration and labile DOM would hold in watersheds devoid of WWTP efuents where fresh DOM is of autochthonous biological origin and is still unclear if this relationship exists in other human-impacted watersheds because the available evidence is limited (Marcé et al. 2008a, b).

# Global signifcance of river–reservoir boundary hot spots

Our results suggest that hot spots for DOM changes may be abundant in river networks receiving nutrient and organicrich efuents just upstream from dams and weirs. The role of wetlands and inland waters on the continental carbon cycle has been frequently stressed (Abril and Borges 2019), and this study points towards the possibility of a myriad of additional carbon processing hot spots related to small and large dams in human-impacted basins. It has been shown that even relatively small but numerous engineered structures built by beavers may profoundly impact the carbon budget of a river network (Wohl 2013), but we do not have a convenient framework to anticipate which are the driving factors (residence time, nutrient and carbon levels, temperature, hydrodynamics, and possibly others) that will promote the existence of hot spots for DOM changes along the fuvial network. The behavior of the river water entering reservoirs may be an important factor in the time domain since it deeply afects the development and functioning of the reservoir bacterial and planktonic communities (Šimek et al. 2011; Rizo et al. 2020).

Our results also suggest that researchers must be cautious when planning sampling networks devoted to the analysis of carbon dynamics along human-impacted watersheds. Although from a pure mass balance perspective missing carbon processing hot spots is not necessarily critical, a sampling program collecting data in rivers and the most lacustrine section of reservoirs and weirs may promote biased conclusions concerning the functioning of the system. This is especially true if some processes playing a role in the mass balance are arithmetically derived (e.g., sedimentation and burial), while others are measured at the lacustrine section (e.g., carbon emissions). If the actual whole system fuxes are enhanced by boundary hot spots, the measured fuxes in the lacustrine section will constitute a biased fgure, compromising the subsequent mass balance calculations and further interpretations.

This study highlights the role of the boundary of the river–reservoir system on DOM changes, especially from a qualitative point of view, and poses the question of the role of these hot spots in the export and processing of organic carbon through river networks receiving WWTP efuents. We also showed that spectroscopy indices should be interpreted with caution in systems impacted by humanderived DOM, and that chloride concentration might be a convenient proxy of labile DOM from WWTPs.
