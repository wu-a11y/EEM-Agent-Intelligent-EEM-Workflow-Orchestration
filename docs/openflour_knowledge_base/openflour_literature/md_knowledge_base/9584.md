Both organic and inorganic constituents play a major role in the formation and speciation of disinfection byproducts (DBPs) under chlorination.In this study,the removal of organic and inorganic precursors to DBPs by ion exchange and coagulation was examined,and the relationships between characteristics of dissolved organic matter(DOM),chlorine reactivity,and the formation of DBPs in high-bromide natural water were investigated. Formation potential tests were conducted for trihalomethanes (THMs)and haloacetronitriles (HANs) before and after the diferent treatments (coagulation, magnetic ion exchange (MIEX) resin,and combined MIEX/coagulation).Atalow resin dose,MIEX was more efective than coagulation for DOMremoval.The advantages of the MIEX resin were more prominent at a high resin dose because substantial amounts of DOM and bromide were simultaneously removed.The maximum bromide uptake on the MIEX resin was $0 . 0 2 \ : \mathrm { m g - B r / m L }$ . A 4-component PARAFAC model was developed using a dataset of 148 fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) generated from pre-chlorinated and post-chlorinated samples.The maximum fluorescence intensities of humiclike components were found to be strongly correlated to THMs formation $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 9 - 0 . 8 9 )$ . The MIEX treatment substantially lowered the formation of THMs $( 1 9 - 5 1 \%$ and HANs $( 7 0 - 9 5 \% )$ due to its ability to remove humiclike DOMand bromide.The fluorescenceloss for humic-like DOM was found to increase over a chlorination time of 3 days,suggesting that these compounds had stronger reactivity to chlorine compared to protein-like DOM. The chlorine reactivity of protein-likeDOM increased after treatmentsas the residual chlorine was able to react with the less reactive fractions of DOM after chlorine-reactive compounds were removed.

# 1．Introduction

Disinfection is an important process in water treatment plants to inactivate pathogens that are responsible for waterborne diseases. Chlorination is the most common disinfection process due to its effectiveness,low cost,and ability to leave a residual disinfectant in distribution systems.However,chlorine can also react with organic and inorganic constituents in water,leading to the formation of disinfection by-products (DBPs） such as trihalomethanes (THMs） and haloacetic acids (HAAs).These by-products are carcinogenic and can cause health problems to downstream users,and they can also negatively affect the environment [1].Natural organic matter (NOM) is known to be an important precursor of organic DBPs [2]. During chlorination processes, bromide is one of the halogens that can be substituted in NOM to

complicate the formation of DBPs.

When bromide is present in source water,it can be oxidized to hypobromous acid (HOBr) by hypochlorous acid (HOCl).HOBr shows stronger halogenating activity than HOCl and promotes the formation of brominated DBPs (Br-DBPs) [3,4]. Compared to their chlorinated analogues,Br-DBPs are more genotoxic and carcinogenic [5].The effect of bromide concentration on THMs formation during chlorination has been previously evaluated [2,3,6].Increasing the ratios of bromide to dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and bromide to chlorine favors the formation of Br-THMs [7].In raw water,the formation of Br-DBPs is a concern at bromide concentrations higher than $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m g / L }$ [8].In the USA,the maximum combined contaminant levels of four THMs and five HAAs are $8 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ and $6 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ ，respectively [9].In Thailand,water authorities have adopted the less stringent WHO guidelines,and the regulated limits for chloroform (TCM), bromodichloromethane (BDCM), dibromochloromethane (DBCM),and bromoform (TBM) are 300,60, 100,and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ ,respectively.

To comply with the water quality standards regarding DBPs concentration,water utilities typically remove DBPs precursors and/or optimize their disinfection processes (e.g., by adjusting the pH or using alternative and combined disinfectants).Enhanced coagulation is the most commonly employed method for the removal of dissolved organic matter (DOM),and it can effectively remove DOMwith high molecular weight and negative charge [7].However,bromide is not effectively removed by conventional coagulation and sand filtration,and a shift in speciation toward the formation of Br-DBPs after coagulation has been previously reported [1O]．The chlorination of low-molecular-weight DOM fractions,which are recalcitrant to coagulation,results in a higher concentration of Br-DBPs due to the greater bromide to DOC ratio [11].Thus,to enable water utilities to treat raw water sources containing high levels of both organic matter and bromide,the control of DBPs formation is essential,and a treatment process that can simultaneously remove both organic and inorganic precursors is desirable.

Magnetic ion exchange (MIEX) resin has been employed by water utilities as a pre-treatment step prior to coagulation-flocculation processes to enhance precursor removal.MIEX resin is a strong base-anion exchange resin with unique features,such as a smaller bead size and a magnetic core,that provide excellent ion exchange capacity as well as rapid separation and regeneration properties [l2,l3]. The ability of MIEX resin to uptake both organic and inorganic anions means that it can adsorb both DOC and bromide,which reduces DBPs formation under chlorination.Previous studies [1O,14-17] have focused on the application of MIEX resins alone or in combination with coagulation or activated carbon adsorption for precursors removal and the subsequent control of DBPs formation.However,only a few studies [18-21] have investigated the changes in DOM characteristics and chlorine reactivity under the chlorine and bromine substitution reactions related to short-term and long-term DBPs formation before and after MIEX treatment.

DOM is a complex mixture of aromatic and aliphatic structures,and a sub-fraction of DOM can fluoresce when excited by UV and blue light. The chemical composition, functional groups, concentration,and source of DOM affect its fluorescence spectra.Fluorescence excitation-emission matrix (EEM) spectroscopy can be used to distinguish different fractions of DOM.Using this spectroscopic technique,a series of emissions scans are taken overa range of excitation wavelengths [22,23].Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) is a multivariate data analysis technique that has been applied to deconvolute fluorescence EEM spectra into underlying components of the same luminescence properties.Previous studies [20, 24-26] have reported that EEM-PARAFAC approaches can be used to characterize DOM and relate DOM characteristics to DBPs formation. However,no studies have been reported that investigate changes in DOM characteristics related to DBPs formation during MIEX/coagulation treatment and chlorination.In particular,changes in the fluorescence properties of DOM could potentially be an indicator of chlorine reactivity and a surrogate parameter forDBPs formation.Therefore,this phenomenon should be explored to help water utilities control problematic Br-DBPs formation.

This study was conducted to evaluate the removal of organic and inorganic precursors to DBPs using either coagulation or MIEX resin treatment alone compared to MIEX pre-treatment prior to coagulation. The Chi River,a water source for a water utility in Khon Kaen,Thailand, was selected as a sampling site because previous studies [27,28] have reported issues with high brominated DBPs in the finished water from this river.The characteristics of DOM that contributed to the formation of DBPs were studied using an EEM-PARAFAC approach and high-pressure size exclusion chromatography (HPSEC).Moreover, the relationship between the extracted PARAFAC components,chlorine reactivity,and formation of DBPs in the water treated with chlorination at different contact times was examined.

# 2．Materials and methods

# 2.1.Raw water and chemical reagents

Raw water samples were collected in February 2o22 from an intake of the water treatment plant(WTP) in Tapra,Khon Kaen,Thailand, which uses the Chi River as a source.Tapra WTP was selected as a sampling site because previous studies have indicated that the concentration of Br-THMs in this water is the most problematic during this time of the year [27,28].Grab samples were collected in pre-cleaned HDPE bottles.The samples were refrigerated at $4 ~ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark to minimize changes to the DOM characteristics.All water samples were allowed to reach room temperature before coagulation and resin contact experiments.MIEX GOLD resin from IXOM (Australia) is a strong base anion exchange resin with quaternary ammonium functional groups. The resin has an exchange capacity of $0 . 5 2 \pm 0 . 0 2 \mathrm { m e q / m L }$ [29] and an average pore diameter of $2 . 3 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ [20].The macroporous structure of the resin is presented in Fig. S1 in The Supporting Information. The resin was stored in $5 \%$ sodium chloride (NaCl) solution to maintain its exchange capacity and was rinsed with DI water multiple times before use. Sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl,Loba Chemie Pvt.,Ltd.) was used to prepare a chlorine dosing solution.All the reagents used in this study were of analytical grade,with the exception of polyaluminum chloride (PACl),which was of commercial grade.

# 2.2. MIEX and coagulation experiments

The raw water was initially treated with various MIEX resin doses and times.To determine the optimum contact time,MIEX resin contact experiments were conducted using $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ L ~ } }$ of raw water and $5 \mathrm { m L }$ of resin mixed ina jar test apparatusata speed of $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ .Treatedwater samples were collected for $5 { \mathrm { - } } 9 0 { \mathrm { m i n } }$ .To performan isotherm study,1L of rawwaterwas treated with $1 { - } 1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ ofMIEX resin with the optimum contact time.After $1 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ of settling,the supernatant was filtered through a $0 . 7 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ carbon-free glass fiber filter(GF/F,Whatman） to remove suspended solids and resin fines that might interfere with DOM characterization.The optimum resin dose and contact time were determined at the point where DOC removal did not significantly increase with increasing contact time or increasing resin dose.After the optimum resin dose and contact time were determined,a large volume of the raw water was treated with MIEX resin under these optimum treatment conditions.

Preliminary coagulation experiments were performed using raw water and a higher dosage of PACl $( 1 0 { - } 6 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L } )$ to determine the optimum coagulant for obtaining the greatest DOC removal without MIEX pretreatment as a control.The coagulation procedure entailed rapid mixing at $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ for $1 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ ,slow mixing at $3 5 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ for $2 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ ，and settling for $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ [1].For the MIEX pre-treated water,subsequent coagulation experiments with lower dosages of PACl $( 5 { - } 3 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L } )$ were conducted to evaluate the impact of MIEX pre-treatment on coagulant demand and DOC removal.All MIEX resin and coagulation experiments were conducted in duplicate at the native $\mathsf { p H }$ of the raw water. The treated supernatant from each experiment was collected and the suspended solids were removed using a $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ carbon-free glass fiber filter before chlorination and DOM characterization.All experiments were conducted and analyzed in duplicate.

# 2.3. Water quality parameters analysis

The raw and treated water samples were evaluated by measuring their pH,conductivity,turbidity,alkalinity,UV-Vis absorbance in the wavelength range of $2 0 0 { - } 7 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ,and dissolved organic carbon (DOC), total dissolved nitrogen (TDN),sulfate $\left( S 0 _ { 4 } \right)$ ，nitrate $\mathrm { ( N O } \bar { 3 } \mathrm { ) }$ ,and bromide $( \mathrm { B r } ^ { - } )$ concentrations.Turbidity was measured using a $2 1 0 0 \mathrm { ~ P ~ }$ turbidimeter (Hach,USA) following USEPA method 180.1.Alkalinity was determined by titration method (APHA 232oB).UV-Vis absorbance was measured using a DR6oo0 spectrophotometer (Hach, USA) with a1 cm quartz cell. Specific UV absorbance (SUVA) was calculated as the ratio of the UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { ( U V A _ { 2 5 4 } ) }$ normalized to the DOC concentration.A multi $\mathrm { N } / \mathrm { C } { \mathfrak { R } }$ 2100 TOC Analyzer (Analytik Jena AG, Germany）was used to determine the DOC and TDN concentrations. Sulfate,nitrate,and bromide concentrations were measured by colorimetric method using a DR6ooo spectrophotometer (Hach，USA) following USEPA method 375.4, the cadmium reduction method (Hach, USA),and the APHA 45o0 phenol red colorimetric method,respectively.

# 2.4. Chlorination and DBP formation potential

Chlorination and DBP formation potential (DBP-FP) studies were conducted using raw water,MIEX-treated water,PACl-coagulated water,and water that was pre-treated by MIEX followed by subsequent coagulation (MIEX/Coag).For DBP-FP evaluation,chlorination was performed following uniform formation conditions (UFC)[30] Various concentrations of NaOCl were added to the water samples at pH 8,and the chlorine dose that yielded a $1 \pm 0 . 4 \ : \mathrm { m g - C l / L }$ residual after 24 h incubation in the dark at ${ 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ was selected for DBPs extraction.To investigate the changes in the properties of DOMand the species of DBPs due to chlorination,reaction times of 1,24,48,and $7 2 \mathrm { h }$ were selected based on the decay curves of the disinfectant [3].A higher dosage of chlorine (3 times the DOC concentration in the samples) was used in this experiment to ensure that the residual chlorine concentration was detectableafter $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$

Chlorinated samples were quenched with ascorbic acid for DBPs determination [31].THMs were selected as representative groups for C-DBPs while HANs were selected as representative groups for N-DBPs as they are listed in WHO guidelines and are the DBP species primarily formed under chlorination.The concentration of four THMs (chloroform (TCM)，bromodichloromethane(BDCM),dibromochloromethane (DBCM),and bromoform (TBM)) and three HANs (dichloroacetonitrile (DCAN)，trichloroacetonitrile(TCAN)，anddibromoacetonitrile (DBAN)) were determined according to EPA method 551.1.It should be noted that bromochloroacetonitrile (BCAN) was not evaluated in this study due to the prohibition of the production,import,export,or possession of BCAN according to the Hazardous Substance Act of Thailand (1992).The samples were extracted in methyl tert-butyl ether (MtBE) and analyzed by gas chromatography-electron capture detector (GC-ECD,Shimadzu Nexis GC -2030,Japan) equipped with an $\mathrm { R t } \mathbf { \mathbf { \& } } ( \mathbf { \mathbf { \bar { B } } } - 5 $ column (Restek,USA).All extractions and analyses were performed in duplicate.The detection limits were $1 . 0 ~ \mu \ g / \mathrm { L }$ for all THMs and HANs.

# 2.5.DOM characterization

The DOM in the water samples was analyzed with fluorescence EEM spectra and HPSEC.The EEM spectra of the raw,treated,and chlorinated samples were collected using FS5 spectrofluorometry (Edinburgh, UK) with an excitation wavelength range of $2 0 0 { - } 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ increments.Emissions were recorded from 250 to $5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ at $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ increments.The bandwidths of the excitation and emission scans were both set at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ .All samples were analyzed in duplicate and average values were reported.A blank EEMof the deionized (DI) waterwas also collected on the same day prior to sample analysis.Sample EEMs were corrected for the instrument excitation-emission specific inner-filter effect [32],and they were blank-subtracted.Then,the corrected fluorescence intensities were Raman-normalized by dividing the intensities by the integrated area of the water-Raman peak (excitation wavelength of $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and emission wavelength between 380 and $4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ ,and data were reported in Raman units (RU).

PARAFAC analysis was used to model the dataset of 148 EEMs. The model was developed using Matlab (R2018a,MathWorks) and DOMFluor Toolbox [23].During the preliminary data exploration,one EEM was removed as an outlier due to an instrumental error.Data from the excitation at wavelengths lower than $2 3 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ was also removed for scatter removal. Preliminary 2-7 component models were developed. Residual and sum-of-squared error (SSE) monitoring,split-half analysis, and random initialization were used for model validation [33].A 4-component model was able to achieve $9 9 . 5 8 \%$ explained variance. Thus,this model was selected based on its randomness of the residuals and small SSE values.To identify the source and chemical characteristics of each component,the obtained component profiles were compared with previous literature in the OpenFluor database [34],and spectra with similarity scores (Tucker's congruence) simultaneously exceeding 0.95 on the excitation and emission spectra were considered to be similar.Based on the model output, the position of maximum intensity $( \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } }$ and $\lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } , \mathrm { n m }$ for each component was determined and the relative concentration of each component was reported as the maximum fluorescence intensity $( F _ { m a x } )$ in Raman units [35].

HPSEC analysis was performed with a UV-DAD-HPLC (Agilent1100 series) equipped with an SB-802.5 HQ column (Shodex OHpak).The mobile phasewas18MΩDI water witha flow rate of $1 . 0 \ \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ .The injection volume was $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ .AUV detector was incorporated into the system to measure wavelengths of 192,214,245,and $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m }$ .The detected peak areaat $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ was used for a semiquantitative determination of the molecular weight and humic fraction size of the DOM. Water samples were analyzed without any pre-treatment other than filtration using a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ nylon filter.The column separated the DOM according to molecular size/weight,and the weight-average molecular weight (MW)of the DOMwas then calculated based on a standard curve generated from polyethylene glycol (PEG) in the range of 20o-12,000 Da.The difference in the area under each peak in the chromatogram was used to compare the removal of each DOM fraction.

# 3.2.PARAFAC components

Table 2 and Fig.1 show the spectral characteristics and profile of the four components identified by the PARFAC model.During the initial model development, PARAFAC analysis with 3-7 components was performed on the dataset of 148 EEMs. Only the models containing three, four,and six components could be split-half validated.For 4-way splithalf analysis,the data were split into four halves,and each half was modeled independently.Then,the spectra properties of components identified in each half were compared.The model was considered robust when Tucker's congruence coefficients of the comparison during splithalf analysis were greater than O.95 [23].The 4-component model was selected based on the randomness of residual, small sum of square error during random initialization,and a comparison of the spectral profiles of the model components with previous studies in the OpenFluor database [34].The four components exhibited spectral properties of true organic fluorophores,including one emission maximum in the spectra loading and minimal overlap between excitation and emission maxima [33] (Fig. 1).

The PARAFAC model in this study was developed from EEMs of samples collected at the pH of natural water before (7.26) and after (6.28-7.43) treatments.MIEX treatment resulted in a minor pH increase while PACl coagulation slightly reduced the pH of the treated water due to alkalinity consumption,similar to previous studies [38,39].In this narrow pH range,the quenching effect of the fluorescent peaks of the humic-like and protein-like fluorophores was marginal [4O,41].This study follows the conventions for the descriptions of fluorophores,as symbolized by PARAFAC components C1 to C4 according to the order of decreasing variation.A comparison of the spectral contours shown in Fig.1 with previously identified PARAFAC components in the OpenFluor database indicates that the samples in this study contained both humic-like and protein-like fluorophores [34]. Components Cl and C2 have been previously ascribed to humic-like fluorophores of terrestrial or anthropogenic origin [42,43]. Component C3 had a similar profile to either microbial humic-like or tryptophan-like DOM. Component C4 was previously classified as tyrosine protein-like DOM[44,45].

# 3.3. Removal of organic and inorganic precursors

# 3.3.1. Removal of $U V _ { 2 5 4 } ,$ DOC,TDN,and bromide

The uptake of DOC by the MIEX resin rapidly increased with increasing contact time during the first $1 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ of mixing (Fig. S2). This rapid removal was attributed to the large number of available exchange sites of the fresh resin as well as the favorable removal of DOM with high charge density by the resin [48].The uptake of DOC on the MIEX resin gradually increased during the next $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ .After that, the DOC uptake remained relatively constant with increasing contact time,indicating that equilibrium was reached.Thus, the optimum time for DOC removal by the MIEX resin was determined to be $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ ,which was similar to previous studies [1,16]．Moreover,the DOC removal kinetics were wel-fitted with the pseudo-second-order (PSO) model $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } \ : = \ : 0 . 9 9$ Fig.S2 and Table S1). This indicated chemisorption behavior involving the exchange or sharing of electrons between DOC and functional groups on the surface of the MIEX resin [49].Ion exchange has been reported to be the predominant removal mechanism of DOMby MIEX resins,rather than adsorption [29].Therefore,these results are in good agreement with the literature.

Both ion exchange by the MIEX resin and charge neutralization by coagulation selectively removed DOM with higher charge density.Thus,UV-adsorbing materials (that is,the humic fraction of DOM) were preferentially removed[39].

For MIEX resin treatment (Fig.2a,presented as a normalized concentration $\mathrm { ( C / C _ { 0 } ) }$ ，theremoval of $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and DOC increased with increasing resin dose in the range of $1 { - } 3 \ \mathrm { m L / L }$ .Further increasing the resin dose did not improve the removal of $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ or DOC, while TDN and bromide removal gradually increased over the resin dose range of $1 { - } 1 0 \ \mathrm { m L / L }$ .Thus,the effective resin dose(ERD) for DOC removal was selected to be $3 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { L }$ .This dosage corresponded to $7 3 \%$ $5 6 \%$ $4 1 \%$ ,and $7 . 3 \%$ removal of $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 . }$ ，DOC,TDN,and bromide,respectively.This finding is consistent with the anion selectivity for the MIEX resin $( \mathrm { D O C } >$ nitrate $>$ bromide) provided by the manufacturer.The DOC removal of $5 6 \%$ in this study was lower than that of another study,which reported a DOC removal of up to $8 0 \%$ using the same resin dose [21].The lower removal achieved in this work was attributed to the presence of other anions (nitrate,sulfate,and bromide),which can compete with DOC for the anion exchange sites on the resin [14].On the other hand,because the MIEX resin showed a high affinity for DOM over bromide [29], the higher DOC concentration potentially inhibited bromide removal.

The concentrations of DOC and bromide play a major role in the formation of DBPs because they respectively act as organic and inorganic precursors to DBPs [3,51].Ideally,WTPs will simultaneously remove DOC and bromide to control DBP formation.In this study, the highest bromide removal from the initial concentration of $0 . 6 \mathrm { m g / L }$ was $3 4 \%$ ，which was achieved at a resin dose of $1 0 \mathrm { m L / L }$ . This dose was much higher than the effective dose required for DOC removal.The MIEX resin has an ion exchange capacity of $0 . 4 – 0 . 5 5 \mathrm { m e q / m L }$ [16]. Thus,the theoretical capacity of the resin was sufficient for the simultaneousremoval ofDOC $( 1 0 \mathrm { m e q } / \mathbf { g } { \mathrm { - C } } )$ [29]andbromide (12.5 meq/g-Br).However,at low resin doses,the accessible exchange sites were insufficient and not effectively utilized for bromide removal due to their greater affinity for DOC.Thus,the resin dose was the limiting factor of bromide removal [52].At higher resin doses,plenty of vacant exchange sites were available for both DOC and bromide uptake, resulting in a rapid increase in bromide removal for resin doses greater than $3 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { L }$ (Fig. 2a).

The adsorption equilibrium of DOC on the MIEX resin is shown in Fig.S4.Freundlich and Langmuir isotherm models were used to fit the equilibrium experimental data,and the constants and correlation coefficients of these two models are reported in Table S2.Compared with the Langmuir model, the Freundlich model provided a better prediction for the removal of DOC by the MIEX resin,as indicated by the greater coefficient of determination $( \mathbb { R } ^ { 2 } )$ values.Previous studies have also reported that the Freundlich model is more suitable for describing the removal of phosphate [53],perchlorate [49],bromide [52],and DOC [21].The Freundlich model describes the adsorption of a wide range of concentration values on heterogeneous surfaces [49],which is consistent with the macroporous and heterogeneous structure of the MIEX resin (as indicated by SEM images) (Fig. S1).

In the range of $1 0 { - } 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ ,coagulation was less effective than the MIEX resin for precursor removal (Fig.2b).The superior DOM removal performance of the MIEX resin compared to coagulation has been previously reported over a range of water sources [13].The optimum enhanced coagulation dose to achieve a reduction in the DOC concentration was $5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ ，which corresponded to the treated water with $\mathrm { C } / \mathrm { C } _ { 0 }$ of 0.58,0.65,0.82,and 0.96 for $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ,DOC,TDN,and bromide, respectively.The coagulant dose applied at the full-scale WTP using the same water source was in the range of $2 0 { - } 4 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ for turbidity removal．Moreover，bromide concentration was not significantly affected by coagulation,which was unable to effectively remove the bromide ions.The $\mathrm { { C } / \mathrm { { C } _ { 0 } } }$ for bromide was in the range of $0 . 9 6 { - } 1 . 0 2 $ for all coagulation experiments.In a previous study,an increase in the bromide ion to organic carbon ratio $( \mathrm { B r / D O C ) }$ was reported after coagulation treatment, in agreement with this result [7].

Performing coagulation after MIEX pre-treatment had no significant effect on any of the measured water quality parameters (Fig.2c). Thus, the optimum subsequent coagulant dose was selected based on turbidity removal.The turbidity of the $3 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { L }$ MIEX pre-treated water was 3.28

NTU.The subsequent coagulation treatment with a dose of $5 \mathrm { m g } / \mathrm { L }$ reduced the turbidity to 0.48 NTU (compared to the $1 . 1 9 \mathrm { N T U }$ turbidity of the raw water treated with $5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ PACl coagulation alone) (Fig.S3).Thus,MIEX pre-treatment of the raw water significantly reduced the amount of PACl required for the subsequent turbidity removal,which was consistent with previous studies [1,21,54].The ineffective removal of DOC, $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ,and TDN by subsequent coagulation indicated that the recalcitrant DOM unable to be removed by the MIEX resin was also not removable by coagulation.

# 3.3.2.Removal of PARAFAC components

The characteristics of DOM in the raw and treated waters were analyzed using an EEM-PARAFAC approach.Representative EEMs obtained from the raw and treated waters are shown in Fig. S5a.For a quantitative comparison,the corresponding results for the $F _ { m a x }$ C1, C2, C3,and C4 identified by the PARAFAC model for the raw water and treated water (MIEX,coagulation,and MIEX/Coag treatments）are presented in Fig.3.For each specific water sample,a humic to protein ratio was calculated as the summation of the $F _ { m a x }$ values of terrestrial humic-like components (C1 and C2) divided by the summation of tryptophan protein-like (C3)and tyrosine protein-like (C4) components. In the raw water, the $F _ { m a x }$ values of humic-like components were greater than those of protein-like components,resulting in a humic to protein ratio of 4.38 (Fig.3a and Fig. S6a). The greater fluorescence intensity of humic-like DOM compared with protein-like DOM has been previously reported for the same water source [28] and other water sources in Thailand [55,56].

Because MIEX treatment consistentlyachieved greaterDOC removal compared to coagulation (Fig.1),MIEX treatment was also more effective for the removal of PARAFAC components than coagulation (Figs.3a and 3b). Coagulation generally removes the hydrophobic fraction of DOM more efficiently than the hydrophilic fraction, while MIEX treatment removes DOM with higher charge density and DOM with aromatic functional groups [57]．The hydrophilic and low-molecular-weight DOM fraction was also removed by MIEX treatment,which explains the greater removal of all PARAFAC components by MIEX treatment compared to coagulation [58].Both the MIEX and coagulation treatments led to the preferential removal of humic-like components (C1 and C2) over protein-like components (C3 and C4).In the range of doses tested，MIEX treatment removed $4 8 { - } 8 9 \%$ of humic-like components and $0 { - } 7 1 \%$ of protein-like components.In contrast,PACl coagulation only removed $8 { - } 3 8 \%$ of humic-like components and $0 { - } 3 8 \%$ of protein-like components.This preferential removal of humic-like components was more evident in the MIEX treatment, which resulted in the generation of treated water enriched in protein-like DOMcompared to the raw water.PACl coagulation reduced the humic to protein ratio from 4.38 to 3.19,and MIEX treatment reduced this ratio to 1.90 (Fig. S6a and S6b). Performing coagulation after MIEX pre-treatment did not significantly change the $F _ { m a x }$ of any component, indicating that the fluorescence components not removed by the MIEX resin were also not removable by coagulation (Fig. 3c and Fig. S6c).

# 3.4.DBPs formation and species transformation

# 3.4.1.Impact of treatment processes on THM formation

The formation potential (FP) of four THMs (TCM, BDCM,DBCM,and TBM)and the bromine incorporation factors (BIF) in the raw and treated waters were investigated during 24-hr chlorination (Fig.4).BIF is an indicative value of the incorporation of Br into DBPs,and this value can be calculated according to Eq.(1) [51]:

$$
B I F _ { T H M s } = \frac { 0 \times \left[ C H C l _ { 3 } \right] + 1 \times \left[ C H C l _ { 2 } B r \right] + 2 \times \left[ C H C l B r _ { 2 } \right] + 3 \times \left[ C H B r _ { 3 } \right] } { \left[ C H C l _ { 3 } \right] + \left[ C H C l _ { 2 } B r \right] + \left[ C H C l B r _ { 2 } \right] + \left[ C H B r _ { 3 } \right] }
$$

For the raw water,chlorination resulted in a formation potential of the four THMs(THM4-FP) and BIF of $1 8 5 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ and 0.78,respectively. The formation of TCM contributed to $3 6 \%$ of THM4-FP,while DCBM, DBCM,and TBM contributed to $3 3 . 9 \%$ $2 5 . 1 \%$ ,and $4 . 9 3 \%$ ,respectively. Thus,a high Br concentration in the raw water led to the formation of brominated species (including DCBM and DBCM) in a similar concentration range to that of TCM.This was unlike many other surface water sources in Thailand,in which TCMhas been observed to account for the highest portion among the THMs under chlorination [21,61].

Different THM distribution trends were observed between the water treated with MIEX resin and that treated with PACl coagulation.With MIEX treatment,THM4-FP gradually decreased with increasing resin dose.Ata dose of $3 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { L }$ ,which was the ERD forDOC removal,MIEX treatment resulted in a $3 1 \%$ THM4-FP reduction.However,BIF rapidly increased to 1.28 as the MIEX dose increased within the dose range of $1 { - } 3 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { L }$ (Fig.4a).Changes in the Br/DOC ratio after the MIEX and coagulation treatments were investigated to determine the selectivity of target contaminant removal (Fig.S8).The Br/DOC ratio is an important factor that influences THM speciation,and an increase in Br/DOC leads to an increase in the relative abundance of brominated THMs [62].The increase in BIF with the low resin dose treatment can be explained by the preferable removal of DOC over bromide leading to an increased $\ B r / \ D O C$ ratio (Fig. S8a).The formation of TBM (the fully brominated THM species) also sharply increased from $9 . 1 3 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ to $2 5 . 2 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ with $3 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { L }$ resin treatment.The percentage distribution of TCM,DCBM, DBCM,and TBMafterMIEX treatmentwasat $1 9 . 3 \%$ $2 6 . 3 \%$ $3 4 . 7 \%$ ,and $1 9 . 7 \%$ ,respectively.

![](images/fa324ba70806f41bc2585de7ff08a7389b2add5b47ac535d36c55179fdaf71cd.jpg)  
Fig.4.THM formation potential (THM-FP) and bromine incorporation factor (BIF) of the water after treatments with (a) MIEX resin,(b) PACl coagulation, and (c) MIEX pre-treatment followed by PACl coagulation.

At higher resin doses of $3 { \mathrm { - } } 1 0 \ \mathrm { m L / L }$ ，BIF slightly decreased with increasing resin dose.This was because Br/DOC decreased as the excess resin began to take bromide into its vacant exchange sites.It should be noted that TCM-FP did not drastically decrease over the resin dose range of $2 { \mathrm { - } } 1 0 \ \mathrm { m L / L }$ .Instead,the decrease in the formation of brominated species (DCBM,DBCM,and TBM) was observed to account for the reduction of THM4-FP.Thus,even though performing the MIEX treatment at its ERD was the most economical option for DOC removal, operatingat the higher resin dose might provide more benefit in terms of bromide removal, leading toa reduction in THM4-FP.Froma practical pointof view,MIEX resin is a proven technology that is readilyavailable on a commercial scale for water treatment.Moreover,the operation cost of a high-rate MIEX system is stillower than other technologies that can remove bromide, e.g.,RO and NF membrane filtration [63].

For the coagulated water (Fig.4b),a significant reduction in the THM4-FP was not observed within the PACl dose range of $1 0 { - } 4 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ At $5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ ,which was the optimum PACl dose for DOC removal,only $1 4 \%$ THM4-FP reduction was achieved,which was much lower than the DOC removal efficiency $( 3 4 . 7 \% )$ .Coagulation effectively removed DOC but had no effect on bromide,resulting in an elevated Br'/DOC ratio in the coagulated water. TCM and DCBM formation decreased after coagulation treatment,while DBCM and TBM formation increased,which was due to the lower DOC with a high bromide concentration. The formation of TBMwas favored at high coagulant doses,and an increase in TBM formation of up to 1.8-fold was observed. The BIF of the coagulated water gradually increased with increasing PACl dose within the tested range,which was consistent with the $\ B r / \ D O C$ results (Fig. S8b). After treatment with PACl at its optimum dose,the percentage distribution of TCM,DCBM,DBCM,and TBM was at $2 7 . 8 \%$ $3 1 . 0 \%$ $3 0 . 6 \%$ and $1 0 . 5 \%$ ,respectively.Thus,coagulation treatment can significantly enhance Br-DBP formation because coagulated water tends to have high Br-DBP-forming water quality conditions,including high bromide,high alkalinity,and low DOC [10].

For the MIEX/Coag treated water,the effects of the subsequent coagulation treatment on THM-FP,BIF,and Br'/DOC were marginal (Fig.4c and Fig. S8c). The THM-FP results under optimized treatment conditions indicated that the combined MIEX/Coag treatment was very similar to the MIEX treatment without coagulation and much better than enhanced PACl coagulation without MIEX treatment.These results were as expected because no significant removal of DOC,bromide,or PARAFAC components was achieved by subsequent coagulation. This was in good agreement with previous studies,which have reported that subsequent coagulation (both alum and PACl coagulation） provides no additional benefit for the reduction of THM-FP reduction [1,21,64].

# 3.4.2.Impact of treatment processes on HAN formation

Concentration data for the formation of the three HANs (DCAN, TCAN,and DBAN) is depicted in Fig.5.Chlorination of the raw water resulted in a formation of the three HANs (HAN3-FP) of $2 2 . 7 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ which was a much lower concentration than THM4-FP.This was consistent with the low DON nature of this water source.Despite the high concentration of bromide in the raw water, the dominant HAN species was DCAN $( 1 8 . 3 \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ ， $8 0 . 6 \%$ of HAN3-FP),which is a fully chlorinated HAN.This was followed by DBAN $( 3 . 6 2 \mu \mathrm { g / L }$ ， $1 5 . 9 \%$ of HAN3-FP),and TCAN had the lowest concentration $( 0 . 7 8 \mu \mathbf { g } / \mathrm { L }$ ， $3 . 4 5 \%$ of HAN3-FP).The presence of bromide favored bromine merging into THMs more than HANs [65] and inhibited DCAN formation [51], resulting in a low concentration of HANs.

3.4.3.1.Chlorine demand and chlorine reactivity of organic substances. Raw water and water samples treated with MIEX,coagulation,and MIEX/Coag at the dose optimized to achieve the greatest DOC removal were selected for chorine activity evaluation.The DOC concentrations of the four selected water samples were 3.88,1.72,2.53,and $1 . 5 2 \mathrm { m g / L }$ Chlorine demand,defined as the difference between the total chlorine added and residual chlorine,is mainly attributed to the chlorine oxidation reaction with DOM.The presence of some inorganic constituents,e.g.,ammonia,iron,manganese,and bromide,can also influence chlorine demand [67]. Because the ammonia concentration in the raw water was lower than the detection limit,only the effect of bromide was investigated forinorganic constituents.

The chlorine demand of the four samples followed the same order as that of DOC concentration:raw water $>$ coagulated water $>$ MIEXtreatedwater $\approx$ MIEX/Coag water (Fig.6).As the chlorine reacted with oxidizable constituents in the water, the chlorine demand increased over time.For all waters tested, the chlorine demand rapidly increased during the first $_ { 1 - 4 8 \mathrm { h } }$ of chlorination, then slowly increased after $4 8 \ \mathrm { h r }$ .The chlorine demand measured at $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ contributed to $7 2 \mathrm { - } 8 7 \%$ of the chlorine demand determined at $7 2 \mathrm { { h r } }$ .The MIEX-treated waterand MIEX/ Coag water had the same chlorine demand rates.Chlorine demand is a surrogate of oxidation and a predictor for DBP formation in many waters because it indicates the degree of halogen substitution [26].The ratio of chlorine demand to DOC concentration corresponds to the chlorine reactivity of organic substances [68].After $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of contact time,the chlorine reactivity values of the raw,coagulated,MIEX-treated,and MIEX/Coag waters were 1.30,0.59,0.87，and $0 . 6 2 \mathrm { m g \mathrm { - } C l / m g \mathrm { - } C , }$ respectively.Thus,compared to PACl coagulation，MIEX treatment removed the chlorine reactive fractions more effectively,which was consistent with a previous study [64].In the presence of bromide, the chlorination of DOM constitutes a complex chemical process involving competing reactions such as bromide oxidation,the oxidation of DOM, and DBPs formation [69].In this study,MIEX treatment,which was able to simultaneously remove bromide and DOM, had a greater impact on the reduction of chlorine demand compared to coagulation,which was able to remove DOM but could not remove bromide.

# 3.5.Relationship between THMs formation and precursor surrogate

Linear correlations were sought between THM4-FP during chlorination under uniform formation conditions and various THM precursor surrogates,including DOC, $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 } ,$ and $F _ { m a x }$ of the samples before chlorination.Linear best-fit models were determined based on least squares analyses of the raw water and MIEX-treated,PACl-coagulated, and MIEX/Coag waters (with various treatment doses,38 samples from duplicate experiments).For this data set, the strongest correlations were found between THM4-FP and the $F _ { m a x }$ of C1,C2,and C3 $( \mathbf { R } ^ { 2 } \mathbf { \Phi } = \mathbf { \Phi }$ 0.85-0.89,Fig. S9).The correlations between THM4-FP and fluorescence surrogates were stronger than those of DOC and $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 } \left( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 6 \right.$ and O.80,Fig.S1O).No significant correlation was observed between THM4-FP and the $F _ { m a x }$ of C4 $( p > 0 . 0 1$ , not shown).

Thus,both terrestrial humic-like (C1 and C2) and microbial humiclike/tryptophan protein-like (C3) components with aromatic structures were established as a potent indication of THMs precursors.For the tyrosine protein-like C4,[26] also reported that protein-like components were not strongly correlated with DBPs formation.Strong correlations between the initial maximum fluorescence intensity in the water samples before chlorination and DBP-DP have been previously established [20,24].These correlations revealed that the greater performance of MIEX resin treatment compared with PACl coagulation for THM4-FP reduction could be explained by the greater removal of important precursors (C1,C2,and C3).

Fluorescence loss can be related to changes in DOM characteristics and the chlorine oxidation of DOM under varying chlorine exposure. Therefore,the relationships between THM4-FP and fluorescence loss were also explored.The data set included raw water and water treated with MIEX resin,PACl coagulation,and MIEX/Coag at their optimum doses for DOC removal under $\mathbf { 1 - 7 2 \ h }$ chlorination (32 samples from duplicate experiments). Stronger linear correlations were found between THM4-FP and $\triangle F _ { m a x } , \mathrm { C 1 }$ $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 8 9 $ ,Fig. 9a)and $\triangle F _ { m a x } , \mathrm { C 2 } ( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } =$ 0.79, Fig. 9b). $\triangle F _ { m a x } , \mathrm { C 3 }$ was also correlated with THM4-FP to a smaller extent $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 3$ ,Fig. 9c),but a significant correlation was not found between $\triangle F _ { m a x } , \mathrm { C } 4$ and THM4-FP (not shown). Thus,fluorescence surrogates $( \triangle F _ { m a x }$ of Cl and C2 over a 3-day chlorination period) are a stronger predictor of short-term and long-term chlorine reactivity and DBPs formation even when a high concentration of bromide is present in the source water.

Fluorescence loss is an indication of the existence of oxidation reactions,while DBPs formation occurs via addition reactions and electrophilic substitution reactions [72].The strong correlations between THM4-FP and the $\triangle F _ { m a x }$ of DOM fractions with greater chlorine reactivity (C1 and C2) under short-term and long-term chlorination prove thatan EEM-PARAFAC approach can be used to track the chlorine oxidation of DOM and that the degree of chlorine oxidation can be related to addition reactions and electrophilic substitution reactions.

# 4. Conclusions

This study uses an EEM-PARAFAC approach to characterize the impacts on DOM,chlorine reactivity,and DBPs formation due to MIEX and coagulation treatment processes for a water sample containing high bromide concentration.The following insights were obtained:

·Ata dose lower than the effective resin dose,MIEX resin treatment primarily removed DOC.However,treatment at a higher dose simultaneously removed a substantial amount of DOC and bromide. Coagulation treatment achieved moderate DOC removal but did not contribute to bromide removal. Both MIEX resin and coagulation treatment preferentially removed humic-like DOM over protein-like DOM,but this behavior was more evident in the MIEX treatment.This resulted in treated water enriched in protein-like DOM compared to the raw water.   
·The chlorination of the raw water resulted in THMs and HANs concentrations of $1 8 5 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ and $2 2 . 7 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ ，respectively.MIEX resin treatment outperformed coagulation treatment for both THMs $( 1 9 - 5 1 \% )$ and HANs $( 7 0 - 9 5 \% )$ precursors removal due to its higher degree of removal eficiency for organic substances and bromide. MIEX resin treatment was also very effective at controlling the formation of di-brominated species (DBCM and DBAN),while coagulation was not.   
·For raw and treated water samples,chlorination resulted in substantial fluorescence loss $( \triangle F _ { m a x } )$ of the humic-like components (C1 and C2),suggesting that these compounds were more reactive to chlorine.The maximum fluorescence intensity of C1 and C2 was significantly correlated with THMs formation. The strongest correlations $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 8 9 )$ were established between the $\triangle F _ { m a x }$ of C1 and the formation of THMs during $_ { 1 - 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ chlorination.   
· The results of this study provide new guidance for water utilities using high-bromide water sources to improve their treatment process.MIEX pre-treatment followed by low-dose coagulation and chlorination with an optimized chlorine contact time is a potentially effective solution for controlling the formation of both chlorinated and brominated carbonaceous and nitrogenous DBPs.
