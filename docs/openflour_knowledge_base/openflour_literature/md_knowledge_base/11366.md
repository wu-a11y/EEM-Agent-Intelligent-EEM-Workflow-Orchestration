# 1．Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is ubiquitously found in all water sources used for drinking water and is a concern for utilities because it can react with disinfectants used during water treatment,as well as other constituents present in the water,to create disinfection byproducts (DBPs).Haloacetic acids (HAAs) and trihalomethanes (THMs) are two classes of DBPs that are suspected carcinogens and are regulated by the United States Environmental Protection Agency (USEPA） under the Disinfection Byproduct Rule [1].THMs and HAAs are organic molecules that have incorporated one or more halogens into their structure.All four chloro-and brominated THMs are currently regulated,while only five $\mathbf { ( H A A 6 5 ) }$ of the nine $\left( \mathrm { H A A } _ { 9 } \right)$ chloro- and brominated HAAs are currently regulated.The four unregulated HAAs are currently monitored via the fourth Unregulated Contaminant Monitoring Rule (UCMR4) [2] and have been added to the fifth Contaminant Candidate List (CCL5) [3], indicating that additional regulations may be implemented in the coming years.As many utilities face challenges meeting the current regulations,appended DBP regulations will impose a further challenge.

Depending on the source water matrix and factors in the distribution system that may promote higher DBP concentrations,some utilities may require supplementary treatment steps beyond traditional treatment techniques to meet the USEPA Stage 1 and 2 DBPR compliance goals. Someoptions for supplementary treatment include enhanced coagulation,ozonation,membrane filtration,and ion exchange.This study focuses on magnetic ion exchange (MIEX) as it can be added upstream of an existing conventional treatment plant with little to no downstream modification of either the treatment plant or the distribution system.It can also supplement enhanced coagulation,which is already in practice at the full-scale plant examined in this study.MIEX is a patented resin,which consists of small beads that are fluidized in the treatment contactor but settle quickly [4],as well as a patented treatment process [5] that is commercially available worldwide and intended to supplement coagulation.A completely mixed flow reactor (CMFR) contains the resin,which acts as an anion exchange site where individual beads function as weak magnets to extract anions from the water. Negatively charged acids from DOM exchange with chloride ions on the active sites of the resin. Once exhausted,the resin is regenerated with sodium chloride and the organics are released into a concentrated brine solution via reverse anion exchange.By adding MIEX treatment to an existing treatment train,higher DBP precursor removal may occur, thus lowering the overall DBP formation potential.

Though invented in the early 2ooOs,relatively few studies have been performed using MIEX.Several studies have taken place at the bench scale,using MIEX resin in jar tests [6-15].These studies have demonstrated DOC removal of anywhere from 22 to $7 4 \%$ ,with $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ removal following similar patterns and sometimes exceeding DOC removal. Some of these bench-scale studies have found DBP formation potential to be lower in MIEX-treated water than in coagulated water [8-11]. Fewer studies have taken place at the pilot or full scale [16-22],and those pilot studies still involved some bench-scale components in lieu of a pilot scale treatment train.Additionally, few studies have evaluated MIEX performance through fluorescence spectroscopy to assess changes in organic matter character [1O,11,22].To the knowledge of the authors, no study has yet taken place with a complete pilot-scale conventional treatment train with MIEX pretreatment examining THMand HAA formation alongside fluorescence analysis.The water chemistry matrix of source waters determines the formation potential of DBPs and the efficacy of different treatments,thus,a comparison of a pilot-scale MIEX treatment with an adjacent full-scale plant is critical for understanding the effect of MIEX on the source water.

The objectives of this study are to 1) compare THM and $\mathrm { { H A A } } _ { 9 }$ formation potential in a pilot-scale conventional treatment plant with MIEX to a full-scale conventional treatment plant without MIEX under various distribution system conditions and 2)assess changes in fluorescent DOM in pilot-scale conventional treatment plant with MIEX compared to a full-scale conventional treatment plant without MIEX.

# 2. Methodology

# 2.1. Pilot setup and sampling

A pilot-scale drinking water treatment facility on the site of a fullscale conventional treatment plant in central Alabama was utilized in this study.The research facility consists of a mobile trailer equipped with a pilot-scale treatment train that simulates full-scale coagulation, flocculation，sedimentation，filtration,and disinfection processes (Intuitech, Salt Lake City,UT) (Fig.S1).For the duration of this study, the pilot facility was preceded by a pilot-scale MIEX treatment mobile unit (IXOM Corporation,Melbourne,Australia) (Figs.1,S2) such that the influent water underwent MIEX treatment prior to ferric sulfate (Kemira,Helsinki, Finland） coagulation, flocculation,sedimentation, filtration through mature sand and anthracite,and disinfection treatments.IXOM Corporation performed routine resin condition monitoring throughout the study to ensure the quality of the resin was maintained.

The influent water consisted of a blend of two surface source waters and initial testing occurred during the months of August and September of 2022,as historical plant data demonstrated DBP formation potential to be highest at this time.The results of the pilot study were compared to data collected from the finished water at the full-scale treatment plant, which employs enhanced coagulation through pH-targeting with ferric sulfate,followed by flocculation,sedimentation,filtration through mature sand and anthracite,disinfection,and pH adjustment.

# 2.2. Bulk parameter testing

Samples were collected from the influent water,Pilot:MIEX,Pilot: MIEX $^ +$ conventional treatment (CT),and Full Scale: conventional treatment (CT) sample points indicated in Fig.1.Water sample characterization included dissolved organic carbon (DOC)，ultraviolet absorbance at a wavelength of $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 , }$ ，pH,and alkalinity. Alkalinity and pH were tested on-site using a bench-scale auto-titrator (OrionStar T91o,ThermoScientific,Waltham，MA） and pH meter respectively (Orion Verastar, ThermoScientific,Waltham, MA). $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ was tested on-site using a HACH DR6ooO spectrophotometer.DOC samples were filtered through a $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ cellulose filter (S-Pak Filters, Millipore Sigma, Burlington,MA) and stored in $4 0 \mathrm { m L }$ glass vials with no headspace and was measured with a TOC Analyzer $( \mathrm { T O C - V _ { W P } } ,$ Shimadzu,Kyoto,Japan) via Standard Method 531oC [23] at the Birmingham Water Works'EnviroLab,whichis certified by the Alabama Department of Environmental Management and the USEPA.

# 2.3.Fluorescence spectroscopy

Samples were collected from the influent water,Pilot:MIEX,Pilot: $\mathbf { M I E X + C T } _ { : }$ ,and Full Scale: CT sample points indicated in Fig.1. These samples were filtered through a $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ cellulose filter (S-Pak Filters, Millipore Sigma,Burlington,MA) such that any suspended solids were removed,then stored in $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ ambervials.Sampleswererefrigerated until analysis,which was performed within seven days of sampling.

Samples were analyzed on a HORIBA Aqualog at excitation wavelengths of 240 to $5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ in $1 0 { \cdot } \mathrm { n m }$ increments [24] and emission wavelengths of approximately 240 to $8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in $4 . 6 7 – \mathrm { n m }$ increments per the capabilities of the instrument[25].Abandpass length of $5 \mathrm { n m }$ and an integration time of O.1 s were used to follow standard scientific practice [26].Ablank sample of Milli- $\mathbf { \nabla } Q \mathbf { \circled { R } }$ Ultra-Pure water was collected on each day of sampling and subtracted from the samples to correct for background fluorescence.The samples were corrected for inner filter effects, Raman normalized,and corrected for first-and second-order Rayleigh masking using the Aqualog software.Excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) were generated from the corrected fluorescence data and were presented in Raman units to be analyzed both visually and statistically. Statistical analysis was performed with the Parallel Factor (PARAFAC) Analysis package in the Eigenvector Solo software.PARAFAC analysis statistically decomposes EEMs into their individual fluorescent components and allows for evaluation of changes in those individual componentsacross different samples [27-3O].After PARAFAC analysis,these individual fluorescent components were uploaded into the OpenFluor database [31] such that they could be compared against known entities in published literature.

# 2.4.Disinfection byproduct testing

Simulated distribution system (SDS) testing determined the DBP formation of the water sources in distribution system after seven days. Samples (2 L) were collected from the Pilot:MIEX $^ +$ conventional treatment (CT) and Full Scale:CT sample points (Fig.1) throughout August and September of 2022( $\mathbf { \tilde { n } } = 5 0$ samples). The Pilot: $\mathbf { M I E X + C T }$ samples were tested for a chlorine residual,as sodium hypochlorite (Allied Corporation,Morristown,NJ) is added prior to filtration in the pilot plant.The chlorination at this point mimics the full-scale CT plant, where sodium hypochlorite is added prior to filtration to oxidize manganese into a manganese dioxide precipitate.The samples were then chlorinated after filtration to achieve a residual of $2 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ , comparable to that of the finished water leaving the full-scale plant,and lime (Mississippi Lime, St.Louis,MO) was added to achieve a pHof $8 . 0 \pm 0 . 1$ No additional chemicals were added to the Full Scale: CT sample,but plant feed rates for chlorine and lime,as well as the chlorine residual and final pH,were recorded.All samples were stored in $^ \mathrm { ~ 1 ~ L ~ }$ amber bottles and placed inan incubatorat ${ 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ to simulate conditions in the distribution system.SDS testing at ${ } ^ { 2 5 } \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ with a hold time of seven days is a system-specific method that has been historically implemented in research at the full-scale plant.

In the first round of testing,samples were removed from the incubator and tested for chlorine residual, temperature,and pH and preserved for THMand HAA testing within $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ (Day O sample) and again after seven days (Day 7 sample).Chlorine residual was measured via a colorimetry test (DR6ooo,HACH,Loveland, CO),and temperature and pH were measured with a benchtop pH meter (Orion Verastar, ThermoScientific,Waltham,MA).Samples were then transferred into $2 5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ amber bottles with no headspace and with $2 5 \mathtt { m g }$ of ammonium chloride (Reagent Chemical,Ringoes,NJ) for preservation for HAA analysis. In the second round of testing，performed between October and November of 2022,the process above was repeated with hold times of 15 days.Additionally,a second incubator was employed such that SDs testing could be performed at ${ } ^ { 2 5 } \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ $( \mathtt { n } = 6 )$ and $3 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ $( \mathtt { n } = 6 )$ .This second round of testing was performed to better simulate the higher temperatures experienced in the summer and the higher water age experienced at points in the distribution system further away from the plant.

# 3．Results and discussion

# 3.1．Source water characteristics

The source water consisted of a blend of two surface waters with parameters described in Table 1.The specific ultraviolet absorbance (SUVA),a measure of aromaticity, was $1 . 8 3 \pm 0 . 2 8 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 1 }$ ,indicating some hydrophilic,microbial organic matter content. DOM of terrestrial origin typically yields a higher SUVA value of ${ > } 4 \mathrm { L m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ while DOMof microbial origin typically yields a lower SUVA value of $< 3 ~ \mathrm { L } ~ \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ [32,33]. The utility's monthly monitoring of bromide ions on an ion chromatographer (Dionex ICS-5ooo, ThermoFisher Scientific,Waltham,MA,USA) via EPA Method 3oo.0 consistently yielded a value below the quantification limit of $1 . 0 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$

# 3.2. Organics removal

The DOC concentrations and $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ absorbance values averaged over the 8-week study for the influent water,the Pilot: MIEX effluent,Pilot: $\mathbf { M I E X + C T }$ effluent,and Full-Scale:CT finished water are presented in Fig.2.Full-Scale: CTachieved $5 5 \pm 5 \%$ $\left( \mathtt { n } = 3 8 \right)$ and $7 5 \pm 8 \%$ $[ \boldsymbol { \mathbf { n } } = 3 8 ]$ removal of DOC and $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ,respectively,with a ferric sulfate coagulation dose of $1 0 9 \pm 1 4 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ .MIEX treatment alone reduced the influent DOC concentration by $4 4 \pm 1 1 \%$ $\mathbf { \tilde { \mathbf { n } } } = 3 9 $ and $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ absorbance values by $7 2 \pm 6 \% ( \mathrm { n } = 3 9 )$ .MIEX treatment reduced SUVA from $1 . 8 3 \pm 0 . 2 8 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 1 }$ to $0 . 9 \pm \ : 0 . 2 7 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g } ^ { - 1 } \ : \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ，indicating a preferential removal of $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ -absoring compounds compared to bulk DOC.This finding corroborates the findings of previous studies,which saw significant SUVA reductions by MIEX treatment across many waters, though efficacy of DOC and $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ removal varies based on influent water quality and operational parameters. Singer et al.[2O] observed $3 5 \mathrm { - } 6 7 ~ \%$ reduction in DOC concentration and $5 5 \mathrm { - } 8 8 ~ \%$ reduction in $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ absorbance ranging across four different source waters.Kitis et al. [13] observed $5 4 \mathrm { - } 7 4 \ \%$ reduction in DOC concentration and $7 5 \mathrm { - } 8 5 ~ \%$ reduction in $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ absorbance across five different source waters. Jutaporn et al. [1O] observed $2 8 \mathrm { - } 4 4 \mathrm { ~ \% ~ }$ reduction in DOC concentration and $3 7 - 5 3 \%$ reduction in $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ absorbance across two different source waters.

The MIEX pretreatment alone achieves the majority of the organic compound removal accomplished by the Full-Scale: CT,allowing the Pilot: $\mathbf { M I E X + C T }$ treatment train to accomplish the same organic compound removal as the Full-Scale: CT with a lower coagulant dosage. The Pilot: $\mathbf { M I E X + C T }$ achieved $5 5 \pm 8 \%$ $\left( \mathbf { n } = 5 0 \right)$ ,and $7 9 \pm 7 \%$ $( \mathbf { n } = 5 2 )$ removal of DOC and $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ,respectively,witha ferric sulfate coagulation dose of $2 4 \pm 1 1 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ ,which is $7 8 \%$ lower than the coagulant rate of the full-scale plant. The reduction in coagulant dose is consistent with previous studies,which found that coagulant feed rates could be reduced by $5 0 \mathrm { - } 7 5 ~ \%$ with MIEX pretreatment [34-37].Therefore, the Pilot: $\mathbf { M I E X } + \mathbf { C T }$ water exhibited effluent DOC concentrations that are comparable to those of the Full-Scale: CT finished water,yet with lower coagulant feed rates.

# 3.4.PARAFAC components

The excitation-emission matrices (EEMs） of 178 samples were analyzed using PARAFAC software to determine the number and character of fluorescent components.A three-component nonnegativity model was fitted to the data with an explained variance of $9 7 ~ \%$ and a core consistency of 9o.These components were compared against others in published literature with a congruence coefficient of ${ > } 0 . 9 9 $ via the OpenFluor library [31].Three fluorescing components were found in the samples (Fig.5) including,Cl:a tyrosine-like component [4O],C2:a “humic-dominated microbial material linked to degradation of algal and terrestrial sources”[41],and C3:a terrestrial humic-like component [42].All three components are typical findings for surface water in this region [43].PARAFAC gives each sample a component loading value, which represents the relative abundance of fluorescence across components and can provide insight into whether a given component is removed preferentially bya certain treatment process.

The Pilot: MIEX demonstrates affinity for humic-like material. This finding is consistent with the findings of previous jar tests with MIEX resin[11].MIEX is intended to supplement or replace traditional coagulation practices,thus the removal of humic-like fluorescent organic matter by MIEX can be expected as the same type of fluorescence organic matter is targeted by traditional coagulation [32,44,45].Previous studies have found a strong association between humic-like fluorescence and DBP formation [11,46,47].Therefore,using MIEX to assist in removal of humic-like fluorescing organic compounds likely contributes to reducing overall DBP formation.However,the full-scale treatment EEMs diverged from typical patterns of fluorescence observed after coagulation, flocculation,and sedimentation practice by removing the tyrosine-like C1 component.While most settling basins in the United States are concrete-lined reservoirs,the settling basin at the Full-Scale: CT plant used in this study is an unlined bottom basin with an area of over $2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ .Algal blooms occur seasonally during the summer months within the basin,and it is hypothesized that biological activity in the basin contributed to the removal of the tyrosine-like Cl component, as protein-like compounds are preferentially removed through biodegradation [43].

# 4. Conclusions and recommendations

This pilot-scale experiment produced the following results:

·TTHM and HAAg formation was reduced by $2 0 \ \%$ and $2 5 \ \%$ respectively,in Pilot:MIEX $+ \mathrm { C T }$ effluent when compared to FullScale:CT without MIEX over seven days of incubation at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ·When the incubation time was increased to 15 days and the temperature increased to $3 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ,the Pilot: MIEX $+ \mathrm { C T }$ reduced HAAs by $3 6 ~ \%$ when compared to full-scale treatment. ·MIEX pretreatment removed $7 2 \ \%$ of $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $4 4 ~ \%$ of DOC. By reducing influent organic carbon,the coagulant feed rates were reduced by $7 8 \%$ in the pilot-scale plant. ·MIEX pretreatment targeted removal of humic-like fluorescent organic matter,which is associated with DBP formation.

MIEX shows the potential to serve as an important contributor to DBP reduction,as well as a potential solution to other problems faced by utilities,such as diminishing chlorine residuals throughout distribution and problems incurred by high coagulant feed rates,including high costs,possible corrosion,and increased solids handling. Significant DOC and $\mathrm { U V } _ { 2 5 4 }$ reduction by MIEX allow for lower coagulant feed rates, which will lead to cost savings for the utility and a reduction of potentially corrosive chemicals in the plant and the distribution system. Higher chlorine residuals are also maintained after Pilot:MIEX+CT treatment when compared to Full-Scale: CT without MIEX,which is advantageous as higher chlorine residuals prevent biological regrowth throughout the distribution system.Future research directions include MIEX resin reuse ability and service life in full-scale treatment studies.
