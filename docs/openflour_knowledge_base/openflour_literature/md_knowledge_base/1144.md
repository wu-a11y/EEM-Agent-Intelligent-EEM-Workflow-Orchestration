Soil organic matter (SOM) play an important role in soil ecology and global carbon dynamic. As one of the most sever and irreversible land use change, urbanization could alter the regional carbon storage and composition pattern. However how urbanization influence on SOM is still unclear. In this study, we collected soil samples from highly urbanized area of Beijing, China and explore the quantity and quality variations of SOM by using fluorescence spectroscopy in combine with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC). The results shown that the soil physic-chemical properties were shaped by urbanization. Comparing to nature soil, moisture content, total organic carbon and total nitrogen in urban and rural soil significantly decreased. The fluorescence spectrum demonstrated that SOM quality was also altered by urbanization induced environmental changes. Five fluorescent compounds in SOM was identified by PARAFAC model and three of them was assigned to humic-like substances. The fluorescence intensity of humic-like substances in nature land was significantly higher than of rural and urban land, meanwhile microbial related substance accumulated in urban land in comparison with rural and nature land. The multivariate analyses further reveal the relationship between soil physic-chemical properties and SOM composition. These results suggest that urbanization could not only decrease the SOM quantity but also change the SOM composition. The SOM loss caused by urbanization was mainly consist of humic-like substance loss. Besides urbanization also stimulate the accumulation of microbial related substance in SOM which highlight the importance of microorganism is SOM dynamic.

# 1. Introduction

Soil organic matter (SOM) is the mixture of organic components in soil (Sparks, 1995; Lehmann and Kleber, 2015). SOM is the largest carbon pools on the earth, which is more than three times of the atmosphere and sea (Schmidt et al., 2011). Therefore, SOM is of great importance to global carbon dynamic. SOM also plays a fundamental role in maintaining soil ecosystem function (Young et al., 2008), and particularly well documented are nutrient cycles and soil structure (Chen et al., 2013a,b; Chen et al., 2014; Riggs et al., 2015). The importance of SOM stimulated increasing interests in exploration of its internal stability mechanism and the potential disturbance factors. Proposed by Schmidt et al. (2011), the stability of SOM is predominated by soil biochemical and environmental process rather than its original chemical composition. The land use change, such as composting, reclamation, urbanization, could dramatically alter the biochemical process of SOM and the dynamic therein (Post and Kwon, 2000; Beesley and Dickinson, 2011; Chen et al., 2014). These results highlighted the potential catastrophic effect of humans dominated land use change on SOM dynamics.

Urbanization refers to the conversion of nature or agricultural land use to urban land use and is one of the most irreversible human impact on the global biosphere (Xu et al., 2014). A growing body of literatures focus on the impact of urbanization on soil organic matter dynamics in terms of carbon storage, fluxes and compositional variations (Pouyat et al., 2002; Hui et al., 2017). In comparison with nature soil, urban soil is subject to severe anthropogenic activities such as the construction of impermeable layer on the soil surface, stirring, compacting, fertilization, anthropogenic pollutions and so on (Xu et al., 2014; Hui et al., 2017). These perturbations could decrease the botanical-origin organic matter input, which is the main source of SOM, affect the microbial activities and therefore cause the disruption of carbon storages in soil (Lorenz and Lal, 2015). However, there are some other researchers suggested that the long-term effect of urbanization on soil carbon dynamics is positive, particularly in public green spaces (Golubiewski, 2006). Pouyat et al. (2006) indicated that the urban green space (e.g. lawn, park) sequestrated more carbon than agricultural land and shrub land, even approximately equals to forest which was regarded as one of the most important carbon pools.

Despite the contradictory results and varying opinions on urbanization, we still lack in-depth evaluations on how SOM composition and soil biochemical processes are changed during urbanization. Many studies suggested that SOM quantity alone is not sufficient to understand the key SOM dynamic (Hoyos-Santillan et al., 2016), and the SOM quality, especially on molecular level, could greatly improve our understanding of underlying biochemical processes. For example, by using Py-GC/MS, Hoyos-Santillan et al. (2016) found organic matter decomposition in peatland is more related to its chemical composition rather than carbon content. Wilson and Xenopoulos (2009) found that agriculture land use greatly increase the microbial derived soil organic matter while decreased the complexity of SOM by using fluorescence spectroscopy. Therefore, to better understand the influence of urbanization on soil quality, study of SOM composition variations caused by urbanization, particular on molecular level, is still wanted.

Beijing, as a fast developing city with distinct phases of development in space was selected as a model in this study. To study the potential catastrophic effect of anthropogenic activities on SOM, soil samples from public green space and road lawn of nature, urban and rural lands were collected. Alkaline hot extraction method was employed since the alkaline buffer could dissolve oxygencontaining function groups in SOM and hot extraction enhance the extraction efficiency (Kleber et al., 2015). 3-dimension fluorescence spectroscopy, as known as excitation emission matrix (EEM), is a rapid, sensitive and reagent free method to study the organic matter composition. EEM in combined with parallel factor model (PARAFAC) were used to study the compositional alkaline extracted organic matters. The objectives of this study were to: 1) characterize the physical-chemical properties; 2) analyze the compositional variations of soil organic matters in urban, rural and nature land and 3) thus further explore the potential biochemical processes caused these variations by urbanization.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study area and sampling collection

Beijing City is the capital city of China, with its center at $1 1 6 ^ { \circ } 2 3 ^ { \prime }$ E and $3 9 ^ { \circ } 5 5 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { ~ N ~ } .$ . The climate of Beijing is generally described as monsoon-influenced humid continental climate, with mean annual temperature around $1 1 . 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and mean precipitation of $5 8 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . To the north and west of the city area is the Yanshan Mountains covered with forest and to its south and east is the main body of plain with primary land use type of tillage. In the past century, Beijing City has become the fastest urbanized area in China. 5 mains concentric rings expressways divided the urban area of Beijing City with the Forbidden City marked as the central.

Soil samples were collect in the summer of 2015 and rainy days were avoided to prevent the influence of weather. Total of 67 soil sampling sites were established in green belts, public lawn or neighbor green space long highway G6 (from north to south) and six ring expresses around Beijing city. At each sampling site, surface litters were removed and soil $( 0 { - } 1 0 \mathrm { c m } )$ were collected with a stainless steel shovel from five spots selected randomly in a $1 0 \times 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ square. The margin of every $1 0 \times 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ square was at least $5 0 \mathrm { m }$ away from nearest building. The mixed soil was packed in sampling paper bag and transported back to the lab for further analysis.

Among all these 67 samples, samples located within 5th ring expressway were marked as urban group $\left( \mathtt { n } = 2 6 \right)$ (Fig. 1a) since land in the range within 5th ring expressway had the longest urbanization history and intensive anthropogenic activities. To the northern of the urban group, samples were marked as nature group $( \mathtt { n } = 2 1$ ) given its main land use of forests (Fig. 1b). To the southern part, samples were marked as rural group $\mathtt { ( n = 2 0 ) }$ ) given its mains land use of agriculture (Fig. 1c). The location and grouping of all the soil-sampling sites were presented in Fig. 1.

# 2.2. Soil physical-chemical properties

Except the soil subsample for soil moisture analysis, all soil samples were dried at room temperature to constant weight prior to soil physical-chemical analysis. Soil bulk density was obtained by dividing the dry soil weight by $1 0 0 \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 }$ intact soil sample. Soil pH value was determined with precise pH meter in soil solution (water soil ratio of 1:2.5). To determine soil moisture value, a fresh subsample of soil (approximately $_ { 5 \mathrm { g } }$ ) was put in oven and dry to constant weight. The soil moisture value is the weight difference before and after drying and present by percentage.

Soil organic carbon (SOC) content was determined by the potassium dichromate volumetric method described by Chan et al. (2001). Soil total nitrogen (TN) was determined be semi-micro Kjeldahl method described by Fawcett (1954). Soil total phosphorus (TP) value was determined by sodium hydroxide melt method (Smith and Bain, 1982). The C:N ratio were calculated by divide TOC content by TN content, and N:P ratio was calculated by divide TN content by TP content (Tezuka, 1990).

# 2.3. Alkaline hot extraction of SOM

Alkaline extraction organic matter (AEOM) extracted according to the protocol described by Wright et al. (1996). $_ { 1 \mathrm { g } }$ soil was mixed with $8 \mathrm { m l } 5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ sodium citrate buffer $\mathrm { ( p H = } 8 . 0 $ ) in a $5 0 \mathrm { m l }$ tube and autoclaved at $1 2 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 60min. Immediately after autoclaving, the tube was centrifuged at $5 0 0 0 \times g$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ (at $4 ^ { \circ } C .$ ) and the supernatant was collected in a new tube. The extraction procedure was repeated until the reddish-brown color in supernatant was faded.

# 2.4. EEM data acquisition

To prevent the markedly inner filer effect (IFE) caused by typical reddish-brown color of AEOM, the sample solution was diluted 50 times before fluorescent analysis (Gu and Kenny, 2009; Kothawala et al., 2013). Varian Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrometer was used in EEM analysis. We obtained the EEM of AEOM by scan the emission spectra from $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $2 \mathrm { n m }$ interval and increasing excitation wavelength from $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to $5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ increment. The measurement was conducted at room temperature and the instrument bias was corrected with correction file provided by manufacture.

# 2.5. Fluorescent indices

Three fluorescent indices, including Fluorescent Index (FI), Humic Index (HIX) and Biological index (BIX), were calculated based on the EEM spectra, according to the methods described by Zsolnay et al. (1999), McKnight et al. (2001), Ohno (2002) and Huguet et al. (2009). FI was the ratio of emission intensity at wavelength of $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to than of $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at excitation wavelength of $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Eq. (1)). FI has been widely used in ocean, coastal, fluvial, lake ecosystem and wastewater treatment plant to identify the source of organic matter in water.

$$
\mathrm { F I } = \mathrm { I } _ { \mathrm { E m } 4 5 0 \mathrm { n m } / \mathrm { I } _ { E m 5 0 0 n m } } \ ( \ \lambda _ { e x 3 7 0 } \ )
$$

HIX was calculated by comparing the sum of fluorescence intensity at longer wavelength $( 4 3 8 - 4 8 0 \mathrm { n m } )$ to that the sum of fluorescence intensity at shorter wavelength $( 3 0 0 - 3 4 4 \mathrm { n m } )$ (Eq. (2)). HIX was used to quantify the relative degree of soil humification based on the theory that the decomposition and humification of soil organic matter will lead to a lower H:C ration and a red shift on EEM spectra.

BIX was use to decipher the present of autochthonous biological organic matter which was operationally calculated by dividing fluorescence intensity of $\beta$ fluorophore $( \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } = 3 1 0 / \lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } = 3 8 0 )$ by that of $\pmb { \mathscr { L } }$ fluorophore $( \lambda _ { \mathrm { e x } } = 4 3 0 / \lambda _ { \mathrm { e m } } = 3 8 0 )$ (Eq. (3)). The BIX was also referred as $\beta { : } \mathfrak { a }$ in some other studies (Wilson and Xenopoulos, 2009).

$$
\mathsf { B I X } = I _ { e m 3 8 0 } / \iota _ { e m 4 3 0 } \ ( \ \lambda _ { e x 3 1 0 } \ )
$$

# 2.6. PARAFAC model

PARAFAC model is a multi-way decomposition method that has been widely used in EEM data analysis (Stedmon and Bro, 2008; Murphy et al., 2013). By using PARAFAC model, the 3-dimension EEM dataset could be decomposed into trilinear terms and a residual array using Eq. (4). Each term in PARAFAC model represents a separated fluorescent substance.

$$
x _ { i j k } = \sum _ { f = 1 } ^ { F } a _ { i f } b _ { j f } c _ { k f } + \varepsilon _ { i j k } \quad i = 1 , . , I ; \ j = 1 , . , J ; \ k = 1 , . , K .
$$

In Eq. (4), ${ \bf X } _ { \mathrm { i j k } }$ presents the fluorescent intensity of sample i at emission wavelength j and excitation wavelength k. F is the number of components. $\mathsf { b } _ { \mathrm { f } }$ and $\mathbf { C } _ { \mathrm { f } }$ are two vectors represent the excitation and emission loading of component f, and vector $\mathbf { a } _ { \mathrm { i } }$ represents the scores of components f on sample i. The scores vector gives estimation of the relative concentration of different components in the samples. $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { i j k } }$ is the sum of squared residuals (SSR), which represents the variability that could not be explained by the model. Minimizing the SSR is of principal importance to validate the model. To perform the PARAFAC analysis, a 3-dimension dataset, that were excitation wavelength, emission wavelength and sample numbers, was built containing 69 EEM samples spectra. The modeling process and plot were conducted on Matlab 2012b platform and drEEM toolbox following the instruction of Murphy et al. (2013).

$$
\mathrm { H I X } = \sum I _ { e m 4 3 4 - 4 8 0 } \bigg / \big ( \sum I _ { e m 3 0 0 - 3 4 4 } + \sum I _ { e m 4 3 4 - 4 8 0 } \big ) \ ( \ \lambda _ { e x 2 5 4 } \ )
$$

# 2.7. Statistical analysis

The differences of soil physical-chemical properties and fluorescent characteristic was compared using one-way Anova with post-hoc Tukey HSD test. Relationship between soil physicalchemical properties and SOM fluorescent components were evaluated using Pearson's correlation analysis. The variables were ordered based on hierarchical clustering order in Pearson Correlation coefficient matrix described in R packages “corrplot”. Besides, soil physical and EEM fluorophore were analyzed with Principal Component analysis (PCA). Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin's (KMO) test of sampling adequacy was used to assess the feasibility of PCA. KMO ranges from 0 to 1 and should be well above 0.5 if variables are interdependent and a PCA is useful (Tabachnick and Fidell, 2007). These statistical analysis were all performed and plotted with R (version $3 . 4 . 4 \mathrm { R }$ Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria) with the ‘ggplot2’, ‘corrplot’, ‘vegan’, ‘factoextra’ and ‘psych’ packages.

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Soil physical-chemical properties

As shown in Fig. 2, most of the soil physical-chemical properties, including moisture content, pH, bulk density, SOC, TN, and TP showed significant differences among adjacent urban, rural and nature soil. To be more specific, the soil moisture, SOC and TN contents of nature soil were significantly higher $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ than those in urban and rural soil. Conversely, the pH, bulk density and TP in nature soil were significantly lower in comparison with urban and rural soil. All the soil physical-chemical properties showed no significant differences between urban and rural group (Fig. 1). The median of C:N ratio and N:P ratio in nature is significantly higher than urban and rural soil. These results indicated that the soil physical-chemical properties were substantially altered by urbanization in Beijing.

# 3.2. Fluorescent indices

In this study, FI ranged from 1.0 to 1.5, which was significantly lower than those reported in researches on dissolved organic matter (DOM) in aquatic ecosystem (Wickland et al., 2007; He et al., 2016) and approximately equal to DOM in terrestrial ecosystems (Gao et al., 2018). Even through the value of FI did not show significant difference among three groups $\left( \mathsf { P } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ (Fig. 3a), the median of HIX in nature soil was higher than that in rural and urban soil, while there's no significant difference between rural and urban soil (Fig. 3b) neither. BIX shown converse trend between nature and rural/urban soil. The median of BIX in soil of nature soil was lower than that in rural and urban soil. No significant difference was overserved for BIX between rural and urban soil either (Fig. 3c). Significantly higher HIX but lower BIX was observed in nature soil than in rural and urban, suggesting longer decomposition period, lower H:C ratio and higher humification degree of the SOM in nature soil.

# 4. Discussion

It is generally accepted that urbanization alter the soil carbon storage, however whether the urbanization increase or decrease the carbon storage is still an enigma (Golubiewski, 2006; Liu et al., 2011; Chen et al., 2013a,b; Williams et al., 2016). In this study, we found that urbanization, as an integration of various human activities (Liu et al., 2007), caused the loss of soil carbon in urban and rural land compared with nature land (Fig. 2d). Similar pictures were also presented by Chen et al. (2013a,b)\`s study in Guangzhou, Liu et al. (2011)\`s in Beijing and Wang et al. (2017)\`s in Xiamen of China. The loss of soil carbon storage caused by urbanization, which was considered as the most sever and irreversible land use change, could be explained by severe anthropogenic activities during urban construction, such as compacting, stirring, landfill and impermeable layer on the soil surface (Xu et al., 2014; Mills et al., 2017). These activities reduce the input of botanical litter inputs of SOM and hence affect the molecular composition of soil organic matter (Chen et al., 2014; Lorenz and Lal, 2015). However, the result is contradictory with some other studies in USA, which all confirmed the hypothesis that carbon storage of urban soil is larger than native land and agricultural land (Golubiewski, 2006; Pouyat et al., 2006). These conflicting results between studies in China and USA suggested that the urban public green apace soil in USA storage more carbon than in China. This scenario is likely caused, in part, by the different green space management strategy between these two countries and longer urbanization history in US compared to China.

In this term, soil organic carbon storage might be greatly effected by green space age and humification time after the urbanization of the land (Golubiewski, 2006; Hui et al., 2017). The significantly higher HIX in urban land in our study (Fig. 3b) also support that humification time in Beijing urban area is shorter and the organic matter quality is lower (Zsolnay et al., 1999). To sum up, the effect of urbanization on soil carbon storage is varied between different locations and soil management strategy. It is questionable to regard urban green space as a major carbon sink without further considering of the local soil management policy and condition.

# 4.1. Nutrient cycle

Despite the soil organic carbon storage, nutrient cycle in urban soil was also altered by urbanization induced environmental changes. In this study, the various distributions of C, N and P in nature, urban and rural land indicated the urbanization-induced environmental change caused the loss of C and N and retention of P (Fig. 2). The differences in C, N and P accumulation behavior in soil were more evident when comparing their correlations between different groups (Fig. S1). The decoupling of P cycle from C and N cycle is likely caused by urbanization induced disruption of soil microbial activity, which is also in consistent with accumulation of microbial related FC5 (Fig. 5). Despite the fact that the C, N and P cycles are biologically coupled from molecular scale to global scales (Delgado-Baquerizo et al., 2013), the fates of C, N and P in soil environment are quite different. The C and N in soil mainly comes from botanical origin litter decomposition (Wang et al., 2015a,b). Given its close relationship with biological mineralization and immobilization, such as photosynthesis, humification, microbial mineralization and nitrogen fixation, soil C and N contents are susceptible to the change of microbial activity. By contrast, the content of $\mathrm { P }$ in soil is more rely on physical process as rock weathering and less rely on decomposition of organic matters (Finzi et al., 2011; Mooshammer et al., 2017). Severe environmental perturbation could result in disruption of soil microbial activities and hence imbalance of C, N and P cycle in soil. In combine with the soil microbial activity decline, the SOM input in urban land is considered of low quantity and quality, which lead to low C and N availability (Chen et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2015a,b). The decoupling tendency of soil nutrient cycle has been widely observed when environment subject to the perturbation of temperature (Mooshammer et al., 2017), aridity (Jiao et al., 2016) and climate change (Yuan and Chen, 2015). However, to our knowledge, it is the first time the urbanization induced nutrient cycle decoupling was found.

# 4.2. SOM quality

Soil organic matter is the complex mixture of progressively decomposing organic components (Cotrufo et al., 2015; Lehmann and Kleber, 2015). Over the last decade, a growing body of literature has unraveled the importance of SOM quality on sustaining soil ecosystem function, such as harboring carbon fluxes (HoyosSantillan et al., 2016), maintaining nutrient cycle (Castellano et al., 2015) and primary production (Shrestha et al., 2015). However, our knowledge of SOM composition variation under the effect of urbanization is scarce. In the present study, we found the SOM composition was dominated by humic-like substances in Beijing. Among all the five identified components, three components were identified as humic substance (FC1, FC2 and FC4) (Fig. 4 and Table 1) of the total fluorophores. The concentration and ratio of humic substances in the given sample varied depending greatly on its location alone the nature-urban gradient (Fig. 5a, b, d).

The humic substance, which is characterized by its large molecular masses, aromatic/carboxylate-rich structure and low C:H ratio, was once considered as secondary synthesized products by soil microbial, although its explicit chemical structural is unclear (Stevenson, 1994; Sparks, 1995; Pickett et al., 2001; Lehmann and Kleber, 2015). Given its recalcitrance trait and resistant to decomposer, humic substance was inherently stable and insensitive to anthropogenic perturbations (Stevenson, 1994; Schmidt et al., 2011). However, in the present study, the significant drop in humic-like substance alone nature-urban gradient in FC1 FC2 and FC4 level suggested that urbanization greatly altered the dynamic of humic substance. Besides, the nexus between the decline of SOC and TN content and the decline of humic substance content (FC1, FC2 and FC4) is fairly close based on their high correlation coefficient and similar PC1 loading (Figs. 6 and 7). This result hinted that the loss of SOM caused by urbanization-induces environment change was mainly due to the loss of humic substances. In other words, the humic substance is more vulnerable to anthropogenic activities than previously considered (Stevenson, 1994). This result is likely explained by soil continuum model (SCM) proposed by Lehmann and Kleber (2015), which depicted that the decomposition of soil organic matter is a progressive one-way process. The humic substance (sometimes as synonymous of SOM) (Sparks, 1995) can be considered as “mixture of identifiable compounds” that are progressively decomposing towards smaller size and higher bioavailability rather than secondary synthesis metabolite. In this regard, the disruption of soil organic matter inputs caused by urbanization induced environmental change could lead to the major decline of certain components, which has been formerly recognized as humic substances, of the SOM.

Base on its fingerprint characteristic (Fig. 4), FC3 was characterized as the terrestrial humic-like substance by Osburn et al. (2011) as well (Murphy et al., 2014). However, its obviously excitation loading below $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and emission loading at $4 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ showing great similarity to oxidized quinones (Cory and Mcknight, 2005), which is also the main functional group of the humic substances (Sparks, 1995). The present of quinones in soil has a close relationship with soil microbes and had been proven to be able to service as an electron shuttle for extracellular respiration (Newman and Kolter, 2000; Kang and Choi, 2008). The relatively higher median of oxidized quinones structure in urban land (Fig. 5c), in urban and rural land likely hinted the possible anthropogenic activity induced accumulation of the microbial derived quinones served as the response to condensed soil structure in urban land (Fig. 2b and c).

Despite the predominance of humic-like substances in SOM fluorescence, a trend of protein substance accumulation in urban land was also unraveled (Fig. 5e). Previous studies documented that protein components in SOM has a close relationship with biodegradable organic matter, due to it is microbial metabolites (Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003, 2004). When liable organic matter (e.g. carbohydrate) in soil is deficient, microorganisms tend to utilize recalcitrant organic matter (e.g. lignin lipid) as carbon source (Liu et al., 2017). During this process, microbial metabolites such as peptide generally are accumulated as a response to starvation (Wang et al., 2015a,b). In present study, the median of FC5 content in urban land is significantly higher than that in rural and natural land $\left( \mathsf { P } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ , indicating the drop of liable organic matter in SOM caused by urbanization and therefore stimulated microbial activities. In this regard, the accumulation of tyrosine-like protein substance in urban land further proved the hypothesis that urbanization alter the regional carbon dynamic.

Based on all the analysis above, we are programmed to receive more information from PCA of the relationship between SOM fluorescent composition and soil physical-chemical properties. Generally speaking, better soil quality means higher soil carbon content, nutrient content, and lower soil density which give more pore space between soil particles (Sparks, 1995). SOM PC1\`s positive loading from SOC, TN and negative loading from soil density, pH and TP (Fig. 6) implied that soil sample with higher PC1 scores could be considered of better soil quality. Similarly, SOM PC2 could be regarded as a proxy of autochthonous with positive loading contributed from FC5 and BIX and negative loading contributed from HIX. The significant positive correlation between pH and FC5 further confirmed close the relationship between autochthonous SOM.

Taken together, four quadrants with distinct physical-chemical properties and fluorescent characteristics defined by PCA model (Fig. 6) typified the different sample from urban, rural and nature. The cluster of nature soils on the right half of the biplot indicated the soil quality in nature is better than that in rural and urban land in Beijing. The positive loading of urban soil and negative loading of rural suggested the soil in urban with more autochthonous organic matters and less humic degrees although the SOM quality and other general soil properties.

# 5. Conclusion and implication

Urbanization-induced environmental changes greatly altered the regional SOM quantity and quality. By using EEM-PARAFACs, SOM in Beijing can be divided into five components and three of them were assigned to humic-like substances. Humic-like substance, as the predominant components of SOM, are more vulnerable to urbanization-induced environmental changes than previously considered, which further verified the progressive decomposing theory of SOM. The accumulation of protein-like substances in urban land indicating the disruption of liable fraction in SOM and the microbial activities change induced by that.

The correlation analysis between soil physical-chemical properties and SOM fluorophore suggested that the decline caused by urbanization is mainly consist of the loss of humic substances. In comparison with rural, urban soil contained more autochthonous organic matters, although the difference of most soil properties between these two groups is insignificant.

This study provided insights into the effect anthropogenic activities on urban soil ecosystem. Urbanization could not only decrease the SOM quantity decoupled element cycling, but also change the SOM quality. The humic-like substance, which was found as the main components of soil organic matter, was more vulnerable to anthropogenic activities than previously considered. Therefore, the concept of humic substance, to some extent, is inaccurate to describe the bio-chemistry reaction in soil. EEMPARAFAC in combined with other technology (such as mass spectrum based analysis or nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)) would greatly help us understand the accurate SOM molecular structure and function. The accumulation of tyrosine-like protein substance, on the other hand, hint the importance of microorganism is SOM dynamic. The further study of function and construction of soil microorganism during urbanization would answer the question.
