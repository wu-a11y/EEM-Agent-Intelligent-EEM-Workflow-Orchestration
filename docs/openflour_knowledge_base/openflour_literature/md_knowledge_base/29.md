# INTRODUCTION

Coastal rivers and estuaries transport and transform dissolved and particulate materials from terrestrial to marine environments. Recent work indicates that organic matter (OM) cycling in rivers and estuaries responds to regional changes in land use and climate.1,2 The characteristics of dissolved (DOM) and particulate (POM) organic matter are often quite different from one another in river-dominated estuary systems. The distinction between these OM pools typically is by filtration. Often, fresher material dominates the POM pool and more degraded, recalcitrant material comprises the DOM pool, with both pools containing large fractions of humic substances.3,4 Transfer of OM between these pools occurs in the water column and sediments, and possibly through photoexposure of particles to sunlight, but these process are not well-known in estuaries.5−9 Simultaneous study of both POM and DOM composition in estuaries is rare in the literature but can reveal very dynamic biogeochemical cycling of terrestrial and autochthonous sources within and between these carbon pools.10

Fluorescence spectroscopy has been used to characterize DOM and the base-extractable organic matter (BEOM) from soil/sedimentary organic matter pools from most aquatic environments.11−14 By measuring and concatenating the emission spectra of OM at several excitation wavelengths, an excitation−emission matrix (EEM) of fluorescence can be created, which contains qualitative information about the OM in a sample. Early comparisons of extracted humic substances to whole river, estuary, and seawater revealed several EEM peaks, originally designated A and C (terrestrial humic), T (protein resembling the amino acid tryptophan), and M (microbial humic).12 In the past 10 years, EEMs have been combined with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) to characterize OM sources.15,16 This methodology provides a statistical means to analyze the spectral components changing in a sample and use this information to understand the sources and transformations of OM in many environments.

These extraction and fluorescence techniques have not been used to examine water column POM within a river−estuary continuum, even though POM likely contains fluorescent components.17 Moreover, POM photodissolution produces chromophoric DOM (CDOM), indicating that fluorescent molecules in the DOM pool partly have their origin in the

POM pool.8,9 River POM tends to be enriched in labile substrates such as amino acids relative to DOM, a signal which is maintained during mixing in estuaries as lingering terrestrial signals are diluted and commingle with signals from in situ primary production.6,18,19 Thus, compositional differences between POM and DOM could be assayed by fluorescence, and this information should be useful to understand phase transfer between POM and DOM pools as well as estuarine water quality.20,21

The aim of this study was to examine POM and DOM quality in the Neuse River Estuary (NRE), a river-dominated estuarine system located in the coastal plain of North Carolina, USA, through a combined PARAFAC model. As a proof of concept for using the combined POM−DOM PARAFAC model, we determined the change to OM quality in the NRE after passage of a major tropical cyclone, Hurricane Irene (August, 2011), which was expected to increase the loading of terrestrial OM throughout the estuary. The POM−DOM PARAFAC model demonstrated this overall change in OM quality in the NRE. Also, the model provided insight into the watershed contribution of organic nitrogen to the NRE, showed the relative importance of phase transfer of organic matter from POM to DOM pools in the water column and sediments, and indicated OM degradation by sunlight exposure.

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

Surface water samples $\left( N = 9 4 \right)$ from the Neuse River Basin (NRB, $N = 3 9$ ) and the NRE $( N = 5 5 )$ were collected during August 24−September 17, 2011, before and after passage of Hurricane Irene on August 27, 2011, which made landfall at Cape Lookout, NC, and tracked northerly across Pamlico Sound. Between 17 and $3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ of total rainfall fell across the basin, with higher amounts localized on the upper part of the NRE near New Bern, NC.22 Only results from the estuary proper before and after passage of Hurricane Irene are reported in this study (Supporting Information Table S1), though all 188 samples were used in fluorescence modeling. Samples (no replicates) were collected midchannel in the estuary as part of the Neuse River Estuary Modeling and Monitoring Program, ModMon (Figure S1), and included measurements of temperature, salinity, turbidity, and in situ chlorophyll a (Chl a) fluorescence, using a calibrated Yellow Springs Instrument 6600 multiparameter sonde. In situ Chl a fluorescence was normalized to actual Chl a concentrations by calibration against a certified Chl a standard (DHI, Hørsholm, Denmark).23,24 POM was collected separately on $2 \ : 0 . 7 – \mu \mathrm { m }$ porosity, precombusted glass fiber filters ( $2 5 \mathrm { - m m }$ diameter; Whatman GF/F), and the filtrate was used for DOM. On one filter, particulate organic carbon (POC) and particulate nitrogen (PN) were measured using a Perkin-Elmer 2400 Series II elemental analyzer, after vapor acidification to remove carbonates. On a separate filter, POM fluorescent material was extracted into $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ N ~ N a O H }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { { h } }$ in the dark at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ following a procedure for estuarine soil and sediment.14 After neutralization with HCl of the BEOM to a $\mathrm { p H }$ near that of the original sample (which always was ${ > } 6$ thus avoiding precipitation of humic acids), the resultant solution was filtered through $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ polyethersulfone filters (Millipore Sterivex), and then absorbance and fluorescence were measured. In May 2012, pore water DOM from surface sediment $\left( 0 - 5 \ \mathrm { c m } \right)$ at station (Sta.) NR70 was isolated by centrifugation $\left( 3 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m } , \right.$ , $1 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ ) and filtered to $0 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ . BEOM was extracted from the remaining wet sediment using methods described for POM. Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) was quantified by wet chemical oxidation on $2 { \cdot } \mathrm { m L }$ sample volumes.25 Dissolved organic nitrogen (DON) was calculated as the difference between total dissolved N (TDN) and total dissolved inorganic N (DIN: sum of nitrite, nitrate, and ammonium) measured on a Lachat Quick-chem 8000 auto analyzer (Lachat, Milwaukee, WI, USA).

Absorbance spectra of DOM and POM solutions were measured from 200 to $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { \ n m } }$ on a Varian Cary 300UV spectrophotometer in $_ \mathrm { 1 - c m }$ quartz cells against air. Milli- $\mathrm { Q }$ water or the $0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ N ~ N a O H }$ extractant solution for POM (and sediment BEOM) were measured separately and used as a blanks. Blank-corrected absorbance values were converted to Napierian absorption coefficients, ${ a } _ { ( \lambda ) } ,$ in units of $\mathbf { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , and the amount of CDOM was quantified at 250 (POM: $a _ { 2 5 0 } \mathrm { p }$ ; DOM: $a _ { 2 5 0 } \mathrm { d } )$ ). Slope ratios of POM and DOM absorption $( S _ { \mathrm { { R P } } } , S _ { \mathrm { { R } } } \mathbf { d } ,$ respectively) were calculated to assess molecular weight changes of OM.26

Fluorescence spectra were acquired on a Varian Eclipse spectrofluorometer. Excitation $( \mathrm { e x } )$ wavelengths were sampled from 240 to 450 at $_ { 5 - \mathrm { n m } }$ intervals; emission $\left( \mathrm { e m } \right)$ wavelengths were sampled every $2 \ \mathrm { n m }$ from 300 to $6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . For POM samples, corrections were made for volume of water filtered, volume of extractant used, and for any dilution that was performed (see Supporting Information). For DOM, corrections were made for dilution if necessary. Appropriate instrument excitation and emission corrections were applied and all fluorescence measurements were corrected for innerfiltering effects before being calibrated against the Raman signal of the instrument and standardized in quinine sulfate units (ppb QSU).27 A total of 188 EEMs $\mathrm { \Delta } ^ { \prime } \mathrm { N R B } = 7 8 .$ , $\mathrm { N R E } = 1 1 0 _ {  }$ each equally split between POM and DOM samples collected before and after the storm) were modeled with PARAFAC using the DOMFluor toolbox for Matlab.27 Due to the wide range in fluorescence intensity of POM and DOM samples, all EEMs were normalized to their individual maxima prior to PARAFAC modeling. 28

Photoexposure Experiment. Triplicate samples of unfiltered Neuse River water (Sta. NR0 from March 2012) that was not used in the POM−DOM PARAFAC modeling were placed in quartz bottles and exposed to solar radiation for 3 days.29 The bottles were shaken periodically to resuspend particles. Afterward, the samples were filtered and processed as described above. The POM−DOM PARAFAC model was then applied to these data to test (1) the effect of sunlight on POM and DOM fluorescence properties of Neuse River water, and (2) the application of the POM−DOM PARAFAC approach to modeling EEMs in separate data sets. Details of the model application are provided in the Supporting Information.

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

EEM Characteristics of POM and DOM. Sample EEM plots for stations NR0 (river) and NR180 (estuary) prior to Hurricane Irene exhibited characteristics common to many coastal rivers and estuaries (Figure 1). Overall, the fluorescence intensity in the neutralized base-extracts of POM was much less than the corresponding DOM after correction for the volume of water filtered. Similarly, OC concentrations of the extracted POM were $7 - 2 4 \%$ of bulk POC concentrations (Table S1). Before passage of Hurricane Irene, the mean $( \pm 1 \sigma )$ $a _ { 2 5 0 }$ of the POM solutions ( $\mathrm { \Delta } a _ { 2 5 0 } \mathrm { p }$ in Table S1), when corrected for volume filtered, was $0 . 9 6 \pm 0 . 8 1 \mathrm { \ m } ^ { - 1 } .$ , roughly 40 times less than the mean $a _ { 2 5 0 } \mathrm { d }$ (for DOM), $4 2 . 3 8 \pm 1 4 . 4 2 \mathrm { { m } ^ { - 1 } }$ . Nine days after the storm, $a _ { 2 5 0 } \mathrm { d }$ was $2 0 5 . 7 5 \pm 1 3 . 4 6 \ \mathrm { m ^ { - 1 } }$ , about 9 times greater than $a _ { 2 5 0 } \mathrm { p }$ $\left( 2 2 . 9 3 \pm 1 . 2 8 \mathrm { \ m } ^ { - 1 } \right)$ in the regions of the NRE that were freshwater (Stas. NR0-NR50, unpaired $t$ test, Welchcorrected: $p < 0 . 0 0 1$ , $N = 3 ,$ , $\mathrm { d f } = 4$ ).

Previously identified peak regions of EEM fluorescence are indicated in Figure 1 which includes their probable origins.12,13 The DOM EEMs show a common feature in which the major fluorescence in the peak A region elongates into a plateau situated between the peaks M and C. The DOM EEMs exhibited little peak T fluorescence, which generally is assigned to tryptophan fluorescence, and indicative of recent biological activity. Other than intensity, little change was apparent in the DOM between the freshwater head of the NRE (Sta. NR0, salinity $= 0 . 0 8$ ) and its more saline mouth at the entrance of Pamlico Sound (Sta. NR180, salinity $= 2 5 . 6 3$ ; Figure 1). By contrast, the POM EEMs showed similar intensities of fluorescence between Stas. NR0 and NR180, yet slightly different patterns. Similar to DOM, the POM EEM for Sta. NR0 showed an elongation of the peak A to peak C region and weak peak T fluorescence. However, this elongation was lacking in the POM EEM from Sta. 180, which instead showed three discrete peaks nearly centered on the ranges shown for peaks A, $\mathrm { C } ,$ and T (Figure 1). Relative to NR0 POM, the fluorescence intensity of NR180 POM was greater at each of these peak regions. The EEM pattern exhibited by the POM at NR180 strongly resembled the distinct peaks in EEMs produced in DOM from phytoplankton cultures subjected to microbial degradation.30

EEM peak ratios in the POM (T:A, M:C) tracked closely with Chl a concentrations in the NRE (Figure S3). The mean carbon to nitrogen (C:N) ratio of POM in the NRE before Irene, $7 . 6 \pm 0 . 3$ , was not significantly different after Irene, $7 . 7 \pm$ 0.3 (paired t test on log transformed values: $p = 0 . 5 8 4$ , $N = 1 1$ $\mathrm { d f } = 1 0 $ ; Table S1). POM carbon stable isotope values $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } )$ in the NRE increase with salinity, and have indicated a shift from allochthonous to autochthonous sources of OM in this poorly flushed estuary.31 Together, these results suggest a distinct change in POM quality from the riverine to saline portion of the NRE and not just the dilution of a riverine signal. Results from widely different systems (culture work in seawater, wastewater) have showed the production of fluorescent molecules (amino sugars, exopolymeric substances) as components of cellular material from aquatic microbes.30,32,33

Our results agree with the observation from those studies that autochthonous OM can exhibit peaks (A and C) that have traditionally been considered as originating from allochthonous and terrestrial sources (Figure 1).

Analysis of POM−DOM PARAFAC Components. Our combined POM−DOM model produced spectral components of terrestrial and autochthonous OM with $\mathrm { e x / e m }$ maxima similar to those of PARAFAC components modeled for a variety of riverine and estuarine environments (Table 1; Figure 2). For comparison to previous PARAFAC models, the EEMs of each component are shown along with the excitation and emission loadings for each component and the split-half validation. Loadings are supplied in the Supporting Information. C1 was similar to a terrestrial component from the estuarine North Sea−Baltic Sea transition zone, and closely resembled fulvic acids, though its emission maximum also ranged between standard humic acids from the Suwannee River $( 4 8 \dot { 5 } \ \mathrm { n m } )$ and from soil $\left. 5 5 0 \mathrm { n m } \right.$ .34−36 C2 and C3 were close together with respect to their $\operatorname { e x } / \operatorname { e m }$ maxima and both components were similar to DOM from a eutrophic estuary.37 C2 has not been identified previously, but C3 in our model matched closely with maxima of C3 (ex ${ < 2 5 0 / 3 1 5 }$ , em 440) from a three-component PARAFAC model of International Humic Substances Society (IHSS) terrestrial humic acid standards.15 C4 appears ubiquitous in agriculturally influenced streams, near-shore coastal waters, eutrophic lakes and estuaries, and wastewater. $^ { 3 3 , 3 7 - 4 2 }$ Its origin also is unknown but was theorized to be microbial processing of terrestrial DOM, yet a common theme among all of these natural waters is nutrient enrichment and eutrophication.43 C5 was the ubiquitous “protein” peak bearing the spectral features of the amino acid tryptophan but we also note that lignin-derived phenols fluoresce readily at ex 280/em 340.12,44 C6 had $\operatorname { e x } / \operatorname { e m }$ maxima $\left( 3 5 0 / 4 4 0 ~ \mathrm { n m } \right)$ strongly resembling possible molecular components of aquatic fulvic acids as well as constituents of tannins (gallic acid).11,45

The amounts of each component, measured as $\%$ of the total fluorescence maxima for the six components $( \% F _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } )$ varied substantially between OM pools (Table 1). C1 ranged between 1 and $3 0 \%$ of POM yet stayed relatively consistent in DOM $( 1 2 - 1 4 \% )$ . C2 was less present in POM than in DOM, and $\%$ $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ C2 was greatest in the DOM. C3 varied widely in both OM pools and at times was not modeled in DOM. C4 and C5 dominated the POM pool. In both pools, C6 comprised relatively small fractions $\left( { < } 1 0 \% \right)$ .

Neuse River DOM (NR0) showed marked increases in $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ for component 1 (C1d), and for C3d−C6d after the storm. Interestingly, $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ C2d decreased when overall the amount of DOC at Sta. NR0 had tripled (Table 2). This indicated that C2 was not a marker for the watershed OM that was flushed into the NRE after the storm. At the estuary mouth (Sta. NR180),

C1 and C3 only were both present in DOM after the storm, and along with large increases to C6, likely corresponded to the freshwater flux down the estuary (Table 2). The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ values for POM responded differently; at Sta. NR0, component 6 (C6p) decreased, but all other components increased. At Sta. NR180, POM also showed an increase in C1p and C3p, but decreases in $\mathrm { C 4 p }$ and C5p, and an absence of $\mathtt { C 2 p }$ and C6p. These results indicate substantial changes to OM quality in the NRE occurred from the freshwater pulse that were not evident from examining changes in salinity or bulk C and N measurements of POM and DOM (Table 2). To investigate these fluorescence results further, we examined the distribution of the PARAFAC components in POM and DOM with respect to other biogeochemical properties of the NRE and with respect to flux of material into the estuary after Hurricane Irene in August 2011. Also, we examined the changes to PARAFAC components of POM and DOM in unfiltered Neuse River water after 3 days of sunlight exposure.

Relation of PARAFAC Components to Neuse River Water Quality. Eutrophication is a lingering problem in the NRE.2 Primary production is dominated by phytoplankton in this microtidal estuary, which lacks substantial saltwater marshes that could supply autochthonous $\mathrm { O M } ^ { 4 6 }$ A principle components analysis (PCA) was conducted on $\% F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of fluorescence components and water quality data before (preIrene) and after (post-Irene) passage of Hurricane Irene across the NRB (Figure 3; Table 2). Trends between OM quality, measured by fluorescence, and water quality parameters in our data set were evaluated by the clustering of loadings for the POM and DOM PARAFAC components with loadings for water quality parameters.

Prior to Hurricane Irene, along PC1 (which accounted for $6 9 . 7 8 \%$ of the variance) samples were discriminated by salinity, which had a negative linear relationship with PC1 $( r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 9 6 ; p$ $< 0 . 0 1$ ; $N = 1 1$ ). This is sensible given the low riverine flow at Ft. Barnwell in late August 2011 $\bar { ( 2 4 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 } }$ ; Table 1). Positive loadings on PC1 should therefore indicate freshwater sources. Components 1, 3, and 6 all had positive loadings and contributed roughly equally to PC1. The inverse relationship to salinity indicated these components were terrestrial markers for DOM. PC2 explained $2 0 . 8 5 \%$ of the variance in the data set, with roughly equal contributions from both PN and POC. NR0 had a negative score on PC2 indicating its relatively lower POC and PN concentrations compared to other stations in the NRE.

The water residence time in the NRE averages 52 days annually, can increase to several months at low discharge, and therefore can enable substantial primary production and algal biomass accumulation in the upper estuary above Sta. NR100 (Figure S1).23 Along PC1, negative loadings for C2d, C4d, $\mathrm { C 4 p } .$ , and C5d grouped with salinity (Table S2), indicating they are becoming prominent during estuarine transport. This could be due to autochthonous production, but also degradation, sorption/desorption reactions, and/or advective release from surface sediment pore water. These components clustered opposite Chl a, however, and therefore do not appear related to recent primary production of autochthonous OM. C5p was a marker for recent autochthonous POM, however, as evidenced by its positive loadings on PC1 and PC2, which was similar to Chl a and $a _ { 2 5 0 } \mathrm { p }$ . The score for NR20, which had the highest Chl a concentration during that time, was similar to these loadings (Figure 3; Table 1). Moreover, only $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ C5p had significant correlations with Chl a, POC, and PN concentrations among the six fluorescent components (Table S2). Given the history of the NRE system in terms of nutrient sensitivity, it is not surprising this system’s OM shows a strong mixing of autochthonous sources with terrestrial sources delivered by the watershed during low discharge.2,31

Overall, DON and DOC decreased with salinity, as did $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ for the DOM components (Table S1). Linear regression showed a strong relationship between DON and $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ C6d $\scriptstyle ( r ^ { 2 } =$ 0.85; $p < 0 . 0 5$ ). C:N ratios calculated for DOM from Table 1 were 17−25, values mostly representing terrestrial and soil OM. These results indicate that most of the DON in the NRE originated from the watershed, rather than from in situ production.

Prior work on DOM in estuaries suggested that C4 was related to eutrophication, or at least prominent in nutrientimpacted systems (Table 1). However, this component does not persist in the DOM pool in coastal waters.39,47 Linear regression showed that $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } } \mathrm { C 4 d }$ decreased as a function of $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ $\mathrm { C 4 p }$ in the estuary before the storm ${ r ^ { 2 } = 0 . 4 5 }$ , $p = 0 . 0 2$ , $N =$ 11, $\operatorname { d f } = 9$ ; Table S1). The ratio of ${ \mathrm { C 4 p : C 4 d } }$ increased with salinity, a trend which was comparatively similar to the increase in POM $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values downstream in the estuary (Figure 4).31 This enrichment in POM could be explained by C4 in autochthonous DOM from the NRB sorbing onto POM in the estuary, giving the appearance of its production in the NRE. High molecular weight phytoplankton-derived OM contains amino sugars (especially galactosamine), which, when incubated in seawater, have produced EEM fluorescence similar to C4; these compounds could aggregate in HMW DOM and particles in estuaries, potentially through the formation of Schiff base derivatives.7,11,49 However, in culture work, the magnitude of C4-type fluorescence in DOM produced by phytoplankton cultures (ca. 0.04 QSU $\mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ ) suggest that in natural systems this sorption is small.29

A more likely possibility is that C4 accumulates in POM relative to DOM from autochthonous production in the NRE and is transferred into the sediment pore water DOM pool after deposition. The amount of fluorescence in POM was often $^ { 1 0 - }$ 100 times less than that in DOM (Table S1), which argues against the phase transfer of OM in the water column of the NRE. The accumulation of particles in the surface sediments is a major pathway to pore water DOM formation through phase transfer during diagenesis and has been implicated in the interpretation of pore water DOM fluorescence.50 At Sta. NR70, for example, pre-Irene POM fluorescence was dominated by C4 and C5 (Table S1). Surface sediment pore water DOM at Sta. NR70 also was dominated by C4 and C5, whereas BEOM extracted from the corresponding sediment had a stronger riverine signature (Figure 5). Uncorrected matrix correlation (UMC) values confirmed these similarities (Table S4). Further, the intensity of NR70 pore water DOM fluorescence was four times greater than NR70 DOM. These results suggested that C4 fluorescence properties of POM are transferred into the fluorescent DOM pool in estuarine and coastal sediment pore waters during POM diagenesis.20,50 If compounds that exhibit C4 fluorescence are labile in surface sediment pore water DOM, this could contribute to the observations that C4 does not persist in DOM in the coastal ocean away from land.39,47,50

The loading for Chl a did not cluster with loadings for PARAFAC component fractions after the storm (Figure 3), but possibly indicates movement of phytoplankton POM downstream as discharge increased rapidly and remained about 3-fold higher than prestorm flow, even 10 days after the storm $\left( 1 3 7 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } \ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 } \right.$ Table 2). The large flux of freshwater is evident in the depressed salinity values and increases in DOC and DON concentrations as well $a _ { 2 5 0 } \mathrm { d }$ and $a _ { 2 5 0 \mathrm { P } }$ values, compared to prestorm conditions (Table 2). The increased riverine flow in the NRE correspondingly reduced residence time, which may have reduced the coupling between POM and DOM fluorescence properties and autochthonous signals, likely by flushing phytoplankton out of the system faster than they could grow.48 Notably, the ${ \mathrm { C 4 p : C 4 d } }$ ratio decreased after the storm; if phytoplankton are indeed the source of $\mathrm { C 4 p }$ to the estuary, their flushing could explain this switch from the relative enrichment in $\mathrm { C 4 p }$ observed before the storm. Implications for Estuaries. Base-extraction of fluorescent POM is a rapid approach for simultaneously evaluating POM and DOM in estuarine waters and is easily extendable to other freshwater and marine environments. With proper calibration of fluorometric instruments and appropriate corrections for volume of sample filtered and dilutions, POM fluorescence (even at low concentrations as shown for the NRE) can be routine as for DOM fluorescence measurements, potentially expanding the analytical window of the fluorescence technique to the whole of OM cycling in natural waters. Two important caveats when using fluorescence to investigate OM cycling are that (1) fluorescence measures a small fraction of total organic matter pool (Table S1), and (2) we should not expect a “global” PARAFAC model for OM in all aquatic systems.42

Moreover, further testing of fused PARAFAC models of POM and DOM against separate models is warranted. Our approach and results should be interpreted within the context of additional environmental data for any particular system. Future applications of this approach could utilize behavior of components C4 and C6 to study POM dynamics in estuaries, perhaps with respect to contaminant transport and C and N cycling, in addition to pore water DOM release from sediments. Historically, the NRE is nitrogen sensitive and questions remain as to the role of DON in the system.2 The ex/em maxima of C6 corresponded to EEM features recently utilized to estimate contributions of wastewater DOM to stream ecosystems.55 The dynamics of these signals, and their correspondence with water quality parameters, strongly suggest POM fluorescence data can be integrated with DOM fluorescence data into a comprehensive model of estuarine water quality.
