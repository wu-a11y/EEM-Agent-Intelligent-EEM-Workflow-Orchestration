# Stemfow dissolved organic matter in mixed temperate forests: temporal and interspecifc variation of optical indices and development of a stemfow‑specifc PARAFAC model

Robyn C. O’Halloran $\textcircled{1}$ · Jennifer J. Guerard $\textcircled { 1 0 }$ · Delphis F. Levia $\textcircled { \scriptsize { 1 } }$

Received: 15 November 2023 / Accepted: 13 June 2024 / Published online: 21 June 2024   
$^ ©$ The Author(s) 2024

Abstract Stemfow is a conduit for the transport of canopy-derived dissolved organic matter (DOM) to the forest foor. This study examined the character of stemfow DOM for four tree species over four phenophases (leafess, emergence, leafed, and senescence for deciduous species and leafed-winter, emergence, leafed- spring/summer, and senescence for coniferous species) occurring in temperate forests; namely, Betula lenta L. (sweet birch), Fagus grandifolia Ehrh. (American beech), Liriodendron tulipifera L. (yellow poplar), and Pinus rigida Mill. (pitch pine). American beech exhibited the lowest average specifc UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $( \mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } )$ values, while yellow poplar displayed the highest values. $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were largest in senescence and smallest in emergence. The spectral slope ratio was lower for pitch pine than the deciduous tree species. Humifcation index (HIX) values decreased across all species during the emergence phenophase. The developed and validated stemfow-specifc four-component parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) model demonstrated the combined infuence of interspecifc and temporal fuctuations on the composition of humic and proteinlike substances within stemfow. By separating and examining stemfow DOM independent of throughfall, our study provides fresh insights into the spatiotemporal dynamics of stemfow inputs to near-trunk soils that may inform hot spots and hot moments theories.

# Introduction

Throughfall and stemfow are important pathways for the transfer of water, solutes, and particulates to the forest foor (Parker 1983; Levia et al. 2011, 2023; Levia and Germer 2015; Siegert et  al. 2017; Chen et al. 2019; Cayuela et al. 2019). Similar to throughfall (McDowell et  al. 2020), stemfow mediates the transfer of dissolved organic matter (DOM) from tree canopies to the forest foor, helping to shape biogeochemical cycling in wooded ecosystems (e.g., Levia et al. 2012; Ryan et al. 2022). Due to the high aqueous solubility and mobility of DOM, it is leached and transported from the foliage, bark, and twigs via throughfall and stemfow, thereby enriching the surrounding ecosystem (Van Stan et  al. 2017; McDowell et  al. 2020). As a spatially localized input (e.g., Carlyle-Moses et al. 2018; Llorens et al. 2022), stemfow can impact soil moisture levels, groundwater recharge, as well as the composition and functioning of the soil microbial community (Levia and Germer 2015).

Characterization of absorbance spectral indices have been reported for throughfall and stemfow of a variety of deciduous and coniferous species, including Acer saccharum Marsh. (sugar maple), Betula alleghaniensis Britt. (yellow birch), Fagus grandifolia Ehrh. (American beech), Liriodendron tulipifera L. (yellow poplar), Juniperus virginiana L. (eastern red cedar), Picea abies L. (Norway spruce), and Pinus sylvestris L. (Scots pine) (Inamdar et al. 2012; Levia et al 2012; Cooper et al. 2016; Stubbins et al. 2017; Van Stan et  al. 2017; Thieme et  al. 2019; Wagner et al. 2019; Ryan et al. 2022). These studies reported throughfall and stemfow specifc UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ $( \mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } )$ ) values within the range of 1.6 to $6 . 2 ~ \mathrm { L ~ m g – C ^ { - 1 } ~ m ^ { - 1 } }$ , demonstrating that the distribution between aliphatic vs. aromatic compounds vary among tree species. However, few studies (Levia et  al. 2012; Ryan et  al. 2022) have reported absorbance ratios at $2 5 0 { : } 3 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m } ~ ( \mathrm { E } _ { 2 } { : } \mathrm { E } _ { 3 } )$ , spectral slope, or spectral slope ratio $( \mathbf { S } _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ values for throughfall and stemfow, fnding that these characterizations may be associated with soluble lignin degradation byproducts and humic acid isolates in stemfow. Such data are valuable because stemfow plays an important role in shaping the dynamics of the soil carbon cycle and groundwater recharge (Levia and Germer 2015). Variations in these metrics among tree species, especially within individual storms, could exert notable impacts on the temporal dimension of biogeochemical reactivity in near-trunk soils (Levia et al. 2012).

Some studies have reported fuorescence characterization using parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) modeling of both stemfow and throughfall samples, fnding that throughfall and stemfow DOM consists of humic-like, fulvic-like and protein-like components (Cooper et al. 2016; Van Stan et al. 2017; Thieme et al. 2019; Ryan et al. 2022). The distribution of the components difer between the tree species examined, emphasizing the impact of speciesspecifc processes. For sugar maple, yellow birch, and Bahamas pine, Cooper et  al. (2016) and Ryan et  al. (2022) found the fuorescence index (FI) and biological index (BIX) were consistent with terrestrial, aromatic, tree-derived sources.

A few studies have analyzed the temporal variation of stemfow DOM absorbance (Levia et al. 2012; Van Stan et al. 2017; Ryan et al. 2022). These studies examined diferences in stemfow DOM within individual rainfall events (Levia et al. 2012), among rainfall events (e.g., Van Stan et al. 2017), between leafed and leafess states (Levia et  al. 2012),  from mature green to senescence to leafess states (Ryan et  al. 2022), and with or without epiphyte coverage (Van Stan et al. 2017). Ryan et al. (2022) determined that climatic and seasonal efects impacted the character of the DOM components as a function of the leafed and senescence seasons. However, the relationship between DOM composition and these processes is not fully understood, especially stemfow DOM quality with respect to phenophase and carbon cycling in forest ecosystems.

The objective of this study is to quantify the absorbance and fuorescence signatures of stemfow DOM for four major tree species throughout diferent phenophases (i.e., leafess, emergence, leafed, and senescence for deciduous species and leafed-winter, emergence, leafed- spring/summer, and senescence for coniferous species) of a temperate eastern forest of the United States. Our emphasis on stemfow is partly motivated by its localized nature to forest soils (e.g., Levia and Germer 2015; Michalzik et al. 2016; Llorens et  al. 2022) which can engender the creation of hot spots in the proximal area of tree trunks. For instance, the median DOC fux-based stemfow enrichment ratio (expressed per unit trunk basal area) for yellow birch was 72 as compared to 6 for the throughfall fux-based enrichment ratio, illustrating the potential for stemfow to infuence forest soils (Ryan et al. 2022). To the authors’ knowledge, this is the frst study to characterize the optical spectroscopy of stemfow DOM in relation to four diferent phenophases, developing a solely stemfow-based PARAFAC model. Analyzing stemfow DOM independently from throughfall promotes a deeper interpretation of its chemical composition, which may improve understanding surrounding the creation of localized hot spots and hot moments in near-trunk soils.

# Materials and methods

Study area

This study was conducted within the Fair Hill Natural Resources Management Area in Cecil County, Maryland, USA $( 3 9 ^ { \circ } ~ 4 2 ^ { \prime } ~ \mathrm { N } .$ , $7 5 ^ { \circ } ~ 5 0 ^ { \prime }$ W). Situated in the Chesapeake Bay watershed, the site has an average elevation of $7 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ above sea level (Siegert et  al. 2016). The region experiences a continental climate characterized by hot and humid summers, as well as mild to cool winters, featuring well-defned seasons. Annual mean high and low air temperatures are $1 9 . 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $7 . 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , respectively, with an average annual precipitation of $1 1 9 3 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ for the period from 1991 to 2020 (U.S. Climate Normals 2023). The summer months (July through September) typically constitute the driest period, while spring (April through June) emerges as the wettest season (Siegert et  al. 2016). The geological substrate of the study area is the metamorphic Mt. Cuba Wissahickon Formation, predominantly comprised of pelitic gneiss and schist, with minor occurrences of pegmatite and amphibolite. Soil classifcation places it within the Glenelg series, characterized by its deep, well-drained nature and moderate topography. The valley soils are Oxyaquic Dystrudepts, occasionally experiencing seasonal water saturation, while the shallower hillside soils may be characterized as loamy, mixed, mesic Lithic Dystrudepts. Geologically, the site falls within the Piedmont geologic regime of the Mid-Atlantic region (Inmadar et al. 2012).

Vegetation primarily consists of a deciduous forest, complemented by a small Pinus rigida Mill. (pitch pine) community. Notable tree species include Liriodendron tulipifera L. (yellow poplar), Fagus grandifolia Ehrh. (American beech), and Betula lenta L. (sweet birch). Canopy trees in the study area exhibit a mean tree height of $2 7 . 8 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ , a stand density of 225 trees ha−1, a stand basal area of $3 6 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } \mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 }$ , and an average diameter at breast height (dbh) of $4 0 . 8 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ (Levia et  al. 2010). To account for the full diameter range of each tree species at the study site, care was taken to collect stemfow from trees of all diameters over the course of study. The dbh range of trees sampled for each species is as follows: American beech 14.2 to $6 5 . 3 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ , sweet birch 14.3 to $4 9 . 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ , yellow poplar 17.9 to $7 9 . 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ , and pitch pine from 27.7 to $4 6 . 6 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ .  It is important to note that the bark morphology difers among the four test species. Whereas mature yellow poplar and pitch pine are rough-barked species with furrows and ridges in the bark, American beech is a smooth-barked species without furrowed bark. The bark of sweet birch consists of a platy structure that has smoother surfaces with some fssures between plates. Its bark roughness increases with tree size as the smoother bark of smaller trees becomes more platy and more fssures develop between the platy structures.

# Field instrumentation and sample collection

Stemfow collars, crafted from plastic tubing, were afxed to each tree, with the tubing length customized based on tree circumference (measured from dbh). The tubing was halved lengthwise and sealed to the tree using silicone sealant to ensure a watertight connection. The remaining uncut tubing was linked to a $1 2 1 \mathrm { ~ L ~ }$ high-density polyethylene container. New polyethylene plastic bags were put in the stemfow collection bins just prior to each sampled rain event. Stemfow was collected over a 14-month period. Stemfow was collected in clean $1 \mathrm { ~ L ~ }$ amber jars that were rinsed with stemfow water specifc to each tree before sample collection. These samples were fltered on the same day to prevent degradation and stored at $4 \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for subsequent analysis. To confrm that polyethylene bags did not impact carbon analyses, we introduced $1 8 \ \mathrm { ~ M } \Omega$ ultrapure water (Milli-Q, Millipore, Burlington MA, USA) into the bags, subjected them to agitation, allowed them to stand for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , fltered them, and conducted carbon concentration analysis. The results revealed no signifcant changes.

Dissolved organic carbon (DOC)

Following determination of electrical conductivity and $\mathrm { p H }$ (WTW ProfLine pH/Cond 3320 Multiparameter Meter), samples were vacuum-fltered through a $0 . 7 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ , $4 7 \ \mathrm { m m }$ glass fber flter (Whatman GF/F 1825-047) (Shigihara et  al. 2008). Filtered samples were analyzed for non-purgeable organic carbon (NPOC), akin to dissolved organic carbon (DOC), using a Shimadzu TOC-LCSH/CSN (Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan) with the $6 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ combustion catalytic oxidation method and quantifed against a $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m g } }$ $\mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ standard prepared from a $1 0 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ total organic carbon standard solution (Thermo Scientifc, Specpure $\textsuperscript { \textregistered }$ ), with auto-dilutions ranging from 2 to $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g L ^ { - 1 } }$ . Stemfow DOC concentrations were analyzed over a 14-month period.

Stemfow optical properties

Optical properties of stemfow were measured for samples collected over a 14-month period. Filtered DOC samples were diluted with $1 8 \ \mathrm { M } \Omega$ ultrapure water (Milli-Q, Millipore, Burlington MA, USA) to $1 0 \ \mathrm { m g \mathrm { - } C L ^ { - 1 } }$ to ensure the absorbance was less than 1.0 to prevent inner flter light screening efects (IFE) (Kalbitz et  al. 2000). The ultraviolet (UV)-visible absorbance spectrum (Fig.  S1) was analyzed every $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ from 200 to $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ on an Agilent Cary 60 UV–Vis Spectrophotometer (Agilent Technologies,

Inc., Santa Clara, California) with a $1 { \mathrm { - c m } }$ quartz cuvette. Specifc ultraviolet absorbance $\mathrm { ( S U V A _ { 2 5 4 } ) }$ , spectral slope ratio $( \mathbf { S } _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ , and $\mathrm { E } _ { 2 } { \cdot } \mathrm { E } _ { 3 }$ were determined using the equations in Table 1.

Stemfow DOM fuorescence was measured on a Horiba Scientifc Aqualog (Horiba Instruments, Edison, NJ, USA) with a 1-cm quartz cuvette of fltered samples diluted to an absorbance less than 1. Excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) were scanned using excitation from 200 to $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , every $3 \mathrm { n m }$ and emission from 200 to $6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ every $4 \ \mathrm { p x }$ $( 2 . 3 3 \ \mathrm { n m } )$ (Fig. S2). EEMs were blank subtracted using $1 8 ~ \mathbf { M } \Omega$ ultrapure water, and prior to export corrected for IFE in the Aqualog software v. 4.0. FI, humifcation index (HIX), BIX, and freshness index $\left( \beta { \mathrm { : a ) } } \right)$ were determined using the equations in Table 1.

Statistical analysis

Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was employed to model EEM spectra to resolve the fuorescent components in stemfow DOM using the StaRdom package (Pucher et al. 2019) in R v. 4.2.2. Raman and Rayleigh scattering were removed as well as excitation wavelengths below $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and emission wavelengths above $5 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ to minimize noise outside the spectral range of interest. A total of 153 EEMs from sampled stemfow DOM were normalized to their maximum fuorescence intensity to prevent high correlation from low fuorescence signals. A 4-component model (Fig. S3) converged explaining $9 9 . 3 \%$ of fuorescent data and was validated by both split-half analysis (Fig. S4) with Tucker’s congruence coefcient $> 0 . 9 9$ for all samples and also by random initialization, to ensure results are global rather than local minima (Stedmon and Bro 2008).

# PARAFAC model

The validated PARAFAC model components (labeled as C#) (Fig.  2) were analyzed on a per species and per phenophase basis. Each identifed component (Table 4) represents fuorescent compounds or groups of compounds whose fuorescence varies independently from the other components (Stedmon and Bro 2008). Statistically signifcant variations were observed in the distribution of component loadings across species and phenophases (Fig.  3). Yellow poplar exhibited notable diferences from the other species in terms of C1 and C3 percent loadings (yellow poplar ${ \mathrm { C l } } = 2 6 . 6 9 \%$ and $C 3 = 4 3 . 3 1 \%$ , American beech/pitch pine/ sweet birch $\mathrm { C 1 } = 3 0 . 8 8 \%$ and ${ \mathrm { C } } 3 = 3 4 . 1 0 \%$ ; $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 0 0 1$ , Dunn’s test; Fig.  3). # Discussion

Stemfow DOM characterization by optical indices

Our stemfow optical indices are generally congruent with prior studies (see review by Chin et  al. 2023), indicating a diversity of functional group types that could be attributed to microbial or terrestrial sources. Bacteria inhabiting corticular surfaces (Hudson et al. 2023), for example, could be a source of stemfow

DOM, while bacterial and hyphal feeders populating bark and associated epiphytic mosses and lichens (Ptatscheck et  al. 2018) could also modify the carbon pool that contributes to stemfow DOM. For the optical indices based on absorbance, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values (standardized for comparison using the decadic absorption coefcient) of stemfow DOM samples fell within previously reported ranges of 1.9 to $6 . 2 \ \mathrm { L \ m g \ C ^ { - 1 } \ m ^ { - 1 } }$ (Levia et al. 2012; Stubbins et al. 2017; Thieme et al. 2019). Generally, elevated values of $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ indicate greater proportions of aromatic moieties within the DOM (Chin et  al. 1994). $\mathrm { E } _ { 2 } { \cdot } \mathrm { E } _ { 3 }$ and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ also fall within previously reported ranges (2.9 to 6.7 and 0.9 to 1.4 respectively; Levia et al. 2012). Diferences in $\mathrm { E } _ { 2 } { \cdot } \mathrm { E } _ { 3 }$ and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ have correlated to diferences with molecular mass in other studies (Helms et al. 2008), but it is not certain that this relationship necessarily applies in the same manner in the context of stemfow.

Likewise, the fuorescent indices are also consistent with previous literature. For example, FI (1.31 to 1.51), is within expected ranges reported for a variety of both terrestrial surface waters (Chin et  al. 2023), and DOM end-member isolates (McKnight et  al. 2001; Cawley et al. 2013). It is worth noting that the end-member values from isolates extracted from surface water sources (but not stemfow) fall within the range of our data. The FI corresponds to the spectral range associated with various quinoid moieties (Cory and McKnight 2005). Consequently, fuctuations in FI, given its nature as a ratio, signify shifts in the composition and/or relative proportion of these quinoid groups. Compositional diferences may include: (1) variation in the specifc molecules, such as lignin and varying extent of its degradation byproducts; (2) alterations in the redox states (which can impact fuorescence wavelengths); or (3) the presence of microbially derived material (Cory and McKnight 2005). For example, a lower FI can indicate terrestriallyderived matter (McKnight et  al. 2001), such as that formed by decomposition of plant materials (Malcom 1990; McKnight et al. 2001). In the case of stemfow, such DOM may relate to humic substances released through the decomposition of bark (Guggenberger et al. 1994).

HIX refects the relative fuorescence loading between the area of the EEM that includes humic peaks vs. the area where more amino-acid (protein) or small aromatics tend to fuoresce. In this study, HIX ranges from 0.54 to 0.91, with an average of 0.77. These generally lower values indicate limited humifcation for the DOM (Ohno 2002), which is consistent with their origin in the canopy rather than the soil (D’Andrilli et  al. 2022). It is, however, worth noting that humic acids are found in trees and absorb nutrients that promote robust growth (Fernandez-Escobar et  al. 1996). For BIX, a range of values within 0.8 to 1.0 typically signifes the infuence of microbial sources within DOM. Our fndings are consistently below 0.6, which suggests a minimal contribution from microbial precursors (Hansen et al. 2016) and/or high concentrations of soluble lignin (Guggenberger and Zech 1994) which fuoresces at lower wavelengths, potentially overpowering microbial signals.

# Stemfow DOM PARAFAC components

This is the frst study to our knowledge to develop a PARAFAC model derived solely from stemfow EEMs. Identifying and characterizing stemfow DOM components in relation to other water systems generally implies that stemfow is a major source for hot spots, i.e., stemfow-infuenced zones, such as neartrunk soils where stemfow may engender a disproportionately high rate of biogeochemical activity in the soil. Inamdar et  al. (2012) found similar fuorescent components through PARAFAC in other environmental compartments at the Fair Hill Natural Resource Management Area, the same overall site as this study. For example, C5, one of their humiclike components, was quantifed in litter, wetland soil water, shallow groundwater and deep groundwater. Stemfow, enriched with organic material from bark, leaves, and other canopy sources (e.g. aphid honeydew) delivers a concentrated pulse of carbon directly to the soil (Michalzik et  al. 2016). This distinctive characteristic of stemfow underscores its signifcance as a concentrated input of carbon within localized areas around tree boles.

Components from the PARAFAC model matched 172 previously reported components $( > 0 . 9 5$ similarity score) in the OpenFluor database (Murphy et  al. 2014). Based on these similarities, C1 is identifed as consistent with others derived from microbially derived “humic-like” or “fulvic-like” compounds (Garcia et  al. 2015; Li et  al. 2016; Zhuang et  al. 2022). This is a group of chemical compounds that fuoresce in a region in which decomposed plant material typically fuoresces, which would explain an increase in C1 across all species during senescence. C2 is identifed as consistent with an oxidized humic-like peak and C3 as consistent with a reduced humic-like peak, which matches fuorescent components from other terrestrially sourced DOM (Stedmon et al. 2003; Yamashita et al. 2011; Lambert et al. 2016; Imbeau et al. 2021). C4 is identifed as consistent with a protein-like, low molecular weight peak (Osburn et  al. 2016; Wheeler et  al. 2017; Cabrera et  al. 2020; Ryan et  al. 2022). Several compounds containing diferent fuorophores are known to fuoresce in this region with similar signature to proteins and amino acids (Aiken 2014). Tannins and lignin phenols are among these compounds (Aiken 2014), which are commonly found in the bark of the trees (Guggenberger et al. 1994).

# Species variation in DOM characterization

In general, stemfow serves as a transport vehicle for nutrient-rich and bioavailable inputs into the forest foor, and these “hot spots” of concentrated material may play a number of ecologically important roles, such as afecting the microbial diversity in near-stem soils (Rosier et al. 2016). Diferent plant species will contribute to this infux of nutrients and materials from stemfow to various extents, depending on both canopy structural characteristics and precipitation regime (Levia and Frost 2003). Thus, the species-specifc stemfow composition is critical in understanding where and to what degree such hot spots may occur in a forested ecosystem. For instance, stemfow composition and fuxes of major cations and organically bound C, N, and S from European beech are infuenced by low infestation levels of aphids, which, in turn, afect the trophic interactions in the phyllosphere (Michalzik et al. 2016).

An example of species variation with stemfow DOM is that yellow poplar and pitch pine exhibit higher $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values compared to sweet birch and American beech trees. These elevated values suggest greater proportions of aromatic compounds (Chin et  al. 1994) within stemfow (Table  2). The primary source of these aromatic substances in stemfow DOM is believed to be lignin and its degradation products, major polyphenolic components of vascular plants that are washed from tree surfaces (Guggenberger and Zech 1994), particularly bark (Guggenberger et al. 1994). Yellow poplar also exhibits the highest $\mathrm { E } _ { 2 } { : } \mathrm { E } _ { 3 }$ ratio, indicative of lower molecular mass solutes, possibly containing lignin byproducts that are degraded to a further extent. This is consistent with having higher-normalized absorbance around $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , which would increase $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ as well. In contrast, American beech shows the opposite trend with respect to both $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $\mathrm { E } _ { 2 } { : } \mathrm { E } _ { 3 }$ (Levia et  al. 2012), suggesting the lignin in its stemfow is less degraded.

Garcia et  al. (2015) reported a decline in C1 in surface water of a forested catchment during emergence, consistent with our observations in American beech. The site primarily featured Nothofagus pumilio, a member of the Fagaceae family, suggesting that deciduous tree species in the vicinity of catchments can infuence DOM composition, specifcally C1 and FI. Our fndings exhibit signifcant variation among species, specifcally with American beech having the highest FI. It is unclear whether stemfow C1’s relationship holds for other species within the Fagaceae family.

Pitch pine has the lowest $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ and is the only statistically diferent value from the other species (Table  2). A possible explanation for this is the presence of terpenes in coniferous trees. Certain terpenes such as myrcene, terpinene, and pinene, absorb at longer wavelengths (Bones et  al. 2009), which would infuence the denominator of the $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ (Table  1). Pinene is the most prominent terpene in pitch pine (Son et al. 2015).

In terms of fuorescent components, pitch pine stemfow also stands out from the other tree species. C4 is signifcantly diferent between pitch pine and sweet birch, although the precise reason for this is unclear. However, all species show unique diferences between C2 and C3 relative loadings across phenophases (Fig.  4). As these components are consistent with oxidized/reduced humic-like materials, fuctuations in the extent of oxidation on a per-species and per-phenophase basis demonstrates a potential cyclic yearly relationship for each tree species. Such fndings may demonstrate the impact of redox moieties on variations in DOM among tree species, perhaps by comparing C2 and C3 loadings to understand quinine composition on near-trunk soils.

# Phenophase variation in DOM characterization

Stemfow is a signifcant driver of carbon cycling dynamics (Ryan et  al. 2022). Seasonal variation underscores how hot moments, triggered by changes in seasons, intensify the fow of water down tree trunks, enhancing the leaching of organic matter and enriching stemfow with carbon-rich compounds. Ultimately, these “hot moments”, temporal events caused by factors such as temperature, amplify carbon fuxes through stemfow, afecting soil carbon dynamics, microbial activity, and decomposition rates. We observe that phenophases alter the carbon composition in stemfow, consistent with previous work by Levia et  al. (2012) with stemfow absorbance, Ryan et  al. (2022) in a throughfall/precipitation/ stemfow model, and Wheeler et al. (2017) in a litter leachate model.

During senescence, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values are highest (Table  3), indicating increased entrainment of aromatic carbon, such as lignin and its byproducts, as leaves drop and scar tissue forms to protect branches. Compared to Ryan et  al. (2022), who observed no trend, and Wheeler et  al. (2017), with senescence being the lowest in litter leachate, this observation is unique to stemfow. In contrast, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ is lowest during emergence (Table 3), suggesting that either (1) lignin utilization and other necessary chemical species for leaf formation; or (2) lignin is potentially less degraded (i.e., fewer molecules absorbing at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ). Among the deciduous species investigated, a drop in HIX was observed during emergence. One explanation for this decrease may be the quenching of fuorescence by Fe (II), causing a shift toward shorter wavelengths (Jia et al. 2021). Bennet and Oserkowsky (1933) showed a rapid rise in iron concentrations in sap fux in early spring before stabilizing to a lower concentration for the rest of the year, similar to the trend for HIX witnessed in this study. It could also refect an increase in components that fuoresce in the protein-like region, perhaps derived from amino acids or other small molecules. However, in the case of pitch pine, variations in HIX were evident during both senescence and emergence (Fig. S5). Interestingly, we observed no signifcant changes in $\mathrm { E } _ { 2 } { \cdot } \mathrm { E } _ { 3 }$ and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ between phenophases, suggesting  consistent degradation of lignin within a species. The FI and BIX did not show trends either, suggesting that the components of DOM that give rise to those regions where those indices derive are more consistent throughout the year.

Prior research indicates that during the leafess season, an absence of foliage and lower air temperatures enhances stemfow-bark contact time (Levia and Frost 2003), thereby increasing $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Levia et al. 2012), consistent with our study which also shows $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ increasing with the onset of lower air temperatures during senescence (Table 3), although winter $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were lower, possibly due to the inclusion of the evergreen pitch pine. C1, C2, and C4 also exhibited seasonal groupings, with leafed/ leafed-spring/summer and emergence phases differing signifcantly from leafess/leafed-winter and senescence phases. This observation suggests that the components in stemfow DOM undergo seasonal patterns despite not being revealed by the other fuorescent indices above, which may be due to those indices being largely defned as ratios, so a lack of change in FI, BIX, etc. could be from a counterbalance of component shifts. C1 and C2 both increase during the leafess/leafed-winter phenophase (leafed to leafess, $C 1 \sim 2 7 \%$ to $3 1 \%$ , $C 2 \sim 1 7 \%$ to $2 1 \%$ ) but C4 decreases during that time $C 4 \sim 2 2 \%$ to $8 \%$ ). Part of the reason for this may be the increased infuence of substantial quantities of canopy-based biodegradable, hydrophilic neutral carbohydrates during the leafed season creating an increase in microbial activity feeding on the carbon pool. This increase in activity will alter the type of carbon observed and change the proportions of components (Guggenberger and Zech 1994; Michalzik et al. 2001). The higher percentage of C4 in stemfow during emergence and the leafed season may be partly due to the larger aboveground surface areas (both foliar and woody) compared to dormancy, leading to the degradation and leaching of diverse and complex aromatics, including lignin and tannins (Guggenberger et  al. 1994), which fuoresce in this region.

Further research is required to validate a relationship between $\mathrm { E } _ { 2 } { \cdot } \mathrm { E } _ { 3 }$ and solute molecular mass in stemfow. $\mathrm { E } _ { 2 } { \cdot } \mathrm { E } _ { 3 }$ is largely species but not phenophase dependent in this study, raising an interesting question as to what this index actually indicates in stemfow. Perhaps is it simply a marker of conjugation, as more conjugated molecules (longer polyphenolic polymer chains) will absorb at higher wavelengths, which could be consistent with extent of lignin degradation. Also, the precise factors contributing to HIX variations in emergence remain unclear, but further experiments on humic substances and iron concentrations in diferent species throughout the four phenophases is recommended to understand the in-situ processes utilizing humic substances for regrowth during emergence in these species.

# Conclusion

The study illuminates the role of stemfow in the transfer of water and solutes to the foor of forest ecosystems that likely help contour near-trunk water and biogeochemical cycling. A comprehensive analysis of the absorbance and fuorescent signatures of stemfow DOM across diferent phenophases of four major tree species in temperate eastern forests has shown that aromatic, metal-binding, and large molecular mass solutes play a signifcant role in stemfow DOM. Further, this study developed a stemfow-specifc PARAFAC model that identifed key components in stemfow DOM and their relationship with diferent species, which ofers a deeper understanding of stemfow’s chemical composition and its potential in creating localized hot spots and hot moments in neartrunk soils. The fndings confrm the presence of aromatic DOM compounds in stemfow, partly derived from lignin and its degradation products. Signifcant variations in DOM properties among tree species and phenophases were observed, highlighting the importance of species-specifc analyses and the impact of phenological shifts on stemfow DOM quality.

The stemfow-specifc PARAFAC model has yielded insights, such as identifying components, cyclic redox relationships, and seasonal patterns, on the composition and dynamics of stemfow DOM across diferent tree species and phenophases. The model has identifed four components, with C1 representing microbially derived "humic-like" or "fulvic-like" compounds, exhibiting increased presence across all species during senescence. C2 was identifed as an oxidized humic-like peak, while C3 represented a reduced humic-like peak, aligning with fuorescent components found in other terrestriallysourced DOM. Additionally, C4 was characterized as a protein-like, low molecular weight peak. Besides interspecifc diferences, C1, C2, and C4 display patterns with phenophase, which shed light on the relative distribution of specifc aromatic moieties within stemfow DOM throughout diferent times of the year, and in general reveal more variation than ratios from fuorescence indices alone can describe, providing a more specifc understanding of shifts in molecular composition with respect to stemfow processes. The study raises questions about the relationships between certain fuorescent components of stemfow DOM, such as the interplay between oxidized and reduced quinones and their potential cyclic interactions within certain species. This research provides valuable insights into the fner temporal variations that help reveal carbon cycling dynamics with respect to phenophase.
