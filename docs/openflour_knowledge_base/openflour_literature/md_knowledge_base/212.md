# High phosphorus content in leachates of the austral beech Nothofagus pumilio stimulates bacterioplankton C-consumption

Marcela Bastidas Navarro1,2, Veronica Diaz Villanueva1,3,and Beatriz Modenutti1,4

1Laboratorio de Limnologia, INIBIOMA, CONICET - UNComahue, 8400 Bariloche, Argentina

Abstract: In the Northern Patagonian Andes,the austral beech Nothofagus pumilio dominates the upper limit of the temperate forest and is an important source of allochthonous dissolved organic mater (DOM) in mountain lakes.Inaddition,shallow lakes in these mountains develop benthic algal assemblages (Spirogyra and Mougeotia) that provide autochthonous DOM.We assessed the effect of differences in the nutritional quality of these DOM inputs on bacterial metabolism in a mountain lake located in Northern Patagonia (Argentina). Weanalyzed DOM labilityand changes in bacterial assemblages experimentally by enriching treatments with either algal exudates $( + \mathrm { E X U } )$ or senescent $N .$ pumilio leaf leachates $( + \mathrm { L E A } )$ together with a control without enrichment (-ENR). We determined nutrient concentrations and DOM optical properties with spectrofluorometric excitation-emission matrix analysis. We also assessd bacterial assemblage composition by next-generation sequencing the 16S rRNA gene.DOM optical properties and phosphorus (P) content differed among treatments. The $+ \mathrm { L E A }$ treatment had a lower C:P ratio (more P content) than the $+ \mathrm { E X U }$ treatment. In addition, DOM optical characterization revealed that 1 protein-like compound (tryptophan) was present in the $+ \mathrm { L E A }$ treatment, but a different protein-like compound (tyrosine) was present in the $+ \mathrm { E X U }$ treatment. The $+ \mathrm { L E A }$ treatment had higher bacterial respiration rates than the control, particularly during the first $2 \mathrm { d }$ of incubation. These higher respiration rates were probably a result of the lower C:P ratio and the diffrence in fluorescence compounds. However,the bacterial assemblage composition did notchange in the diferent treatments.Our study suggests that high relative Pcontentfrom allchthonous DOM increases bacterial C-consumption and highlights the importance of C:nutrient ratios in DOM for C-cycling in aquatic ecosystems.

Key words: Patagonian shallow lake,algal exudates, deciduous tre leachates, C:P ratio, dissolved organic matter lability, bacterial respiration, bacterial assemblage composition

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is the major organic C pool in aquatic ecosystems (Cole et al.2oo7).It can originate from either autochthonous or allochthonous sources and is structurally diverse and complex (Aitkenhead-Peterson et al. 2003, Attermeyer et al. 2014). On one hand, the autochthonous C contribution to a waterbody can vary because of seasonal and spatial changes in autotrophic constituents (benthic or pelagic algae and macrophytes),and its quality can be affected by community composition (Bertilsson and Jones 20o3) or physiological state (Xenopoulos et al.2002). On the other hand,allochthonous DOM inputs (vascular plants and soil leachates) have increased consistently over the past few decades (Hanson et al.2014) because of increased atmospheric $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration, temperature increases,and hydrological changes from increased precipitation (Porcal et al.20o9). Consequently, surface water browning from increasing terrestrial DOM concentrations has become an environmental concern (Kritzberg et al. 2014, De Wit et al. 2016).

In aquatic ecosystems, the strategies bacteria use to consume resources depend on the specific DOM source available to them (Guillemette et al.2016).Autochthonous phytoplankton DOM is the most important source of organic matter for bacterial production (Kritzberg et al.2005), whereas allochthonous DOM is used slowly or inefficiently (Pérez and Sommaruga 2Oo6) and usually fuels bacterial respiration rather than bacterial production (Farjalla et al.2009). This difference occurs because allochthonous DOM contains more complex and aromatic compounds that have lower nutritional content than autochthonous DOM (McKnight and Aiken 1998).Indeed, bacterial assemblages preferentially remove algal C from the terrestrially dominated DOM pool in lakes (Guillemette et al. 20l6). However, inputs of labile, algal-derived organic C may stimulate bacterial assemblages to incorporate more of the recalcitrant terrestrial C pool (Guillemette et al. 2016).

Aquatic bacterial metabolism is affected not only by the chemical attributes of different DOM sources, but also by the stoichiometric ratios of growth-limiting nutrients (Berggren et al.2015,Soares et al.2017).Both DOM sources (algae and terrestrial vegetation) have variable elemental ratios because of changes in the autotrophic metabolic state or inherent characteristics of the autotrophic source.Algal exudates are generally dominated by carbohydrates and have lower concentrations of essential nutrients such as nitrogen (N) or phosphorus (P) (Myklestad 1995). Conversely, soil DOM has variable C: nutrient ratios because it is affected by the vegetation present (Grieve and Marsden 2Oo1) as well as by the nutrients released from the watershed (Osborn et al. 2007, Jaffé et al.20o8).Pand Nare the 2 key macronutrients that control bacterioplankton production,and therefore influence foodweb structure, biodiversity,and biogeochemical cycles (Jones 1998).Further, in oligotrophic freshwater ecosystems,Pis often the element that limits primary and heterotrophic bacterial production (Vadstein 2000).

Bacterioplankton are efficient competitors for P,and they sequester a substantial proportion of the total P available in the environment (Jürgens and Güde 199o). High P concentrations can increase bacterial C-specific productivity rates by alleviating P-limitation for bacterial biomass production (Lennon and Pfaff 2oo5). Thus, the C:P ratio can indicate differences in DOM quality and influence the productivity of freshwater bacterioplankton. Aquatic and terrestrial plant species have different elemental compositions (Osborn et al. 2007),so the C:P ratio in DOMleached from different species represents resources of variable nutritional quality for bacterial production.

In addition to changing bacterial metabolism, resource supply can also affect bacterial assemblage composition (BAC) (Foreman and Covert 2003). Common fingerprinting methods have shown that BAC changes when bacteria are grown at different organic C concentrations (Eiler et al. 2003,Jones et al. 2009) or on DOM of different origins (Cottrell and Kirchman 20oo). In addition, a recent study demonstrated that different aquatic bacterial clades consume different amounts of inorganic P and ATP (Rofner et al.20l6).In particular,Actinobacteria and Betaproteobacteria have varying degrees of stoichiometric homeostasis and different intracellular C:P ratios that result in different strategies for coping with P-limited systems (Rofner et al.20l6).Bacterial assemblages have different P content and elemental stoichiometry (Godwin and Cotner 2015)

depending on their P supply, probably because of changes in their BAC (Godwin and Cotner 2014).

In Northern Patagonia $( 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { S } )$ the deciduous austral beech Nothofagus pumilio (Poepp.et Endl.） Krasser dominates high elevations.Erect trees decrease in abundance with increasing elevation and culminate in krummholz that dominate the timberline (Daniels and Veblen 2004).We have observed that the quality of the DOM pool and P concentrations change along an altitudinal gradient in mountain lakes of the Northern Patagonian Andes (Bastidas Navarro et al. 2014). These changes highlight the potential importance of the terrestrial vegetation (N. pumilio) at and below timberline to the structure and functioning of aquatic bacterial assemblages.

Here,we assessed whether differences in nutritional quality of DOMaffects bacterial metabolism in a mountain lake located below timberline.We hypothesized that: 1） algal exudates and leaf leachates would differ in DOM quality (C:P ratio and DOM optical properties),and 2) bacterial C-consumption would increase with a decrease in the C:P ratio (increase of P concentration) of the DOM. To test these hypotheses,we compared bacterial response to waters experimentally enriched with algal exudate,enriched with leaf leachate from senescent $N .$ pumilio leaves, and a nonenriched control. We measured C consumption in the different treatments with 2 approaches: short-term bacterial respiration experiments (2 d) and long-term C consumption bioassays (28 d). Additionally, we used DNA sequencing to assess if natural bacterial assemblages differed among the treatments after $9 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ of incubation. Thus, we aimed to determine the importance of both allochthonous and autochthonous DOM resources,which may produce differential $\mathrm { { C } : \mathrm { { P } } }$ ratios in the resources available to bacteria. The source of DOM is of particular importance in P-limited environments, such as the mountain lakes of Patagonia.

# METHODS

# Study site

We conducted this study with water and natural bacterial assemblages from Lake Los Patos,a small, shallow Andean mountain lake (area $= 1 . 5$ ha, maximum depth: $3 \mathrm { m }$ Lake Los Patos is located at $1 5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ asl in the Northern Patagonian Andes on the eastern slope of Chall-Huaco Mountain (lat $4 1 ^ { \circ } 1 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { S }$ ,long $7 1 ^ { \circ } 2 0 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { W }$ ). The surrounding vegetation is a forest composed of the deciduous austral beech $N .$ pumilio,and the lake bottom is covered by green filamentous algal mats of the genera Spirogyra and Mougeotia. The deciduous forest and algal mats are the main sources of DOM in Lake Los Patos.

# General experimental overview

We carried out the experiments in $5 0 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ sterilized flasks on 3 different dates: in austral autumn (16 May 2013) just after leaves began to fall, in spring (13 November 2013), and in summer (26 February 2014).On each date,we collected $^ { 1 5 \mathrm { ~ L ~ } }$ of water from $0 . 3 \mathrm { m }$ below the surface of Lake Los Patos $^ { 5 \mathrm { h } }$ before beginning the experiments.We stored the water in acid-cleaned containers and transported it to the laboratory immediately after collection in thermally insulated containers.

In each experiment we incubated natural bacterial assemblages with the 2 different DOM sources (leaf leachates or algal exudates: $+ \mathrm { L E A }$ or $+ \mathrm { E X U } ,$ ).Each experiment also included a control treatment that had only lake water and no extra source of DOM(-ENR).Each treatment included 6 replicates (6 flasks) (Fig.S1).All the treatment flasks were incubated in a dark,controlled temperature incubator at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ Three flasks from each treatment were equipped with a non-invasive sensor,which allowed us to measure dissolved oxygen (DO) concentration from outside the container (Fig.S1).We measured DO in these flasks over $2 8 ~ \mathrm { d }$ and used the measurements to assess DOM lability(Cconsumption in short and long-term).We used the remaining 3 flasks from each treatment to assess how DOM sources affected C and P concentration,DOM optical characteristics,and bacterial assemblage composition (DNA sequencing) over $9 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ of incubation (Fig. S1).

# Laboratory procedures

Algal exudates and leaf leachatesWe obtained algal exudates by collecting algal mats directly from the lake bottom 2d before each experiment. We assessed algal composition of the mats under a microscope on each experimental run (autumn, spring,and summer),and Spirogyra spp.and Mougeotia spp. were always the dominant species.We created algal exudates by carefully washing the algal mats in the laboratory with MilliQ water and placing $2 . 5 \ \mathrm { g }$ of the mat in filtered $( 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ lake water in sterile $2 5 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ flasks. Flasks were then placed in a controlled temperature incubator for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ t $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ t $9 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ photon $\mathrm { m } ^ { - \bar { 2 } } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ (daylight fluorescent tubes).After this incubation, we considered the liquid to be exudate.

We obtained $N .$ pumilio leachates by collecting senescent leaves in autumn with plastic leaf traps,which prevented the leaves from becoming contaminated by soil contact. We used autumn-collected leaves in all 3 experiments because we aimed to test if the leaf effect associated with autumn leaffall was maintained through spring and summer. We stored the trap-collected leaves in sterile bags in the dark at $4 ^ { \circ } C$ over the course of the 3 experiments.To create the experimental leachate before each experiment, we soaked $_ { 1 \mathrm { \textrm { g } } }$ of leaves in $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of MilliQ water in a refrigerator $( 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ for $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ (Anesio et al. 2000).

We filtered both the exudates and leachates through $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ cellulose acetate sterile filters.We ensured that the filters did not add dissolved organic carbon (DOC) by comparing the DOC concentration in filtered and non-filtered

MilliQ water.There was no difference,so we concluded that the filter did not add DOC to the water.

Bacterial inoculumWe prepared the bacterial inoculum by filtering lake water through a $2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate filter to eliminate bacterial predators (nanoflagellates and ciliates).We ensured that all predators were eliminated by fltering a $4 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ formaldehyde-fixed sample of the filtered lake water onto $1 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate black membrane filters (Poretics,Livermore, California).We then used epifluorescence microscopy staining with $^ { 4 ^ { \prime } , 6 ^ { \prime } }$ -diamidino-2- phenylindole (DAPI) to look for nanoflagellates and ciliates.We found no flagellates or ciliates in the 2- $\mu \mathrm { m }$ filtrate. We did not measure viruses, but we did not see any sharp decreases in bacterial abundance that could be attributed to viruses during the incubation experiments.

Experimental setupWe filtered lake water from Lake Los Patos onto pre-combusted ( $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 1 \mathrm { h } }$ ）Whatman GFF filters and then through $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ cellulose acetate sterile filters (Sartorius, Goetingen, Germany).All glassware was sterilized (autoclaved for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ ）prior to each experiment (Fig. S1).

We used 3 treatments in our experiments: 1) without enrichment $( \mathrm { - E N R } ) { \mathrm { - } } 1$ part bacterial inoculum with 2 parts filtered lake water $( 0 . 2 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ ,2)with exudates enrichment $( + \mathrm { E X U } ) { - } 1$ part each of the bacterial inoculum,filtered lake water,and algal exudates,and 3) with leachates enrichment $\mathrm { ( + L E A ) { - } 1 }$ part each of the bacterial inoculum, filtered lake water,and $N .$ pumilio leachates (Fig. S1). We standardized the experimental treatments at the beginning of each experiment by diluting the final filtrates with MilliQ water, if necessary, to create a final DOC concentration similar to that recorded in the lake.

Sampling and DoM lability We measured C and $\mathrm { P }$ concentrations $( 5 0 \mathrm { m L } )$ , characterized DOM optical properties $( 2 ~ \mathrm { m L } )$ ，and measured the bacterial assemblage composition $( 1 0 0 \mathrm { m L } )$ ) from samples collected at both the beginning of incubation $\left( t _ { i } \right)$ and after $9 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ of incubation $( t _ { f } )$ (Fig. S1).

We measured DOMlability in the flasks that had oxygen probes (3 replicates per treatment) over $2 8 { \mathrm { d } }$ following Guillemette and del Giorgio (201l) (Fig. S1).We measured the concentration of DO every $^ { 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ with an optical-oximeter (ODO) that measures DO with non-invasive oxygen fluorescent sensors (PreSens) located inside the flasks.We used a dual approach to assess DOMlability in the different treatments and experiments: short-term bacterial respiration experiments and long-term C consumption bioassays. We estimated the short-term C-consumption rate (STCC) by measuring the decrease in DO concentration during the first $2 \mathrm { d }$ and assuming that the initial slope of the consumption curve during that period was the STCC.We used a respiratory quotient of 1 (McCallister et al. 20o6) to convert the oxygen consumption rates to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ production when calculating the STCC. We estimated the long-term Cconsumption rate (LTCC) by measuring DOC every 2 to $^ { 4 \textup { d } }$ over the $2 8 \textup d$ incubation and estimating the slope between day 3 and day 28 (Guillemette and del Giorgio 2011). We measured the proportion of C in the residual pool by dividing the final DOC concentration (day 28) by the initial DOC concentration.

Dissolved C and P measurementsWe measured total dissolved $\mathrm { P }$ (TDP) and DOC concentrations from water filtered through pre-combusted GFF filters.We measured TDP by digesting the filtered water with sodium persulphate at $1 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 1.5 atm for $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and then measuring TDP concentrations with the colorimetric ascorbatereduced molybdenum method (APHA 2005).We measured DOC with a high-temperature combustion analyzer (TOC V-CSH; Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan) with a potassium hydrogen phthalate standard.

DOM fluorometric optical characterization We characterized the DOM from each treatment in each of the 3 experiments at $t _ { i }$ and $t _ { f } \left( 9 6 \mathrm { h } \right)$ by measuring sample fluorescence. Single measurements at specifc excitation-emission wavelengths were taken with a Perkin-Elmer LS 45 fluorescence spectrometer equipped with a xenon discharge lamp. We used these values to construct excitation-emission matrices (EEMs). The excitation wavelength intervals ranged from 240 to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with $2 \mathrm { - n m }$ increments,and the emission wavelength intervals ranged from 300 to $5 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with $5 \mathrm { - n m }$ increments.The measurements were done at a constant room temperature of $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a 1-cm quartz fluorescence cell. We normalized the fluorescence data by dividing the measured values (relative units) by the Raman peak of MilliQ water at $3 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ excitation on the measurement day (Murphy et al. 2013).Fluorescence was then expressed as Raman Units (RU) (Lawaetz and Stedmon 2009).

DNA extraction and sequencing Samples ( $\mathrm { 1 0 0 ~ m L }$ ）from each treatment were filtered through a $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ Nuclepore filter ( $2 5 \mathrm { - m m }$ diameter).We transferred the filters into MoBio UltraClean Microbial DNAIsolation Kit Bead Tubes under sterile conditions and extracted the DNA with MoBio following the manufacturer protocol. We purified the DNA with the UltraClean PCR Clean-Up Kit. Purifed DNA samples were sent to Instituto de Agrobiotecnologia Rosario (INDEAR) for paired-end sequencing.

We quantified the metagenomic DNA with the Quant$\mathrm { i T } ^ { \mathbf { T M } }$ PicoGreen@ dsDNA Assay Kit (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, California) according to the manufacturer protocol (high range curve - half area plate). We used PCR amplification of the V1-V3 variable region in the 16S rRNA gene to construct amplicon sequencing libraries.Amplicon and shotgun libraries were sequenced with 454-FLX-Titanium chemistry to quantify the number of bacteria operational taxonomic units (OTUs). We processed the raw data following standard manufacturer-suggested procedures (Roche, October 2009).We obtained 169,496 reads with an average size of $5 3 4 . 5 \pm 4 1 . 6$ bp from 25 samples with 454-FLX shotgun metagenome sequencing.We analyzed this dataset with QIIME v1.5 software package (Boulder, Colorado) (Caporaso et al. 2010).

# Statistical analysis

We tested for significant differences in TDP,DOC,and C:P (dissolved) atomic ratios with a 2-way repeatedmeasures ANOVA (rmANOVA) in each of the 3 seasonal experiments ( $^ { \mathrm { 9 6 ~ h } }$ of incubation).In each of these tests, the factors were the treatments ( $( + \mathrm { E X U }$ $+ \mathrm { L E A } .$ and -ENR) and time $( t _ { i } , t _ { f } )$ .When these tests resulted in significant differences we did a post hoc multiple comparison Tukey test. We checked for normality and homoscedasticity before analysis. These statistical analyses were done with SigmaPlot 12.5 (Systat Software Inc., San Jose, California).

The fluorometric excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) were analyzed with a Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC) with the DOMFluor toolbox in MatLab R2015a(The MathWorks, Inc., Natick, Massachusetts) (Stedmon and Bro 2008, Murphy et al.2013).PARAFAC was applied to the full data set of 54 samples from all 3 experiments. PARAFAC decomposes trilinear multi-way data arrays and facilitates the identification and quantification of the fluorometric peaks that correspond to components, which are groups of chemical compounds with similar optical properties (Murphy et al. 2013).Thus,PARAFAC is a valuable tool for evaluating changes in DOM fluorescence and tracing different DOM fractions (Stedmon et al. 2008, Cory et al. 2012).PARAFAC is based on fluorometric peaks, so nonfluorescent C compounds are not considered in the analysis,even though they can be abundant in leaf leachates and can modify biodegradation rates (Boulton and Boon 1991, Baldwin 1999).Nevertheless,our estimates of C content in leachates and exudates (i.e., DOC concentration) included these non-fluorescent compounds,so the C:P reflects the actual elemental ratio.We cross-referenced the values of the fluorescing peaks with the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al. 2014) by comparing spectra from other studies with ours to contextualize our results.

We used split-half method analysis (Stedmon and Bro 2008) to validate the PARAFAC model. We estimated the $\%$ change of each PARAFAC component during the experiment ( $9 6 \mathrm { h }$ of incubation) as $\%$ change $= ( t _ { f } - t _ { i } ) / t _ { i } \times 1 0 0 $ where $t _ { f }$ is the maximum fluorescence (Fmax) of the component at the end of the experiment, and $t _ { i }$ is the Fmax at the beginning.

We tested for significant differences in $\textrm { C }$ consumption based on the $\%$ change of different DOM fluorescence peaks and the variables that represent DOM lability (STCC,LTCC, and the residual pool) with 1-way ANOVA.When these tests resulted in significant differences we did a post-hoc multiple comparison Tukey test. We checked for normality and homoscedasticity before these analyses.These statistical analyses were done with SigmaPlot 12.5 (Systat Software Inc., San Jose, California).

We selected the model that best described bacterial Cconsumption rate (STCC and LTCC) with the Akaike information criterion (AIC) (Johnson and Omland 2004). We included the variables TDP,DOC,DOM fluorescence peak ratio,and C:P (atomic) in these models.We checked for normality and homoscedasticity before these analyses and performed square-root transformation when necessary.DOM fluorescence peak ratios were calculated based on components that were exclusively found in either the leachates or exudates,as calculated by the PARAFAC analyses (see above).Thus,we used C5 for exudates and C1 for leachates,and calculated this ratio as C5:C1 (see Results). We considered all variable combinations except C:P with TDP or DOC because C:P was highly correlated with TDP and DOC (Pearson's product-moment correlations $>$ 0.60).We calculated the corrected AIC (AICc) value for each model because it measures the model fit but corrects for small sample sizes.All the candidate models were ranked according to their AICc value,with the best model having the smallest AICc value.We then calculated △AICc, which is the difference between each model and the bestfit model within the model set.We used a set of the bestsupported models based on a model criterion of $\Delta \mathrm { A I C c } < 2$ (Burnham and Anderson 2004).

We used the Bray-Curtis similarity index to measure differences in the initial bacterial assemblage composition between the 3 experiments (autumn, spring and summer). To test if bacteria assemblage composition of the different treatments with ( $\mathrm { + L E A }$ and $+ \mathrm { E X U } ,$ ）and without enrichment（-ENR） were significantly different,we applied a

PERMANOVA test to the Bray-Curtis distance matrix. These analyses were done with the vegan package in R (version 3.0.2) (R Development Core Team 2018).

# DISCUSSION

In our experiments bacterial C-consumption depended on whether the DOM source was autochthonous (algal exudates) or allochthonous (leaf leachates). C-consumption increased when allochthonous sources (N. pumilio leachates) were added to lake water because of an increase in P concentration (lower C:P ratio) and the presence of a particular fluorescent peak (tryptophan-like compound). Cconsumption in lake water enriched with autochthonous (algal exudates) increased relative to the unenriched treatment, but was lower that the treatment enriched with the allochthonous source.In this sense, bacterial assemblages would be generalists if they have high functional plasticity, which may be an intrinsic property of aquatic microbial assemblages (Allison and Martiny 2oo8,Comte et al. 2013).

Little information exists about freshwater benthic algae as a DOM source (Cantonati and Lowe 2014). However, previous studies have shown that epipelic algae release organic C into lake water and stimulate pelagic bacterial production (Rodriguez et al. 2013,Wyatt et al. 2014).In our experiments, benthic algae released more C than P, generating an increase in the C:P ratio of DOM. Thus, DOM produced by algae was significantly lower in quality than that from leaf leachates, supporting our $1 ^ { \mathrm { s t } }$ hypothesis that the 2 DOM sources would differ in quality.

The quantity of algal exudates appears to depend on the nutritional status of algae,pH, light intensity,and osmotic status (Sell and Overbeck 1992,Myklestad 1995,Obernosterer and Herndl 1995,Marsalek and Rojickova 1996). We conducted all laboratory incubation experiments under the same conditions (same light intensity, i.e., $9 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ photon $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ , and temperature,i.e., $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ,but in nature the light conditions for benthic algae differ among seasons. In summer the high solar exposure in Patagonia (Villafane et al. 2004) frequently surpasses $1 0 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ photon $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ at the water surface (Corno et al. 2Oo9,Modenutti et al. 2013). These high-light conditions are an additional stress factor that can negatively affect algal photosynthesis (Martyniuk et al. 2016) and the C:P ratio (Xenopoulos et al. 2002).The nutritional status of algae is intrinsically linked to the composition of the organic matter it releases (Sterner and Elser 2O02). The highest C:P ratio in algal exudates in our experiments occurred in summer,which is probably a result of the environmental conditions. The high C:P ratio we observed in Spirogyra spp. and Mougeotia spp. exudates during summer could be the result of high solar radiation, especially in a nutrient limited environment.

Bacteria interact with DOC through the differential uptake and processing of diverse C compounds (Ruiz-Gonzalez et al.2015a).In boreal lakes,bacterioplankton functional composition is influenced mainly by the terrestrial DOM pool (Ruiz-Gonzalez et al.20l5b),but some bacterial strains specialize on algal exudates (Simek et al. 2011). Thus, we expected bacterial assemblages to differ between enriched and unenriched treatments.However, in our experiments we did not observe any significant differences in bacteria assemblages between the enriched ( $\mid +$ LEA and $+ \mathrm { E X U }$ and control $( \mathrm { - } \mathrm { E N R } )$ treatments. This result may indicate that bacterial assemblages present in Lake Los Patos have the capacity to use a wide variety of resources.In this sense,bacterial assemblages would be generalists if they have high functional plasticity, which may be an intrinsic property of aquatic microbial assemblages (Comte et al. 2013).

The consumption and degradation of DOM by heterotrophic bacteria is governed by interactions with nutrients as well as by chemical composition (Guillemette et al.2013). Thus, our $2 ^ { \mathrm { n d } }$ hypothesis predicted that bacterial C consumption would increase with decreasing DOM C:P ratios that result from P derived from leaf leachates of terrestrial origin.We used a dual approach to assess C consumption in the different treatments and experiments: short-term bacterial respiration experiments and long-term C consumption bioassays.Both of these 2 methods measure Cconsumption, but differ in their estimates of this process (Guillemette and del Giorgio 2011). Short-term changes result from the consumption of a highly reactive C pool with rapid turnover, whereas the long-term differences are also influenced by the loss rates of more recalcitrant C compounds (del Giorgio and Pace 20o8). In our experimental treatments, terrestrial sources (N. pumilio leachates) led to higher STCC rates than the control and exudate treatments. However, the exudate and leachate treatments did not differ in LTCC rates except in summer, when the leachate treatment had lower consumption rates.In summer the residual C increased in the leachate treatment, which may be a result of the low consumption of more recalcitrant C compounds.

Previous studies have shown that STCC and LTCC respond to distinct components of DOM revealed by PARAFAC (Guillemette and del Giorgio 20l1). In our experiments, we observed that the fluorescence peak C4 (identified as a terrigenous humic substance) did not change throughout the 3 experiments and treatments, so this type of DOM is probably not consumed by bacteria. In contrast, the protein-like components C5 (tyrosine-like) in exudates and C1 (tryptophan-like） in leachates,decreased significantly during the experiments,especially in autumn and summer. Guillemette and del Giorgio (20l1) observed that bacteria preferentially consume tryptophan-like components,and the presence of C1 in N. pumilio leachates and its decrease throughout our experiment suggests a similar pattern of bacteria preference.In addition, the model showed that both C:P and the DOM fluorescence peaks (C5:C1) ratios affect STCC. More P (low C:P) and the presence of C1 (leachates) contributed substantially to increases in STCC (2 d). However, the C:P ratio was the only variable associated with LTCC (28 d) in the model. The difference probably occurred because increasing $\mathrm { P }$ concentration appears to enhance the amount of terrestrial DOC that bacteria can consume (Guillemette et al. 2013).

In nature, many studies have demonstrated a decline in planktonic bacterial productivity (Lennon and Pfaff 2005, Bertoni et al. 2008,Motegi et al. 2015)and bacterial C uptake (Rodriguez et al.20l3) when environmental P concentrations decrease. Our results suggest that the high relative P content from allochthonous DOM (leaf leachates) promotes a higher C-consumption rate than that from autochthonous DOM (algal exudates).Interestingly, the highest C-consumption occurred in autumn when Lake Los Patos receives natural leachates that originate from the deciduous $N .$ pumilio forest (Diaz Villanueva etal. 20l6).Nothofagus pumilio does not recover $\mathrm { P }$ from its leaves before abscission (Diehl et al. 20o3).Plant tissue chemistry is species-specific (Osborn et al. 2007, Jaffé etal. 2008, Cuss and Guéguen 20l3), so the N. pumilio forest of the Andean Patagonian region may be a more important P contributor to aquatic systems than other forest types would be. Nothofagus pumilio leaf litter biomass that enters lakes and streams can reach $2 0 0 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ dry mass in May (autumn) (Albarino et al. 2oo9),and this amount of litter can increase the TDP concentration in streams by ${ \sim } 3 5 \%$ (Diaz Villanueva et al.20l6). Similarly, our results suggest that the higher P content in $N .$ pumilio leachates causes the C:P ratio of lake DOM to decrease.Results from other ultraoligotrophic Northern Patagonian lakes showed that bacterial production increased in experimental treatments enriched with P (Bertoni etal.2oo8).Moreover,an increase in C-consumption was also observed because of the decrease in $\mathrm { P }$ limitation associated with the input of P-rich volcanic ashes to the lakes (Modenutti et al.2016).Accordingly, here we showed that the P-rich DOM of $N .$ pumilio leaf leachates increases bacterial C-consumption. Our study therefore advances our understanding of the key role of P in bacterial metabolism in ultraoligotrophic lakes and highlights the importance of C:nutrient ratios in DOM for C cycling in lake ecosystems.
