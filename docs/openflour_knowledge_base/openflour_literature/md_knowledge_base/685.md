# Seasonal and storm-driven changes in chemical composition of dissolved organic matter: a case study of a reservoir and its forested tributaries

Penghui Li1 $\cdot$ Sang Hee Lee 2 & Soo Hyung Lee 3 $\cdot$ Jun-Bae Lee 4 $\cdot$ Yun Kyung Lee1 & Hyun-Sang Shin5 & Jin Hur 1

Received: 6 May 2016 / Accepted: 14 September 2016   
$©$ Springer-Verlag Berlin Heidelberg 2016

Abstract Many drinking water supplies are located in forested watersheds, which operate as an important source of dissolved organic matter (DOM). In this study, monthly sampling campaigns were conducted from a reservoir (Daecheong Reservoir, South Korea) and its forested tributaries for five consecutive months (June to October) to examine the variations of DOM composition. Excitation-emission matrix fluorescence spectroscopy combined with parallel factor analysis (EEM-PARAFAC) was applied to track the variations of different fluorescent components within bulk DOM. Selected samples were further separated into hydrophobic (Ho) and hydrophilic (Hi) fractions. Water quality and DOM composition varied greatly with the sampling locations including the upstream and the downstream tributary sites, and the reservoir. Non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) provided the information on the DOM sources and the potential processes leading to the observed DOM changes. Four of the five fluorescent components, identified by EEM-PARAFAC, were well correlated with the flow rates of the tributaries, suggesting hydrological control on DOM composition. The greatest effects were found on two terrestrial humic-like components (C1 and C2). The Ho fraction of DOM was more abundant for the post-storm samples versus the non-storm samples, supporting the important roles of hydrology on the changes in chemical composition of DOM. The amounts of the DOM resin fractions, either Ho or Hi, showed strong relationships with C1 and C2, suggesting that DOM fluorescence could be successfully applied to estimate different DOM chemical constituents in forested watersheds.

Keywords Fluorescence $\cdot$ Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) $\cdot$ Flow rate $\cdot$ Hydrology $\cdot$ Resin fractionation

# Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) plays an important role in aquatic ecosystems, affecting drinking water quality (Findlay and Sinsabaugh 2003; Papageorgiou et al. 2015). DOM is involved in the cycling of carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus, acting as resources for food webs. Light-absorbing fraction of DOM influences the penetration and the distribution of solar radiation in water bodies, indirectly involving phytoplankton photosynthesis. It is also known that DOM reacts with disinfectant (halogen species) to form disinfection by-products (DBPs) (Nguyen et al. 2013; Papageorgiou et al. 2015; Yang et al. 2015). Knowledge about chemical composition of DOM is essential for understanding and predicting DOM fate and the reactivities in aquatic ecosystems. Dissimilar DOM constituents may have different reactivities with respect to various

biogeochemical processes (e.g., biodegradability) (He et al.   
2016) and also to DBP formation potential (Chow et al.   
2005; Imai et al. 2002; Imai et al. 2003; Wang et al. 2013).

Forested watershed is an important element of ecosystems, occupying $31 \%$ of total land area on earth (FAO 2010). Many rivers and lakes in forested areas are used as important water resources and habitats in mountainous countries. Forested watersheds are typically susceptible to climatic events, such as snowmelt, extreme drought, and heavy storms (Fasching et al. 2016). Moreover, such extreme climatic events occur more frequently these days due to global climate change (Easterling et al. 2000; Zhang et al. 2016). In South Korea, a mountainous country, climate and precipitation pattern are strongly influenced by East Asian Monsoons and most of the storms occurred primarily in the summer season.

Many studies have been conducted to evaluate the effects of storm events on DOM amounts and its composition in forested areas, in which increased DOM concentration and aromaticity were reported during a high precipitation period (Chen et al. 2015; Nguyen et al. 2013; Park et al. 2007; Zhou et al. 2016a). Biodegradable fraction of DOM was found to rise with increasing discharge in a forested stream (Wilson et al. 2016), while different trends were reported in other studies (Nguyen et al. 2010). However, there are few studies concerning chemical fraction information during storm events (Nguyen et al. 2010).

Resin fractionation is a conventional and well-developed method for chemical composition (hydrophobicity and hydrophilicity) of DOM (Benner et al. 2003; Imai et al. 2001; Leenheer and Croue 2003). However, the technique is rather laborious and time-consuming, and it needs additional treatment of the sample (e.g., acidification) (Imai et al. 2001). During the sample treatment processes, the addition of reagents (acid and alkali) could introduce contamination on the samples and it may result in environmental pollution due to improper disposal of reagents and waste cartridges. Fluorescence excitation-emission matrix (EEM) spectroscopy combined with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) has been widely used for exploring DOM composition, sources and fate in natural environments (Li et al. 2015; Nguyen et al. 2013; Wilson et al. 2016; Yang et al. 2015). Recently, EEMPARAFAC has been successfully applied to resinfractionated DOM (He and Hur 2015). However, there was no prior study on the estimation of DOM resin fractions through direct fluorescence measurement of original water samples.

Daecheong Reservoir (or Daecheong Lake), located in a forested watershed, is a major drinking water supply for three million people in a nearby city (Daejeon, Korea). The water quality and DOM of the reservoir and its forested tributaries are likely to be influenced by precipitation pattern and storm events during summer monsoon period. In this study, based on resin fractionation and fluorescence EEM-PARAFAC, we aimed to (1) characterize the spatial and temporal variations of DOM composition in the area, (2) evaluate hydrological roles on water quality and DOM, and (3) test the applicability of fluorescence EEM-PARAFAC to predict DOM chemical fractions. This study focuses on the instant changes in DOM chemical composition upon heavy storms occurring in a forested watershed rather than long-term variations of DOM composition.

# Materials and methods

# Study area and sample collection

Daecheong reservoir is located in the central part of South Korea, serving as one of the major water supplies in South Korea (Fig. 1). The reservoir is a large branch-type lake impounded in 1981 for the purposes of water supply, irrigation, hydropower generation, and flood control. It has a gross storage capacity of $1 4 9 0 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 }$ and a surface area of $7 2 . 8 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ . The precipitation of the basin mostly occurs during the months of June to August, and the dry season corresponds to the months from November to April, constituting only $20 \%$ of the annual precipitation $\left( 8 2 3 \mathrm { m m } \right)$ for the year of 2015. The highest precipitation was observed in June for the year.

Four forested tributary sites and three reservoir sites were chosen for sample collection (Fig. 1). Among the tributaries and the main channel flowing into the reservoir, Juwon (Ju) and Pumgok $( \mathrm { P u } )$ streams are surrounded by forest-dominant catchments $89 \%$ for Ju and $71 \%$ for $\mathrm { P u }$ ) although other types of land uses exist, including agricultural area $5 \%$ for Ju and $20 \%$ for $\mathrm { P u }$ ), grassland ( $1 . 7 \%$ for Ju and $2 . 3 \ \%$ for $\mathrm { P u }$ ), and residential area ( $1 . 8 ~ \%$ for both). Surface samples were collected from seven sites once a month from June to October. In late June, in which there was one heavy storm event with the daily precipitation of $5 3 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ for the selected tributaries (Fig. S1), four additional stream samples were taken from the chosen sites immediately after the storm in order to examine the post-storm effects on water quality and DOM composition. The post-storm sampling time was denoted as June′ (prime) for this study. Flow rates were measured for the tributary sites using a flowmeter (Valeport BFM002, UK).

# Water quality measurement

Temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen (DO), and electrical conductivity (EC) were measured on-site with a portable instrument (YSI 600R, USA). The collected samples were kept refrigerated upon return from the field. In the laboratory, raw water samples were used for total nitrogen (TN), total phosphorus (TP), chemical oxygen demand (COD), biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), and chlorophyll-a (Chl-a). The basic water quality parameters were determined following the standard methods (APHA et al. 2005). Water samples were filtered through $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ pre-ashed GF/F filter (Whatman, UK) for the measurements of dissolved organic carbon (DOC), and UV-visible and fluorescence spectroscopy.

# Spectroscopic analysis

Fluorescence spectra of DOM samples were obtained using a fluorescence spectrometer (Hitachi F7000, Japan). EEM spectra were generated for all samples in the range of $2 2 0 { - } 5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with $5 \mathrm { n m }$ -interval for excitation $\left( \operatorname { E x } \right)$ wavelengths, and $2 8 0 { - } 5 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ with 1-nm increment for emission wavelength (Em). Both excitation and emission slit widths were set to $1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and the scanning speed was set at $1 2 , 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m / m i n }$ . The voltage of PMT was set to $7 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m V } ,$ , and the instrument was run at automode. Ultrapure water $( { > } 1 8 . 2 \mathrm { M } \Omega ) _ { \mathrm { \Omega } } ^ { }$ ) was checked daily for the stability of the instrument and to normalize the spectra of all samples.

UV absorbance of all DOM samples was measured at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ with 1 cm cell using a UV-1800 spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Japan). The absorbance value (absorbance at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m } \ < 0 . 3 \ \AA$ was used to determine if sample dilution is necessary (Ohno 2002). All the samples had the absorbance values less than 0.3 at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , so no dilution was conducted for our samples.

# PARAFAC modeling

PARAFAC modeling was performed on MATLAB 2013a (Mathworks, USA) with the DOMFluor toolbox (http://www. models.life.ku.dk) (Stedmon and Bro 2008). Prior to the PARAFAC modeling, all EEM spectra were corrected using the instrument correction factors (both Ex and Em) in MATLAB (Murphy et al. 2010). The sample spectra were then blank subtracted by the ultrapure water EEM spectra and normalized to Raman peak area, which were reported in Raman unit (R.U.) (Lawaetz and Stedmon 2009; Murphy 2011; Murphy et al. 2010). Rayleigh and Raman scattering bands were cut for all EEM spectra, and non-negativity constraint was applied to all parameters to obtain only chemically relevant information. Split-half validation, Tucker’s congruence coefficients, and visual inspection were used to determine the number of fluorescent components (Stedmon and Bro 2008).

# Resin fractionation

In order to evaluate the post-storm effects on the changes of DOM chemical composition and to explore the correlations between DOM fluorescence and the composition, resin fractionation was performed using selected samples including those collected immediately after a heavy storm in late June (i.e., June′) and during a non-storm period in August according to the procedure described by Imai et al. (2001). Before the fractionation, the resin (Amberlite XAD-7, SIGMA) was purified by the sequential passage of $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m o l / L }$ NaOH, $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of DI water, $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of HCl, and $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of DI water through the column. The procedure was repeated three times to remove resin impurities. All samples for fractionation were acidified to a $\mathrm { p H }$ of ${ \sim } 2$ using HCl, and they were applied to a column packed with the cleaned XAD-7 resin $( 2 0 { - } 6 0 ~ \mathrm { m e s h } )$ to fractionate DOM into different chemical fractions. Briefly, $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ acidified samples were passed through the column with the flow rate of $1 \ \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ , and the fraction passing through the column was designated to hydrophilic fraction (Hi). Hydrophobic fraction (Ho) was calculated by subtracting Hi from the total DOC (samples before resin fractionation).

# Statistical analyses

Both $t$ test and paired $t$ test were conducted using the function Bt.test^ on R3.2.1 (R Core Team 2015) to compare the samples collected from different sites and periods. Non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) was conducted using the function BmetaMDS^ of the Bvegan^ package (Oksanen et al. 2016) on R3.2.1 (R Core Team 2015) to cluster sampling sites and also to elucidate the relationships among the water quality parameters and DOM variables.

# Results and discussion

# Spatial and temporal variations

# Variations of water quality

A large variation of water quality was found among the upstream and the downstream sites of the tributaries and the reservoir sites (Fig. 2). The levels of DOC, BOD, and TP were higher in the downstream sites than in the upstream sites for both two tributaries $( p < 0 . 0 5$ ; paired $t$ test), similar to European rivers reported by previous studies (Camargo et al. 2005; Rodgers et al. 2010), which could be explained by the enhanced flushing of soil organic matter due to the higher flow rates in downstream sites than in upstream sites (Zhang et al. 2011). TN showed similar concentrations for the upstream and the downstream sites in Ju $( p = 0 . 3 4$ ; paired $t$ test) and $\mathrm { P u }$ $\left( { p = 0 . 2 6 } \right)$ ; paired $t$ test). No significant differences in the concentrations of DOC, COD, and BOD were observed between the samples from the reservoir and the tributaries $( p > 0 . 1 0 )$ . However, TP was lower in the concentration for the reservoir compared to the tributaries $( p = 0 . 0 5 )$ . As expected, Chl-a concentrations were consistently higher in the reservoir than in the tributaries $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ . All the water quality parameters showed similar levels among the three reservoir sites except for BOD and Chl-a, which covaried with each other. Increased Chl-a level followed by autochthonous production appears to contribute to the enrichment of labile organic substances, resulting in the higher BOD concentrations ( $\mathrm { { X u } }$ and $\mathtt { X u 2 0 1 4 }$ ).

The temporal patterns in the variations were not the same for the water quality parameters in the tributary sites as well as in the reservoir sites (Fig. 2). For the tributary sites, nearly all water quality parameters (except for Chl-a) displayed sharp increases after the storm in late June (June′) and then showed declining trends from July to October. The temporal variations of the water qualityparametersweresimilartothetemporalpatternoftributary flow rates, and there were significant positive correlations between the flow rates and the water quality parameters $( n = 2 4$ , $p < 0 . 0 1 $ ), indicating the flushing effects and the hydrological control on these parameters (Zhang et al. 2011; Zhou et al. 2016a). In contrast, DOC and COD concentrations of the reservoir samples increased from June to October, while TN showed a decreasing trend in the reservoir sites, indicating that different biogeochemical processes might be involved. In the reservoir, BOD and Chl-a concentrations were higher in the months from July to October than in June, which could be explained by the enhanced primary production due to increased input of phosphorus during the high flow period (Camargo et al. 2005).

# Variations of DOM fluorescent components

Five fluorescent components were identified by PARAFAC modeling, which include four humic-like components (C1 to C4) and one protein-like component (C5) (Fig. 3). All the identified components were matched with those reported by previous studies using OpenFluor database (Table S1) (Murphy et al. 2014). Both C1 and C2 resemble a combination of the two traditional EEM peaks representing terrestrially derived humic substances (i.e., peak A and peak C) (Coble 1996; Garcia et al. 2015; Stedmon and Markager 2005a). C3 displays similar spectral pattern to a traditionally defined peak A (Coble 1996), which has been assigned as an agricultural humic-like substance (Zhou et al. 2016a). C4 has two excitation maxima at 295 and $2 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with its emission maxima at $4 1 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . C4 resembles a traditionally defined peak M, and it has been described as the products from microbial processes or autochthonous production (Kowalczuk et al. 2013; Walker et al. 2009; Zhang et al. 2009; Zhou et al. 2016b). Lastly, C5 exhibits its emission maxima at $3 5 2 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , and its primary and the secondary excitation maxima at ${ < } 2 2 0$ and $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. The protein-like component is comparable to another traditionally defined peak T, which has been depicted as tryptophan-like and/or protein-like substances (Coble 1996).

The five fluorescent components showed different spatial variations in the upstream, the downstream and the reservoir sites (Fig. 4). Two humic-like components of C1 and C2 were the highest in $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ values in the downstream sites, which was consistent with the characteristics of terrestrial sources (Coble 1996; Garcia et al. 2015; Stedmon et al. 2003). C3 and C5 showed higher concentrations in the downstream versus the upstream sites $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ ), suggesting that the related sources may be distributed along the tributaries, receiving their continuous inputs. However, the two fluorescent components did not exhibit significant differences between the downstream tributary sites and the reservoir sites $( p = 0 . 1 1 0$ and 0.128 for C3 and C5, respectively) except for C3 in October and C5 in September and October. No obvious difference was observed for C4 between the upstream and the downstream sites $( p = 0 . 7 9 5 )$ ), while C4 mostly showed the highest level in the reservoir sites. These results may be explained by the microbial nature of C4 (Stedmon and Markager 2005b) and the predominant autochthonous production in the reservoir evidenced by high Chl-a concentration. This is also supported by the long water retention time ${ \tt > } 2 0 0 $ days) of the reservoir (Jeong et al. 2011).

Different temporal patterns were found for the five fluorescent components (Fig. 4). Two humic-like components (C1 and C2) sharply increased after the heavy storm in late June and then steadily decreased from July to October in both the upstream and the downstream sites. These patterns were similar to the temporal variations of flow rates (Fig. 2), which could be explained by the flushing of soil organic matter (Zhang et al. 2011; Zhou et al. 2016a). Humic-like C3 and protein-like C5 showed similar temporal patterns as C1 and C2 in the downstream sites, while no substantial temporal variations were found for C3 and C5 in the upstream sites. In the reservoir, C1 and C2 had continuous increasing trends from June to October. The contrasting trends of C1 and C2 between the downstream and the reservoir sites from July to October imply that the two fluorescent components with recalcitrant nature may be accumulated in the reservoir. C3 was in a slight decreasing trend from June to July, but it showed a continuous increase afterward, also indicating the recalcitrant nature and its accumulation in the reservoir. C4 exhibited a sharp decrease from June to July regardless of the sampling sites, followed by a general increasing pattern since then. The trend could be attributed to the enhanced terrestrial input and dilution effects during the high flow period.

# NMDS clustering of sampling sites

NMDS could provide more detailed information on the spatial variations of water quality parameters and fluorescent components. For this study, all the samples were clustered into three groups by their locations (upstream and downstream tributaries, and reservoir) (the stress value of NMDS is 0.157) (Fig. 5). The upstream samples were highly associated with TN and COD, while the downstream samples were close to C1, C2, and TP. Meanwhile, the reservoir samples were more related to C4 and Chl-a, which can be attributed to the common characteristics of C4 and Chl-a related to the biological activities prevailing in reservoirs. It is notable that C4 and Chla are far from each other in the location of the NMDS matrix, suggesting the operation of different processes for the two parameters. Chl-a is directly associated with algal abundance and the primary production, while C4 is more related to microbial processing or photochemical processes (Helms et al. 2013; Li et al. 2015; Stedmon and Markager 2005b; Zhang et al. 2009). C3 and C5 lie in the positions between the downstream and the reservoir samples, suggesting that these two components are influenced by both terrestrial input and autochthonous processes. Furthermore, C3 was nearer to the downstream samples in the position. This result suggests that the input of terrestrial DOM sources in downstream sites may be more important than the primary production for the spatial distribution of C3. On the contrary, the location of C5 is nearer to those of the reservoir samples, implying that the primary production prevails over the terrestrial input.

# Effects of a heavy storm on water quality and DOM

# Response of water quality

After a heavy storm event in late June, all measured water quality parameters were largely increased in the concentrations of the upstream and the downstream tributary samples (DOC, COD, BOD, TN, and TP) (Fig. 2), suggesting strong effects of the storm on water quality (Nguyen et al. 2013). Compared to early June, DOC concentrations increased by three folds in the upstream sites of both tributaries and by more than two times in the downstream sites, indicating that DOC concentrations in the upstream sites were more susceptible to the storm event. However, BOD showed more enhancement in the downstream versus the upstream sites by the storm with percent increases of ${ \sim } 3 0 0 ~ \%$ and 25 to $100 \%$ , respectively. This could be partly explained by high flow rates at downstream sites since there was a good correlation between BOD and flow rate $( R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 9 6$ , $p \ < \ 0 . 0 0 1$ , $n = 2 4 ,$ ). However, even for the differences of the flow rates between the upstream sites and the downstream sites, the BOD enhancement at the downstream sites was obvious. The results suggest that BOD or a labile fraction of organic matter may be largely derived from anthropogenic and biological activities including agricultural lands in the downstream areas (Hur et al. 2008). The storm-driven increase of TN was also more pronounced in the upstream versus the downstream sites, suggesting that the sources of TN might be dominant in the upstream catchment. TP showed a large increase in the tributary sites after the storm, consistent with a previous study (Bida et al. 2015). Similar to BOD, the higher concentrations (6.6 to 14.6 times higher) were found for TP in the downstream sites than in the upstream sites. This is also supported by the previous NMDS result (i.e., TP is located within the downstream sites group), suggesting that TP could be derived more from downstream watershed than the upstream catchment.

# Response of DOM fluorescent components

Compared to the pre-storm period (i.e., early June), all the fluorescent components of the tributary samples were significantly increased after the storm with the exception of C4 $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ (Fig. 4). Both two terrestrial humic-like components (C1 and C2) showed large increases after the storm by 2–16 times, which coincided with previous studies (Garcia et al. 2015; Stedmon and Markager 2005a). The enhancement of C1 and C2 was more pronounced for the upstream sites (more than 4 times) than for the downstream sites (approximately 2 times), suggesting that the two humic-like components in the upstream sites were more susceptible to the storm event. The percent increase of C3 reached $2 5 \mathrm { - } 1 0 9 \ \%$ in all tributary sites except for an upstream site (Ju1).

Encouraged by the dramatic increase of the fluorescent components after the heavy storm, we examined the correlations between measured flow rates and each fluorescent component. As a result, four fluorescent components (C1 to C3, and C5) were significantly correlated with flow rates (Fig. 6), suggesting hydrological effects on fluorescent DOM (FDOM) (Park et al. 2007; Zhou et al. 2016a). The flow rate explained more than $50 \%$ of the variations in the two terrestrial humiclike components (i.e., C1 and C2) (Fig. 6). This could relate to the flushing of terrestrial DOM sources by surface runoff (Garcia et al. 2015; Stedmon and Markager $2 0 0 5 \mathrm { a }$ ; Zhang et al. 2011; Zhou et al. 2016a). The fluorescent components of C3 and C5 also showed good linear relationships with the flow rates $( R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 3 1 3$ , $p { = } 0 . 0 1$ for C3; $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 4 2 1$ , $p { = } 0 . 0 0 2$ for C5), indicating partial contribution of hydrological factors to the variations of the components. A prior study reported that the protein-like component could be related to the export of tryptophan-like fluorescent materials from forested catchments (Stedmon and Markager 2005a), corroborating our observation that C5 was enhanced with increasing flow rates. Although C3 is described as terrestrial/agricultural humiclike substance, its variation was not well explained by flow rates compared with those of C1 and C2. In fact, C3 was not a common fluorescent component found in aquatic environment as revealed by much fewer matches in OpenFluor database. No correlation between C4 and flow rate $( p = 0 . 9 4 )$ suggests that the abundance of the fluorescent component might not be controlled by hydrological factors. This finding also supported the previous NMDS result that C4 was located in the negative side of NMDS1, relating to microbial processes. Previous studies have demonstrated that the sources of the component could be diverse, including phytoplankton degradation (Stedmon and Markager 2005b; Zhang et al. 2010), wastewater and agriculture sources (Coble 2007), or photobleaching of humic-like fluorophore (Helms et al. 2014; Helms et al. 2013).

Meanwhile, the relative abundance of two humic-like components (i.e., $\% C 1$ and $\% { \mathrm { C } } 2$ ) exhibited positive but weak correlations with flow rates $( p < 0 . 0 5$ , $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 2 7$ and 0.37, respectively) (Fig. S2).

# DOM resin fractions and their correlation with DOM fluorescence

The concentrations of individual DOM resin fractions were affected by the sampling periods (i.e., post-storm period in June and non-storm period of August) and the locations (Table 1). Ho was the most dominant fraction $( 5 8 \pm 8 \ \% )$ in the tributary samples during the post-storm period. For the non-storm period, Hi was more dominant in the reservoir versus the tributary samples with the percent distribution of $8 6 \pm 4 \%$ . This is consistent with a previous study which found that DOM hydrophilic fraction prevailed hydrophobic fraction in the lake samples and vice versa in river samples (Imai et al. 2001). There was a significant difference in the relative abundance of Ho between the poststorm samples $( 6 1 \pm 6 \% )$ and those during the non-storm period $( 4 2 \pm 8 \% )$ $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ . A similar trend was reported in a storm flow driven by snowmelt with the hydrophobic fraction increased from 37 to $50 \%$ (Sebestyen et al. 2008).

# Conclusions

NMDS clarified the spatial variations of water quality parameters and FDOM components, distinguishing their sources and implying the potential biochemical processes. The greatest changes of water quality and FDOM components were found during the post-storm period, suggesting the strong effects of hydrology on water quality and DOM composition. The responses of the individual parameters were not the same depending on the sources at different sampling locations (i.e., upstream and downstream tributaries, and reservoir). Except for C4, the other four FDOM components were well correlated with the flow rate in the tributary samples, suggesting the hydrological control on DOM composition. The monthly and spatial distributions of DOM resin fractions revealed that the transport of DOM sources with more hydrophobic nature, driven by storms, might be important for the variation of DOM chemical composition. Strong correlations were found between terrestrial FDOM components and DOM resin fractions, suggesting the potential application of EEM-PARAFAC to predict the chemical composition of DOM in forested watersheds.
