Dissolved organic mater (DOM) is very important in determining the speciation,behaviors,and risk of metal polutants in aquatic ecosystems.Photochemical and microbial degradation are key processs in the cycling of DOM,yettheir effects on the DOM-Pb(II) interaction remain largely unknown.This was studied by examining the complexation of river DOM with Pb(I) after degradation, using fluorescence quenching titration and excitationemission matrices-parallel factor analysis (EEMs-PARAFAC).Three humic-like and two protein-like components were identified, with strong removals of humic-like components and decreasing average molecular weight and humification degree of DOM by photo-and photo-microbial degradation.The changes in humic-like abundance and structure resulted in notable weakening of their interaction with $\mathrm { P b ( I I ) }$ .The tryptophan-like C2 was also mainly removed by photo-degradation,while the tyrosine-like C3 could be either removed oraccumulated.The Pb(I)-binding of protein-like components was generally weaker but was enhanced in some degradation groups, which might be related to the lowering competition from humic-like components.The binding parameters correlated significantly with the DOM indices,which were dominated by photo-degradation for humic-like components but by seasonal variations for the tyrosine-like component.These results have implications for understanding the key mechanisms underlying the variability of the DOM-metal interaction in aquatic environments.

# 1．Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is a mixture of organic compounds from different sources and with variable structures,which is ubiquitous and plays important roles in the biogeochemistry and ecosystem functioning of aquatic environments (Hansell and Carlson, 2O15; Zhang et al.,2O21).In particular, the abundant functional groups of DOM can bind with heavy metal pollutants and have profound implications for the speciation,mobility,and risks of these harmful contaminants (Aiken et al., 2Ol1; Zhang et al., 2O2O). Lead (Pb) can persist and accumulate in aquatic environments,which represents an important heavy metal pollutant that poses risks to aquatic organisms and humans (Zhou et al., 2020;Fan et al.,2O2l).The environmental behaviors and ecological risks of Pb(I) are strongly affected by its complexation with DOM (Al-Reasi et al.， 201l； Habibul and Chen， 2018;Ren et al., 2022). Therefore,it is important to study the interaction between DOM and Pb (II) for pollutant controls and ensuring environmental quality.

The DOM-metal interaction is largely dependent on the concentration and chemical composition of DOM such as the aromaticity and molecular weight (Yamashita and Jaffe, 2008; Xu et al., 2019; Wang et al.，2022a; Fan et al.，2022,2023).Photochemical and microbial degradation are key processes in changing the concentration and composition of aquatic DOM (Lonborg et al., 201O; Chen and Jaffé, 2014; Cory and Kling, 2018; Zhao et al., 2023). Such changes may have profound implications for the metal-binding ability of DOM.For example,photo-irradiation can change the leading Hg(II)-binding sites of humic acid and fulvic acid,with stronger effects on the fulvic-like fraction than the protein-like fraction (Luo et al.， 2O22a，b).

Bio-degradation of lake DOM can remove protein-like components but produce humic-like components and enhance the binding affinity of humic-like components with $\operatorname { C u } ( \operatorname { I I } )$ and Pb(II) (Ren et al., 2022). In the bio-degradation of algal DOM,the release of microbial metabolites (e.g., the thiol-containing compounds) can bind with methylmercury and limit its uptake by phytoplankton (Li et al.,2O23). Similar to aquatic DOM that is rich in functional groups (e.g., COOH, OH, NH,and SH) to complex with metals such as Pb,soil DOM also contains abundant aromatic and humic substances that can bind with metals and changes their bioavailability (e.g.,Cd and Pb,Wang et al., 2O22b),with additional complexation with protein-like components (Chen et al., 202O; Zhang et al.，2O22).In fact,the aquatic DOM is mainly derived from allochthonous (e.g., soil) and autochthonous (e.g.,algae) sources. The response of the DOM-metal interaction to degradation may be different between the algal and soil DOM.For example, the DOM-Cu(II) binding is enhanced after microbial degradation for both algal and soil DOM,while photo-degradation increases the binding affinity of algal DOM but decreases that of soil DOM due to changes in the aromaticity and molecular size (Xu et al.,2Ol8a).However, there is little information on the effects of photo-degradation and combined photo-microbial degradation on the river DOM-Pb(II) interaction.In particular,the correlation between the DOM-Pb(II) binding and optical indices of DOM abundance/composition during the degradation remains not established.

Absorption and fluorescence spectroscopy are widely used in studying the biogeochemistry and environmental effects of DOM (Murphy et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2021). In particular, fluorescence excitation-emission matrices coupled with parallel factor analysis (EEMs-PARAFAC) can identify different fluorescent components of DOM and track their environmental behaviors (Murphy et al., 2014; Shi et al.,2021). The EEMs-PARAFAC,combined with the fluorescence quenching titration and modeling,is used in a number of studies to quantitatively investigate the metal-binding stability and binding fraction of different DOM components (Yamashita and Jaffe, 2008; Xu et al., 2018a, 2019; Chen et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022; Ren et al.,2022; Fan et al., 2023). Therefore,this study aimed to examine the changes in the interaction between different DOM components with Pb (II) by different degradation processes (i.g.，photo-,microbial,and combined photo-microbial degradation),using fluorescence quenching titration and EEMs-PARAFAC.The DOM samples from three typical sites (reservoir,river,and urban area) of a large subtropical river in the contrasting rainy and dry seasons were used for the study.The results would provide a scientific basis for understanding the variability of DOM-Pb(II) interaction under the influences of key biogeochemical processes.In particular,this is the first study to test the correlation between the metal-binding parameters and the fluorescence indices for the DOM subject to photo-and microbial degradation,and further to reveal the mechanisms underlying the correlations.

# 2.Materials and methods

# 2.1. Sample collection, degradation experiments,and fluorescence quenching titration

The DOM samples for degradation and binding experiments were collected from the surface water of three typical stations (M2,A6,and A9) in the Minjiang River (SE China,Figs. SlA-B),which is a large subtropical river with an annual freshwater discharge of about 58 billion $\mathfrak { m } ^ { 3 }$ that ranks 3rd in China (Ministry of Water Resources of the People's Republic of China,2023).A large hydropower station (the Shuikou Hydropower Station) is located in the middle river with a reservoir zone of ${ \sim } 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { k m }$ ，while the lower river flows through Fuzhou City,the capital city of Fujian Province with a population of 7.66 million (Zhuang et al.，2023).The A6 $( 1 1 8 ^ { \circ } 4 6 ^ { \prime } 2 7 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E }$ $2 6 ^ { \circ } 1 8 ^ { \prime } 5 3 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N } )$ ，A9 $( 1 1 9 ^ { \circ } 6 ^ { \prime } 3 2 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E } ,$ $2 6 ^ { \circ } 8 ^ { \prime } 4 3 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N } )$ ,and M2 $( 1 1 9 ^ { \circ } 1 5 ^ { \prime } 5 6 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E }$ $2 6 ^ { \circ } 5 9 ^ { \prime } 5 5 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N } )$ stations were located in the reservoir zone, free-flowing river zone,and urban zone,respectively. Samples were collected in summer (June 2022,namely A6s,A9s,and

M2s)and winter (December 2021,namely A6w,A9w,and M2w),corresponding to the rainy and dry seasons with monthly precipitation of 351 and $5 6 \mathrm { m m }$ (Fig. S1C).

Salinity and $\mathsf { p H }$ were measured in situ using a handheld Thermo Scientific Orion Star A Meter (Waltham,USA).The salinity was an indicator for the level of total dissolved ions,which showed a narrow range of $0 . 0 8 \% 0 0 { - } 0 . 1 4 \%$ in this study that were typical values for freshwater (Table S1).The pH was also generally stable around neutral conditions at 6.73-7.21.The Chl a was extracted by $9 0 \%$ acetone and was determined by the absorbance at 750,664,647,and $6 3 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (Table S1; Wang et al., 202O). The DOM samples were filtered sequentially through $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ GF/F filters which were pre-combusted at $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3h and $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate filters which were washed with the HCl acid and Milli-Q water.The concentration of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) was $1 . 1 7 { - } 2 . 5 9 ~ \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ ，which was measured using the high-temperature catalytic oxidation method with a Shimadzu TOC-L analyzer (Kyoto, Japan; Table S1; Yang et al., 2O19). The same DOM filtrates of the M2 station were subject to three types of degradation including photo-,microbial, and combined photo-microbial degradation (groups P,M,and PM, respectively).The DOM samples were transferred into a series of pre-combusted quartz tubes ( $\Omega 5 \mathrm { m L }$ ）which were then placed in tap water tanks and were subject to photo-degradation under natural solar irradiation on the roof of the college building in Fuzhou University.One-third of the M2s and M2w samples were added with $1 \%$ volume of microbial inocula before solar radiation to simulate combined photo-microbial degradation,which was the river filtrate through 2.7 $\mu \mathrm { m } { \mathrm { G F / D } }$ filters (Fellman et al., 2010; Yang et al., 2017). One-third of the M2s and M2w samples were added with similar microbial inoculaand were wrapped with aluminum foils as the dark group of only microbial degradation.The DOM of A6 and A9 stations were subject to photo-degradation which was the dominant process based on the results of M2 station and previous studies of DOM degradation (Helms et al., 2008; Yang et al., 2O19).All degradation experiments were performed for 5 days,and the local average solar radiation intensity was 3o4,735, and $6 5 3 ~ \mathrm { W ~ m } ^ { - 2 }$ during the degradation of M2w,M2s,and other DOM (A6 and A9),respectively (Fig. S2).

The original and degraded DOM were taken for the fluorescence quenching titration.The samples were added with the freshly prepared $\bar { 1 0 } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { \bar { L } } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { P b } ( \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } ) _ { 2 }$ solution at a series of concentrations of 0-100 $\mu \mathrm { M }$ (0,10,20,30,40,60,80,and $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ (Chen et al., 2022). The Pb $\mathrm { ( N O _ { 3 } ) _ { 2 } }$ concentrations were similar to the ranges in the literature (Habibul and Chen, 2018; Xu et al., 2018a), and the addition volume of $\mathrm { P b } ( \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } ) _ { 2 }$ solution was limited to $\leq 1 \%$ of the DOM sample.Then, the samples were shaken at ${ } ^ { 2 5 } \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark for $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ to reach the equilibrium (Plaza et al., 2O06; Pan et al., 2012),and were taken for optical measurements thereafter.The titration experiments were carried out in 2-3 duplicates for each Pb(I) concentration and the mean analytical precision (RSD) was $1 . 2 \% \pm 1 . 0 \%$ and $1 . 6 \% \pm 3 . 1 \%$ for absorption and fluorescence indices.

# 2.2. Optical measurements and fluorescence modeling

The absorbance of each DOM was scanned over the wavelength range of $2 4 0 { - } 8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ using a Shimadzu UV-1780 UV-Vis spectrometer (Kyoto,Japan).The measured absorbance values were corrected for the baseline shift by subtracting the average absorbance over $7 0 0 { - } 8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ The absorption coefficient at $2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m } \ ( a _ { 2 8 0 } )$ was used for indicating the level of chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM),and the absorption slope at 275-295 nm $( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ was also calculated from the spectra,which correlated negatively with the aromaticity and average molecular weight of DOM (Helms et al., 2008; Yang et al., 2017). The EEMs of DOM were scanned over excitation wavelengths (Ex） of $2 4 0 { - } 4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and emission wavelengths (Em) of $2 8 0 { - } 5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ,using an Agilent Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer (Santa Clara, USA; Yang et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2022). The measured fluorescence at each Exand Em wavelength of each EEM was corrected for the inner-filter effect with the absorbance values of each sample at the corresponding Ex and Em wavelengths (Kothawala etal., 2O13).The blank was subtracted with the signals of Milli-Qwater that were scanned on the same day,and the corrected fluorescence intensities were calibrated to the Raman Unit (RU) with the Raman peak of Milli-Q water.A total of 1079 EEMs were subject to the PARAFAC modeling,and the number of fluorescent components was determined with the split-half validation (Fig. S3, Stedmon and Bro, 20o8; Yang et al., 2017). The fluorescence intensity $\left( F _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } \right)$ of each component was used to indicate its abundance in each sample.

The changes in fluorescence intensity of each component $( F )$ with increasing Pb(II) concentration $( C _ { \mathrm { M } } )$ were modeled with the RyanWeber model using the following equation:

$$
\begin{array} { c } { { F = F _ { 0 } + \left( F _ { M L } - F _ { 0 } \right) \left( \displaystyle \frac { 1 } { 2 K _ { M } C _ { L } } \right) \left( 1 + K _ { M } C _ { L } + K _ { M } C _ { M } \right. } } \\ { { \left. - \sqrt { \left( 1 + K _ { M } C _ { L } + K _ { M } C _ { M } \right) ^ { 2 } - 4 { K _ { M } } ^ { 2 } C _ { L } C _ { M } } \right) } } \end{array}
$$

where $F _ { 0 }$ is the initial fluorescence intensity without the metal addition, $F _ { \mathrm { M L } }$ is the limited value when all ligands are complexed with Pb(II), $K _ { \mathrm { M } }$ is the conditional stability constant,and $C _ { \mathrm { { L } } }$ is the ligand concentration (Yamashita and Jaffe, 2Oo8). The binding fraction of each component $( f )$ was calculated as:

$$
f = \frac { \left( F _ { 0 } - F _ { M L } \right) } { F _ { 0 } } \times 1 0 0 
$$

The humification index (HIX) and biological index (BIX) were also calculated from EEMs,which were the ratio of fluorescence over Em $4 3 4 { \ - } 4 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ to that over $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 6 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (at $\operatorname { E x } 2 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ ),andtheratio of fluorescence at $\mathrm { E m } 3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that at $4 3 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (at $\operatorname { E x } 3 1 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ .HIXand BIX were widely used as indices for the humic content and autochthonous contribution of DOM,respectively (Zsolnay et al.,1999; Huguet et al., 2009).

# 4.Discussion

# 4.1.Changes in the Pb(I)-complexionof humic-like components by degradation

The changes of $\mathrm { P b ( I I ) }$ -binding with humic-like components were most evident after photo-degradation in this study.The fluorescence intensities of humic-like C1 and C5 were quenched effectively by Pb(II) for all samples (Figs.3 and 4, S4-6). The quenching degree,conditional stability constants $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { \mathrm { M } } ,$ and binding fraction $f$ of C1 and C5 were lower after photo-degradation than the initial DOM for most samples.These results indicated notable weakening effects of photo-degradation on the interaction between humic-like components and Pb(II). On one hand, the humic-like componentswere removed strongly by photodegradation,thus decreasing their concentrations and available sites for binding the metal (Fig.2). On the other hand, the lower binding parameters of the residuals than those for the initial DOM suggested weaker binding ability of humic-like C1 and C5 after photo-degradation, which might be related to changes in their structures such as the bond cleavage that resulted in the loss of certain functional groups for binding the metal. This was partly supported by the increasing $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and decreasing HIX in this study,which indicated the lowering aromaticity, molecular weight,and humic content of DOM (Fig.2, Text S1; Helms et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2019). Previous studies showed that the spatial variations of the aromaticity and molecular weight of DOM were important in determining the metal-binding of DOM (Xu et al., 2019; Wang etal., 2022a; Fan et al., 2022, 2023). The molecular-level analysis using ultra-high resolution mass spectrometry in previous studies also revealed effective photo-degradation of aromatic and unsaturated compounds such as lignins and tannins which contained rich carboxyl and hydroxyl groups (Stubbins et al., 201O; Harir et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023).

The fluorescence quenching of another humic-like component C4 was also weakened after photo-degradation of most samples (Fig. 4).

However,C4 fitted with the quenching model only in the titration of five DOM samples (Fig.5) and its fluorescence increased with the addition of Pb(II) in some titration groups (Figs. 3 and 4, S4-S6). The increasing fluorescence by metal can be attributed to potential higher fluorescence quantum yield after complexation,the release of fluorophore-metal complexes from the initial DOM matrices,and/or the replacement of the metals that are present in the original sample and bind DOM with stronger quenching than the added metal (e.g.,Pb(I) in this study) (Yamashita and Jaffe, 2Oo8).In addition, C4 had a similar Em wavelength with C5 buta longer Ex wavelength than C5, thus the potential red-shift of C5 by the complexation with Pb(II) might also partly contribute to the increase of C4 (Fig.1).

Microbial degradation also weakened the fluorescence quenching of humic-like C1 in the M2s and M2w samples and C4 in the M2s sample, but had little effect on the quenching of C5 in the M2s sample and even enhanced the quenching of C5 in the M2w sample (Fig.4). This indicated the potential effects of microbial degradation on the metal-binding of DOM which could be attributed to the changing DOM properties such as the molecular weight and humic content.However, there were limited changes in the $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { \mathrm { M } }$ and $f$ of Cl,C4,and C5 by microbial degradation, except for a larger decrease in the $f$ of C1 in the M2s sample (Fig.5).In addition,the effects of combined photo-microbial degradation on the quenching of humic-like components were closer to the effects of photo-degradation (Fig.4). Overall, the results indicated weaker effects of microbial degradation than photo-degradation on the Pb(I)- binding of humic-like components in this study.This was consistent with the dominant role of photo-degradation in changing the DOM properties in the short-term photo-microbial degradation (Fig.2, Text S1).However,a recent study showed stronger changes in the DOMmetal binding after 28-day bio-degradation (Ren et al.,2022).Therefore,the importance of microbial degradation would increase with a longer degradation time and for more biologically labile DOM.In addition,our degradation experiments were performed in clear surface water and microbial degradation may be more important in more turbid and/or dark deep waters with lower light availability and/or higher microbial abundance and activity.It is worth noting that the same DOM filtrates of the M2 station were subject to the photo-,microbial,and photo-microbial degradation in the same water bath under the same solar radiation for the same period,to make the photo-degradability and microbial degradability comparable.The DOM from A6 and A9 stations were also photo-degraded under the same conditions.The different condition was mainly related to the lower solar radiation for the M2w DOM, which might result in a weaker photo-degradation degree. However,there were still notable photo-degradation signals for the M2w DOM such as the large decreases of humic-like C1, C5,and HIX (Fig. 2, Table S3), which might be related to an enough irradiation time of up to five days.

# 4.2.Changes in the Pb(I)-complexion of protein-like components by degradation

The effects of degradation processes on the Pb(II)-binding of proteinlike components were different from those of humic-like components. The fluorescence of tryptophan-like C2 was generally quenched weakly $( < 1 0 \% )$ by $\mathrm { P b ( I I ) }$ in this study,although the quenching was enhanced in a few samples $( 1 3 . 9 \% - 2 1 . 8 \%$ for the microbially degraded M2s and photodegraded M2w and A6s DOM, Fig.4).The quenching of tyrosinelike C3 was also enhanced after the microbial degradation of M2s DOM but was either weakened (e.g.,A9s) or enhanced (e.g.,M2w) by photodegradation.In addition，the quenching modeling revealed a low binding fraction for C2 (f $1 0 . 6 \% { - 2 3 . 1 \% } )$ and a variableffor C3 $( 1 6 . 4 \% -$ $6 2 . 1 \%$ ,although the number of modeling results was partly limited by the weak quenching of protein-like components in many samples (Figs. 4 and 5).Generally,the preferential degradation of humic-like components by photo-degradation would decrease their competition with protein-like components for binding metals (Timko et al. 2O14; Yang et al.,2O19).In addition, the protein-like components could be either photochemically/microbially degraded or accumulated as products of photo-degradation and microbial activity,which would further complex the Pb(II)-binding behaviors of protein-like components (Fig.2,Text S1; Chen and Jaffé, 2014; Zhu et al., 2018).It is noteworthy that DOM is composed of several thousands of compounds with different structures, thus a PARAFAC component is probably representative of several compounds that share similar fluorophores but maybe with diferent structures,reactivities,and metal-binding abilities.For example,a recent study successfully applied ultra-performance liquid chromatography coupled to quadrupole time of flight mass spectrometry to identify the release of microbial metabolites with a high binding afnity with methylmercury during the bio-degradation of algal DOM (Li et al., 2023).However, the metal-binding of one or more compounds in the PARAFAC component results in fluorescence quenching，and the modeling of the relationship between fluorescence intensity and metal concentration can provide quantitative information on the conditional stability constant and binding fraction.These quantitative results can be seen as the average values of the compounds within each PARAFAC component,which are important for understanding the variability of DOM-metal interaction as proved by many studies (Yamashita and Jaffe, 2008; Xu et al., 2018a, 2019; Chen et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023).

# 4.3.Correlations of binding with optical indices and the underlying mechanisms

The influences of changing DOM properties by degradation processes on the metal binding of DOM were further supported by the correlation analysis between binding parameters and DOM indices (Table 1, Fig. 6). Itis noteworthy that the DOM samples in this study were collected from different sites in contrasting seasons and the M2 samples were subject to three types of degradation processes.In particular,the quenching percent of humic-like C1 and C5 and the $\mathrm { l o g } K _ { \mathrm { M } }$ of C5 correlated positively with the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of humic-like components (C1,C4,and C5) but correlated negatively with $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ .These results suggested more active interaction between humic-like components (C1 and C5) and $\mathrm { P b ( I I ) }$ at a higher humic-like abundance and higher average molecular weight of DOM,which were consistent with previous results from spatial investigations (Xu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022a; Fan et al., 2022). The $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of humic-like C1, C4, C5,HIX, and $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ changed to a larger degree by photo-degradation and photo-microbial degradation than the spatial and seasonal variations (Figs.2 and 6). Therefore, the degradation of humic-like components (e.g.，the preferential removal by photo-degradation) played a major role in affecting their binding with Pb(II),i.e.weakening the complexation due to both decreasing abundance and structural changes (Fig. 2).

In contrast,the quenching percent of tyrosine-like C3 correlated negatively with $a _ { 2 8 0 }$ ,the $F _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of C2 and C3,and BIX,which indicated weaker $\mathrm { P b ( I I ) }$ -bindingat higher levels of CDOMand protein-like components with a higher autochthonous contribution.Protein-like C2 and C3 were much higher in winter than in summer samples,which played a major role in their variations (Figs.2 and 6).Therefore, the correlations for the quenching of C3 were largely due to the seasonal changes in the protein-like abundance,which were probably related to the higher autochthonous production in winter with higher ( $\operatorname { \tt A l } a ( 2 . 8 { - } 2 0 . 6 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ ， lower HIX (2.3-2.4),and higher BIX (0.82-0.87) than in summer $( 1 . 4 { - } 1 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ ,7.9-8.2,and 0.65-0.69,respectively, Table S1, Fig. 2; Zhang et al., 2009; Romera-Castillo et al., 2010).

Finally,environmental factors such as $\mathsf { p H }$ and major ions,in particular,if they changed notably,could also afect the metal-binding of DOM in aquatic environments (Xu et al., 2018b; Chen et al., 2022). However, the $\mathsf { p H }$ values were generally stable at 6.73-7.21 around neutral conditions for all samples in this study.The major cations are $\mathsf { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ $\mathrm { M g } ^ { 2 + }$ $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ ,and $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , while the major anions include $\mathrm { H C O } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ ， $\mathrm { C l ^ { - } }$ and $S O _ { 4 } ^ { 2 - }$ in the Minjiang River (Li, 2009), which might compete with Pb (II) and DOM for the complexation (Chen et al.,2022).However,all sampling sites in this study were located in the freshwater zone of the Minjiang River,and the salinity that indicates the level of total dissolved ions was low and within a narrow range of $0 . 0 8 \% 0 0 { - } 0 . 1 4 \%$ ，The urban site M2 might be further affected by the anthropogenic inputs of ions (phosphates,nitrates,etc),although the effect was probably weak in the large Minjiang River since the salinity was similar for M2 and the other two sites.These results indicated an overall limited effect of variable solution chemistry on the DOM-Pb(II) interaction in this study,which was consistent with the dominant role of photo-degradation in shaping the correlations between the $\mathrm { P b ( I I ) }$ -binding of humic-like components (Fig.6).The effects of solution chemistry of larger variability on the changing DOM-Pb(II) interaction after degradation is worth further exploring in the future studies.

Overall,this study demonstrated the weakened Pb(I)-binding of humic-like components and the enhanced binding of tryptophan-like C2 by photo-irradiation,which was similar to previous findings for the interactions between $\operatorname { C u } ( \operatorname { I I } )$ and soil DOM (Xu et al., 2018a) and between $\mathrm { H g ( I I ) }$ and fulvic acid (Luo et al.,2O22a,b). The effect of microbial degradation was limited in our study but can result in stronger binding of humic-like components with Cu(II) and Pb(II) after longer degradation of more labile samples (Ren et al.,2O22). Both production and removal of protein-like components were observed in different groups of this study,which resulted in variable changes in the Pb(I)-binding of protein-like C2 and C3 (section 4.2).This was similar to previous findings of the balance between degradation and the releases of either microbial metabolites or photodegraded products (Ren et al.， 2022； Li et al.,2023). Compared with previous studies,this study provided new evidence for the effect of photo-degradation on the Pb(I)-DOM binding that was also dominated in the short-term photo-microbial degradation. The correlation between the metal-binding parameters and the fluorescence indices for the DOM subject to photo-and microbial degradation was established, which clearly proved the importance of changing DOM properties in the metal binding and was useful for further identifying the relative importance of degradation and seasonal variability in shaping the correlations.

# 5．Conclusions

The changes in DOM properties by degradation had significant implications for the DOM-Pb(II) interaction. The Pb(II)-binding of humiclike C1 and C5 generally decreased after photo-degradation and photo-microbial degradation,which were probably related to both their decreasing concentrations and structural changes.The Pb(II)-binding of protein-like components was generally weaker but was enhanced in some samples after degradation, which might be caused by the lowering competition from humic-like components.The binding parameters and the DOM properties were correlated significantly，with the photodegradation dominating the correlations for humic-like components and the seasonal variations dominating the correlations for the tyrosinelike component. The tight relationship between DOM properties and its Pb(II)-binding suggested the potential of inferring the metal binding of DOM based on spectroscopic indices that can be measured conveniently. The results would have implications for better understanding the behavior and effects of metal pollutants under the influences of different DOM and key degradation processes.
