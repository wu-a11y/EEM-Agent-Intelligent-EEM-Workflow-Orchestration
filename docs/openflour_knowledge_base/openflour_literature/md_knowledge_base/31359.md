# Abstract

Some bacteria can degrade organic micropollutants (OMPs) as primary carbon sources. Due to typically low OMP concentrations, these bacteria may benefit from supplemental assimilation of natural substrates present in the pool of dissolved organic matter (DOM). The biodegradability of such auxiliary substrates and the impacts on OMP removal are tightly linked to biotransformation pathways. Here, we aimed to elucidate the biodegradability and effect of different DOM constituents for the carbofuran degrader Novosphingobium sp. KN65.2, using a novel approach that combines pathway prediction, laboratory experiments, and fluorescence spectroscopy. Pathway prediction suggestedthatring_hydroxylationreactionscatalysedbyRieske-type dioxygenases and flavin-dependent monooxygenases determine the transformability of the 11 aromatic compounds used as model DOM constituents. Our approach further identified two groups with distinct transformation mechanisms amongst the four growth-supporting compounds selected for mixed substrate biodegradation experiments with the pesticide carbofuran (Group 1: 4-hydroxybenzoic acid， 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde; Group 2: $p$ -coumaric acid, ferulic acid). Carbofuran biodegradation kinetics were stable in the presence of both Group 1 and Group 2 auxiliary substrates. However, Group 2 substrates would be preferable for bioremediation processes，as they showed constant biodegradation kinetics under different experimental conditions (pregrowing KN65.2 on carbofuran vs. DOM constituent). Furthermore，Group 2 substrates were utilisable by KN65.2 in the presence of a competitor (Pseudomonas fluorescens sp. P17). Our study thus presents a simple and costefficient approach that reveals mechanistic insights into OMP-DOM biodegradation.

# INTRODUCTION

The global occurrence of organic micropollutants (OMPs)，including pesticide and pharmaceutical residues，is of major concern for aquatic ecosystems (Fenner et al.， 2013; McKnight et ai., 2015;Wikinson et al.， 2022). In this context, microbially mediated biodegradation plays an important role for OMP removal (Benner et al., 2013; Davenport et al., 2022; Rucker & Kummerer，2012)． In particular， growth-linked OMP biodegradation can be key for effective bioremediation or bioaugmentation processes (Helbling， 2015). Since OMP concentrations are usually low, bacteria growing on OMPs can benefit from additionally taking up natural carbon and energy sources for their survival or growth (Branco et al.， 2024; Egli, 2010; Egli & Mason,1991; Wang et al., 2022). These auxiliary substrates may be comprised of monomers present in the complex mixture that represents natural dissolved organic matter (DOM; Kaplan & Cory，2016； Kaplan & Newbold， 2003;

Perdue& Ritchie,2014). Yet, the classes of DOM constituents that can be metabolised by OMP degraders and the net effects of their metabolism on OMP biodegradation remain unclear.

We postulate that OMP_degrading bacteria may grow on DOM constituents with structural similarities to OMPs,as they may harbour the enzymes required for their biodegradation. Many OMPs have aromatic moieties (Fenner et al., 2013; Wilkinson et al.,2022), hence natural aromatic compounds that are known to be constituents of DOM， such as lignin-phenols or aromatic amino acids (Kaplan & Newbold，2003； Perdue & Ritchie, 2014; Thurman, 1985)，might serveas growth-supporting auxiliary substrates. Novosphingobium species from various habitats, for instance， harbour oxygenases required for both the biodegradation of various natural and xenobiotic aromatic compounds (Gan et al., 2013; Kumar et al., 2017). Even though aromatic compounds typically show similar transformation pathways (i.e.， aromatic ring hydroxylation followed by electrophilic ring fission；Ulrich & Hofrichter，2007; Vaillancourt et al., 2006), the specific type of oxidative reaction required for compound transformation can change significantly depending on small structural entities，such as the type of aromatic ring substituents. These entities can thus significantly affect compound biodegradability. For example，aromatic compounds withelectron-donating substituents (activated compounds） are often hydroxylated by flavin-dependent monooxygenases (FDMOs； Paul et al.， 2021)，while dioxygenases with a non-heme iron centre are commonly required for dihydroxylating unactivated aromatic compounds (compounds lacking electron-donating ring substituents; Kal&Que, 2017; UlIrich& Hofrichter, 2007). We recently tested the transformability of 13 model DOM constituents by the pesticide degrader strain Novosphingobium sp. KN65.2. Despite structural similarities amongst all 13 compounds (small monoaromatic compounds)， only compoundswith para-hydroxylated aromatic rings could be transformed (Schitich et al.,2022). Hence,linking substrate biodegradability to structural characteristics and biotransformation mechanisms isessential to classifyand ultimately predict the classes of DOM constituents that serve as auxiliary substrates for a certain type of OMP degrader.

Beyond assessing their biodegradability, investigatingthe biotransformation mechanismsof growthsupporting DOM constituents is important as they can impact OMP removal. In particular,auxiliary substrates that are degraded by the same pathway as OMPs can bebeneficial for bioremediation purposes (Deng et al.，2022; Sun et al.， 2016). Such substrates can induce and maintain the OMP biodegradation pathway and thus enhance biodegradation kinetics (Deng et al.,2022; Sun et al., 2016), when substrate competition for the degrading enzymes is minor (Rentz et al.， 2Oo4). For instance,a recent study found that dioxane biodegradation by Azoarcus sp. DD4 was promoted through pathway induction by the auxiliary substrate propane in microcosm experiments representing different freshwater environments (Deng et al., 2022). Conversely，if OMPs and auxiliary substratesare degraded by different pathways, the presence of auxiliary substrates might increase the growth of the OMP degrader,but not the expression of the OMP biodegradation pathway (Cheyns et al., 2012; Deng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2010). Such auxiliary substrates can promote OMP bioremediation through enhanced overall growth as long as they do not repress the OMP biodegradation pathway (Cheyns et al., 2012； Li et al., 2017; Rentz et al., 2004). Revealing these different mechanisms is essential to understanding the fate of OMPs in natural systems and to supporting models simulating DOMOMP interactions (Brandt et al., 2003; Liu et al., 2014, 2019).Insights can be used to further support the development and appropriate deployment of bioremediationprocesses(Aidas-Vargasetal., 2021; Helbling, 2015; Liu et al., 2019).

The experimental investigation and interpretation of biodegradation pathways is a challenging and timeconsuming task. The process can be supported by predictive tools like the Eawag pathway prediction system (PPS)anditsaccompanyingbiodegradation/ biocatalysis database (BBD; Eilis & Wackett， 2012). The Eawag PPS predicts aerobic biotransformation reactions based on a compound's chemical structure and provides information on enzyme classes possibly catalysingthepredictedreactions(Ellis& Wackett， 2012).The tool thus provides meaningful insights regarding the transformation mechanisms of compounds with specific structural moieties and can guide the experimental investigation of biodegradation pathways and transformation intermediates (Achermann et al.， 2018). During such experiments, OMP biotransformation intermediates are usually analysed with high-resolution mass spectrometry (HRMS). A time- and cost-efficient alternative to HRMS,when studying aromatic moieties, is the use of fluorescence spectroscopy， particularly when combined with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC； Cabugao et al.， 2022; Dubber et al.， 2021； Logue et al.， 2016； Paulsen et al., 2019). PARAFAC analysis allows the decomposition of a sample's bulk fluorescence signal into its underlying independentcomponents(Murphy et al.,2013).When working with chemically identifiable compounds and mixtures thereof, the approach is further capable of isolating the autofluorescence of the individual substrates from the background media and allows tracing their development at nanomolar concentrations (Paradina-Fernandez et al.， 2023； Sjostedt et al., 2022).

In this study,we aimed to explain the biodegradabilityandbiodegradationmechanismsofdifferent monoaromatic DOM constituents by the carbofuran (pesticide)degraderstrain Novosphingobium sp. KN65.2 (Nguyen et al.， 2015, 2014). We used a novel approach that applies the Eawag PPS prediction system in combination with laboratory experiments and fluorescence spectroscopy. First, a theoretical Eawag PPS analysis aimed to reveal enzymatic reactions responsible for the transformability of 11 selected model DOM constituents，whose transformation by KN65.2 was tested previously (Schittich et al.， 2022). We then selected four growth-supporting compounds representing different chemical classes (two benzoic acid derivatives and two cinnamic acids), to further elucidate their effects on carbofuran biodegradation together with their transformation mechanisms.For this purpose，mixed substrate biodegradation experiments were conducted， containing the pesticide carbofuran and one auxiliary substrate at a time. The approach aimed to identify auxiliary substrates that have the same biodegradation pathway，postulating that these compounds may have the same impact on carbofuran biodegradation.Furthermore，potentialoverlaps between enzymes catalysing carbofuran- and auxiliary substrate biodegradation were investigated by comparing the biodegradation kinetics of carbofuran and auxiliary substrates for different experimental conditions.

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Theoretical investigation

Background: DOM constituents and pesticide degrader

A theoretical investigation of transformation pathways was carried out for 11 model DOM constituents, represented by compounds with known chemical structures(Figure 1). Sixofthesecompounds (4-hydroxybenzoic acid,4-hydroxybenzaldehyde, vanillic acid, $p$ -coumaric acid， ferulic acid，and L-tyrosine) had previously been shown to support the growth of the carbofuran (carbamate pesticide) degrader strain Novosphingobium sp. KN65.2 (LMG 28221； Nguyen et al., 2014; Schitich et al., 2022), which is used as the reference OMP degrader in this study. The remaining five compounds (benzoic acid，salicylic acid [SAL], piceol [Pl], cinnamic acid,and L-phenylalanine) do not support the carbofuran degrader's growth.All growthsupporting compounds contain a hydroxyl group in the para-position of the aromatic ring (Figure 1A)，while onlyoneofthenon-transformable compounds (PI)contains this feature (Figure 1B Schittich et al., 2022).

# Pathway prediction and identification of key enzyme classes

The Eawag PPS (http://eawag-bbd.ethz.ch/predict/) was used to predict possible aerobic transformation pathways for the 11 model DOM constituents. The PPS is based on so-called biotransformation rules (bt-rules), which define a set of 249 reactions that describe microbial catabolic transformation mechanisms for specific chemical structures (Ellis & Wackett， 2012). in our study, the sequences of predicted transformation reactions were analysed until the cleavage of the aromatic ring.Downstream pathways for cleaved intermediates were not further considered since cleaved compounds are commonly more easy to degrade，and thus not expected to influence biodegradability (Schwarzenbach et al., 2016). The predicted pathways were expressed as the sequence of the predicted bt-rules.The identified bt-rules were further linked to fourth-level enzyme commission (EC) numbers using the Eawag biodegradation/biocatalysis database (BBD) to provide insights into enzyme classes likely catalysing certain biotransformation reactions. The predicted pathways were then compared amongst the different compounds，investigating patterns between transformable versus nontransformable compounds and against the pathway suggested for the biodegradation of the pesticide carbofuran by KN65.2 (Nguyen et al., 2014).

# Comparing the draft genome sequence of KN65.2 to the key enzyme classes identified by pathway prediction

The Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) was used to investigate if the mono- and dioxygenases, which were identified as critical drivers for the biodegradability of the model DOM constituents， are encoded in the genome of KN65.2. For this purpose, we first searched the genome of KN65.2 for mono- and dioxygenases acting on aromatic rings (EC classes 1.13 and 1.14). This was done by investigating the presence of the prototype oxygenases listedin the NCBl database under the EC classes 1.13 and 1.14 in the draft genome sequence of KN65.2 (accession no. CCBH00000000; Nguyen et al., 2015) using protein BLAST (https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Blast.cgi). In particular, we compared the EC 1.13 and 1.14 prototype enzymes to the 70 genes of KN65.2 that have been classified as putative oxygenases (Nguyen et al.,2015). All BLAST queries were run with an ‘expect value threshold’ (e value threshold) of 0.05. Amongst the detected hits，sequences showing the lowest e value and the greatest bit score were considered as significantly similar to the query sequence. The prototype oxygenases found to be encoded in KN65.2 were subsequently compared against the key enzymes identified by pathway prediction (Eawag PPS-BBD). It should be noted that the Eawag BBD analysis was based on fourth-level EC numbers,which refer to specific enzymes.On the contrary, the protein sequences of prototype enzymes represent third-level EC numbers,defining enzyme sub-classes, but lacking information on specific enzymes due to insufficient NCBl database entries for fourth-level EC numbers.

# Biodegradation batch experiments

Fourofthegrowth-supporting compounds (4-hydroxybenzoic acid， 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde， $p \mathrm { . }$ coumaric acid，and ferulic acid，see Figure 1）were selected and further studied in mixed substrate biodegradation experiments with the pesticide carbofuran.

These four compounds are referred to as auxiliary substrates in the following.

# Mixed substrate experiments

Biodegradation experiments were conducted under aseptic conditions as described previously (Schittich et al.，2022). Each mixed substrate experiment contained two carbon sources: the pesticide carbofuran and one of the four auxiliary substrates. The total carbon concentration at the beginning of each experiment was $1 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ C / L }$ ， consisting of equal amounts (wt/wt) of both carbon sources. To ensure that carbon was the limiting growth factor，all other nutrients and minerals were provided in excess using an ABT mineral medium (Schittich et al., 2022). All experiments were conducted with the carbofuran degrader strain KN65.2,which was inoculated to provide a starting cell concentration of $1 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 }$ cells/mL at a total batch volume of $1 2 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ . All experiments were run in duplicate.Furthermore,abiotic controls,containing growth medium without cells,were run for each substrate combination to validate that substrate disappearance was linked to biodegradation. Samples for optical density (OD） and fluorescence measurements were taken at regular time intervals. Samples for fluorescence analysis were further filtered through a $0 . 2 \mu \mathsf { m }$ Agilent nylon filter and stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ until analysis. Sample pH was measured at the beginning and end of each experiment to ensure a pH in the range of 6.5-7.5.

# Varying pre-culturing conditions

All mixed substrate experiments were conducted twice under two different pre-culturing conditions. Pregrowing KN65.2 on the pesticide carbofuran (referred to as standard experimental conditions） ensured the activation of the carbofuran pathway at the start of the experiment. In addition, pre-culturing KN65.2 solely on the auxiliary substrate of the respective experiment (varied pre-culturing condition) aimed at activating the enzymes required for auxiliary substrate biodegradation at the start of the experiment. In both cases, the pre-culturing of KN65.2 was conducted in two sequentialpre-growingsteps， asdescribedpreviously (Schittich et al., 2022).

# Mixed substrate biodegradation in the presence of a competitor

For two auxiliary substrates( $p$ -coumaric acid and 4-hydroxybenzoic acid)，mixed substrate experiments were additionally carried out in the presence of a competitor strain. For this purpose,KN65.2 was inoculated together with the Pseudomonas fluorescens sp. P17—a strain commonly used to determine assimilable organic carbon in water samples (Hammes & Egli, 2005; Tang et al., 2018; van der Kooij, 2002). This setup allowed us to investigate the influence of a competitor (P17） on mixed substrate biodegradation by KN65.2. For all competition experiments，KN65.2 was pre-cultured on carbofuran, while P17 was pre-cultured onacetate.Thestartingcellinoculumwas $2 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 }$ cells/mL，consisting of equal concentrations of both strains. In addition， control experiments with a pure P17 inoculum were conducted in parallel, to verify that the strain grew as expected, and as a reference for biodegradation endpoints.

# Single substrate controls

Single substrate,pure culture (KN65.2) control experiments were conducted for carbofuran and each of the four auxiliary substrates individually. Single substrate experiments served as a reference for biodegradation kinetics and further helped to link the transformation intermediates that were observed in mixed substrate experiments to parent compounds. The experiments were conducted under the same conditions as mixed substrate experiments but with a total starting concentration of $5 \mathrm { m g } \ \mathrm { C / L }$ . Carbofuran control experiments were conducted for five different pre-culturing conditions，pre-culturing KN65.2 on carbofuran and each of the auxiliary substrates individually,while auxiliary substrate controls were conducted only under standard conditions (KN65.2 pre-grown on carbofuran).

A more detailed overview of all the experimental combinations(substrates， pre-culturingconditions, strains) is provided in Tables S1 and S2.

# Monitoring cell growth

Total cell concentrations for pure and mixed culture experiments were estimated from OD measurements at $5 0 0 \mathsf { n m }$ ，which were transformed to cell concentrations (cells/mL）basedonapreviouslyestablished regression between OD and cell density. To estimate strain-specific cell concentrations for mixed culture experiments， samples from these experiments were plated in different dilutions on selective media，as described previously (Schittich et al.， 2022). In brief, KN65.2 was cultivated on carbofuran agar, which solely supports the growth of the OMP degrader,while strain P17 was cultivated on benzoic acid agar,which does not support the growth of KN65.2 (see Figure 1B). Selective plating was conducted at two specific time points (after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and after 48 h) for each experiment.

# Tracing auxiliary substrate biodegradation and transformation intermediates with fluorescence spectroscopy

Fluorescence measurements and treatment

Organic matter fluorescence was measured with a HORIBA Aqualog fluorometer using a 1 cm quartz cuvette. Emission excitation matrices (EEMs) were collected for excitation wavelengths between 240 and $6 0 0 \mathsf { n m }$ at $3 \mathsf { n m }$ increments, for which emission wavelengths were recorded between 210 and $6 2 0 \mathsf { n m }$ (incrementsof $3 . 2 8 \mathsf { n m }$ ，instrumentdefault).All measurements were processed in MATLAB R2020b using the drEEM toolbox v.0.6.5 (Murphy et al., 2013). Samples were blank,subtracted, corrected for inner filter effects，and converted to Raman units (R.U.; Murphy et al.， 2013). First- and second-order water scatter was removed and interpolated across if required for further data processing.

# Signal decomposition

Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC) was used to decompose the bulk fluorescence signals into their underlying fluorophores (Murphy et al., 2013). The fluorescence from each experimental setup (individual substrates and each substrate combination) was modelled independently. Theindividual datasets contained between 23 and 112 samples (Tables S1 and S2). Abiotic controls were not included to not bias the PARAFAC decomposition by having the majority of the signal in the dataset due to high substrate (i.e., parent compound) fluorescence alone,which would challenge the identification of fluorescent metabolites. Similarly, the measurements from time point $^ { 0 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ were normalised by their maximum fluorescence before performing the PARAFAC analysis and subsequently reversed afterwards.Outlier measurements,deviating excessively in character from the other samples due to measurement errors (sample handling or instrument performance), were removed from the datasets.To speed up convergence，the models were initialized with pure fluorescence spectra of specific substrates used in each experiment. All models were constrained to _nonnegativity，applying a convergence criterion of 10-6.

The validity of the PARAFAC decompositions was evaluated by looking for the presence of each fluorescent component across replicate samples and judging the plausible development over time (i.e.， gradual production and/or removal).Moreover, the different models were cross-validated by identifying similar fluorescent components across different mixed and single substrate experiments.For cross-validation， the Tucker congruence coefficient (TCC) was used (Lorenzo

Seva & ten Berge, 2006; Tucker, 1951). Once the fluorescent components for each experiment had been identified, they were fitted to the entire dataset, includingabiotic controls，before calculating their fluorescence intensity at the wavelengths of maximum fluorescence ( $\boldsymbol { F } _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ ; Murphy et al., 2013).

# Identification and quantification of auxiliary substrates and transformation intermediates

Analytical standards were purchased from SigmaAldrich for carbofuran, the four auxiliary substrates,and the intermediates predicted with the Eawag PPS.For all these compounds， fluorescence standards were then created by measuring the respective analytical standard's fluorescence signal for a series of different dilutions.Dilution series were prepared in phosphate buffer at pH 7.The fluorescence spectrum of each compound was further characterised by a onecomponentPARAFAC model (Figures S1-S3; Table S3; Wünsch et al., 2015) to derive a calibration curveforconvertingfluorescence $( F _ { \mathrm { m a x } } )$ to concentration.

For parent compounds (carbofuran and auxiliary substrates), the spectral fluorescence properties of the standardsandrespectivesignalsisolatedfrom the PARAFAC modeling of the experimental data matched，in all cases，with TCC values $2 9 9 \%$ and $2 9 7 \%$ for excitation and emission spectra, respectively. Fluorescent components that peaked in intensity at specific time points were classified as transient fluorescence peaks (i.e., transformation intermediates). Their fluorescence spectra were compared to the fluorescence standards of the predicted intermediates,aiming to reveal their identity. Fluorescent components which steadily increased over time were classified as persistent fluorescence peaks.Detection limits for all PARAFAC components were approximated operationally by twice the standard deviation of the detected fluorescence intensities in the abiotic controls.

# RESULTS

# Biodegradability of model DOM constituents

The 11 model DOM constituents investigated in this study are monoaromatic compounds with short transformation pathways (two to three biotransformation reactions until ring cleavage). Pathway prediction with the Eawag PPS indicated that the main difference between all predicted pathways lies in the reactions describing aromatic ring hydroxylation (see detailed description of all predicted pathways in Tables S4-S7).

Linking the predicted hydroxylation reactions to the Eawag biocatalysis/biodegradation database (BBD) further indicated that flavin-dependent monooxygenases (FDMOs) or monooxygenases with a di-metal active site(e.g.，Fe/S） catalyse the hydroxylation of compounds that support the growth of KN65.2 (Table 1). Moreover， FDMOs might be involved in the hydroxylation of non-transformable compounds with activated (mono-hydroxylated) aromatic rings (i.e.， Pl and SAL). Pl shows structural similarities to the growth-supporting compounds (para-hydroxylated aromatic ring， see Figure 1) and subsequently shares the biotransformation rule (bt-rule) bt0014 with them (Table 1). Hydroxylation of the ortho-hydroxylated SAL,on the other hand, was linked to different bt-rules (btoo60 and bt0064) compared to the hydroxylation of the growthsupporting， para-hydroxylated compounds(bt0343, bt0023 and bt0014; Table 1).Their respective BBD entries only list one overlapping fourth-level EC number (1.14.13.07, shown in bold in Table 1) out of 19 examples provided, indicating that different specific FDMOs are required for the hydroxylation of SAL versus transformable compounds.The fourth-level EC numbers predictedforthehydroxylationofthenontransformable， unactivated compounds were exclusively linked to Rieske-type dioxygenases (RTDOs; Table 1). This suggests that different enzyme types are requiredforhydroxylatingunactivated，nontransformable versus para-hydroxylated, transformable compounds.In addition，epoxidation reactions were predicted for the non-transformable,unactivated compounds (Tables S6 and S7). They are not taken into account here since they represent an alternative strategy for opening aromatic rings compared to the more common pathways that consist of ring hydroxylation followed by ring cleavage.

The genome of KN65.2 contains a total of 70 genes associated with mono- and dioxygenases (Nguyen et al.,2015). BLAST analysis classified $1 1 \%$ of these 70 genes as putative single-component FDMOs (SCFDMOs；Figure 2，EC 1.14.13)，which represent the major class of enzymes predicted for the hydroxylation of the mono-hydroxylated test compounds (Table 1). Differentiating these FDMOs by their ability to transform para-hydroxylatedversusortho-hydroxylated compounds was,however， not possible due to insufficient reference protein sequences for specific fourth-level EC numbers. Notably， the BLAST analysis also revealed the presence of three putative RTDOs (Figure 2, EC 1.14.12, $4 \%$ of all identified oxygenases), which were linked to the hydroxylation of unactivated (non-transformable) compounds by pathway prediction. Despite these findings, the most abundant oxygenases $( 5 6 \% )$ aresingle-donor dioxygenases，whichare, amongst others，required for cleaving aromatic rings (Figure 2, EC 1.13.11).

# Transformation groups of growthsupporting compounds

To further explore and group the transformation mechanisms of the documented growth-supporting compounds， the predicted pathways of four auxiliary substrates (4-hydroxybenzoic acid, 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde, $p$ -coumaric acid，and ferulic acid)were investigated in detail and compared to the carbofuran pathway suggested by Nguyen et al. (2014) for KN65.2.

The pathways predicted for the four auxiliary substrates until ring cleavage are shown in Figure 3A. Two transformationgroupswererecognised.Group 1includesthe twobenzoic acid derivatives 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde and 4-hydroxybenzoic acid. Pathway prediction indicated an initial oxidation step that transforms 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde to 4-hydroxybenzoic acid，which is then hydroxylated to either 3,4-dihydroxybenzoic acid or hydroquinone and subsequently undergoes ring cleavage (Figure 3A, Group 1). The two cinnamic acids, $p$ -coumaric acid and ferulic acid， form transformation Group 2. Taking into account previously established biodegradation endpoints (i.e., the time until complete substrate biodegradation tested in a single substrate， pure culture experiments with KN65.2； Schittich et al.， 2022)，it appears likely that both substrates are transformed via the hydroxylation product caffeic acid (Figure 3A, Group 2, green arrows). $p$ -Coumaric acid is degraded faster (28 h) than its possible biodegradation intermediate 4-hydroxybenzoic acid (43 h), making it unlikely that $p$ -coumaric acid forms this compound as an intermediate (Figure 3A, ${ \tt b t 0 3 3 7 ^ { \star } }$ , greyed out). A similar observation was made for ferulic acid and its predicted intermediate vanillic acid (Figure 3A, ${ \tt b t 0 3 3 7 ^ { \star } }$ ， greyed out). Thus, Group 2 compounds are expected to form a joint and clearly distinct transformation group compared to Group 1.

The carbofuran biodegradation pathway for KN65.2, suggested by Nguyen et al. (2014)，is shown in Figure 3B. Both transformation groups,determined for auxiliary substrates, show reactions that might be supported by enzymes from the carbofuran pathway (Figure 3A，red arrows). For Group 1，bt0014 and bt0343 were predicted as hydroxylation reactions (Figure 3A). # Transient fluorescence peaks confirm transformation groups

Subsequent to the theoretical pathway prediction，we conducted mixed substrate biodegradation experiments, containing carbofuran and one of the four auxiliary substrates at a time.Tracing the development of the samples' organic matter fluorescence allowed us to experimentally investigate the transformation pathways predicted for the two transformation groups and to simultaneously evaluate their impact on carbofuran biodegradation (see further below). The PARAFAC analysis，conducted individually for the four different mixed substrate experiments，identified five to nine components (Figures S4-S7). Plotting their fluorescence intensities over time revealed six different fluorescent components (TP1-TP6) that represent transient signals (i.e., transformation intermediates; Table S8). Each of the six components could be cross-validated with components isolated from single substrate controls，thus providing insights regarding their source (i.e.，parent compound). In addition， cross-validation between the different mixed substrate experiments revealed substrates with shared transformation intermediates.

# Persistent fluorescence peaks and model validity

Each of the PARAFAC models，describing single or mixed substrate experiments，included at least one component representing a persistent peak (i.e.,a fluorescent component with a steadily increasing signal over time; Tables S9 and S10). Persistent peaks showed highly similar spectral characteristics amongst the nine different models (four mixed substrate and five single substrate models) and could be described by a total of three PARAFAC components (PP1-PP3) that were cross-validated amongst the different datasets (Table S9). All three components show a double shoulder excitation curve with one peak in the UVA fluorescent region $( \mathsf { E x } . < 3 0 0 \mathsf { n m } )$ ）and one peak in the visible wavelength region $( \mathsf { E x } . > 3 0 0 \mathsf { n m } )$ ，while their emission peaks occur at wavelength ${ > } 4 5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ (Table S9).

Notably,a clear trend was observed where persistent peaks reached higher fluorescence intensities for mixed substrate experiments $( 1 0 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } / \mathsf { L } )$ with comparably higher cell densities，while lower fluorescence intensities were observed for single substrate experiments that were conducted with half the amount of substrate $( 5 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { L } )$ thus reaching comparably lower cell densities (Figure 6). This observation strongly indicates a link between the persistent peaks (PP1-PP3)and microbial growth,where PP1-PP3 likely represent soluble microbial byproducts.In addition to cross-validation and time series validation (see methods)， the steady occurrence of persistent peaks across all models, their similar fluorescence characteristics,and the good correlation between fluorescence intensities and cell concentrations further confirm the validity of the different PARAFAC models,and thus the observed transformation intermediates.

# Biodegradation kinetics during mixed substrate experiments

Carbofuran biodegradation by KN65.2

The presence of auxiliary substrates showed a minimal influence on carbofuran biodegradation kinetics for the OMP degrader strain KN65.2 (Figures 7A,C and S8a,c). Independent of the experimental conditions, carbofuran biodegradation endpoints $( > 9 0 \%$ substrate removal) did not change significantly in the presence of either Group 1 (4-hydroxybenzoic acid, 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde） or Group2（ $p$ -coumaric acid, ferulic acid) auxiliary substrates,compared to single substrate carbofuran controls (Figures 7A,C and S8a,c). However, higher peak intensities of transformation intermediates were observed during pure carbofuran biodegradation when pre-culturing KN65.2 on auxiliary substrates (i.e.，varied pre-growth) compared to standard pre-culturing conditions (Figure S9). This trend might indicate a slightly lower enzyme concentration under varied pre-culturing conditions despite the overall stable carbofuran biodegradation kinetics.

# Auxiliary substrate biodegradation by KN65.2

Auxiliary substrate biodegradationkineticsvaried amongst the different substrates and for different preculturing conditions (Figures 7B,D and S8b,d). Under standardexperimentalconditions(pre-growthof KN65.2 on carbofuran)，all auxiliary substrates were degraded slower than carbofuran, indicating sequential substrate uptake for both Group 1 and Group 2 auxiliary substrates by KN65.2. For example， carbofuran was always degraded within 9 h (Figure 7A,C)，while 4-hydroxybenzoic acid (Group 1) and $p$ -coumaric acid (Group 2) were only degraded after 46 and $^ { 2 0 \mathfrak { h } }$ respectively，when added as an auxiliary substrate (Figure 7B,D, red curves). Especially for experiments with carbofuran and 4-hydroxybenzoic acid, the large gap between the biodegradation endpoints is clearly reflected in the growth curve of KN65.2，which shows two distinct phases,thus clearly indicating sequential substrate biodegradation (Figure S10a). When changing pre-culturing conditions to pre-growth on auxiliary substrates， degradation endpoints changed significantly for Group 1 substrates but stayed constant for Group 2 compounds (Figures 7B,D and S8b,d)，confirming two distinct transformation mechanisms for the two groups.The 4-hydroxybenzoic acid biodegradation endpoint changed from $4 6 \mathsf { h }$ under standard conditions to $^ { 9 \mathfrak { h } }$ under changed pre-culturing conditions (Figure 7B red and orange curves), resulting in the simultaneous substrate biodegradation of carbofuran and 4-hydroxybenzoic acid. In contrast， $p$ coumaric acid was steadily degraded within $^ { 2 0 \mathfrak { h } }$ ,independent of the pre-culturing conditions (Figure 7D).

# Biodegradation kinetics in the presence of competitor P17

The presence of an additional degrader strain (P.fluorescens sp. P17) during mixed substrate experiments with carbofuran and either 4-hydroxybenzoic acid (Group 1） or $p$ -coumaric acid (Group 2) did not affect carbofuran biodegradation kinetics (Figure 7A,C, grey curves). However, the presence of P17 (mixed culture experiments) resulted in a faster uptake of 4-hydroxybenzoic acid $( 2 0 \mathsf { h } )$ compared to the equivalent experiment conducted with a pure KN65.2 culture $( 4 6 \mathsf { h }$ ；Figure 7B，compare grey and red curves). This results from the fact that the pure P17 culture degrades 4-hydroxybenzoic faster than the pure KN65.2 culture under standard experimental conditions (Figure 7B, compare green and red curves). Selective plating of the mixed culture showed a ratio of $60 \%$ P17 to $40 \%$ KN65.2after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ (Figure S11a)， supporting the hypothesis that 4-hydroxybenzoic acid is biodegraded purely by the general degrader P17,while the growth of KN65.2 is solely supported by the pesticide carbofuran. Contrarily， P17 does not grow on $p$ -coumaric acid (Figure 7D, green curve and Figure SiOe) and thus did notinfluence $p$ -coumaric acidbiodegradationby KN65.2 during mixed culture，mixed substrate experiments (Figure 7D， grey curve). Selective plating revealed that KN65.2 outcompetes P17 with a percentage of $18 \%$ P17 to $82 \%$ KN65.2after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ (Figure S11b), indicating that KN65.2 can make full use of both substrates.

# DISCUSSION

# The role of ring hydroxylating enzymes for the biotransformation of DOM constituents

Pathway prediction identified ring hydroxylation as the keyreaction determiningthebiodegradabilityof the 11 model DOM constituents (i.e., the 11 monoaromatic compounds investigated).

This finding is plausible，specifically for the three unactivated(i.e.，non-hydroxylated)andnontransformable compounds (benzoic acid， cinnamic acid,and L-phenylalanine)，considering that enzymes hydroxylatingunactivated aromatic ringsarenot required for carbofuran biodegradation by KN65.2 (see Figure 3B). Unactivated aromatic compounds are generally more recalcitrant to ring hydroxylation compared to mono-hydroxylated compounds.Their activation for ringcleavage requires either two successiveor simultaneous oxygen insertion steps by either monooxygenases or by RTDOs， respectively (Ulrich & Hofrichter, 2007). In our study， the fourth-level bt-rules predicted for the hydroxylation of the three unactivated compoundswere exclusively linked to RTDOs (Table 1). Notably, BLAST analysis revealed the presence of three RTDOs in the genome of KN65.2. However，several factors may influence their capability to transform the studied compounds. First， the RTDOs might have been lost or not been induced in the strain used for experiments.For instance， the presence of particular contaminants can cause genome streamlining due to environmental stress (Kumar et al., 2017). Second, enzyme specificity and processes like oxygen decoupling for non-native substrates might play a role (Bopp et al., 2020; Bopp et al., 2022). For instance， a recent study found that the RTDOs nitrobenzene- and 2-nitrotoluene dioxygenase show $2 0 \% { - } 1 0 0 \%$ unproductive ${ \sf O } _ { 2 }$ activation when transforming non-native but structurally related compounds, thus limiting or preventing substrate hydroxylation (Bopp et al., 2022).

In contrast to unactivated compounds, the hydroxylation reactions predicted for mono-hydroxylated, transformable， and non-transformable compoundswere primarily linked to FDMOs (Table 1). The relevance of FDMOs for the hydroxylation of natural and xenobiotic phenolic compounds has previously been highlighted (Ballou et al.， 2005； Huijbers et al.， 2014; Paul et al.，2021； UIIrich & Hofrichter，2007).FDMOs are commonly highly regioselective,catalysing either paraor ortho-hydroxylations (Huijbers et al.， 2014；Paul et al., 2021; van Berkel et al., 2006). This explains our finding that different types of FDMOs are required to transform ortho-hydroxylated， non-transformable compounds (i.e.， SAL) compared to the para-hydroxylated, growth-supporting compounds (Table 1). An exception is the non-transformable,para-hydroxylated compound piceol Pl，which shows overlapping pathway predictions with growth-supporting substrates (Table 1). One possible reason for Pl not supporting the growth of KN65.2 might be steric hindrance. FDMOs are highly sensitive to this as not only the position of the -OH group at the aromatic ring determines enzyme activity, but also the position and composition of additional side chains can play a critical role in substrate recognition (Deng et al., 202O). For example, Pl contains no carboxylic or phenolic group in the ring substituent para to the -OH group (Figure 1). Interestingly， this feature was highlighted as important for substrate recognition by4-hydroxyphenylacetate3-hydroxylase(HpaB) (Deng et al., 202O), a TC-FDMO that is distantly related to the putative enzyme CdfC from the carbofuran pathway (Nguyen et al., 2014).

Amongst growth-supporting compounds， two distinct transformation groups were identified (Group 1: 4-hydroxybenzoic acid and 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde, Group 2: $p$ -coumaric acid and ferulic acid).The two groups show different transformation intermediates; however， the enzymes catalysing their transformation might overlap (Figure 3A). Notably, the substrate range of KN65.2 shows high similarity to that of the TC-FDMO HpaB (Deng et al.， 202O)， suggestingthat all growth-supporting substrates might be hydroxylated by a single monooxygenase，possibly CfdC from the carbofuran pathway (see Figure 3). Nevertheless, small changes in the structure of FDMOs can change their substrate specificity significantly (Deng et al.， 2020;

Dhammaraj et al., 2016; van Berkel et al., 2006)， and entries in the Eawag BBD,as well as the genome of KN65.2，revealed a range of different FDMOs that might catalyse the hydroxylation of the different parahydroxylated， growth-supporting auxiliary substrates. Future studies should therefore experimentally test the expression and activity of the different ring hydroxylating oxygenases (i.e.，RTDOs and FDMOs) during bidegradation experiments with KN65.2. Insights may further elucidate the exact mechanisms driving the biodegradability of the 11 compounds and reveal which specific enzymes catalyse the reactions of the two different transformation groups.

# Tracing transformation intermediates with fluorescence spectroscopy

Fluorescence spectroscopy combined with PARAFAC modelling is increasingly being used to trace the biologicalturnoverofenvironmental DOM(Dubber et al., 2021; Logue et al., 2016; Paulsen et al., 2019). However, the use of PARAFAC analysis to trace transformation intermediates from biodegradation experiments with pure fluorophores is not commonly applied. For our study， this novel approach presented a timeand cost-efficient solution that enabled monitoring the biodegradation of carbofuran and auxiliary substrates, and beyond that allowed us to confirm the two transformation groups suggested based on pathway prediction (Figure 3A).

The fluorescent component representing the transformation intermediate TP1 could clearly be matched with the fluorescence spectra of 4-hydroxybenzoic acid (Figure 4A). TP1 occurred as an intermediate during 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde biodegradation， thus confirming transformation Group 1 (Figure 3A). Moreover, the good match between intermediate TP3 in experiments with $p$ -coumaric and ferulic acid confirmed a distinct transformation Group 2,even though TP3 could not be identified (Figures 3A and 5A). Transformation intermediates commonly occur at low concentrations.Hence, the high fluorescence intensities observed for TP3 indicate that the compound responsible has a high fluorescence eficiency.Accounting for starting substrate concentrations $( 5 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { C / L }$ per substrate） and peak values of TP3 (5.94 R.U.; Figure 5C), its fluorescence efficiency must be at least $1 . 2 { \sf R } . { \sf U }$ $\mathsf { m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathsf { L }$ ,but is likely considerably higher，possibly in a similar range as the fluorescence efficiency of hydroxylation products with short-chain ring substituents like hydroquinone (12.23 R.U. $\mathsf { m } \mathsf { g } ^ { - 1 } \mathsf { L } )$ or 3,4-dihydroxybenzoic acid (7.89 R.U. $\mathsf { m g } ^ { - 1 } \mathsf { L }$ ; Table S3). This may indicate that TP3 represents a dealkylation product of $p$ -coumaric/ferulic acid where the long-chain ring substituent was cleaved,and thus,an alternative transformation pathway for Group 2 compared to the predicted one (Figure 3A). However, the chemical structures for such an alternative hydroxylation product are limited, and most possibilities do not match TP3, see Figure S12 and Wünsch et al. (2015). Alternatively, TP3 might represent an intermediate from the predicted Group 2 pathway, formed after ring opening.The products of ring cleavage predicted for the Group 2 pathway show highly conjugated systems (Figure S13),which would be in line with an increased excitation maximum and fluorescence efficiency of TP3 compared to itsparent compounds(Reynolds et al.，2014). However， very little is known about the fluorescence of non-aromatic compounds，and standards for ring-opening products are commonly not commercially available. Investigating the characteristics of TP3in combination with other methods， like， for instance，mass spectrometry，may provide additional insights regarding its identity in future studies. In addition,analysing specific samples with a combination of chromatography， coupled with spectral absorbance and fluorescence analysis,might enable the separation of spectra from compounds with very similar fluorescence characteristics,such as ferulic and caffeic acid, or carbofuran and carbofuran phenol，which could not be clearly separated in our study (Figures S14 and S15).

# Biodegradation mechanisms of the different transformation groups

Mixed substrate biodegradation experiments conducted under different pre-culturing conditions revealed two distinct transformation mechanisms between transformation Groups 1 and 2.Group 1 compounds were biodegraded significantly faster when pre-cultured on auxiliary substrates compared to standard conditions (Figures 7B and S8b). This finding indicates transformation by a different pathway compared to carbofuran. Under standard conditions (pre-growth on carbofuran), the enzymes required for the transformation of Group 1 substrates may not have been actively expressed at the start of the experiment, leading to delayed biodegradation endpoints compared to varied pre-growth conditions.Contrarily，Group 2 substrates showed stable biodegradationkineticsindependent ofthepreculturing conditions (Figures 7D and S8d)， suggesting that these compounds are degraded by the enzymes catalysing carbofuran biodegradation.

Carbofuran biodegradation kinetics stayed stable independent of the experimental conditions and in the presence of both Group 1 and Group 2 auxiliary substrates.This observation indicates an overall stable expression of the carbofuran pathway by KN65.2, which is in accordance with previous studies (Nguyen et al.,2014). Moreover, the finding suggests that compounds from both transformation groups are suitable auxiliary substratesforbioremediationprocesses.

Carbofuran biodegradation was even stable after pregrowing KN65.2 on Group 1 auxiliary substrates, despite presumably diferent transformation pathways, indicating that catabolite repression does not play a role.Instead， the auxiliary substrates and carbofuran were degraded simultaneously. Under such conditions, Group 1 auxiliary substrates may support the survival of the carbofuran degrader KN65.2 (Egli, 2010; Egli & Mason，1991)，or，over the long term，indirectly enhance carbofuran biodegradation because of overall enhanced cell growth (Babel et al.，1993；Deng et al.，2022;Li et al.， 2010). For instance，enhanced biodegradation of the pollutant 1,4-dioxane has previously been reported in the Arctic and American deep groundwater samples in the presence of different alcohols,which caused an overall boost in biomass (Deng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2010). In contrast, the presence of Group 2 auxiliary substrates,which possibly share the carbofuran pathway， might， overthe longterm, enhance carbofuran biodegradation kinetics through enhanced pathway expression or pathway induction (Deng et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2016). Slower degradation of Group 2 auxiliary substrates compared to carbofuran indicates that competitive inhibition does not play a role for carbofuran biodegradation in the presence of Group 2 auxiliary substrates.

The fact that enhanced biodegradation of carbofuran in the presence of Group 1 or Group 2 auxiliary substrates was not observed in our study can be related to the experimental conditions (i.e.,short-term batch experiments). Chemostat or microcosm experiments would be required to observe the long-term effects of mixed substrate biodegradation on individual compounds (Harder et al.， 1982； Li et al.，2010). Future studies should, therefore, investigate the influence of Group 1 and Group 2 auxiliary substrates on carbofuran biodegradation in a time-extended setup (e.g.，chemostat experiments). For our reductionist approach，in particular，Group 2 substrates like $p$ coumaric acid, which are solely degradable by KN65.2 and not assimilable by the competitor strain P17 (Figure 7D)，would be of interest for future studies. Accordingly， for complex，environmental systems, auxiliary substrates that are only accessible for a small range of microbes, including the OMP degrader, would be beneficial,as the competition for these substrates might be reduced.Furthermore，depending on their concentrations, such OMP-degrader-specific auxiliary substrates might be capable of exerting selective pressure on the microbial community and thus maintain an appropriate abundance of the OMP degrader within the community (Deng et al., 2022). For instance, Deng et al. (2022) found that the auxiliary substrate propane was selective towards the dioxane degrader DD4 and capable of maintaining the dominance of DD4 in microbial inocula from various environmental compartments.

# CONCLUSION

In this study,we applied a combination of pathway prediction, laboratory experiments,and fluorescence spectroscopy to gain insights into the biodegradability and transformation mechanisms of different model DOM constituents (monoaromatic compounds) and their role as auxiliary substrates for the carbofuran degrader Novosphingobium sp. KN65.2.

A theoretical investigation with the Eawag PPSBBD system suggested that hydroxylation reactions play a critical role in the biodegradability of the 11 investigated monoaromatic compounds by KN65.2. In particular，the inactivity or unproductivity of RTDOs might hinder the transformation of non-hydroxylated compounds，while the high selectivity of FDMOs might determine the transformability of mono-hydroxylated compounds.Our approach allowed us to rapidly reveal key reactions and enzymes that may determine the biodegradability of the 11 compounds.The specific roles of these enzymes should be further explored in future studies； for example，by testing the expression and activity of RTDOs and FDMOs during biodegradation experiments with KN65.2.

The combination of pathway prediction,mixed substrate biodegradation experiments，and fluorescence spectroscopy further provided insights into the mechanisms driving the transformation of four growthsupportingsubstrates(4-hydroxybenzoicacid, 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde， $p$ -coumaric acid and ferulic acid) by KN65.2 and elucidated their impact on carbofuranbiodegradation. Two distincttransformation groups were identified that each shared common transformation intermediates and showed distinct transformation mechanisms.The biodegradation endpoints of Group 1 substrates(4-hydroxybenzoic acid and 4-hydroxybenzaldehyde） changed significantly when pre-culturing KN65.2 on auxiliary substrates compared to standard conditions，while Group 2 substrates （ $p -$ coumaric acid and ferulic acid) showed stable biodegradation endpoints independent of the pre-culturing conditions. These observations suggest that Group 1 substrates are degraded via a different pathway than the OMP carbofuran，while Group 2 substrates might be degraded via the carbofuran pathway.

Despite the different transformation mechanisms, the presence of auxiliary substrates did not affect carbofuran biodegradation，which stayed stable under all experimental conditions.This finding indicates that both Group 1 and Group 2 compounds are suitable auxiliary substrates for carbofuran bioremediation by KN65.2. Potential positive effects of the different auxiliary substrates on carbofuran biodegradation kinetics,through enhanced cell growth (Group 1) or pathway induction (Group 2)，should be further investigated using longterm experimental setups (e.g.， chemostat experiments). In this context, especially Group 2 substrates like $p$ -coumaric acid are of interest because of their stable biodegradation kinetics and the fact that they are fully available for the carbofuran degrader KN65.2 also in the presence of the competitor P.fluorescens P17.
