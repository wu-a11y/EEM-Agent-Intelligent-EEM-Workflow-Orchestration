Coastal lagoons are among the most productive and biodiverse systems in the world and are important sentinels of climate change. The Mar Menor is one of the largest coastal lagoons in the Mediterranean, providing a variety of ecosystem services and resources to the community. However, in recent decades this lagoon has suffered drastic changes and degradation caused by human activities. We analyzed the concentration of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and the optical properties of dissolved organic matter (DOM) in the water column and sediment pore water during the summer and winter of 2018 and during eighteen months from 2016 to 2018. Overall, we found that the composition of DOM is mainly related to and enhanced by anthropogenic activities and microbial metabolism. DOM enters the lagoon via urban and agricultural runoff, drainage systems, and wastewater treatment plants. Additionally, strong microbial metabolism in sediments leads to differences in DOM composition between water and sediments. In the water column, humic-like components accounted for $7 1 \ \%$ of the total DOM, while protein-like compounds were most abundant in sediment pore water. We observed a strong seasonal variability associated with precipitation and the system collapse in 2016 (phytoplankton bloom), which resulted in the death of $8 0 \%$ of macrophytes. The sediments act as a source of DOM to the overlying water, likely due to relatively high organic matter content and intense microbial activity, primarily through anaerobic pathways. Benthic fluxes of DOC ranged from 5.24 to $3 3 . 3 0 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ , being higher in winter than summer 2018 and decreasing from north to south, likely related to lower residence time in the northern basin, groundwater discharge and accumulation of organic matter from the dead meadows. We estimate a net flux of DOC from the Mar Menor toward the Mediterranean Sea of $1 . 5 7 \times 1 0 ^ { 7 } \mathrm { m o l y r } ^ { - 1 }$ .

# 1. Introduction

Coastal lagoons are among the most productive systems in the world and are important indicators of climate change (Kennish and Paerl, 2010; Brito et al., 2012). They exhibit great biodiversity and provide diverse ecosystem services and resources to society (Kennish and Paerl, 2010; Rodríguez-Gallego et al., 2017; Newton et al., 2018). Despite the global importance of these ecosystems, they are subject to strong anthropogenic pressures such as tourism, agricultural and urban activities, making them particularly vulnerable to trophic imbalances and degradation (Kjerfve, 1994; Bas-Silvestre et al., 2020). Coastal lagoons are susceptible to high loads of nutrients and organic matter, including runoff or direct wastes from human activities, resuspension of sediments, and hydrodynamic changes that can lead to eutrophication processes (e.g., RodríguezGallego et al., 2017; Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2019). The Mar Menor, the largest hypersaline coastal lagoon in Europe, has traditionally been considered oligotrophic (Gilabert, 2001; Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2005). However, drastic changes have occurred in recent decades as a result of human activities. In the early 1990s, the lagoon began to receive high inputs of nutrients and organic matter due to changes in agricultural practices in the watershed (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2002). This resulted in seagrasses and slowgrowing macroalgae gradually being replaced by fast-growing macroalgae and phytoplankton (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2002). However, due to the complexity, heterogeneity, and particular homeostatic mechanism of the system, eutrophication symptoms were not evident until the summer of 2015 and early 2016 (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2019). In this regard, the system recovered quickly after eutrophication (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2019) with the exception of the last events in 2019 and 2021 (Caballero et al., 2019, 2022). The physical and biological processes in the Mar Menor have been widely studied (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2002, 2019; Vallejo et al., 2021) as well as biogeochemical processes mainly focused on the dynamic of inorganic nutrients (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2020). However, little is known about the processes related to the pool of organic matter, especially dissolved organic matter (DOM).

Dissolved organic matter plays a key role in biogeochemical cycles (e.g., photochemistry, support of microbial metabolism, metal complexation, organic alkalinity), and depends on sources and subsequent transformations within the system (Cauwet, 2002; Bauer and Bianchi, 2011). DOM derives from either autochthonous or allochthonous sources. Autochthonous DOM is primarily produced by plankton communities and benthic macrophytes (seagrasses and macroalgae), which can account for high biomass and productivity in coastal systems, and by degradation processes in sediments, with subsequent release of DOM into the water column (Wada and Hama, 2013; Burdige and Komada, 2015; Maher and Eyre, 2010; Bernardeau-Esteller et al., 2023). In general, DOM is higher in sediment pore waters than in overlying waters (Chen and Hur, 2015; Burdige and Komada, 2015), and anthropogenic activities and/or hydrologic alterations have a major impact on DOM benthic fluxes (Hur et al., 2014), which can have important ecological implications for the system (e.g., eutrophication, algal blooms, hypoxia events) (Andrisoa et al., 2019; Valiela et al., 1992; Wang et al., 2016). In addition, DOM in sediment pore water may reflect the productivity of the overlying water and the nature of the particulate organic matter from which DOM is produced (e.g., Osburn et al., 2012). On the other hand, allochthonous DOM is mainly of terrestrial origin, although anthropogenic inputs from watersheds and groundwater have also been shown to be important contributors (Bauer and Bianchi, 2011; Andrisoa et al., 2019). Studying the dynamics of DOM in coastal lagoons can help understand the occurrence of various processes, and assist public administrators and decision makers in implementing strategies to prevent or even stop the degradation of these systems.

The DOM fraction that absorbs light at ultraviolet (UV) and visible wavelengths is one of the most important absorption components in natural water, and is referred to as chromophoric DOM (CDOM, Coble, 2007). CDOM plays an important role in biogeochemical cycles and ecosystem functioning (Coble et al., 2014). The sub-fraction of CDOM that fluoresces is called fluorescent DOM (FDOM) and can comprise up to $7 0 \%$ of the total DOC in coastal areas (Coble, 2007). Using the optical properties of DOM (absorbance and fluorescence) is a relatively fast, accurate, and inexpensive technique for characterizing DOM (Jaffé et al., 2008). Therefore, it is particularly useful when a large number of samples need to be processed, such as in monitoring studies (Coble et al., 2014). The spectral properties of CDOM, such as absorption coefficients at specific wavelengths and spectral slopes, can provide information about the origin, quantity and chemical composition of the DOM pool (e.g., molecular weight and aromaticity) (Weishaar et al., 2003; Coble, 2007; Helms et al., 2008). In addition, the use of excitation emission matrices (EEMs), analyzed either by calculating previously identified peaks (“peak picking” technique, Coble, 1996) or in conjunction with multivariate statistical parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) (Murphy et al., 2013, 2014) has been widely used to assess the sources and behavior of various fractions of DOM in coastal systems (e.g., Murphy et al., 2008; Amaral et al., 2020a; Osburn et al., 2016).

The aim of this work was to investigate the distribution of DOM in the Mar Menor coastal lagoon, which is subject to strong anthropogenic pressures and degradation processes, to determine the sources and factors controlling seasonal and spatial variability. A double approximation was used. First, we presented dissolved organic carbon concentration (DOC) and the optical properties of DOM in both water column and sediment pore water samples in contrasting seasons (winter and summer 2018), focusing on DOM vertical and spatial distribution. Second, we presented data from a temporal study in the water column (18 months, from 2016 to 2018) at three representative sites in the lagoon (north, central and south basins).

# 2. Methodology

# 2.1. Study area

The Mar Menor is a shallow (maximum depth $7 \mathrm { m }$ ), choked, hypersaline coastal lagoon, in the southeast of the Iberian Peninsula with an extent of $1 3 5 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ (García-Oliva et al., 2018). La Manga, a $2 2 \mathrm { k m }$ long sandy barrier, separates the lagoon from the Mediterranean Sea with three narrow channels that regulate water exchange (De Pascalis et al., 2012) (Fig. 1). El Estacio is the main channel connecting the lagoon to the Mediterranean Sea. The Mar Menor is influenced by more than twenty watercourses in the southwestern part and by groundwater flows (Martínez-Álvarez et al., 2011). The watercourses collect rainfall from a watershed $( 1 2 7 0 ~ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 } )$ ) that can reach the lagoon during heavy rainf (Marín-Guirao et al., 2007). Nevertheless, the Albujón is the only watercourse that maintains a permanent freshwater discharge, usually around $0 . 0 2 \mathrm { m } ^ { 3 } s ^ { - 1 }$ (García-Pintado et al., 2007), while estimated groundwater discharges show a large variability with values ranging from $1 1 \ \mathrm { \ h m } ^ { 3 } \ \mathrm { \ y } ^ { - 1 }$ to $8 4 . 9 \ \mathrm { \ h m } ^ { 3 } \ \mathrm { \ y } ^ { - 1 }$ (e.g., Domingo-Pinillos et al., 2018; Alcolea et al., 2019; Senent-Aparicio et al., 2021). Water temperature ranges from $1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in winter to $3 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in summer due to the semi-arid Mediterranean climate (Belando et al., 2017) and is relatively uniform throughout the lagoon (García-Oliva et al., 2018). Evaporation (rates of approximately $9 0 0 \mathrm { m m } \mathrm { y } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) is higher than precipitation $( < 3 0 0 \mathrm { m m } ^ { - 1 } )$ (De Pascalis et al., 2012), which together with the low exchange with the Mediterranean Sea leads to salinity ranges between 42 and 47 (Marín-Guirao et al., 2008). The hydrodynamics of the lagoon are mainly determined by the wind that causes mixing of the water column and affects the circulation (De Pascalis et al., 2012). Based on the general circulation pattern and hydrological conditions, three main basins can be identified in the Mar Menor: 1) the northern basin with the lowest salinity; 2) the southern basin with the highest salinity as a result of water deficit and the greatest degree of confinement with the longest residence time of the water and 3) the central basin with intermediate salinity values, which corresponds to the mixing area of the lagoon and Mediterranean waters. Overall, the average water renewal time is of 318 days (Ghezzo et al., 2015) with the lowest values on the northern coast $( \sim 3 -$ 4 months) and the highest in the southern part of the lagoon (GarcíaOliva et al., 2018).

In earlier times, primary production in the lagoon was mainly based on the phanerogam Cymodocea nodosa (Ucria) Ascherson (1870) until the modification of El Estacio channel in 1973, which resulted in the colonization by the alga Caulerpa prolifera (Forsskål) J.V. Lamouroux, 1809 (PérezRuzafa et al., 2012). Thus, there was a progressive increase in the content of organic matter in sediments covered by the new meadows (Pérez-Ruzafa and Marcos, 1987). However, the system was relatively stable until the 1990s, when a low to moderate eutrophication process began. This was as a result of changes in agricultural activities in the lagoon watershed that lead to a large increase nutrients loading (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2019), mainly through El Albujón watercourse. Nevertheless, the system was relatively stable and no significant increase in chlorophyll $a$ $\operatorname { \rho C h l } a )$ concentration was observed, mainly due to the proliferation of jellyfish in 1995, which are thought to exert top-down control of the trophic web (PérezRuzafa et al., 2002, 2012). In the spring of 2016, a major eutrophication event occurred in which the water column was dominated by dense phytoplankton populations that reduced the depth visibility from $\sim 6 \mathrm { { m } }$ to $0 . 5 \mathrm { m } .$ , resulting in the death of mixed meadow populations (Belando et al., 2017). Subsequently, nutrient inputs from agricultural sources were drastically reduced and the system recovered rapidly, which was observed in the spring and summer of 2018 (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2019). However, new eutrophications events occurred in 2019 due to heavy rainfall (Caballero et al., 2019) and in 2021 related to nutrient inputs (Caballero et al., 2022), followed by massive mortalities due to anoxic conditions.

# 2.2. Sampling strategy and analytical methods

In this work, different sampling strategies were followed: 1) an intensive spatial sampling campaign during two contrasting seasons. In 2018, two cruises were conducted in late winter (March 14–18) and in late summer (September 12–18). We collected water samples from 41 stations at $_ { 0 . 5 \mathrm { m } }$ using a Van Dorn bottle and at $5 \mathrm { m }$ using a Ruttner bottle (Fig. 1). Note that stations 39 and 40 were located outside the lagoon in the

Mediterranean Sea. Vertical profiles of temperature and salinity were measured in situ with a CTD “Cast away” (Yellow Spring Instruments). We collected $5 0 \mathrm { c m }$ long gravity cores with an internal diameter of $9 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ at six sites in the northen, central and southern basins of the lagoon (Fig. 1, CAM 1 to 6).

2) Monthly sampling over 18 months from August 2016 to October 2018 was also conducted. Water samples were collected at 3 stations in the north, middle and south of the lagoon (Fig. 1, A, B and C), which are referred to as the monthly sampling. Salinity and temperature were measured using a multiparametric probe.

For $\operatorname { C h l } a$ , water samples were filtered through Whatman GF/F $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filters and stored frozen $( - 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ until pigment extraction in $9 0 \%$ acetone at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight (Parsons et al., 2013). Chl a concentrations were determined using a spectrofluorometer JASCO FP-8300 (intensive spatial sampling campaign) and a 10 AU Turner Designs fluorometer calibrated with pure Chl $a$ (monthly sampling). A Chl $a$ standard (Sigma-Aldrich, C6144) was used for calibration of the JASCO spectrofluorometer, with an accuracy of $\pm 0 . 0 5 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ .

For the analyzes of DOC, CDOM, and FDOM, water samples were collected in amber glass bottles and filtered through pre-combusted Whatman GF/F filters $( 4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } , ~ 4 ~ \mathrm { h } )$ . All material was previously acid-cleaned, muffled, and rinsed with the sample. From each sediment core sections of $^ { 1 \mathrm { c m } }$ thickness were sliced at different depths ( $\sim 0 . 5$ to $3 0 \mathrm { c m }$ below the seafloor) and pore water samples were collected by centrifugation $( \sim 1 0 0 g$ of sediment, $3 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , $1 0 . 0 0 0 \ g$ and $1 0 \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , SIGMA-18KS) and then filtered (Millipore HPF $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ ). Sediments and pore water samples were treated in an inert atmosphere using $\Nu _ { 2 }$ to minimize oxygen exposure.

Samples for DOC and total dissolved nitrogen (TDN) concentrations were kept at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until analysis by catalytic combustion method using a Multi N/C 3100 Analytik Jena analyzer. Potassium hydrogen phthalate was used to calibrate the system daily and deep seawater low carbon reference material provided by Prof. Hansell (CRM Program, $4 2 { - } 4 5 \ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ was used to assess instrument variability $( n = 5$ , $4 3 \pm 1 . 8 { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ . UV–visible absorption spectra were immediately obtained in the lab, from $2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ t o $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ using a JASCO-V750 spectrometer with a $^ { 1 \mathrm { { c m } } }$ pathlength quartz cuvette.

The corrected absorbance values were then converted to Napierian absorption coefficients $\left( a _ { \mathrm { \lambda } } \right)$ in $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ by multiplying by 2.303 and dividing by the cuvette path length $\left( 0 . 0 1 \mathrm { m } \right)$ (Green and Blough, 1994). We used the absorption coefficients at 254 $\mathbf { \zeta } _ { a _ { 2 5 4 } } )$ , as indicative of the abundance of conjugated carbon double bonds, and 350 $( a _ { 3 5 0 } )$ , as a quantitative indicator of CDOM (Coble, 2007). We calculated the $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ index by dividing the decadic absorption coefficient at 254 $( \mathbf { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ by the concentration of DOC $( \mathrm { m g C L } ^ { - 1 } )$ and used it as a proxy of DOM aromaticity (Weishaar et al., 2003).

EEMs at 250–450/300–560 nm were immediately obtained using a spectrofluorometer with an accessory for temperature control (JASCO FP8300, EHC-813) (intensive spatial sampling campaign) and a spectrofluorometer Perkin Elmer LS (monthly sampling). Before PARAFAC modelling and “peak picking” analysis, EEMs from seawater and pore water samples were standardized (e.g., blank subtraction, instrument correction factor, Raman and Rayleigh scatter band were trimmed, and inner filter effect was corrected), and normalized to Raman Unit (RU) using the drEEM 0.2 toolbox for Matlab (Murphy et al., 2013).

PARAFAC components were validated using split-half validation and random-initialization analysis (Murphy et al., 2013). Further details for the calculation of spectral indexes, EEMs standardization, and PARAFAC analysis are described in previous works (Amaral et al., 2020a, 2020b).

PARAFAC analysis was exclusively used to identify the main fluorophores and sources of DOM in the water column and pore water samples during the intensive campaign in winter and summer 2018. We used the open-access spectral database OpenFluor to describe the fluorescent components (Murphy et al., 2014). We selected a tucker congruence confidence (TCC) higher than 0.96. For comparison with the monthly sampling and benthic fluxes estimations, we decided to use the classical fluorescent peaks intensities selected at excitation–emission wavelength pairs previously established by Coble (1996). The calculated fluorescence Ex/Em wavelength pairs were: peak A $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m } / 4 3 5 \mathrm { n m } )$ due to general humic substances; peak C $3 4 0 \mathrm { n m } / 4 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ due to humic substances of terrestrial origin; peak M $( 3 2 0 \mathrm { n m } / 4 1 0 \mathrm { n m } )$ related with humic substances of marine and/or microbial origin, peak T $( 2 8 0 \mathrm { n m } / 3 5 0 \mathrm { n m } )$ ) and peak B $( 2 7 0 \mathrm { n m } /$ $3 0 4 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ ) due to protein–like substances, tryptophan and tyrosine–like respectively. Fluorescence intensities of these peaks are reported in Raman units (RU).

# 2.3. Statistical analysis

Two ways ANOVA and the post hoc Tukey test were performed to assess differences in DOM in the water column (factor 1: Season and factor 2: Depth) and pore water sediments (factor 1: Season and factor 2: Site). A significance level of 0.05 was selected. Simple linear regression models were used to determine the relationship between DOM variables and statistically significant relationships were considered when $p < 0 . 0 1$ . All analyses were performed with R 1.3.1073 software (R Core Team, 2020).

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Intensive sampling camping during winter and summer 2018

# 3.1.1. Physicochemical characteristics

Statistical analyses showed that physicochemical variables (T, S and $\operatorname { C h l } a ]$ ) were similar between surface and bottom samples $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ . On the other hand, a large seasonal physicochemical variability was observed (Table 1). T and S showed the lowest values in winter $( 1 5 . 7 \pm$ $0 . 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $4 1 . 9 \ \pm \ 1 . 5 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , respectively) and the highest in summer $( 2 7 . 1 \pm 1 . 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $4 5 . 5 \pm 1 . 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , respectively) $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ . Overall, S was relatively higher inside the lagoon than in the stations located in the Mediterranean Sea $( 3 7 . 5 \pm 0 . 3 ) $ ). This is due to the high evaporation and low exchange rates between the lagoon and the Mediterranean Sea. T and S revealed that the water column was well mixed. Chl $a$ showed higher average values during winter than in summer $3 . 3 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 9$ and $1 . 6 \pm$ $0 . 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively).

# 3.1.2. Distribution of DOC, CDOM, and FDOM

Similar to physicochemical variables, DOM variables did not present significant differences between surface and bottom samples $\left( \mathtt { p } > 0 . 0 5 \right)$ . DOC concentration ranged from 119.2 to $5 8 6 . 9 \mu \mathrm { M }$ with no seasonal differences (Table $1 , p > 0 . 0 5 )$ . The highest DOC values were observed inside the lagoon $( > 3 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , decreasing to values typical of coastal open areas $( 1 0 0 { - } 1 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ in the stations located in the Mediterranean Sea (Fig. 2).

CDOM, as $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $a _ { 3 5 0 } ,$ ranged from 1.21 to $2 0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ and from 0.03 to $5 . 0 3 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively, being both higher in summer than in winter $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ as for DOC values. Both absorption coefficients were positively correlated between them $\mathrm { T R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 8$ , $p < 0 . 0 0 1$ , $n = 1 6 4 \mathrm { . }$ ) and showed the highest values in the western zone (Fig. 2). $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ranged from 0.84 to $4 . 8 3 ~ \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ , with higher values during summer than winter $( 3 . 1 8 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 7 0 ~ \mathrm { { m g } ~ C ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ and $2 . 3 6 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 5 8 ~ \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { ~ C ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively, $p < 0 . 0 5 )$ , and the lowest values outside the lagoon.

# 3.1.3. Fluorescent DOM in the water samples of mar Menor

Five fluorescence components were statistically obtained in the PARAFAC model (Fig. S1). The components were distinguished by their excitation-emission spectra (Table 2). Components 1, 2 and 3 $( \mathrm { C 1 } _ { \mathrm { W } }$ $\mathrm { C } 2 _ { \mathrm { W } }$ and $\mathrm { C } 3 _ { \mathrm { w } } )$ ) have been classified as humic- like and were positively correlated between them $\mathrm { T R } ^ { 2 } > 0 . 9$ , $p < 0 . 0 0 1$ , $\mathtt { n } = 1 6 4 _ { \cdot }$ ), indicating a similar origin. $\mathrm { C 1 } _ { \mathrm { W } }$ and $\mathrm { C } 2 _ { \mathrm { W } }$ correspond to Coble's (1996) peak M, related with microbial humic substances, and $\mathrm { C } 3 _ { \mathrm { W } }$ to a mixture of peak A and C, related with general and terrestrial humic substances. Components 4 and 5 ( $\mathrm { C } 4 _ { \mathrm { W } }$ and $\mathsf { C } 5 _ { \mathsf { W } } ]$ have been classified as protein–like, that resembled the amino acids tryptophan and tyrosine and correspond to Coble's peak T and B, respectively (Coble, 1996), and no relationship was observed between them $( p > 0 . 0 1 )$ .

# 3.1.4. Sediment pore water DOM

Overall, sediment cores showed a great heterogeneity with the presence of abundant macrofauna (e.g., bivalves, snails, polychaetes, etc) and the macroalga Caulerpa. DOC concentration in the sediments pore water was two order of magnitude higher than those found in the water column, with values up to $2 0 . 8 5 \mathrm { m M }$ . Overall, a decrease in DOC values was observed with depth in all sediment cores (Fig. 4A). Unlike the water column, seasonal differences were observed in DOC concentration with the highest values during winter. Seasonal significant differences were observed in stations CAM1, 2, 3 and 4 with higher values during winter $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ , while CAM5 and 6 showed on average similar DOC concentrations between seasons $( p > 0 . 0 5 )$ ). TDN concentration varied between 0.15 and $3 . 9 1 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ , with higher values during summer $\left( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ ) that decreased with depth (Fig. 4B), and was highly correlated with DOC concentration $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 8 5$ , $\mathrm { p } < 0 . 0 0 1$ , $n = 1 2 6 )$ . Additionally, the highest TDN values were observed during winter, with significant differences between seasons in CAM1 and 3 $\left( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ . CDOM, as $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $a _ { 3 5 0 }$ ranged from 9.48 to $1 5 5 . 5 5 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ and from 0.84 to $9 . 3 6 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively, and $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ was positively correlated with DOC concentration $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 4 2$ , $p < 0 . 0 1$ , $n = 1 2 4 .$ ).

PARAFAC of pore water samples from the six sediment cores resulted in a five-fluorescent components model (Fig. S2, Table 3). Two of them, $\mathrm { C } 2 _ { \mathrm { S } }$ and $\mathrm { C } 3 _ { \mathrm { } s }$ , correspond to the humic-like $\mathrm { C 1 } _ { \mathrm { W } }$ and $\mathrm { C } 3 _ { \mathrm { W } }$ found in water $( \mathrm { T C C } > 0 . 9 6 )$ related to peak M and $\mathsf { A } / \mathsf { C }$ , respectively. The other three components showed spectral characteristics of protein-like compounds, component 1 $( \mathrm { C } 1 _ { \mathrm { { s } } } )$ known as tryptophan-like substances that correspond to peak T, and components 4 and 5 $\mathtt { C 4 } _ { \mathtt { S } }$ and $\mathbf { C } 5 _ { s } )$ as tyrosine-like (Table 2). Although these protein-like components were also related with the amino acids tryptophan and tyrosine, as those found in the water column, they cannot be considered the same since TCC between them was ${ < } 0 . 9 5$ (Lorenzo-Seva et al., 2006). These could be related with the intense microbial activity occurring in the sediments of the lagoon. Probably, these protein compounds are labile in surface sediment pore water DOM, hence does not persist in DOM when exported to the water column. Fluorescent intensity was one and up to two orders of magnitude higher than in the water column (Fig. 5). Similar to DOC concentration, fluorescent intensities were higher in winter than summer $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ . Significant seasonal differences were observed in the sediment cores CAM1 (humic-like peaks A and M) and CAM3 and CAM4 (protein-like peaks T and B) with higher fluorescent intensities during winter than summer $\left( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ . CAM2, CAM5 and CAM6 showed no significant seasonal differences in FDOM distribution $\left( \mathtt { p } > 0 . 0 5 \right)$ .

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Sources of FDOM in the water column

The fluorescent PARAFAC components found in the water column model have been described previously in other works (Table 2). The three humic-like components were positively related with Coble's peak A, C and M and accounted for $7 1 \% \pm 3 \%$ of the total FDOM. They were similar to those found in wastewater treatment plants (WWTP) from urban and/ or agricultural runoff and drainage systems (Wang et al., 2021; Smith et al., 2021). These could explain the higher values observed of humic-like fluorescence in the western/southern zone of the lagoon where most of the watercourses discharge, and during summer, when higher precipitation took place. The accumulated rainfall during the previous week and the week of the sampling campaign in March 2018 and September 2018 was $1 . 3 \mathrm { m m }$ and $2 8 . 8 \mathrm { m m }$ , respectively (AEMET).

Protein-like components $\mathrm { C } 4 _ { \mathrm { W } }$ and $\mathrm { C } 5 _ { \mathrm { W } }$ were positively correlated with peaks T and B, respectively, containing fractions of autochthonous DOM and related with in situ biological production but also with DOM from municipal WWTP (Table 2). This could be the result of microbial activity from domestic sewage and farm wastes (Stedmon et al., 2011). EEMs fluorescence of farm wastes, sewage, and WWTF effluents are enriched in protein-like fluorescence resembling the amino acid tryptophan (Coble et al., 2014; Osburn et al., 2016). Additionally, we found a weak but positive and significant relationship between $\mathsf { C 4 } _ { \mathsf { W } }$ and the microbial humiclike components $( 0 . 3 1 < \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } < 0 . 3 8$ , $\mathbb { p } < 0 . 0 1$ , $n = 1 5 9 )$ ), suggesting that they could partly share the source. Moreover, its spectral characteristic resembled those reported for the pesticide thiabendazole (Table 2). One of the uses of Thiabendazole is as a fungicide, to control a variety of fruit and vegetable crops and have been found in the El Albujón watercourse and in the lagoon related with citric crops and grassy areas found in the zone (golf courses) (Moreno-González et al., 2014). Therefore, in this work, the tryptophan component could be related with in situ production but also with direct discharge from WWTP and agricultural runoff. The tyrosine-like component, $\mathrm { C } 5 _ { \mathrm { W } }$ , was related with autochthonous DOM and particulate material, probably from phytoplankton production since it was the only fluorescent component that showed an opposite seasonal pattern than the rest with higher values in winter as Chl a. According to previous works, top-down control by ichthyoplankton and jellyfish population maintains the Chl $a$ concentration low (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2002). Jellyfish in winter are in the polyp phase and their activity is relatively low, while in summer, they are in the adult state, and are capable, through zooxanthellae, of removing nutrients from the medium, preventing the proliferation of phytoplankton (Kikinger, 1992). The lower abundance of jellyfish during the winter campaign could contribute to explain the dominance of phytoplankton derived DOM compared with the summer campaign.

To further determine DOM sources in the lagoon we run a PCA using physicochemical (S, T and Chl a) and DOM variables (DOC concentration, CDOM as $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $a _ { 3 5 0 }$ , $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and PARAFAC components), as previously used in other works (Osburn et al., 2012). The PCA explained $7 1 . 3 \%$ of DOM variability in the water column. The first and second principal components (PC1 and PC2), explained $5 5 . 0 \%$ and $1 6 . 3 \%$ of the variability, respectively (Fig. 7). PC1 was explained by microbial and terrestrial humic-like components, $\mathbf { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , S, the aromatic index $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and temperature while PC2 was explained by the tyrosine-like component C5, DOC and $\operatorname { C h l } a$ concentration and temperature. Therefore, PC1 is likely dominated by allochthonous DOM representing more complex compounds with a higher degree of aromaticity while PC2 is related with autochthonous DOM from phytoplankton production and benthic fluxes and probably with aliphatic compounds found in the DOC pool. Two significantly different cluster groups were clearly obtained, one corresponding to summer samples mainly related with PC1, while the other group corresponds to winter samples and was related with PC2. Our results indicate a strong seasonal DOM variability in the Mar Menor, controlled by different processes along the year. In summer, the major source was allochthonous DOM, related mainly with agricultural and urban runoff due to the higher precipitation that occurred during this campaign, while in winter DOM from in situ production and probably benthic fluxes dominate. This result is in agreement with previous work that found higher phytoplankton abundance in winter than in the summer 2018 (Mercado et al., 2021), as also observed for the DOM benthic fluxes (Table 4). The top-down control by jellyfish in phytoplankton population (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2002) could further influence the characteristics of DOM in this lagoon, however it is not clear the specific mechanisms of the jellyfish control over phytoplankton in this lagoon (Fuentes et al., 2011). Noteworthy, during the sampled years, jellyfish population was not as high as those reported by Pérez-Ruzafa et al. (2002). Mercado et al. (2021) concluded that the mechanisms regulating the phytoplankton community in the Mar Menor were substantially altered in the period 2016–2019 related with the break down of the system. This could be also the case of the benthic fluxes estimated in this work. The intensive sampling campings were conducted during the recovery phase after the break down that occurred in 2016 in the lagoon (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2019), which in combination with the complex trophic relationships found in this system (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2020) could be exacerbating and/or altering DOM variability along the study period.

# 4.2. FDOM in pore water samples from sediments

In the pore water samples, the humic-like components were the same than those found in the water column (Table 3), suggesting that this fraction of DOM have a common origin. On the other hand, we found three new protein-like components, indicating a strong microbial metabolism occurring in the sediments that leads to differences in DOM composition between water and sediments. Unlike the water column, protein-like ${ \mathrm { C } } 1 _ { s }$ and $\mathtt { C 4 } _ { \mathrm { { S } } }$ accounted for ${ > } 6 0 ~ \%$ of the total FDOM. ${ \mathrm { C } } 1 _ { s }$ was related with tryptophan-like substances found in black odor waters caused by excessive loads of organic pollutants and organic matter degradation (Lu et al., 2022).

![](images/7d3182dba371f23dcf7fc80847d33a06d2b8d88b138e75073f2d6b3e0ce50b7f.jpg)  
Fig. 7. Principal component analysis using physiochemical and DOM variables (PARAFAC components) analyzed during the intensive sampling campaign in winter and summer 2018.

$\mathbf { C } 1 _ { s }$ was also similar to a mixture of tryptophan-like compounds with polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons related with anthropogenic pollution (Amaral et al., 2021a). The tyrosine-like $\mathtt { C 4 } _ { \mathrm { { S } } }$ was related with dissolved organic nitrogen loading from agro-urban coastal systems (Osburn et al., 2016) and with the activity of sulfate reducing bacteria in pore water sediments typical from submarine mud volcanoes (Amaral et al., 2021b). In most of the sediments core a strong smell of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } S$ was perceived and based on the relationship of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) and dissolved oxygen fluxes $2 . 1 \pm 0 . 6$ in winter and $2 . 0 \pm 0 . 6$ summer), most of the oxidation of organic matter in the sediments of this lagoon occurs by anaerobic pathways, mainly by denitrification, sulfate reduction, or methanogenesis (Vallejo et al., 2021). Finally, $\mathrm { C } 5 _ { \mathrm { S } }$ was similar to other tyrosine-like components found in several aquatic ecosystems, such as, open ocean (Kowalczuk et al., 2013) and subtropical river (Yang et al., 2019), representing the autochthonous fraction of DOM, probably released by phytoplankton and related with the increased production from algae (Kowalczuk et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2019). This component was also related with highly reactive DOM in experiments with terrestrial vegetation (D'Andrilli et al., 2019) and was also found in sediments pore waters from rivers highly affected by anthropogenic pollutants (Chen et al., 2017). Moreover, tyrosine-like compounds are related with both freshly produced and degraded protein DOM (Coble et al., 2014). We can conclude that FDOM in sediment pore water was mainly microbial-derived dominated by proteinaceous material.

In coastal systems, sediments could be important sources of DOM to the overlying water column (e.g., Maher and Eyre, 2010; Brodecka-Goluch and Łukawska-Matuszewska, 2018). In the Mar Menor, the estimated diffusive benthic fluxes of DOC, CDOM and FDOM both in winter and summer were relatively higher than those reported in other estuarine systems $( \sim 0 . 1$ to $3 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ , $< 6 . 0 9$ RU, Burdige et al., 2004; Burdige and Komada, 2015; Brodecka-Goluch and Łukawska-Matuszewska, 2018) but lower than those from inland waters (up to 392 mmol $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ , Yang et al., 2012). Thus, the sediments of Mar Menor act as a source of DOM to the overlying water, dominated by protein-like compounds from biological activity (Tables 3 and 4), as observed in other estuaries (e.g., Maher and Eyre, 2010). The organic matter source fueling bacteria production probably was related with the massive phytoplankton growth that occurred in 2016 that lead to the loss of $8 0 \%$ of the macrophytes respect to 2014 values (Belando et al., 2017) and started to show a recovery in spring and summer 2018 (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2019). Moreover, there is evidence that the species found in the lagoon reach maximum biomass values in summer and tends to decay during the winter (Terrados and Ros, 1995). Hydrolysis typically represents the first step of organic carbon degradation, where particulate organic material is broken down to smaller, dissolved organic compounds such as peptides, long-chain fatty acids, and other nonvolatile biomolecules (Hee et al., 2001). This could explain the higher concentration of DOM and benthic flux during winter 2018. Moreover, we found a positive relationship between the benthic flux of DOC and DIC directly associated with the oxidation of organic carbon (F Cox) calculated in Vallejo et al., 2021 (Fig. 8). These results indicated that the oxidation processes (both aerobic and anaerobic) of organic matter are responsible for the elevated DOC concentration and fluxes found in the sediments pore waters. This relationship is strongest during winter 2018, in agreement with the presence of more labile compounds from in situ production that probably enhance bacterial degradation, hence benthic fluxes. Brodecka-Goluch and Łukawska-Matuszewska (2018) observed a strong positive relationship between the benthic fluxes of DOC and DIC in sediments pore water in the Baltic Sea related with the presence of methane and high content of organic matter. In this regard, Vallejo et al. (2021) found a greater contribution of anaerobic oxidation pathways related to the increase of the content of organic matter in the sediment. Moreover, previous work suggested that methanogenesis could be shaping the composition of DOM in sediments (Amaral et al., 2021a). On the other hand, the decreased in the benthic fluxes of DOM variables observed from north to south of the lagoon could be related with the lower residence time in the north than in the south basin (García-Oliva et al., 2018) in combination with groundwater inputs (Baudron et al., 2015), and with the high spatialtemporal variability in the sedimentary environments (e.g., changes in granulometry, degree of density/coverage by macrophytes and type of benthic macro fauna).

# 5. Conclusions

The distribution of DOM in the Mar Menor showed a strong seasonal and spatial variability. Overall, DOM was mainly associated with anthropogenic activities such as runoff from agriculture and effluents from WWTP, and with in situ production. In the water column, terrestrial and microbial humic-like components dominate the pool of DOM. In winter, DOM was mainly from autochthonous origin related with phytoplankton activity and benthic fluxes while in summer allochthonous DOM dominates. In the sediment pore water, protein-like compounds related with microbial metabolism were the most abundant, probably actively produced and of labile nature. The sediments of the lagoon act as a source of DOM to the overlying water, with higher benthic fluxes during winter than summer 2018. Probably this is associated with the breakdown and posts recovery of the system that occurred in 2016 and spring/summer 2018, respectively. Thus, during winter 2018 organic matter from the dead meadows accumulated enhancing microbial activity decreasing toward the summer 2018. The characteristic of DOM in both the water column and sediments reflect the anthropogenic impact that this lagoon is suffering.
