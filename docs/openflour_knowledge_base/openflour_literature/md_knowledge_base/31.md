ABSTRACT: Recent work has shown the presence of anomalous dissolved organic matter (DOM), with high optical yields, in deep waters 15 months after the Deepwater Horizon (DWH) oil spill in the Gulf of Mexico (GOM). Here, we continue to use the fluorescence excitation−emission matrix (EEM) technique coupled with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) modeling, measurements of bulk organic carbon, dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC), oil indices, and other optical properties to examine the chemical evolution and

transformation of oil components derived from the DWH in the water column of the GOM. Seawater samples were collected from the GOM during July 2012, 2 years after the oil spill. This study shows that, while dissolved organic carbon (DOC) values have decreased since just after the DWH spill, they remain higher at some stations than typical deep-water values for the GOM. Moreover, we continue to observe fluorescent DOM components in deep waters, similar to those of degraded oil observed in lab and field experiments, which suggest that oil-related fluorescence signatures, as part of the DOM pool, have persisted for 2 years in the deep waters. This supports the notion that some oil-derived chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM) components could still be identified in deep waters after 2 years of degradation, which is further supported by the lower DIC and partial pressure of carbon dioxide $( p \mathsf { C O } _ { 2 } )$ associated with greater amounts of these oil-derived components in deep waters, assuming microbial activity on DOM in the current water masses is only the controlling factor of DIC and $p { \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentrations.

# INTRODUCTION

The explosion of the Deepwater Horizon (DWH) MC252 released 4.4 million barrels $\left( 6 . 4 \times 1 0 ^ { 8 } \mathrm { L } \pm 2 0 \% \right)$ of light south Louisiana crude oil, from April to July 2010, into the northern Gulf of Mexico (GOM). This unprecedented event occurred from the seafloor at $1 5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ depth and was accompanied by the release of ca. 7 million L of dispersants into the water column.1−5 It has been suggested that dispersants combined with oil derivatives may pose an even greater threat to marine organisms than the parent oil compounds.6 With regard to the fate of DWH oil in the GOM water column, it remains uncertain if all of the oil has been truly removed. If not, it is possible that some of the oil was transformed into derivatives that are now an integral part of the dissolved organic matter (DOM) pool, no longer recognizable as oil or oil derivatives by traditional hydrocarbon analyses. In fact, recent work has shown that $2 9 - 4 3 \%$ of the suspended particulate organic matter in DeSoto Canyon, GOM, may be derived from fossil methane following the DWH spill.7 This $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -depleted carbon entered the particulate organic matter pool when taken up by methanotrophs during the spill.

Past work has shown that the complex mixture of constituents found in natural $\mathrm { D O M } ^ { 8 , 9 }$ is very reactive, with many contaminants in natural waters.10−12 Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) represents the largest fraction of DOM in natural waters13 and, along with chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM), represents significant and reactive components of the DOM pool.14 Certain aromatic components of CDOM as well as some oil constituents [e.g., soluble polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)] absorb light and fluoresce.15 This allows for detection of these compounds in natural waters through a variety of means, including threedimensional excitation−emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectra, specific ultraviolet absorbance (SUVA), or spectral slope.16−19 Although there have been problems with humic materials interfering with the detection of oil compounds, the advent of modeling EEM spectra by parallel factorial analysis (PARAFAC) has provided a way to deconvolve spectra into an array of scores and loadings that better characterize the materials comprising fluorescent DOM.20,21 More recently, the applications of EEM−PARAFAC have been used in detecting crude and weathered oil in the GOM during and after the DWH spill.22−25

The use of dispersants during the DWH spill appears to have enhanced the aqueous solubility of crude oil, which has resulted in the shift in the EEM spectra as well as degradation of lowmolecular-weight hydrocarbons.22−25 Moreover, ratios of oil components identified by EEM−PARAFAC and attributed to DWH oil are characterized by highly degraded oil, suggesting that, while this oil has changed chemically and optically, it can be tracked in the GOM, even over a year after the DWH explosion. However, this also means that traditional methods of oil detection using fluorescence based on solvent extractions26 may not capture the signals, which have been modeled using native fluorescence of seawater.27 Our objectives here are to (1) determine if oil fluorescence components can still be detected in the water column of the GOM 2 years after the spill and (2) determine if there are any linkages with the metabolism of these oil components, as reflected indirectly by the partial pressure of carbon dioxide $\left( \mathfrak { p } \mathbf { C } \mathbf { O } _ { 2 } \right)$ .

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

Study Site and Sampling. Water samples were collected in the northern GOM in the region of the DWH well on a cruise in July 2012 onboard the $\mathrm { \bar { R } / V }$ Pelican (Figure 1). The hydrographic data and specific sampling locations are listed in Table 1. Our 2012 sampling stations are all located below $2 8 ^ { \circ }$ $3 0 ^ { \prime } ~ \mathrm { N }$ and south of the Macondo well (Figure 1) with more deep-water stations, while stations in 2010 and $2 0 1 1 ^ { 2 2 }$ are all north of $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { ~ N ~ }$ , except one station.

Water samples were collected using Niskin bottles mounted on a CTD rosette system. Samples for DOC, ultraviolet−visible (UV−vis) absorbance, and fluorescence EEMs were gravityfiltered through precombusted GF/F Whatman filters $\mathrm { 4 7 ~ m m }$ diameter and $0 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ nominal pore size) into precombusted amber glass vials and stored immediately at $- 2 0 \mathrm { ~  ~ { ~ } ~ } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ Hydrophobic oil components adsorbed onto the filters were therefore not analyzed; rather the signals that we detected were present in dissolved form, nominally $< 0 . 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ . Unfiltered samples for dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) and alkalinity were collected in precombusted $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ clear Corning bottles, preserved with mercury chloride, and stored at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ .

Chemical Analyses. Measurements of DOC and UV−vis Absorption. Acidified samples ( $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $2 \mathbf { N }$ HCl added to 20 mL of sample) were analyzed for DOC on a Shimadzu TOCVCSH/CSN, using high-temperature catalytic oxidation.28 The detection limit on the instrument is $1 . 1 \ \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ with a precision within $2 \%$ , based on the coefficient of variation.

UV−vis absorption spectra of the samples were measured from 220 to $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ in $1 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ cells against a $1 8 . 2 ~ \mathrm { M } \Omega$ Milli- $\mathbf { \mathcal { Q } }$ water blank. Blank-corrected absorbance values for samples were converted to decadal absorption coefficients ( $[ a ,$ in $\mathbf { \bar { m } } ^ { - 1 } .$ ) by dividing by the path length. Specific UV absorption at 254 nm $\mathrm { ' S U V A _ { 2 5 4 } } ,$ ) was computed by dividing the decadal absorption coefficient at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ by the DOC concentration (in $\mathbf { g }$ of $\mathrm { ~ C ~ m } ^ { - 3 }$ ) giving units of $\mathbf { m } ^ { 2 } \mathbf { \Sigma } _ { \mathbf { g } }$ of ${ \mathrm { C } } ^ { - 1 1 8 }$ (Table 1).

DIC, Alkalinity, $p C O _ { 2 } ,$ and $p H .$ DIC was analyzed using a Marine Analytics and Data (MARIANDA) VINDTA 3D system that extracts $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ from an acidified seawater sample using zero air and quantitatively transfers the released $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ to a coulometric titration unit.29 Total alkalinity of the seawater was acquired through the use of the open cell titration method.29 Calculated $p { \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { p H }$ values were obtained using DIC and total alkalinity in the SeaCarb equation package.30

Measurements of Fluorescence EEMs. EEM fluorescence was measured on a Varian Eclipse spectrofluorometer at $9 5 0 \mathrm { V } _ { i }$ with excitation (ex) and emission $\scriptstyle ( { \mathrm { e m } } )$ slit widths set at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and an integration time of 0.0125 s. The em scans were collected at $2 \mathrm { n m }$ intervals from 260 to $4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ at increasing ex wavelengths from 220 to $4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , incremented in $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ steps. Manufacturer supplied corrections were made for ex energy and em detector responses and inner filtering effects and ultimately calibrated in quinine sulfate units.31

Statistical Analyses. PARAFAC Modeling and Principle Component Analysis $( P C A )$ Analyses. Corrected EEMs from the present study were combined with EEMs from Zhou et al.24 and remodeled using PARAFAC implemented in the DOMFluor Toolbox for MATLAB.21 The PARAFAC model was fit to EEMs measured on samples collected during both cruises in 2011 and right after the spill in 2010.24 The aim was to compare the model fit to a large data set of EEMs measured shortly after and 2 years after the spill. Each EEM was normalized to its total fluorescence to improve fitting.32 Four to eight component models were attempted. Split-half validation, using Tucker’s congruence coefficient and random initialization procedures to determine the lowest sum of squared error, was used to determine statistically that a four-component model best fit the data. Each component from the model was plotted as an EEM (Figure 3). The PARAFAC results were then compared to the model fit to EEMs measured only on the 2010 samples (Table 2).24 PCA was conducted on the 2012 sample scores from the PARAFAC model and DOC and $p { \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentrations, using the PLS Toolbox for MATLAB. Data were autoscaled, and eigenvectors greater than 1 were used to determine the number of components. The PCA model explained ${ > } 5 0 \%$ of the variance in the data.

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

The relationship between the DOC concentration and salinity showed a variety of influences (Table 1 and Figure 2A). In the low-salinity surface waters, the DOC concentrations were high because of the Mississippi River and coastal inputs of DOC. A large range of DOC concentrations were found in the $^ { 1 0 0 - }$ 1500 and $> 1 5 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ depth intervals. These deep interval values were grouped closely to salinity of 34.96, which was the same salinity reported for the deep seawater anomaly described by Zhou and Guo,22 which consisted of low DOC waters with high optical yields.22 The highest values for $p { \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ were found in these deeper intervals as well (Table 1 and panels B and D of Figure 2), indicating a possible oil influence. Moreover, the high $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values at depth indicated a higher degree of aromaticity per gram of organic carbon, which could be derived from a variety of sources, including oil, seeps, and DOM in turbidity currents.22 This may suggest the persistent presence of trace oil and oil-derived CDOM components in the deep waters, leading to persistently high aromaticity and SUVA values.

In comparison to samples taken during the DWH spill, northern GOM deep-water DOC concentrations decreased significantly just 5 months later, although the distribution of DOC was patchier22 than baseline concentrations observed prior to the spill.33,34 Deep-water samples with a salinity of $3 4 . 9 6 \pm 0 . 0 3$ contained DOC concentrations that ranged from 33 to $6 4 \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ , during both 2010 and 2011 cruises, and exhibited high $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values higher than those reported for the 254 Mississippi River.22 These samples were termed the “deep anomaly” and showed a strong influence of oil.22 During July 2012, the average DOC concentration of deep waters (depth $>$ $1 5 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ ; $1 1 5 \pm 1 0 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ ; $n = 1 2$ ) was skewed by a group of three stations with very high DOC concentrations $2 8 4 \pm 6 4$ $\mu \mathbf { M } _ { \mathrm { - } }$ ; $~ n ~ = ~ 3 ,$ (see Figures S1 and S2 of the Supporting Information). The DOC concentrations at these three stations were lower than contaminated waters observed by Zhou et al.24 during the oil spill in May/June 2010 (as high as $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ) but much higher than the baseline level in deep GOM water (ca. $4 0 { - } 5 0 ~ \mathrm { ~ } \mathrm { ~ } \mathrm { ~ } \dot { \mu } \mathrm { M } )$ ,33 although specific sampling locations were different. The presence of these high DOC waters may reflect the oil transformation into DOC during degradation25 or a new source of water-soluble oil in the bottom waters of the GOM. After these three high-concentration stations were removed, the average deep-water DOC concentration $( 5 9 \pm 1 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } ;$ ： $n = 9$ was similar to previous post-spill measurements.22 These samples also had high $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values $( 3 . 3 \pm 0 . 9 \mathrm { { \ m } } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { { g } }$ o f OC; $n = 9$ ), even higher than the “deep anomaly” reported by Zhou and Guo22 $\mathrm { ( < } \breve { 2 } . 5 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { g }$ of OC). Thus, the origin of this oil-derived DOC may be the product of DWH oil degradation25 and/or oil from natural seeps in this region. It has been suggested that seepage can be a significant source of hydrocarbons to the deep marine environment.35 The alteration of this seep-derived DOC occurs via dilution, microbial degradation, and migration of low-molecular-weight hydrocarbons.35

As described above, the $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values that we measured for GOM seawater (up to $4 { - } 4 . 5 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { g }$ of OC; Figure 2D) were remarkably high and actually greater than those measured during 2010 and 2011 ${ ( < 2 . \dot { 5 } \ \mathrm { \ m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { g } }$ of $\mathrm { O C } ) ^ { 2 2 }$ and for river waters flowing into the GOM $( 2 { - } 3 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { g }$ of OC).36 These large $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were likely due to the presence of the peak in the absorption spectra that was persistent among most of our samples (see Figure S1 of the Supporting Information). A comparison of fluorescence component distributions from the PARAFAC model was made between a site in the Zhou et al.24 study and a site sampled in July 2012 that appeared to be near the wellhead $( 2 8 . 2 \bar { 5 } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { ~ N ~ } ,$ , $8 8 . 8 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { ~ W ~ }$ . The fluorescence patterns here also suggested the presence of oil. In general, seawater fluorescence intensities from July 2012 were very low relative to the 2010 and 2011 samples.

Both inorganic carbon $( p \mathrm { { C O } } _ { 2 } )$ and organic carbon (DOC) metrics were used along with PARAFAC components in the analysis to look for common relationships influencing the variability in the data. Over half of the variation in these data was explained by the first two principle components: PC1 $( 3 2 . 6 \% )$ and PC2 $( 2 0 \% )$ .

PARAFAC modeling of the fluorescence EEM for DOM allowed us to uncover four major fluorescent components in the DOM pool and chemical evolution of oil in the water column (Table 2 and Figure 3). These components were matched to other PARAFAC models in the literature, using Tucker’s congruence coefficient, using a value of 0.95 as the threshold for an identical match between spectra (see Table S1 of the Supporting Information). Component 1 exhibited long wave em features typical of terrestrial humic-like DOM and matched components from two PARAFAC models in the OpenFluor database.37 A persistent oil-derived signature, suggesting freshwater-soluble oil from crude oil continues to exist in the deep-water column of the northern GOM, as shown by component 2, similar to a degraded oil component modeled by Zhou et al. (see Figure S4 of the Supporting Information), despite distinct differences between the two sampling periods (see Figure S5 of the Supporting Information).24 Moreover, component 3 was matched to a dominant oil component possibly from released crude oil.22−24 Component 4 resembled amino-acid-like DOM and matched amino-acid-like components from 10 separate PARAFAC models in the OpenFluor database.37

Strong correlations were observed and indeed are expected between PAHs and in situ fluorometers shortly after the DWH spill.38 However, it is important to distinguish between oilderived signals from PAHs and non-oil CDOM. First, many in situ CDOM fluorometers that measure at $\operatorname { e x } / \operatorname { e m }$ wavelength pairing of ca. $3 7 0 / 4 6 0 ~ \mathrm { \ n m }$ , although sensitive at $\mu \mathrm { g } \bar { \mathrm { ~ L ~ } } ^ { - 1 }$ concentrations, completely miss the $\mathrm { e x / e m }$ pairings of the components that we modeled here. In fact, non-oil CDOM fluorescence interferes in this region. Second, the components that we modeled were attributed to PAH fluorescence yet occurred in the aqueous phase and not as solvent extracts.39

Third, the subsequent increase in the aqueous solubility of oilderived fluorescent molecules because of the interaction with dispersants and/or bio-/photodegradation processes moved the molecules out of the analytical window of solvent-extractable materials. Prior work using total scanning fluorescence (TSF), a technique similar to EEM fluorescence, examined oil-derived fluorescence signatures from samples extracted with dichloromethane and found a general lack of a fluorescence signature attributable to oil.26 As described, the technique by Wade et al.26 focuses on a narrow window of fluorescence and was not modeled with techniques such as PARAFAC, which can deconvolve fluorescence signals masked in a mixture of fluorescing substances in natural seawater.

When oil is degraded, alkyl-substituted groups can be reduced,40 leaving behind intact aromatics.41 This may explain the difference in oil-derived CDOM components C2 and C3. Thus, the oil-derived signals in CDOM that we have modeled with PARAFAC likely result from degradation processes operating on the crude oil released during the DWH spill, producing C2 fluorescence, but also likely from oil-contaminated water further south and natural seeps in the GOM, continuing to produce C3 fluorescence. We hypothesize that these processes and perhaps the presence of other substances, such as dispersants, increase the aqueous solubility of oilderived compounds, ostensibly moving compounds originating in crude oil into the marine DOM pool.

Additionally, Zhou and $\mathrm { G u o } ^ { 2 2 }$ have shown that ratios of degraded oil components to fresh oil components are able to track the degree of decay for oil-derived DOM.22 We used the ratio of ${ \mathrm { C } } 2 / { \mathrm { C } } 3$ from our PARAFAC model to track degraded versus fresh oil-derived DOM during 2012 (Figure 4).22 Although the mean ratio increased over depth ranges from ${ < } 1 0 0$ and $1 0 0 { - } 1 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ and was highest in the $> 1 5 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ range, it was not significant $_ { p } = 0 . 6 1 9$ ; $t$ test with Bonferonni posthoc test). Given the abundance of oil seeps in the GOM and the amount of time that has passed since the oil spill, we cannot ignore the possibility of new sources. The large standard deviations of the means likely represent the presence of both degraded and fresh oil-derived DOM at all depths. This suggests a continual release of oil in deep waters of the GOM, likely from seeps, in addition to previously released oil which has been degraded in the water column.

A PCA conducted on samples from 2012 included $p { \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and DOC measurements and explained $5 2 \%$ of the variability in the data (Figure 5). Positive scores along PC1 $3 2 \%$ of variation) indicated an influence of loadings by degraded and fresh oil-like components C2 and C3, respectively (Figure 5). These components were attributed to aged or degraded oil based on comparisons to Zhou et al.24,25 and Zhou and Guo22 and comparisons to PAH fluorescence spectra.39 Positive scores along PC2 $2 0 \%$ of variation) potentially indicated DOM having greater fluorescence from terrestrial, microbial, and oilderived sources given that the loadings for all four components were positive along PC2. Of the samples with higher fluorescence, biogenic (terrestrial $^ +$ microbial) DOM sources appeared stronger in the surface waters of $< 1 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ than at depth. C4 from the PARAFAC model, which is related to aromatic amino acids and may signify microbial activity, trended opposite of the oil-related components C2 and C3, and scores near the loading for C4 were mainly in surface and mid-depths (Figure 6). This could indicate that microbes are not actively using these oil-related components as a food source and/or that a new source of oil was present from natural seeps in the regions.42−45

However, the oil-derived fluorescence components of CDOM did not influence sample scores at all depths. The PCA loads for oil-derived CDOM were opposite the loading for $p { \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ along PC2. Thus, several scores were weak in oil-derived fluorescence yet had high $p { \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ . This could indicate that oilderived organic carbon was oxidized, but the PCA explains only $5 0 \%$ of the variation in these data. Moreover, there are numerous sources of organic carbon that may be controlling $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentrations in these waters. Finally, in addition to microbial oxidation, dissolved $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ in the GOM deep water (ca. below $3 0 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ } ,$ is typically higher than in subsurface waters (between 500 and $1 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ ) because of high $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ in deep waters entering the GOM over the sill from the Caribbean. More work is needed to resolve these complex issues.

This work has shown that, while the DOC concentrations have decreased since just after the DWH spill, they remained higher at certain stations than typical deep-water concentrations for the GOM. Moreover, we continued to observe similar fluorescent DOM components in deep waters that have been linked with DWH oil identified in lab and field experiment s,24,25 which suggests that an oil-related CDOM signature has persisted for 2 years in the deep waters of the GOM. These findings support the notion that some oil-derived CDOM components are still present in the water column, which is further supported by the lower $p { \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ associated with greater amounts of these oil-derived components in deep waters, assuming that microbial activity on DOM in the current water masses is the only controlling factor of $p { \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentrations. In conclusion, the DWH spill, which introduced oil to the deep water of the GOM, has resulted in oil-derived CDOM signatures that could still be identified after 2 years. Further work is needed to better distinguish this persistent signature from local inputs of natural seeps in the region.
