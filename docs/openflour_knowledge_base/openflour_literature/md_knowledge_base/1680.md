# A B S T R A C T

The meridional distributions of fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM) and various hydrologic properties were investigated along $6 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ in the western Indian Ocean. Our results showed that the highest fluorescence of the humic FDOM $( \mathrm { F D O M _ { H } ) }$ was discovered in the Indian Deep Water (IDW), and relatively lower values were observed in the intruding water masses from the upper layer (e.g., Circumpolar Deep Water (CDW), Antarctic Intermediate Water (AAIW), and South Indian Central Water (SICW)). The deep $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ was robustly correlated with apparent oxygen utilisation (AOU), as suggested by previous studies. In particular, the slopes of the regression line AOU on $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ varied for different water masses and the two humic components. In this study, to identify the factor inducing the variations of the slope, we estimated the relative water mass fraction of different water masses using a three-end-member mixing model with a salinity- $\mathrm { \cdot F D O M _ { H } }$ diagram. The distribution of water mass fractions was in good agreement with water mass distribution from the conventional method from temperature and salinity distribution and previous studies. The $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ components were positively correlated with the aged water mass fraction (i.e., IDW; $r = 0 . 9 3$ ) and negatively correlated with fresher ones originating from the upper water $( r = - 0 . 9 3$ , $- 0 . 5 1$ , and − 0.95 for CDW, AAIW, and SICW, respectively). The fluorescence ratio between the two $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ components was also observed to be linked to the water mass fractions. The results indicate that the distribution of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ is attributed to the mixing of various deep-water masses during the global ocean circulation.

# 1. Introduction

Fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM) is a fraction of the coloured dissolved organic matter (CDOM), which fluoresces after absorbing photons. Generally, CDOM and FDOM can absorb penetrating solar radiation up to $9 0 \%$ in clear water over a wide range of ultraviolet (UV) and visible wavelengths (Morel et al., 2007; Stedmon and Nelson, 2015; Tedetti et al., 2010). Accordingly, the optically active organic components are one of the core factors affecting marine biological activities by protecting labile organisms (e.g., planktons and corals) from the harmful UV light (Arrigo and Brown, 1996) or attenuating photosynthetically active radiation (Ferrari et al., 1996; Häder et al., 2007; Slonecker et al., 2016). In addition, the optical properties of CDOM and FDOM enable tracing the biogeochemical processes in marine environments by using the spectral patterns and the location of fluorescent peaks. The CDOM and FDOM have been used to trace the mixing of water masses (Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2016; Stedmon et al., 2010), input of terrestrial dissolved organic matter (Anderson and Amon, 2015; Fichot et al., 2014), bioavailability, and diagenetic state (Burdige et al., 2004).

Based on the peak location of fluorescent excitation and emission spectrum, the FDOM is generally divided into two major groups, including protein-like $\mathrm { ( F D O M _ { P } ) }$ and humic-like components $( \mathrm { F D O M _ { H } ) }$ (Coble, 2007). The maximum peak of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { P } }$ appears sharp and narrow at relatively short excitation and emission wavelengths. $\mathrm { F D O M _ { P } }$ is recognised to be attributed to the labile fraction of the organic matter freshly produced through primary production and the subsequent biological activity (Lønborg et al., 2010). As $\mathrm { F D O M _ { P } }$ is more vulnerable to microbial degradation than bulk organic matter, the $\mathrm { F D O M _ { P } }$ is typically restricted to surface waters. In contrast, $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ has a broad emission spectra in relatively longer wavelengths than those of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { P } }$ due to a chemically complex mixture of heterogeneous organic matter. $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ is known to be produced by the microbial decomposition of organic debris in the sediment and soil (Kim and Kim, 2018; Laane and Kramer, 1990) and that of sinking marine snow (Yamashita and Tanoue, 2008; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2009). The $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ is known to be rapidly photobleached by UV radiation; thus those showed to be depleted in the surface-water layer (Heller et al., 2013; Kim and Kim, 2015; Stedmon and Markager, 2005).

In previous studies, the major production mechanism of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ in the deep ocean was reported to be the in situ microbial activity, supported by a robust correlation between $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ and remineralisationrelated variables (e.g., apparent oxygen utilisation [AOU] and inorganic nutrients) (Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Jørgensen et al., 2011; Nelson et al., 2010; Swan et al., 2009; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2008). Generally, the relationship has been a linear slope in the deep-water layer (Jørgensen et al., 2011; Nelson et al., 2010). However, it was discovered that the slope varied occasionally due to the rapid mixing of fresher water masses in the North Atlantic Ocean (Heller et al., 2013; Nelson et al., 2007; Nelson et al., 2010; Swan et al., 2009), input of sedimentary $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ to the overlying water column of the deep East Sea (Sea of Japan) (Kim and Kim, 2016), and biological activities in the upper water layer of the global ocean (Catalá et al., 2016).

As the deep Indian Ocean is a branch of the global ocean circulation system, it is an ideal location to determine the origins and biogeochemical cycles of FDOM. In this study, we also observed the transition of the slope according to the depth and water masses in the deep Indian Ocean. To determine the major factors controlling the variations of the slope, we investigated the meridional distribution of FDOM components using the parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) coupled with various hydrologic properties in the western Indian Ocean. In addition, we aim to demonstrate the potential of FDOM as a tracer of water mass mixing in the Indian Ocean.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Sampling and measurement of hydrological properties

A sampling cruise was conducted along $6 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ from $5 ~ ^ { \mathrm { { o } } } \mathrm { { N } }$ to $1 6 ~ ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { s } }$ in the western Indian Ocean during July 2017 (Fig. 1) to investigate FDOM and the hydrologic properties (temperature, salinity, and dissolved oxygen [DO]). An SBE 32 Carousel Water Sampler coupled to a conductivity-temperature-depth (CTD) profiler (SBE 911plus; Sea-Bird Electronics, Bellevue, WA, USA) was used for vertical water sampling and the measurement of hydrologic properties. The probes of the CTD were calibrated by the manufacturer approximately 100 days prior to the beginning of the cruise. The DO concentration measured using the CTD profiler was corrected using Winkler titration ([DO_Winkler] $= 1 . 0 3 3 3 \times \mathrm { [ D O \_ C T D ] } + 1 . 0 7 3 9$ ; $\mathbf { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 9 9$ ; $n = 7 9$ ).

# 2.2. FDOM measurements and statistical analysis

Seawater samples for FDOM were filtered through pre-muffled (4 h at $5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ Whatman GF/F glass fibre filters (pore size: $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ on board. Filtered samples were stored in pre-combusted EPA amber glass vials (Fisher Scientific, PA, USA) and kept in a refrigerator below $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Three-dimensional fluorescence excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) were measured using a spectrofluorometer (Aqualog, HORIBA Jobin Yvon, NJ, USA) just after the filtration. The analysis was conducted using scanning emission wavelengths of $2 4 9 . 1 \mathrm { - } 5 9 9 . 2 \mathrm { n m }$ in $4 . 5 \mathrm { n m }$ increments and excitation wavelengths of $2 5 1 { - } 5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in $3 \mathrm { n m }$ increments with an integration time of 3 s. Rayleigh and Raman scattering peaks of the EEMs were eliminated by replacing with missing values. The inner filter effect was further corrected with the UV absorbance values of each sample using the Aqualog Software. The non-negativity constraint was applied in all three modes. PARAFAC modelling for 515 EEMs data was performed using the Solo $^ +$ MIA software package (Eigenvector Research Inc., WA, USA), and two of them were removed as the outlier. The PARAFAC model was statistically validated by splithalf analysis. In addition, core consistency test (Bro and Kiers, 2003) was applied to validate the model and to identify the appropriate number of PARAFAC components. Fluorescent intensities of the samples were normalised every day to the area under the Raman peak of Milli-Q water at an excitation wavelength of $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and these values were expressed as Raman Units (R.U.) (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009). The correlations between the variables were analysed with Pearson correlation using IBM SPSS version 23 for Windows (IBM SPSS Inc., IL, USA). The regression outliers were removed by the Bonferroni outlier test, package car, using R (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria, Version 3.6.1) and RStudio (RStudio Inc., MA, USA, Version 1.2.1335).

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Deep water mass properties in the study area

In the upper $5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ depth, we found Arabian Sea Surface Water (ASSW; T: $2 4 . 0 \mathrm { - } 3 0 . 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , S: 35.5–36.8), Indonesian Throughflow Water (ITFW; T: $8 . 0 { - } 2 3 . 0 \ \mathrm { ^ circ C } .$ , 34.4–35.0), and South Indian Subtropical Underwater (STUW; T: $> 1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , $S \colon > 3 5 . 4$ , DO: $> 1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \ k \mathrm { g } )$ ) based on the temperature-salinity diagram (Fig. 1, Emery, 2001; Talley et al., 2011). Because this study was focused on the water masses deeper than $5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ , we only described in detail the properties of deeper water masses.

Intermediate waters occupying the depth of $5 0 0 { - } 1 5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ are characterised by salinity. The Red Sea Overflow Water (RSOW, Talley et al. (2011); Table 1) or Red Sea-Persian Gulf Intermediate Water (RSPGIW, $5 { - } 1 4 ~ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , 34.8–35.4, Emery (2001)) was observed from $2 ~ ^ { \circ } { \sf S }$ to $5 ~ ^ { \mathrm { { o } } } \mathrm { { N } }$ in our study. It is characterised by a very low concentration of DO $( 3 0 { - } 6 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l / k g }$ in our observation), and the depth is deeper towards the south. The higher salinity (34.6–35.8, Emery (2001)) water within the upper $1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ in the temperature range of $9 { - } 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ is called the South Indian Central Water (SICW), which is formed mainly by the subduction of South Indian Subtropical Surface Waters or Subantarctic Front Waters. Antarctic Intermediate Water (AAIW) possesses minimum salinity (33.8–34.8) in the temperature range of $2 { - } 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Emery, 2001). It was observed from $1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } { \sf S }$ to $1 6 ~ ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { S } } ,$ , and the minimum salinity value increased in higher latitude with higher oxygen $( 1 2 0 { - } 1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mu \mathrm { g } )$ . It is noteworthy that the water subducted from the Subantarctic Front and overlays AAIW possesses high oxygen and high salinity. It is difficult to identify the AAIW with only the oxygen profile from $1 6 ~ ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { S } }$ . The deep water of the Indian Ocean comprises the Circumpolar Deep Water (CDW) and Indian Deep Water (IDW). CDW is the water mass characterised by a temperature range of $1 . 0 { - } 2 . 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and a salinity range of 34.62–34.73 (Emery, 2001) with a relatively higher oxygen level. This cold CDW is observed under $3 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ in most of the sampling stations (Fig. 1). IDW is known as the aged deep-water mass with low oxygen and high nutrient contents. The elevated salinity of IDW is associated with the diffusive addition of saline water in the northwest Indian

Ocean (Arabian Sea).

# 3.2. FDOM components

One to five components were retained for the PARAFAC model. Three components (C1, C2, and C3) were identified by the PARAFAC model based on the results from core consistency and split-half validation. For the three-component model, the explained variance was $9 6 . 9 5 \%$ for the data set, and core consistency was $9 8 \%$ . It was also validated through the split-half analysis (similarity: $9 8 . 3 \%$ ). The residual, which represents variability not identified by the model, showed no significant systematic deviations (Fig. S1). However, more than three components were not validated. The three components were matched to those identified in previous studies from the OpenFluor database with Tucker congruence coefficients exceeding 0.95 (Murphy et al., 2014a). The FDOM components were distinguished by two humic-like (C1 and C2) and one protein-like (C3) components according to each peak location and the literature (Table 2, Fig. 2). The C1 $( \mathrm { M a x } _ { \mathrm { e x / e m } } = 2 5 0 / 4 8 5 \mathrm { n m }$ for the primary peak; $\mathrm { M a x } _ { \mathrm { e x / e m } } = 3 7 0 /$ $4 8 5 \mathrm { n m }$ for the secondary peak) resembled aromatic macromolecular substances originating from terrestrial and microbial-degraded organic matter (Osburn et al., 2016). Moreover, it is generally observed to be ubiquitous in various marine environments (Walker et al., 2009; Kowalczuk et al., 2013; Tanaka et al., 2014; Catalá et al., 2015; Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2016). C2 $( \mathrm { M a x } _ { \mathrm { e x / e m } } = 3 3 0 / 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for the primary peak; $\mathrm { M a x } _ { \mathrm { e x / e m } } = 2 5 0 / 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for the secondary peak) corresponded to $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ with shorter emission maximum that has been traditionally documented to originate from marine organic matter via microbial remineralisation (Stedmon et al., 2011; Catalá et al., 2015; Wünsch et al., 2018; Murphy et al., 2014b). Deep $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ is known to remain at centennial timescales. Thus, both $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ components can be suitable indicators for investigating the processes in the timescale of hundreds of years (Catalá et al., 2015). The peak location of C3 $\bf ( M a x _ { e x } /$ $\mathrm { \Omega } _ { \mathrm { e m } } = 2 8 0 / 3 2 0 \mathrm { n m } )$ was similar to that of the protein-like or the tryptophan amino acid-like component (Bianchi et al., 2014; Fellman et al., 2010; Cawley et al., 2012; Yamashita et al., 2010). The turnover time in case of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { P } }$ is on a timescale of days in the surface water because it is attributed to biologically labile organic compounds (Jørgensen et al., 2011; Lønborg et al., 2010).

# 3.3. Distribution of FDOM components

Fluorescent intensities of $\mathrm { \ F D O M _ { H } }$ (C1 and C2) were depleted at the surface water layer $\left( 0 . 1 0 0 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 2 0 \right.$ R.U. for C1 and $0 . 0 6 4 \ \pm \ 0 . 0 1 8$ R.U. for C2) and increased with an increase in depth $( 0 . 3 7 3 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 3 7$ R.U. for C1 and $0 . 2 7 6 \pm \ : 0 . 0 3 1$ for C2 in the bottom layer) (Fig. 3(b) and (d)). It is due to the photochemical degradation by UV radiation in the surface water and microbial production from particulate organic matter in deep water (Álvarez-Salgado et al., 2013; Arıstegui et al., ́ 2002; Nelson et al., 2010; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2009). Comparison of meridional $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ distributions with the hydrological parameters demonstrated patterns that are similar to those of water masses in the western Indian Ocean, and these unique characteristics of each water mass (Fig. 3). Near the AAIW core located in the southern part of the study area, the relatively low salinity and the high DO level $( 3 4 . 5 7 9 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 2 5$ for salinity, $1 3 5 . 5 \pm \ : 6 . 4 \mu \mathrm { m o l / k g }$ for DO) were found at approximately $5 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ depth (Fig. 3(c) and (e)), and concurrently lower concentrations of C1 and C2 $0 . 2 8 9 \ \pm \ 0 . 0 1 7$ R.U. for C1, $0 . 2 4 9 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { R } .$ U. for C2) were observed (Fig. 3(b) and (d)). The highest concentrations of C1 and C2 were observed in the northern part of the deep Indian Ocean showing the lower DO levels (Fig. 3(b) and (d)). In addition, relatively lower concentrations of C1 and C2 in the bottom-water layer were observed from ${ 1 5 ~ ^ { \circ } S }$ to the equator (Fig. 3(b) and (d).

# 3.4. Relationship between $F D O M _ { H }$ and AOU

The statistically positive correlation between deep $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ and remineralisation-related variables (e.g., AOU) was typically found in the Pacific and the Indian Ocean, and it was used to elucidate the microbial production of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ (Hayase and Shinozuka, 1995; Jørgensen et al., 2011; Nelson et al., 2010; Swan et al., 2009; Yamashita and Tanoue, 2008). In this study, based on the result from the high-resolution investigation, the significant correlation of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ against AOU was observed in the deep-water layer (below $5 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ , and the slope of the relationship showed a significant variation according to the depth and water masses (Fig. 4(a) and (b)). The slope corresponding to C1 was steeper in depth ranging from 500 to $1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m } ( [ \mathrm { C 1 } ] = 0 . 0 0 1 9 \times [ \mathrm { A O U } ]$ - 0.0473; $\mathbf { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 4 , p \ < \ . 0 1 $ than that in the depth ranging from 1000 to $2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ $( \mathrm { [ C 1 ] } = 0 . 0 0 1 0 \times \mathrm { [ A O U ] } + 0 . 1 9 4 0$ ; $\mathbf { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 2 4$ , $p \ < \ . 0 1 )$ . However, this slope was more gradual than that in the depth under $2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ $( [ \mathsf { C 1 } ] = 0 . 0 0 2 6 \times [ \mathsf { A O U } ]$ - 0.1029; $\mathbf { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 5 5$ , $p \ < \ . 0 1 $ ) (Fig. 4(a)). However, the relationship of C2, another humic-like component, against AOU showed clearly different from that of C1, and a relatively larger scatter with AOU was also observed in this case (Fig. 4(b)). The fluorescence of C2 appeared to be weakly correlated to AOU in each depth range compared to that of C1 $( \mathbf { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 4 7$ , p < .01 in $5 0 0 { - } 1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ ; $\mathrm { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 9$ , $\mathbf { p } < \mathbf { \nabla } . 0 1$ in $1 0 0 0 { - } 2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ ; $\mathbf { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 9$ , $\mathbf { p } \ < \ . 0 1$ under $2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ ). Thus, C2 seems to be attributed from other processes rather than the in situ microbial activity in the water column. This was also supported by the relationship between the ratio of the two humic components (C1/C2) and AOU (Fig. 4(c)). Relatively fresher water masses in the depth ranging from 500 to $0 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ } ( \mathrm { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 4 7 , \mathrm { ~ p ~ < ~ } . 0 1 )$ and under $2 0 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ } ( \mathrm { r } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 3 2 , \mathrm { ~ p ~ < ~ } . 0 1 )$ showed a more robust correlation than those in the depth range of $1 0 0 0 { - } 2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ $( \mathbf { r } ^ { 2 } < 0 . 0 1$ , $p = . 8 9 9 \}$ . This indicates that the production rate of C1 per unit of the consumed DO concentration compared to those of C2 is likely to be different in the depth and water masses. Catalá et al. (2015) also reported the different production rate between two humic-like components, perhaps due to the potential mechanisms (e.g., the diversity of microbial producers and environmental aspects) causing the trend.

# 3.5. Water mass fraction

To identify the factor inducing the variation of the $\mathrm { F D O M } _ { \mathrm { H } ^ { - } } \mathrm { A O U }$ relationships with the depth and water masses, the representative water masses were identified, and the relationships were compared between the water masses. The respective findings are plotted in Fig. 5. We considered CDW, IDW, and RSOW as the representative water masses based on their hydrological properties and the pattern of the diagram. The mixing diagram between C1 and salinity, which are conservative parameters, showed the distinct mixing of water masses. Thus, we calculated the relative water mass fraction of each water mass in the deep layer $( > 5 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ using a salinity-C1 diagram. The highest salinity was used as the end-member of RSOW, because our investigation was extended to the southern margin of RSOW, and the salinity value appears to be diluted in this study. # 3.6. Linking $F D O M _ { H }$ to water mass fractions

The fluorescence of deep C1 was positively correlated to fIDW $( r = 0 . 9 3$ , $p \ < \ . 0 0 1 $ . However, it was negatively correlated to fCDW $( r = - 0 . 9 3$ , $\mathrm { ~ p ~ < ~ } . 0 0 1 $ , fAAIW $( r = - 0 . 5 1 $ , $p \ < \ . 0 0 1 )$ ), and fSICW $( r = - 0 . 9 5$ , $\mathsf { p } < . 0 0 1 $ (Table 3). In particular, the highest fluorescent intensities of C1 were simultaneously observed in the $\operatorname { h i g h } f _ { I D W }$ and the low DO level in the northern part (Figs. 3 and 6). Based on the positive relationship of C1 against AOU (Fig. 4), this high fluorescence of C1 is likely to be attributed to the in situ microbial remineralisations of organic matter in the aged Indian Ocean during the global water circulation (Jørgensen et al., 2011). On the other hand, the fluorescent intensities of deep C1 observed in the fresher water masses (i.e., CDW, AAIW, and SICW) were lower than those in the same depth range. It may result from the dilution caused by the depletion of C1 due to photobleaching in the upper layer.

The fluorescence intensities of C1 and C2 in the deep-water layer were correlated in the whole dataset $( r = 0 . 7 3$ , $p \ < \ . 0 0 1 $ ) owing to their similar properties and origins (Baker and Spencer, 2004; Zhao et al., 2016); however, their relationships with AOU were significantly different according to the depth values (Fig. 4). Based on the results from this study, the varied patterns of the relationships seem to be attributed to the mixing of different deep-water masses with the varied production and removal rates of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ . This is supported by the ratio between the two different humic components (C1/C2) was negatively correlated with fAAIW $( r = - 0 . 4 1$ , $\mathsf { p } < . 0 0 1 $ ) and fSICW $( r = - 0 . 8 4$ , $p = . 0 0 2 )$ and positively correlated with fIDW $( r = 0 . 3 3$ , $\mathrm { ~ p ~ } < \ . 0 0 1 $ ) (Table 3). As AAIW and SICW are mixed with relatively aged water masses at each depth, the relationships observed indicate that C1, rather than C2, preferentially remains in the aged deep-water layer and is depleted in the upper water. It was supported by the fact that the ratio between the terrestrial $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ and marine $\mathrm { \ F D O M _ { H } }$ in the North Pacific Deep Water was reported to be higher than that in the shallow basin (Tanaka et al., 2014). It is possibly associated with $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ with shorter emission maximum that could have occurred due to the UV radiation in the upper layer (Moran et al., 2000). Therefore, our results indicated that the variations in the $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ -AOU relationship was affected by the mixing of various deep-water masses with different production and removal rates of $\mathrm { F D O M _ { H } }$ during the global ocean circulation.
