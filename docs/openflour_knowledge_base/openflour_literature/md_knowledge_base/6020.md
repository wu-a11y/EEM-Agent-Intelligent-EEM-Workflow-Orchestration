# Dry habitats sustain high CO2 emissions from temporary ponds across seasons

Received: 6 July 2017 Accepted: 24 January 2018 Published: xx xx xxxx

Biel Obrador $\textcircled { \textcircled { 1 } } ^ { 1 }$ , Daniel von Schiller $\oplus 2 , 3$ , Rafael Marcé $\textcircled { \scriptsize { 1 } } ^ { 2 }$ , Lluís Gómez-Gener 1,4, Matthias Koschorreck $\textcircled { 1 0 } ^ { 5 }$ , Carles Borrego $\textcircled { 1 0 } ^ { 2 , 6 } \textcircled { \times }$ Núria Catalán $\textcircled { 1 0 } ^ { 1 , 2 , 7 }$

Despite the increasing understanding of the magnitude and drivers of carbon gas emissions from inland waters, the relevance of water fuctuation and associated drying on their dynamics is rarely addressed. Here, we quantifed ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $C H _ { 4 }$ fuxes from a set of temporary ponds across seasons. The ponds were in all occasion net ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ emitters irrespective of the presence or absence of water. While the ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ fuxes were in the upper range of emissions for freshwater lentic systems, $C H _ { 4 }$ fuxes were mostly undetectable. Dry habitats substantially contributed to these emissions and were always a source of $\mathtt { C O } _ { 2 } ,$ whereas inundated habitats acted either as a source or a sink of atmospheric ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ along the year. Higher concentrations of coloured and humic organic matter in water and sediment were linked to higher ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ emissions. Composition of the sediment microbial community was related both to dissolved organic matter concentration and composition, but we did not fnd a direct link with ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ fuxes. The presence of methanogenic archaea in most ponds suggested the potential for episodic $C H _ { 4 }$ production and emission. Our results highlight the need for spatially and temporally inclusive approaches that consider the dry phases and habitats to characterize carbon cycling in temporary systems.

Inland waters are an important component of the global carbon (C) cycle1 . Tey intensely bury C in their sediments and emit high amounts of gaseous C species to the atmosphere, mostly as a result of the input and subsequent biological processing of terrestrial organic matter2 . A myriad of local and regional studies in the last two decades have aimed at quantifying carbon dioxide $\left( \mathrm { C O } _ { 2 } \right)$ and methane $\mathrm { ( C H _ { 4 } ) }$ ) emissions from streams, rivers, lakes and reservoirs, allowing for increasingly accurate global estimates3–10. We have gained a better understanding of the processes determining C emissions across wide environmental and geographical gradients5–9 but uncertainties regarding the variability and controls of such fuxes in some ecosystems still remain. In particular, C processing in temporary systems that recurrently run dry, a natural hydrological feature of many watersheds in the world, has received little attention11. As an example, current global estimates do not consider C emissions from temporary rivers when they are $\mathrm { d r y ^ { 1 1 } }$ .

Small temporary lentic systems are one example of an understudied ecosystem in terms of C biogeochemistry. Small systems in general (i.e. ponds, small lakes and impoundments) are deemed as potential hotspots of C cycling12,13 and host high emission rates at diferent latitudes10,14–18. Te intense rates of C processing in small systems along with their global abundance19,20 $7 7 \%$ of lentic systems have a surface area between 0.002 and 0.01 $\mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 } )$ suggest the potentially major role of small lakes and ponds at the global scale10,13 and an increasing relevance of their C emissions due to their high sensitivity to warming21.

Temporary ponds are small inland water bodies that experience a recurrent dry phase of varying duration, which can imply complete or partial drying22. Tey have unique chemical, biological and physical features and have long been recognised as key elements for biodiversity conservation23,24. Te few existing studies on C processing in temporary ponds show their relevance as C emitters17,25,26. However, their emissions have seldom been measured directly17, for $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration has been used to indirectly estimate gas fuxes in most cases. Moreover, the approaches used to date have been restricted to the aquatic phase, so dry habitats within ponds or temporal phases without any water at all have not been considered10,25,26,31. In contrast, a recent study on Mediterranean temporary ponds has shown the relevance of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions from their dry habitats during the fooding phase17. Tese results agree with recent works reporting high $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions from the dry areas of temporary rivers and reservoirs27–30.

Te paucity of data on C emissions from temporary ponds is paired with a poor understanding of the environmental drivers that shape them. Catalán et al. 17, showed that spatially integrated $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions during the fooding phase of Mediterranean temporary ponds were not related to the properties of the catchment, but rather to the intrinsic properties of the pond such as the amount of organic matter present in water and sediments. In contrast, Holgerson31 found that variables related to the processing of terrestrial organic matter largely explained dissolved $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ dynamics in the water of temporary ponds in temperate forested areas, pinpointing the need for a better understanding of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ regulation in ponds.

Here, we quantifed $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ fuxes in Mediterranean temporary ponds along the diferent phases of their hydrological cycle. We selected ten temporary ponds located on the island of Menorca (Western Mediterranean) covering a wide range of physicochemical, geomorphologic and hydrological properties (Table 1). To characterize contrasting situations along their hydrological cycle, we sampled the ponds in autumn (the fooding phase); in spring when the ponds are mostly full (the wet phase); and in summer, when the ponds were entirely dry (the dry phase). Moreover, to capture all variability across and within ponds, we sampled the three habitats present in the ponds: dry habitats, comprising 1) emerged-vegetated sediments (non-inundated areas with presence of vascular plants), and 2) emerged-bare sediments (non-inundated areas with bare sediments); and 3) inundated habitats (areas with a water layer; see Table S1 for habitat areas). We performed direct measurements of gas fuxes in all these habitats separately and integrated them to estimate total fuxes for each pond. We hypothesized that fuxes would be comparable or higher to those in other lentic ecosystems and highest during the dry phase of the hydrological cycle due to large contributions from dry habitats. We also identifed the main drivers of the variability in the fuxes by evaluating the roles of a wide set of geomorphological, physicochemical and biological variables such as water and sediment organic matter quantity and composition and microbial community composition in the ponds studied.

# Results

Magnitude and variability of ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $C H _ { 4 }$ fuxes. Te ponds were net emitters of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ to the atmosphere irrespective of the hydrological phase, with a mean total fux (i.e. spatially integrated fux including all habitats, $\mathrm { T } { \cdot } \mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 } .$ ) of $1 0 7 . 8 \pm 1 0 3 . 9 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ $\pm$ standard deviation; $\mathrm { n } = 3 0$ ; Table 2; Fig. 1). We observed small negative $\mathrm { T } { \cdot } \mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ only on one occasion (Table 2). Tere was high variability in $\mathrm { T } { \cdot } \mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ between ponds and between phases (range 15.7 to $4 9 2 . 5 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 } )$ . Te maximum $\mathrm { T } { \cdot } \mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ was measured during the dry phase (Table 2; Fig. 1), although we did not observe any signifcant seasonal pattern in $\mathrm { T } { \cdot } \mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ along the hydrological cycle (ANOVA between phases, $\mathrm { F } = 0 . 3 4$ , $\mathrm { p } > 0 . 0 5$ ; Fig. 1). In contrast, $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ fuxes were of minor importance and only detectable on a few occasions in dystrophic systems (Tables 1 and 2; detection limit estimated to be $0 . 0 4 \mathrm { m i o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ).

Te diferent habitats of the ponds (inundated, emerged-vegetated and emerged-bare) were all active in terms of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fuxes (Fig. 2). Te minor role of inundated habitats contrasted with the high efux measured in emerged habitats in all phases of the hydrological cycle. Emerged habitats showed signifcantly higher rates than inundated habitats when present (ANOVA $\mathrm { F } = 6 8 . 9$ and $\mathrm { F } = 3 2 . 2$ for the fooding and wet phases, respectively, $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 1$ ). Within the emerged areas of the ponds, the emerged-vegetated sediments showed higher efuxes than the emerged-bare habitats both in the fooding and wet phases (Tukey test $> 0 . 0 1$ ). Diferences between emerged-vegetated and -bare habitats were indistinguishable in the dry phase (Fig. 2). Interestingly, in four ponds the inundated habitats had negative fuxes (i.e. invasion of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ from the atmosphere into the water) during the wet phase (Fig. 3a) although these fuxes were compensated by the positive fuxes from the other habitats. In contrast, during the fooding phase, all inundated habitats were emitters of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ to the atmosphere.

# Discussion

All the temporary ponds studied were net emitters of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ to the atmosphere. Te emissions reported here (mean $1 0 7 . 8 \mathrm { m m o } \mathrm { \bar { l } m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { \bar { d } } ^ { - 1 }$ , range 15.7 to $4 9 2 . 5 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ) were much higher than a recent global estimate for small $( < 0 . 0 0 1 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 } )$ permanent ponds (mean $3 5 . 2 \pm 5 . 2$ standard error;10) and in the same range described for intermittent rivers28 and temporary wetlands35. Te high variability in the fuxes between and within ponds prevented the identifcation of clear temporal patterns, but depicted the ponds as net emitters of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ regardless of the hydrological phase, as shown by comparable emission rates during the fooding, wet and dry phases (Fig. 1). Tis is a relevant fnding that supports recent studies presenting temporary aquatic systems as extremely active biogeochemical sites during their dry phases11,29.

Interestingly, the emerged habitats were key areas contributing to the total $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions of the ponds. Emerged habitats contributed substantially to the total surface area of the ponds not only during the dry phase but also in both fooding and wet phases ( $8 0 \%$ and $5 1 \%$ on average, respectively, Table S1). More importantly, emerged habitats showed higher emissions per unit area than the inundated habitats, hosting fuxes generally above $5 0 { \mathrm { - } } 1 5 0 \mathrm { m m o l }$ $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 }$ $\mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ depending on the presence or absence of vegetation (Fig. 2). Tis agrees with recent fndings reporting high $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions (well above 100 mmol $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathbf { \bar { d } } ^ { - 1 } )$ from dry sediments in rivers and reservoirs28–30. Tis is much higher than the literature values for C emissions from lakes, reservoirs, and permanent ponds4–10 $( 1 8 { - } 5 5 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { d a y } ^ { - 1 } )$ . Higher emissions from emerged than from inundated habitats may be due to the physical limitation of gas difusion under inundated conditions28,36 or boosted microbial activity in dry conditions37,38. Higher oxygen availability under dry conditions may enhance the release of labile organic materials through microbial cell lysis and physical processes39. However, microbial activity may not behave linearly in relation to drying, as enzyme activity is considerably decreased if water content drops below certain levels during complete desiccation30,40. In any case, the interplay between drying-rewetting events, sediment water content, microbial activity, and gas difusivity is complex, as is demonstrated by the extensive literature on this topic in wetlands, soils, and more recently dry river beds40–42. Indeed, in order to achieve a complete understanding of C mineralization and gas fuxes in the dry sediments of aquatic ecosystems, it must be considered that 1) short-term $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ pulses afer drying-rewetting events may hide a substantial part of total C annual fuxes from these environments39 and 2) biological activity is the most plausible origin for the $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions from dry areas, but other processes such as carbonate reactions with the system or photochemical degradation of organic matter may also contribute to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ efux43–45.

Within the emerged habitats, vegetated sediments showed higher emissions than bare sediments despite the large variability in vegetation types across ponds, which included perennial macrophytes, small annual helophytes and hydrophytes46. Te higher emission in vegetated habitats can be related to either direct $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ production by root respiration (i.e., autotropic respiration) or to indirect changes in sediment texture and porosity facilitating gas difusion47,48. Unfortunately, our results do not allow for establishing complete C budgets in the ponds, since primary production by the vegetation was not measured.

In contrast to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fuxes, $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ fuxes were of minor importance across all the ponds and habitats and exclusive to the inundated habitats of dystrophic systems. Although this observation is contrary to the presence of methanogenic lineages in some of the ponds, the co-occurrence of methanotrophic bacteria in the sediments provides a plausible explanation for the low $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ emissions measured. Moreover, although $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ production has been detected in arid soils, production rates are usually very slow when oxygen is present49 and soil humidity low50. Tus, the conditions in the emerged sediments studied explain the low $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ emissions detected in these habitats despite the microbial methanogenic potential of the community. Also, it is likely that episodic high $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ fuxes were not captured with our sampling design. In an Amazonian foodplain lake, relevant $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ emissions were only observed during the frst few days afer drying and later $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ emissions from dry sediments were negligible51. Te low $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ emissions measured in temporary ponds here contrasts with the high $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ emissions reported for permanent ponds and small lakes, where ebullitive $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ emissions can be signifcant3,10.

Another relevant fnding of this study is the divergent $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fuxes of inundated and emerged habitats. We showed that fuxes from inundated habitats were not only lower than those from emerged habitats but they were negative (i.e., acting as C sinks; Fig. 2) during some phases. Tis observation contrasts with the predominant role of the ponds as $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emitters when all habitats were integrated. However, our emission rates from dry areas are likely a maximum estimate because we did not measure primary production by vegetation. Taking into account the typical hydroperiod of these temporary ponds (Table 1) and the areas of the diferent habitats (Table S1), the inundated habitats are unlikely to dominate the total pond area on an annual basis. Accordingly, ponds likely act very rarely as net C sinks. In this sense, even though this dataset captures spatial fux variability by sampling all pond habitats, the discrete temporal approach used prevents thorough estimates of annual budgets. Such estimates would require continuous monitoring of fuxes along a hydrological year.

Te contrasting pattern between inundated vs emerged habitats undermines traditional approaches of temporary pond biogeochemistry, where those habitats not inundated or those phases without water are typically not considered25,26,31. Our results highlight the need for spatially and temporally inclusive approaches to characterise C biogeochemistry in temporary systems, as has been recently pointed out in other temporary aquatic ecosystems such as rivers11,28,29 or reservoirs30.

Te total $\left( \mathrm { T } { \cdot } \mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 } \right)$ ) and the water $\left( \mathrm { W } { \cdot } \mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 } \right.$ ) fuxes were predicted by descriptors of organic matter composition and quantity that segregated ponds with contrasting trophic state (Figs 3 and 4). On the one hand, ponds with higher $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ showed higher concentrations of humic organic matter in the water and sediments. $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ has been previously related to organic C concentration in water52,53 and dry river beds29 as well as with humic substances5 , typically in boreal or deciduous forest areas. W- $\mathrm { \cdot F C O } _ { 2 }$ is traditionally related to the oxidation of terrestrial substances within the water column. Indeed, the ponds found to have positive W- $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ throughout the campaigns had a notably dystrophic character (Fig. 3; Table 1) with elevated concentrations and coloured DOC. However, these coloured lignin-derived compounds are likely to be related with the standing submerged54 and surrounding riparian vegetation, as no relationship was previously found with catchment characteristics17. Te $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fuxes from emerged habitats could not be explained by the measured variables in an independent PLS model. However, the variables selected in the PLS model for the $\mathrm { T } { \cdot } \mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 } ,$ , which includes the emissions from the emerged habitats, are in agreement with the drivers of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fuxes from dry sediments shown elsewhere29, such as organic matter concentration or relative humidity (Fig. 4).

On the other hand, we found that ${ \mathrm { W } } { \mathrm { - F C O } } _ { 2 }$ was related to protein-like DOM from autochthonous sources. Te switch from positive to negative W- $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ between the two phases with water, which occurred in parallel with a relative increase of the protein-like component, points towards a higher infuence of the in-situ production in some systems. However, DOM sources showed no change between phases in the dystrophic systems (Fig. 3). Enhanced contributions of DOM from autochthonous sources are frequently related to the release of more bioavailable compounds55. Terefore, increased respiration and $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ might be expected. However, if this autochthonous production is due to autotrophic activity, it might lower or even make the fnal fux of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ negative. Accordingly, ponds with a negative W-FCO2 during the fooded phase (Fig. 3) were mostly shallow. In these shallow systems the role of aquatic primary producers is likely to manifest as infux of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ . In addition, evidence of occasional livestock presence in the surroundings and the existence of biological crusts were observed in these systems. Tese can also be important sources of protein-like $\mathrm { D O M } ^ { 5 6 }$ .

Te role of organic C becomes even more apparent when we explore links with bacterial community composition (BCC). Te amount and especially the composition of organic matter in the sediments explained diferences in BCC among sites (Fig. 5). Within these two axes of variability, $\mathrm { T } { \cdot } \mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ is related to the extractable amount of DOM from the sediments and total fuorescence, which mostly relates to the presence of humic compounds. It is challenging to link BCC to an integrative biogeochemical response such as $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ since respiration is a metabolic response for which community members are functionally redundant. However, the BCC of sites rich in sediment organic matter and humic substances (sites with high $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ fuxes) diverged from those sites with protein-like DOM compounds (sites with lower $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ fuxes). Tus, the ability to degrade particular organic compounds does seem to be linked to specifc communities57. Tis fact might explain why community composition is more important to explain DOM composition and ultimately $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ in the studied ponds than bacterial abundance, as found in Morrissey et al. 58.

# Conclusions and Perspectives

Small ponds are still not included in most large-scale inland waters inventories20. Moreover, if these small systems undergo a dry phase during the hydrological year, they are almost totally excluded from biogeochemical studies. Here, we show that these temporary pond systems and their dry habitats in particular (i.e. emerged sediments) contribute signifcantly to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions. Moreover, the dual role of inundated habitats acting both as sink and source of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ depending on the hydrological phase illustrates the partial picture that we can get if studying exclusively the inundated phase of aquatic systems, as traditionally done4 .

Changes in the hydroperiod of these ponds might either increase or decrease the extension of these dry habitats, resulting in changes in the magnitude of these fuxes. Hot moments of high emissions as shown for periodically dry lake sediments51 may signifcantly contribute to the C budget of these systems. Finally, organic matter composition and microbial community composition merit further study, as they provide mechanistic understanding on the gas fuxes controls. Understanding the interplay between physical and biological processes will contribute key information to predict the occurrence of gas efuxes not only in temporary ponds but in all types of temporary inland water ecosystems.

# Methods

Study sites and sampling design. We studied 10 temporary ponds located on the island of Menorca (Balearic Islands, Western Mediterranean) (Table 1). Te climate is Mediterranean with a dry and hot summer period and mean monthly temperatures ranging from $1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in January to $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in August. Te studied systems were selected from a detailed inventory of temporary ponds on the island aimed at covering the widest possible spectrum of physicochemical, geomorphologic and hydrological properties46. Ephemeral ponds located in rocky basins were discarded. Main land uses include pine forests, Mediterranean shrublands, and, in some catchments, dry and irrigated croplands. A previous study showed no signifcant efect of the land cover or catchment lithology on $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fuxes17. Tree samplings were performed during the hydrological year of 2014 (from September 2013 to August 2014) to characterise contrasting hydrological situations. A frst sampling was performed on November 2013 to characterise the fooding phase of the ponds, with a cumulative rainfall of $1 3 0 \mathrm { m m }$ since the end of the previous dry phase (September 2013; Suppl. Figure 1). A second sampling on March 2014 represented the wet phase (cumulative rainfall of $4 5 4 \mathrm { m m }$ ). A fnal sampling on August 2014 characterised the dry phase (all ponds were entirely dry) and took place with a cumulative rainfall of $5 4 8 \mathrm { m m }$ and afer 63 days without any rainfall.

Each pond was divided in three diferent habitats including inundated and non-inundated areas. Te inundated habitats were defned as those areas with a water layer during sampling. Te emerged-vegetated habitats were defned as those non-inundated areas with some vascular plants present. Te emerged-bare habitats were defned as those non-inundated areas with bare sediments or just biological crusts on them. All three habitats were sampled if present. On each sampling campaign the surface of each habitat was measured in-situ and then digitized using aerial photography and the cartographic information in Fraga et al. 46.

Gas fux measurements. To guarantee the comparability of the fux measurements, the same method was used in all habitats of the ponds. Direct measures of the fux of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ $\mathrm { ( F C O } _ { 2 } )$ and of $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ $\mathrm { ( F C H _ { 4 } ) }$ were performed in situ with a chamber method59 and restricted as much as possible to the central time of the day. Tree randomly distributed replicate $\mathrm { F C O } _ { 2 }$ measurements were performed in each habitat using opaque enclosed chambers connected to an infrared gas analyser (IRGA EGM-4, PP-Systems, Amesbury, USA). Te $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration inside the chamber was monitored every $4 . 8 s \mathrm { , }$ , with an accuracy of $1 \%$ . Te foating chamber for air-water fux measurements had a surface area of $0 . 1 9 4 \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ and a volume of $2 7 . 1 \mathrm { d m } ^ { 3 }$ . Te chamber for fux measurements in emerged sediments had a surface area of $0 . 0 0 7 8  { \mathrm { m } } ^ { 2 }$ and a volume of $1 . 1 7 1 \mathrm { d m } ^ { 3 }$ (SRC-1, PP-Systems, Amesbury, USA). Flux measurements in emerged sediments refer to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ emissions from the substrate and thus integrate both sediment and root respiration but no C fxation by vegetation. Te fux measurements lasted until at least $1 0 \mu \mathrm { a t m }$ of change in $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ were reached, with a maximum duration of $6 0 0 s$ in water (minimum of $3 0 0 s$ ) and 300 s in emerged sediments (minimum of 120 s). Fluxes were determined by linear regression between the $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration in the chamber and time $\left( \mathbb { R } ^ { 2 } > 0 . 9 \right)$ , correcting for temperature and atmospheric pressure60.

Data analysis. Diferences in fuxes between hydrological phases were tested with one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). Diferences in fux between the habitats of the ponds (i.e. inundated, emerged vegetated sediments and emerged bare sediments) in each phase were tested with ANOVA followed by Tukey post-hoc comparison. All variables were log transformed prior to analysis. Tese statistical analyses were performed in STATISTICA v9 sofware and were only applied on $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fuxes because of the low number of detectable $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ fuxes.

To identify the main drivers of total $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fux as well as those in water and in emerged sediments, we performed partial least squares projections to latent structures (PLS). VIP-values larger than 1 indicate the most infuential predictors, between 1 and 0.8 moderately important, and $< 0 . 8$ less infuential X-variables. Skewed variables were log-transformed prior to analysis. PLS analysis of data matrices included the physico-chemical descriptors of the whole ecosystem or each specifc habitat. Te annual mean value of all variables was considered for this analysis. Te PLS analysis was performed in SIMCA-P (Umetrics AB, Umeå, Sweden).
