Organic sulfur and humics play a key role in the biogeochemistry of metals in natural waters but their distribution is not well understood. In this study, we measured the concentrations of thiol-like, humic substance (HS) -like and fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM) during the 2015 Canadian Arctic GEOTRACES cruises in the Canada Basin and Canadian Arctic Archipelago (CAA). Concentrations of thiol-like $( 7 - 8 5 \mathrm { n M }$ , expressed as equivalent to glutathione concentration) and HS-like (19 to $1 9 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { C / L } ,$ ) determined by cathodic stripping voltammetry (CSV) were congruent with previous marine CSV-based studies. Three humic-like (C1-C3) and one protein-like (C4) components identified and validated using excitation-emission matrix (EEM) and parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) have been reported in previous ocean studies. The distributions of HS-like and humic-like components (C1 and C2) in the CAA were similar, whereas thiol-like and protein-like component C4 were closely related in the study area. Maximum CSV-based thiol-like levels were associated with chlorophyll-a maximum, confirming the biogenic origin of thiols. The distribution and concentrations of CSV-based thiol- and HS-like, and FDOM varied between four distinct water masses (i.e. surface waters, Arctic outflow waters, Arctic intermediate waters and North Atlantic deep waters) with the lowest levels associated with the surface waters, likely due to photobleaching and sea ice melt dilution. The concentrations in CSV-based HS-like and FDOM in the Arctic outflow waters decreased from western to eastern CAA, reflecting the influence of DOMrich Pacific derived waters.

# 1. Introduction

Trace metal speciation in marine waters is dominated by organic complexation, which impact their cycling and enhances their bioavailability (Laglera and van den Berg, 2009; Powell and Donat, 2001; Thuróczy et al., 2011). Although little is known about the distribution of these ligands, thiols and humics have been identified as potential metal ligands in estuarine and marine waters (Dupont et al., 2004; Hioki et al., 2014; Hirose, 2007; Laglera and van den Berg, 2003; Laglera and van den Berg, 2006; Laglera and van den Berg, 2009; Nakayama et al., 2011; Yang and van den Berg, 2009). Thiols are organic reduced sulfur species that possess -SH functional groups. The most common thiol groups in marine environment are thiourea, cysteine and glutathione (Ahner et al., 2002; Al-Farawati and Van Den Berg, 2001; Dupont et al., 2006; Tang et al., 2000). Thiols can be biologically produced (Dupont et al., 2004; Mangal and Guéguen, 2015) as a response to the increase of free metal ion concentrations in aquatic systems (Ahner et al., 2002; Dupont and Ahner, 2005; Leal et al., 1999).

High performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) and cathodic stripping voltammetry (CSV) have been applied in detecting thiol-like concentrations in ocean studies (Laglera and van den Berg, 2003; Pernet-Coudrier et al., 2013). Although HPLC analysis featured high resolution in thiol determination, time-consuming thiol derivatization is required (Swarr et al., 2016; Tang et al., 2003). On the other hand, the CSV-based thiol-like determination (Kawakami et al., 2006) is rapid $( \sim 1 \mathrm { h }$ per sample) and does not require extensive sample preparation (Marie et al., 2015; Pernet-Coudrier et al., 2013) but can be limited by coalescent peaks (Chen et al., 2017). The limit of detection (LOD) of thiol-like determination (1 nM; Pernet-Coudrier et al., 2013) is compatible with the natural range of seawater thiol compounds (10 to $4 1 0 \mathrm { n M }$ ; Gall and Berg, 1993; Marie et al., 2015; Pernet-Coudrier et al., 2013).

A significant fraction of DOM $1 0 { - } 5 0 \%$ of total organic carbon; Harvey et al., 1983) is relatively resistant to microbial degradation and is referred as humic susbtances (HS; Laglera and van den Berg, 2009: Yang and van den Berg, 2009). These compounds are composed of a mixture of macromolecules and originate from biological activities, sediment interactions and decomposition products of terrestrial material (Hedges, 1992). A portion of the HS pool produced in situ by bacterial activities was proposed to be the weak iron-ligand class (i.e. $\mathrm { L } _ { 2 } ,$ ) measured in electrochemical analysis (Misumi et al., 2013; and references therein). In addition, HS contributes to the humic-like fluorescent DOM (FDOM; Coble et al., 2014 and references therein) measured using excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence (Coble, 1996) associated with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). FDOM distribution has been extensively examined in the Arctic Ocean (e.g. Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; Guéguen et al., 2005, 2014, 2015; Stedmon et al., 2011; Walker et al., 2009). For example, the western Canada Basin and Canadian Arctic Archipelago (CAA) waters were characterized by relatively higher terrestrially derived humic-like FDOM compared to the eastern CAA (Guéguen et al., 2014; Walker et al., 2009). Although CSV and EEM-PARAFAC claimed to measure humics, no systematic comparison has been made yet in marine waters.

The aims of this 2015 Canada GEOTRACES study are: (1) to assess the spatial variability of dissolved organic ligands including thiol- and HS-like, CDOM and FDOM using electrochemical and optical analyses, and (2) to relate DOM composition with water mass mixing in the study area. A comparison between CSV- and EEM-based humics will provide novel insights into HS distribution in the Arctic Ocean.

# 2. Methods

# 2.1. Sampling

Eleven sites in the Canada Basin (Stations A-B; Fig. 1) and in Canadian Arctic Archipelago (Stations C-K; Fig. 1) were sampled in July–August 2015 (leg 2; Stations E-K) and September 2015 (leg 3b; Stations A-C) as part of the 2015 Canadian Arctic GEOTRACES cruise program. Water samples were collected using Niskin bottles mounted on a trace metal rosette together with a conductivity – temperature – depth profiler. Samples were directly filtered using pre-combusted $( 4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 \mathrm { h }$ ) glass fiber filters (GF/F, Whatman) from the Niskin bottles and stored in the dark at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in pre-combusted amber glass vials for ${ \sim } 2$ months. Duplicate samples for voltammetry analysis were acidified to $\mathrm { p H 1 . 9 5 }$ with $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ HCl immediately after filtration and analyzed within the recommended preservation time (i.e. 2 months; Pernet-Coudrier et al., 2013).

# 2.2. Reagents

Milli-Q water (Millipore, Fisher scientific) was used for rinsing glass cells and fluorescence cuvette, and preparation of stock and working solutions. HCl (CALEDON) and NaOH (J.T. Baker) were used to acidify and adjust pH during voltammetric analysis.

The choice of standards is an important factor affecting the CSVbased thiol-like concentration (Filella, 2014). Several thiol groups have been previously used as standards, resulting in huge difference in the reported concentrations. For example, $5 0 \mathrm { n M }$ of glutathione-equivalent thiol compounds was found in ocean studies (Gall and Berg, 1993), while a range of $0 . 7 { - } 3 . 6 \mathbf { n } \mathbf { M }$ of thiourea-equivalents thiol compounds was found in English Channel and North Sea studies (al-Farawati and Van Den Berg, 2001). In this study, glutathione (GSH) was used as a standard for thiol groups (Pernet-Coudrier et al., 2013). GSH stock solution (oxidized, Sigma-Aldrich) was prepared in Milli-Q water and stored in dark at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and diluted daily to $2 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ; the pH was adjusted to 1.95 by adding $2 \%$ HCl. Suwannee river fulvic acid (SRFA) (1S101F; International Humic Substances Society) was selected as a standard to quantify HS-like in voltammetry analysis (Pernet-Coudrier et al., 2013). Stockdale et al. (2014, 2016) showed that terrestrially derived fluvic acid can reasonably predict metal speciation in seawater. The CSVbased HS-like concentrations were expressed as mass unit equivalent of SRFA per volume unit. The use of a standard like SRFA has the advantage of allowing comparison with dissolved organic carbon concentrations. SRFA stock solution $( 2 5 1 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { L } )$ was prepared and diluted daily to $1 5 0 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { L }$ $\mathrm { ( p H = 1 } . 9 5$ ) prior to analysis.

# 2.3. Voltammetry analysis

Voltammetry analysis was carried out using a Metrohm uAutolab Type III potentiostat/galvanostat coupled with a three-electrode basis 663 VA stand (Metrohm) controlled by Autolab GPES software version 4.9. A static mercury drop electrode (SMDE) was applied in the electrochemical cell as working electrode with the mercury drop at size one $( r _ { O } = 1 . 4 1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 } { \mathrm { m } } )$ . A $\mathbf { A g } / \mathbf { A g C l }$ $( 3 \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { L }$ , Fluka) electrode and a glass carbon rod (Metrohm) were used as reference and auxiliary electrodes, respectively. A PTFE stirrer was used with a rotation rate of $1 5 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for better purging and deposition. A daily calibrated dual channel pH meter (Accumet, ThermoScientific) was used during the measurement. The DP-CSV analytical settings were based on PernetCoudrier et al. (2013). Briefly, $2 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of seawater samples was loaded into a voltammetric glass cell and $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $1 0 0 \mathrm { p p m }$ Mo solution (EMD) was added to amplify HS signal. A $6 0 0 { \cdot } s \mathrm { N } _ { 2 }$ purging period was applied, followed by a 120-s deposition time with deposition potential at $0 . 0 0 \mathrm { V }$ with stirring. After deposition, a 5-s rest time was applied and stripping scan using differential pulse mode started from $0 . 0 \mathrm { V }$ to $- 0 . 6 \mathrm { V }$ , where the step potential was $2 \mathrm { m V }$ and amplitude was $6 0 \mathrm { m V }$ . The range of current was determined automatically by the GPES software.

Repeatability, reproducibility, limit of detection (LOD) and recovery (Table 1) experiments were conducted on freshly made artificial seawater (Stein, 1979) without EDTA and trace metal solution. The artificial seawater was adjusted in conductivity and $\mathsf { p H }$ to match that of natural seawater samples. Repeatability calculated as 10 consecutive measurements of artificial seawater (2.50 and 32.11 nA) was $0 . 6 4 \%$ $0 . 6 0 \%$ for thiol- and HS-like, respectively. The CSV reproducibility obtained after 10 consecutive measurements of one sample was $4 0 . 7 5 \mathrm { n M }$ and $6 7 2 . 0 5 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { C / L }$ for thiol- and HS-like, respectively. LODs calculated as 3 times the standard deviation of 10 consecutive measurements, were $1 . 1 2 \mathrm { n M }$ and $2 1 . 1 9 \mu \mathrm { g } \ \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { L }$ for thiol- and HS-like, respectively (Table 1). Repeatability, reproducibility and LODs are comparable to previous study (Marie et al., 2015; Pernet-Coudrier et al., 2013). Recovery was assessed by measuring artificial seawater with known concentration of thiol- and HS-like ${ \bf 1 0 . 3 n M }$ and $1 6 8 \mu \mathrm { g } \ \mathrm { C / L }$ respectively). The recovery was ${ \sim } 1 0 0 \%$ for both thiol- and HS-like. Though the presence of HS-like was reported to overlap the thiol-like signal in CSV (Gall and Berg, 1993), seawater contains low DOM concentration (i.e. $< 1 \mathrm { p p m }$ ; Carlson and Hansell, 2014) which is compatible with CSV determination of thiol-like compounds (Chen et al., 2017).

# 2.4. Colored dissolved organic matter (CDOM)

The absorbance of CDOM samples was measured from 250 to $7 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ using a Shimadzu ultraviolet visible UV 2550 dual beam spectrophotometer and a $_ { 1 0 - \mathrm { c m } }$ rectangular quartz cell (Dainard et al., 2015; Guéguen et al., 2011). Absorption coefficient at $3 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ (a355) was used as a proxy for terrestrially-derived CDOM (Blough et al., 1993; Walker et al., 2013). Absorbance data was fit with a non-linear regression to determine the spectral slope over wavelengths spanning 275–295 nm $( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ as a proxy for CDOM relative molecular weight and/or aromaticity (Helms et al., 2008, 2013). Concentrations of total dissolved lignin phenol (TDLP9) were estimated based on a range of absorption coefficients (Fichot et al., 2016). Because $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 0 }$ values were low $( < 4 \mathbf { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ at all sites and depths, only the “low-CDOM” model; Fichot et al., 2016 was used here.

Similarly, mean DOC concentrations were estimated based on absorption coefficients following Shen et al. (2016).

When $\mathsf { a } _ { 2 7 5 } < 1 . 4 2 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , DOC $( \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ) = \exp .$ $[ 3 . 8 8 1 + 0 . 7 6 3 * \ln ( \mathrm { a _ { 2 7 5 } } ) - 0 . 3 7 5 * \ln ( \mathrm { a _ { 2 9 5 } } ) ]$

When $\mathsf { a } _ { 2 7 5 } > 1 . 4 2 \mathsf { m } ^ { - 1 } ;$ , DOC $\begin{array} { r } { ( \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ) = \exp . } \end{array}$ $[ 3 . 6 4 3 + 1 . 2 2 3 * \ln ( \mathrm { a _ { 2 7 5 } } ) - 0 . 7 0 1 * \ln ( \mathrm { a _ { 2 9 5 } } ) ]$

CDOM fluorescence intensity was measured across excitation wavelengths (Ex) ranging from 250 to $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and emission wavelengths (Em) ranging from 300 to $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ through successive scans with a Fluoromax-4 Jobin Yvon spectrofluorometer. Excitation and emission slit widths were set to $5 \mathrm { n m }$ . Blank Milli-Q EEMs were measured daily to correct background and scatter peaks. Between each sample, the quartz cuvettes were thoroughly rinsed with Milli-Q and scanned at $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ $2 7 0 / 2 8 0 { - } 5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The absence of significant fluorescent intensity at $3 0 0 { - } 5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ confirmed the cleanliness of the cuvette. All EEMs were normalized to Raman units (r.u.) by dividing the signal at all $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ pairs by the integrated area under the Raman water peak at $\operatorname { E x } 3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ using a daily acquired Milli-Q blank EEM (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009). The inner filter effect corrections were not required as absorbance in all samples was below $1 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009).

Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was carried out in Matlab using the DOMFluor (Stedmon and Bro, 2008) for the decomposition of EEMs dataset. The model was constrained to nonnegative values and run for three to ten components. The appropriate number of components was determined by split-half and Tucker congruence coefficient analyses.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Water mass definition

Arctic Ocean serves as a conduit between the Pacific Ocean and the Atlantic Ocean. Pacific-derived water exits the Canada Basin through CAA, Barrow Strait and other passages like Jones Sound into North Atlantic Ocean (Woodgate, 2013). The current description of water masses is based on previous works in Canada Basin (Carmack et al., 2015; McLaughlin et al., 2011; McLaughlin et al., 2004; Rudels et al., 2012; Woodgate, 2013) and CAA (Melling et al., 2008; Michel et al., 2006; Woodgate, 2013). Three main water masses were found (Fig. 2A):

• Surface waters (SW; top $3 0 \mathrm { m }$ ). Surface waters (ASW) in Canada Basin and western CAA were highly affected by runoff and ice melt (Cory et al., 2007; Logvinova et al., 2016; Walker et al., 2009) and characterized by a low salinity and cold temperature $\mathrm { ( T p } \ < \ 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and Salinity $< 3 2$ ; Fig. 2B-C), while Lancaster Sound surface waters SW were affected by warmer and saline Atlantic waters $\mathrm { ( T p } > 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and Salinity $> 3 2$ ). Chlorophyll showed relative low level in ASW while it is relatively higher in Lancaster Sound (Fig. 2D).

• Arctic outflow waters (OW; T $< 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and Salinity $< 3 3 . 7 )$ (AzetsuScott et al., 2010; Tang et al., 2004). OW occupied the top ${ 3 0 0 } \mathrm { m }$ (excluding SW) and included surface chlorophyll maximum (SCM) along the section (Fig. 2D). OW also encompassed Pacific summer waters $( \mathrm { T } \sim - 0 . 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ;$ Fig. 2C) and Pacific winter waters (Salinity $\sim 3 3 . 1 $ ) in Canada Basin. OW is affected by Canadian rivers input and ice melting in CAA (Michel et al., 2006; Myers, 2005).

• Deep waters (DW; Salinity $> 3 4 )$ ). Warm and saline Atlantic origin waters dominated the deep layers $( > 3 0 0 \mathrm { m } )$ with low biological biomass (i.e. low chlorophyll, Fig. 2D). Arctic intermediate waters (AIW; $> 4 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ , Salinity $> 3 4 . 5$ ) were observed in Canada Basin and western CAA, whereas the West Greenland Current (WGC) Atlantic waters $( \mathrm { T } \sim 0 . 9 \mathrm { - } 1 . 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and Salinity \~ 34.1–34.45; Rudels, 1986) was found in Lancaster Sound (Melling et al., 2001; Tang et al., 2004). The colder WGC Atlantic waters were found at the northern most station across Lancaster Sound (Station I; Fig. 1).

# 1.1 CSV-based DOM characterization

Thiol-like concentration of all samples ranged from 6.88 to $8 4 . 9 1 \mathrm { n m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ with an average of $2 3 . 2 8 \pm 1 2 . 5 8 \mathrm { n m o l L } ^ { - 1 }$ (GSH equivalent; Table 2), congruent with CSV-based studies (Gall and Berg, 1993; Marie et al., 2015; Pernet-Coudrier et al., 2013). HS-like of all samples ranged from 19 to $1 9 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \ \mathrm { C / L }$ (Table 2) with an average of $9 4 ~ \pm ~ 3 9 \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { C / L }$ , which is comparable with earlier studies (PernetCoudrier et al., 2013; Whitby and Berg, 2014). Assuming that the dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentration ranged from 52 to $8 3 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (Table 2), the abundance of HS-like represented between $1 . 9 \%$ and $3 0 \%$ of DOC. Similar estimates were found previously (Chanudet et al., 2006; Weishaar et al., 2003).

Thiol-like concentration (Fig. 3A) increased from surface $( 1 6 . 8 7 ~ \pm ~ 5 . 2 9 \mathrm { n m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ to the subsurface chlorophyll-a maximum depth $( 2 7 . 1 8 ~ \pm ~ 5 . 5 9 \mathrm { n m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ . Maximum thiol-like concentrations were coincident with chlorophyll-a maximum, but not universal along the transect (Fig. 3A). The biological origin of thiol compounds was also reported in the English Channel (al-Farawati and Van Den Berg, 2001) and North Atlantic Ocean (Swarr et al., 2016). High HS-like concentrations (Fig. 3B) were associated with the top ${ 3 0 0 } \mathrm { m }$ through the study area and the highest HS-like levels were found in Viscount Melville Sound $\left( \sim 1 5 0 \mathrm { m } \right)$ and in Barrow Strait bottom waters. In deeper layers, the lowest thiol- and HS-like levels were found at $5 0 0 { - } 1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ in AIW. No significant differences in thiol- and HS-like concentrations were found between AIW and WCG $( \mathbf { p } > 0 . 0 5 )$ ).

# 3.2. CDOM/FDOM

The $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ ranged from 0.12 to $0 . 8 6 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ with the highest values found in the cold halocline (OW layer) in the Canada Basin-Barrow Strait region. In the Lancaster Sound region, the $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ values decreased with depth in the top $2 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ (Fig. 3C). Similar $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ ranges were found in CAA (Guéguen et al., 2014) and on the East Greenland shelves (Stedmon et al., 2015). The $\mathsf { S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } }$ ranged from 0.014 to $0 . 0 2 8 \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Table 2) consistent with previous studies in the Arctic ocean (Fichot et al., 2013; Shen et al., 2016). In the Canada Basin-Barrow Strait region, the $\mathsf { S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } }$ values were elevated in SW and decreased rapidly to a minimum in OW before increasing again in DW (Fig. 3D). In the Barrow Strait-Lancaster Sound region, the $\mathsf { S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } }$ decreased rapidly in SW and were relatively uniform below ${ 3 0 } \mathrm { m }$ .

The estimated TDLP9 concentrations were $1 . 4 { - } 6 . 0 \mathrm { n m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ , with an average of $2 . 9 2 \ \pm \ 0 . 9 2$ ${ \bf ( n = 1 0 4 }$ ; Table 2). These values were similar to those measured in Canada Basin and Beaufort shelf (Fichot et al., 2016; Shen et al., 2016). The estimated mean DOC concentrations (Fig. 3F) were elevated in surface layers (up to ${ 8 3 \mu \mathrm { m o l } L } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Table 2) in agreement with previous studies in the CAA $( 8 0 ~ \pm ~ 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Walker et al., 2009). Both TDLP9 and DOC decreased rapidly with depth (Fig. 3 E-F; Anderson and Amon, 2015; Druffel et al., 2017; Shen et al., 2016) with the lowest concentrations found in DW $\mathrm { ( 1 . 3 9 { - } 1 . 7 5 \ n m o l L ^ { - 1 } }$ and $5 2 { \mathrm { - } } 5 8 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ respectively). The higher TDLP9 and DOC estimates were found in the top $^ { 1 0 0 } \mathrm { m }$ in the western part of the study area. The source of these lignin-rich waters is unknown. Estimated TDLP9 and DOC were strongly correlated $( r = 0 . 9 2$ , $\mathtt { n } = 1 0 4 _ { \cdot }$ ) highlighting the strong influence of terrigenous inputs in the study area.

Four components were identified and validated in PARAFAC model (Fig. 4). All cross- validated PARAFAC components were found in the online repository of published PARAFAC components OpenFluor (Tucker congruence $> 0 . 9 5$ ; Murphy et al., 2014). Components C1 to C3 were reported previously and considered as humic-like as their maximum emission wavelengths were above $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . C1 showed an excitation wavelength at $< 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , an emission wavelength at $4 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , which is close to traditionally considered UV/visible terrestrial humic-like components (Coble, 1996). The longest maximum emission wavelength of C1 suggested its elevated degree of aromaticity (Stedmon et al., 2003). Despite of its terrestrial origin, similar components had been found in open ocean studies (Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; Guéguen et al., 2014; Walker et al., 2009). C2 and C3 showed a primary excitation wavelength maximum at $< 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , a secondary excitation maximum at $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and a maximum emission at ${ \sim } 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . These two components were identified as a combination of UV humiclike and marine humic-like components (Coble, 1996), which were observed in both terrestrial and marine systems (Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; McIntyre and Guéguen, 2013; Stedmon et al., 2007; Walker et al., 2009). C4 featured a peak at $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m } 2 7 5 / 3 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , which is similar to tryptophan-like fluorescence component (Coble, 1996) associated with biological production (Coble et al., 1998). This component had been reported widely in aquatic studies (Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; McIntyre and Guéguen, 2013; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Stedmon et al., 2007). The intensity ranges of four fluorescence components (C1: 0.014 to 0.039 r.u.; C2: 0.007 to 0.031 r.u.; C3: 0.006 to 0.033 r.u.; C4: 0.001 to 0.079 r.u.; Table 2) agreed with reported studies in the Arctic Ocean (Dainard and Guéguen, 2013; Guéguen et al., 2014; Stedmon et al., 2007; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Walker et al., 2009).

A significant lateral reduction in a355 and C1-C2 intensities in OW in Lancaster Sound was caused by mixing with humic-poor Atlantic-origin waters (Guéguen et al., 2014). The third humic-like component C3 displayed different distribution (Fig. 3I), suggesting distinct processes and/or sources compared to C1 and C2. For protein like C4 (Fig. 3J), higher intensities in SW and OW were found in western CAA than in eastern CAA (0.023 vs 0.017 r.u., $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ . Since similar protein-like component was previously associated with biological production (Coble et al., 1998), the eastward decrease in protein-like suggested a decrease in primary production which is supported by a decrease in nutrients from western to eastern CAA waters (Michel et al., 2006). In deeper layers, a decreasing trend for C1-C4 was observed from ${ 3 0 0 } \mathrm { m }$ to $1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ in BAW. Only C1 and C3 were significantly different between AIW and WGC $( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ ), with higher C1 and C3 intensities in the latter.

# 3.3. Organic compounds and water masses

Principal components analysis (PCA) was carried out using samples from all depths (Fig. 5 A-B). Principal components 1 (PC1) and 2 (PC2) explained $3 6 \%$ and $1 6 \%$ of total variance (Fig. 5A). Humic-like C1-C2 and protein-like C4 were closely related (negative PC2 values), suggesting similar processes affecting both PARAFAC components. Components C2 and C4 were situated in the same quadrant confirming their association with microbial or bioactivity production (Coble et al., 1998; Shimotori et al., 2012). On the other hand, CSV-based HS-like, TDLP9 and humic-like components (C3) were clustered together (positive PC2 values), confirming the humic character of C3. The PCA revealed differences in DOM characteristics between three main water masses (i.e. SW, OW and DW) (Fig. 5B). The OW samples were grouped together with positive PC1 values and were characterized by high CSV-based HSlike, TDLP9, $\mathbf { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ and humic-like levels. These enrichments were likely due to the inclusion of the humic-rich Pacific halocline waters (Guéguen et al., 2007) in OW. Similar results were found in earlier study (Guéguen et al., 2014). The SW and DW samples showed negative PC1 values, a result of lower levels in terrestrially derived DOM relative to OW. SW showed positive PC2 values while the DW samples had more negative PC2 values. The lower humic level in SW (higher PC2) was mainly due to enhanced photodegradation processes and/or seaice melt dilution (Dainard et al., 2015; Helms et al., 2013; Timko et al., 2015; Yamashita et al., 2017).

When considering the top $6 0 \mathrm { m }$ (Salinity $< ~ 3 2$ ; Fig. 5C-D), the waters above the SCM depths and at or below the SCM were separated along the PC1 axis representing terrestrial character. Humic-like fluorescent components (C1, C3), $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ and TDLP9 displayed negative PC2 loadings whereas protein-like C4, thiol-like and chlorophyll showed positive PC2 loadings. Protein-like C4 was clustered with thiollike and chlorophyll in the top $6 0 \mathrm { m }$ supporting biological influence. The surface samples (above SCM; black dots, Fig. 5D) were situated in the same quadrant $( \mathrm { P C 1 } < 0 )$ , revealing relatively homogeneous DOM composition above the SCM. The deeper samples (i.e. at and below SCM) distributed around both PC axes were characterized by a more variable DOM composition, likely reflecting the heterogeneity in plankton community, primary production, mixing and SCM depths (Tremblay et al., 2012; Varela et al., 2013). # 4. Conclusion

For the first time, thiol- and HS-like were measured in the Arctic Ocean by CSV with suitable sensitivity and reproducibility, enabling the routine monitoring of these important metal-binding ligands in marine waters. The distribution of thiol- and HS-like, and CDOM/FDOM showed significant spatial and vertical changes from the eastern Canada

Basin to Lancaster Sound. The ranges of thiol- (8 to $7 8 \mathrm { n M }$ ) and HS-like (31 to $2 2 2 \mu \ g \mathrm { ~ C / L ~ }$ ) determined by CSV were consistent with previous studies, suggesting that CSV-based DOM characterization is suitable for marine studies. Maximum thiol-like concentration was associated generally with the subsurface chlorophyll-a maximum. Similar distributions were found between humic-like fluorescent components and HSlike, and between protein-like C4 and thiol-like, providing some insights into the chemical composition of the fluorescing compounds.
