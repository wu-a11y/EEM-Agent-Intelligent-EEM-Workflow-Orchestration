# Abstract

Riverine dissolved iron (Fe) affects water color, nutrients,and marine carbon cycling.Fe size and coupling with dissolved organic mater (DOM),in part, modulates the biogeochemical roles of riverine Fe. We used size fractionation to operationally define dissolved Fe $( < 0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ into soluble $( < 0 . 0 2 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ and colloidal $( 0 . 0 2 \mathrm { - } 0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ fractions in order to characterize the downstream drivers,concentrations,and fluxes of Fe across season and hydrologic regime at the freshwater Connecticut River mainstem,which we sampled bi-weekly for 2 yrs.Drivers of coloidal and soluble Fe concentrations were markedly diffrent.The response of colloidal Fe concentration to changes in discharge was modulated by water temperature; collidal Fe decreased with ncreasing discharge at temperatures $< 1 0 . 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ but increased with increasing discharge at temperatures $> 1 0 . 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ Conversely，soluble Fe concentrations were only positively correlated to discharge at high temperatures $( > 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ： Soluble Fe was strongly positively corelated to a humic-ike DOM fluorescence component, suggesting coupling with DOM subsets, potentially through complexation. While average colloidal Fe fluxes varied twofold seasonally,solubleFe fluxes varied ninefold;therefore,solubleFe variabilitywas more important to theoveraldissolved Fe variability than coloidal Fe,despite lower concentrations.Seasonal Fe fluxes were decoupled from discharge: dissolved and soluble Fe fluxes were greatest in the fall whereas discharge was greatest in the spring. Fluxes of soluble Fe, which may be more bioavailable and more likely to be exported to the ocean, were lowest in the summer when downstream biological demand is high, having implications for primary productivity and iron uptake.

Iron (Fe) is coupled to ocean carbon cycling, impacts the fate of heavy metals,nutrients,and pollutants,and is a driving component of brownification in freshwater systems. Fe limitation of primary production in coastal and ocean systems (Tagliabue et al. 2O17) is common,in part because of the loss of riverine dissolved Fe in estuaries by salinity-induced flocculation (Boyle et al. 1977； Sholkovitz et al. 1978). Recently，rivers have emerged as important,yet previously under-emphasized，sources of dissolved Fe to coastal zones and oceans (Rijkenberg et al. 2014; Krachler et al. 2019; Vieira et al. 202O),as complexation of Fe with dissolved organic matter(DOM) often controls the solubility of Fe in river plumes (Batchelli and Lee 2010;Herzog et al. 2017,2020b). In fact, the riverine export of Fe-DOM complexes to coastal zones and oceans (Buck et al. 20O7; Muller 2018) has been shown to play a vital role in ocean productivity and $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ uptake (Laglera et al.2019;Krachler and Krachler 2O21)．Additionally,Fe is coupled with phosphorous in some catchments (Rosenberg and Schroth 2O17),and Fe cycling at sediment-water boundaries controls the adsorption or release of dissolved phosphate (Krom and Berner 198O; Zhang et al. 202O), thus dictating the fate of a commonly limiting macronutrient. Finally, increasing riverine dissolved Fe concentrations contribute to the brownification (i.e., increasing water color) of inland waters globally (Kritzberg and Ekstrom 2O12),since Fe absorbs light in the visible wavelengths.The process of brownification in freshwaters limits ultraviolet and visible light availability in aquatic systems and affects drinking water production (Kritzberg et al. 2020). Effects on downstream productivity and the impact of dissolved Fe on water color depends,in part, on Fe size and concentration,as well as the timing of its downstream flux.

The behavior of Fe is dependent on its phase.Dissolved Fe is operationally defined as Fe passing through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } 0 . 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter.In circumneutral oxygenated waters,operationally dissolved Fe phase can be defined as inorganic Fe,mainly Fe(III)- (oxy)hydroxidesand Fe(III) mineral nanoparticles(Hirst et al.2O17)，or organic Fe，present as mononuclear organic complexes (Herzog et al. 2O17).Complexation of Fe with DOM can increase the solubility of Fe(III) in circumneutral waters,allowing for greater dissolved Fe concentrations than would be expected based on inorganic Fe solubility constants (Liu and Millero 2002;Powell and Wilson-Finelli 2003).In general,Fe(II)-(oxy)hydroxides flocculate in estuaries (Herzog et al.2O17),becoming an important source of Fe in coastal sediment (Jilbert et al. 2O18),whereas mononuclear Fe-DOM complexes are more likely to escape the estuarine mixing zone (Powell and Wilson-Finelli 2003;Herzog et al. 2017,2020b), being a more important source of dissolved Fe to marine aquatic environments. Lastly，Fe phase may also affect the amount and wavelengths of light absorption per unit Fe concentration (Sherman and Waite 1985;Logozzo et al. 2022), thereby differentially impacting water color.

Fe phase is related to Fe size.Dissolved Fe exists along a continuum of sizes within the operationally defined dissolved fraction (Tagliabue et al. 2O17). Size fractionation techniques such as flow field-flow fractionation or ultrafiltration (Shiller 2003; Stolpe et al. 2Oo5） can separate operationally dissolved Fe into continuous or discrete size fractions from which the phase or speciation may be inferred depending on its behavior or coupling with DOM and other elements (Stolpe et al. 2012). The operationally dissolved fraction (here defined as Fe that passes through a filter with a nominal pore size of $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ is often separated by secondary filtration through a $0 . 0 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter into a truly dissolved,or“soluble,” fraction $( < 0 . 0 2 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ and a colloidal fraction $( 0 . 0 2 \mathrm { - } 0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ (Shiller 2003). Previous work has shown that in circumneutral oxic waters,operationally colloidal Fe is predominantly composed of Fe (oxy)hydroxides (Rosenberg and Schroth 2O17)，whereas the operationally soluble Fe fraction is predominantly composed of mononuclear Fe-DOM complexes andsmall inorganic Fe mineral nanoparticles(Baalousha et al. 2006; Shiller et al. 2O06; Joung and Shiller 2016), though there is overlap in phase across size fraction (Aiken et al. 2011; Herzog et al. 2O2Oa). Examining the controls on these Fe fractions is essential to understanding the biogeochemical role of Fe in rivers.

The drivers of dissolved Fe in rivers vary across system and through time. Some studies have shown strong correlations between riverine dissolved Fe and DOM (Neal et al. 2008), which suggests either that dissolved Fe concentrations are controlled by direct chemical complexation with DOM (Skerlep et al. 2021) or that both Fe and DOM are mobilized by similar processes in catchments (Bjorneras et al. 2017). Others have shown weak or lagged relationships between riverine dissolved Fe and DOM amount,and propose the soil microbial reduction of Fe as the main control (Ekstrom et al. 2O16). Reconciling these differences may require explicit consideration of Fe size fraction (Dahlqvist et al. 2OO7; Stolpe et al. 2O12),as the apparent drivers of the bulk operationally dissolved Fe pool will reflect the drivers of the dominant Fe size fraction at a particular site or time.Further, consideration of DOM source and composition is important, as these characteristics influence the ability of DOM to complex with Fe and other metals (Krachler et al. 20O5；Baken et al. 2011；Fujii et al.2O14).Finally,dissolved Fe in rivers demonstrates seasonality (Dahlqvist et al. 2OO7;Rosenberg and Schroth 2017), potentially because DOM amount (Shultz et al. 2018), source (Hosen et al. 202O),and composition (Hosen et al. 2020) vary seasonally，and/or because hydrology and temperature influence the seasonal development of reducing conditions in catchments (Ekstrom et al.2O16). In order to capture these seasonal constraints on dissolved Fe,a high temporal resolution approach that couples seasonal measurements of Fe size and amount in tandem with DOM amount and composition is necessary.

DOM is a heterogeneous mixture of organic compounds, making its characterization complex.In aquatic systems, DOM is broadly classified into two source groups:allochthonous DOM is derived from outside the aquatic systemsuch as from terrestrial plants and soils—and autochthonous DOM is derived from within the water column—from instream carbon fixation (e.g., from gross primary production, GPP).The composition of DOM is dependent on its source, with allochthonous DOM generally being of higher aromaticity and higher molecular weight than autochthonous DOM (Hood et al. 2OO6).Higher aromaticity has been linked to higher rates of Fe complexation (Baken et al. 2O11；Fujii et al.2014),and so allochthonous DOM likely has a greater ability to complex with Fe than autochthonous DOM (Powell and Wilson-Finelli 20O3;Krachler et al.2005).The subset of DOM that fluoresces in the ultraviolet and visible wavelengths is termed fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM).Fluorescence excitation and emission spectroscopy coupled with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) is commonly used to elucidate FDOM composition and source (Fellman et al.2010). Further, the characteristics of the FDOM pool have been shown to be generally well-representative of the bulk DOM pool (Rodriguez-Avella et al. 202O). Utilizing FDOM PARAFAC components allows for a characterization of composition and source at the temporal resolution necessary to understand seasonal drivers of riverine dissolved Fe.

Ultimately, the dissolved Fe available for export to coastal zones and oceans is driven by upstream physical,chemical, and biological processes that vary on short (hours) to long (seasons,years） timescales in rivers.As Fe concentrations in circumneutral oxic waters are controlled both by binding with DOM and the seasonal formation of reducing conditions in catchment soils,we evaluated the concentrations, fluxes,and drivers of two fractions of operationally dissolved Fe and DOM at a freshwater site in the Connecticut River mainstem across a range of hydrologic conditions and in different seasons.We couple this with estimates of DOM source and composition，river discharge,and water temperature.We predict that the operationally soluble Fe fraction will be closely coupled with allochthonous DOM，whereas the colloidal Fe fraction will be independent of DOM and driven by temperature and river discharge—a signal of microbial reduction. Together, these processes will control the timing of dissolved Fe export downstream: we predict that soluble Fe concentrations and fluxes will be greatest when the amount of terrestrially-derived DOMis highest (i.e., in the fall and during summer storms)，whereas colloidal Fe fluxes will be greatest when reducing conditions in the catchment are favorable (i.e., after the spring freshet).

# Site location

Sampling was conducted in the predominantly forested Connecticut River watershed (Fig.1),which is the largest watershed in New England, United States.The sampling site was located on the Connecticut River mainstem at Thompsonville, CT( $4 1 . 9 8 7 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { ~ N ~ } _ { \cdot }$ $7 2 . 6 0 5 8 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { W } ,$ ，which has a drainage area of $2 5 , 0 1 9 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ .Above the sampling point, the catchment is $7 9 . 5 \%$ forested, $4 . 5 \%$ wetland, $5 . 5 \%$ agriculture,and $5 . 5 \%$ developed (Logozzo et al. 2022); the city of Springfield, Massachusetts is the main urban center upstream of the sampling site. # Fe measurements

Total Fe concentrations in the operationally dissolved and soluble fractions were measured colorimetrically as total reducible Fe using the Hach FerroVer method on the Hach DR900 Or the Hach FerroVer Pocket Colorimeter II as in Logozzo et al. (2022),a median of 7 d after collection. Significant dissolved Fe loss through adsorption to HDPE bottle walls in un-acidified samples during short-term storage has been documented in seawater (Fitzsimmons and Boyle 2O12)，as sample acidification prevents Fe loss by adsorption (Jensen et al. 2O2O). # Discharge

As this study aimed to characterize hydrologic controls on Fe and DOM cycling, the sampling scheme was designed to capture most riverine flow conditions in our system.To confirm this,we calculated the 10-yr flow exceedance probability from 2010 to 202O using the R package hydroTSM vO.6-O in R v.4.1.0.Sample collection occurred across all parts of the hydrograph (Fig. S4), indicating that a wide range of flow conditions were captured in the analysis.

# Fe and DOC fluxes using LOADEST

Fe and DOC fluxes,or loads，were calculated using the USGS Load Estimator (LOADEST） program，which creates a series of seasonal-specific regression equations that relate measured constituent fluxes to discharge in order to estimate daily constituent fluxes based on daily discharge.The method is described in detail in Runkel et al. (2Oo4). Briefly,soluble Fe, colloidal Fe,allochthonous-like DOC,and total DOC concentrations and the corresponding discharge for approximately bi-weekly samples collected from April 2018 to March 2020 were used to estimate daily fluxes for the same period. Then, to derive the average daily concentration for each parameter, we divided the daily flux by the daily discharge.It is important to note that we observed a temperature dependence on colloidal Fe concentration-discharge curves (see Results, Eq. 2, Fig. 2,Table S2)，which is not directly addressed using this method.However，because LOADEST uses season-specific regressions of flux vs.discharge，temperature is inherently considered in the calculation of flux.To address the question of seasonality,monthly mean daily fluxes and concentrations for each constituent were calculated from the daily estimates.

# Discussion

Here，we show that the seasonality of downstream dissolved Fe fluxes is predominantly driven by the distinct cycling of two operationally dissolved Fe size fractions:as hypothesized，colloidal Fe was correlated to discharge and water temperature,and soluble Fe was closely coupled with visible-emitting humic-like DOM.While we predicted that the colloidal Fe fraction would be independent of DOM，we instead found that there was a correlation between a humiclike FDOM component and colloidal Fe, suggesting coupling, albeit weaker than for soluble Fe.As hypothesized,soluble Fe concentrations and fluxes matched the seasonality of the allochthonous-like DOM concentrations and fluxes. Colloidal Fe fluxes varied considerably less seasonally than soluble Fe fluxes，as colloidal Fe concentrations were lowest at the highest discharge (spring) and highest at the lowest discharge (summer).

# Colloidal Fe drivers: Temperature and discharge

Colloidal Fe relationships with temperature and discharge suggest microbial redox cycling as a driver. Catchment redox conditions have been proposed as controlling operationally dissolved Fe concentrations in boreal rivers (Kritzberg and Ekstrom 2O12） and German river catchments and in soil experiments (Tittel et al. 2O22),with high discharge and high air temperature together contributing to higher rates of microbial reduction of Fe in catchments (Ekstrom et al. 2016). Under anoxic soil conditions，microbial reduction of soil Fe(III) to Fe(II） mobilizes Fe.If exported to oxygenated circumneutral surface waters such as the Connecticut River, the more soluble Fe(II) can be oxidized back to the less soluble Fe(III).Soil temperature has been shown to control the rate of microbial Fe(II) reduction and export from soils (Schilling et al.2O19),with surface soil porewater dissolved Fe concentrations increasing following flooding to a greater degree at greater soil temperatures (Weber et al. 2O1O). This is all in line with our observations of colloidal Fe concentrations compared to temperature and discharge.Further, the correlation between operationally dissolved Co and colloidal Fe may support redox-driven transport; Co concentrations in a wetland soil incubation were shown to be driven by the microbiallymediated reductive dissolution of Fe-oxyhydroxides，with which Co was associated (Grybos et al. 2OO7). The correlation between Fe and Co thus supports our observation that discharge and temperature may control the mobilization of colloidal Fe through catchment soil microbially-mediated redox reactions.

Taking into account the temperature modulation of colloidal Fe relationships with discharge is important for predicting or modeling Fe concentrations and fluxes of Fe downstream. The slopes of concentration-discharge curves are used to elucidate the source，removal，and transport of biogeochemical constituents at the catchment scale (Moatar et al. 2O17） and to model and predict water quality, concentrations,and fluxes based on hydrology.In the Connecticut River at water temperatures above $1 0 . 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ microbially-mediated mobilization of soil Femeans that riverine colloidal Fe is not source-limited but transport-limited，and colloidal Fe concentrations increase with increasing discharge,since the hydraulic connectivity to Fe sources within the watershed is limiting.Below the $1 0 . 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ threshold, redox-driven mobilization of Fe is not great enough to replenish the colloidal Fe lost by flushing downstream with increasing discharge,and riverine colloidal Fe becomes sourcelimited. This suggests that high discharge events during the summer may be the predominant times in which colloidal Fe is exported to the aquatic system,in line with previous work in temperate catchments (Rosenberg and Schroth 2O17).This would be masked if analyzing seasonal data in aggregate.

The covariance of colloidal Fe and certain FDOMPARAFAC components suggests coupling between colloidal Fe and some subsets of DOM.For example, the sources or mobilization processes might be similar between FDOM associated with C3 fluorescence and colloidal Fe.Redox cycling of soil Fe often results in the mobilization of both Fe and soil organic carbon (Oborn and Hem 1961;Tittel et al. 2022)．A fluorescence component that was $> 9 8 \%$ similar to C3 has been shown to be higher in forested catchments during warmer months (Stedmon and Markager 2Oo5). Given that temperature drives the rate of degradation and resulting quality of organic matter, microbially-mediated reduction reactions may be mobilizing both soil OC and colloidal Fe in tandem，as suggested by Weyhenmeyer et al. (2014). Thus, this elucidates coupling between colloidal Fe and subsets of DOM.

# Soluble Fe drivers: Humic-like DOM fluorescence

Soluble Fe was strongly correlated to humic-like FDOM fluorescence (Figs.3,5)，potentially through direct chemical complexation.FDOM associated with PARAFAC components that were similar to C4 has been shown to have a high Febinding capacity (Ishii and Boyer 2OO6),and similar components were strongly correlated to concentrations of Fe and Al in boreal lakes (Kothawala et al. 2014) and to Fe(II) solubility in the North Pacific Ocean (Yamashita et al. 2O1Oa).Further, Dahlqvist et al. (2OO7) estimated that most dissolved Al in a boreal river was complexed with humic DOM.All these findings were mirrored in our results;we found strong positive correlations between operationally soluble Fe and C4 fluorescence (Fig.3),dissolved Al and C4 fluorescence $( R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 8 8$ $p < 0 . 0 1 )$ ，and soluble Fe and dissolved Al (Fig.4),indicating that soluble Fe (and dissolved Al) is strongly coupled to this subset of DOM，potentially in mononuclear organic complexes.This hypothesis is in line with observations from other systems in which soluble Fe is predominantly organically complexed (Baalousha et al. 2O06; Shiller et al. 2O06; Joung and Shiller 2016). Together this suggests that FDOM associated with C4 fluorescence may control the solubility of Fe and other metals.

Because C4 fluorescence in the Connecticut River was mostly terrestrially-derived, terrestrial DOM influences soluble Fe concentrations.While fluorescence components similar to C4 have been shown to be both allochthonous (Ishii and Boyer 2006;Logozzo et al. 2021） and autochthonous (Ishii and Boyer 2006; Yamashita et al. 2010a; Logozzo et al. 2021), in the Connecticut River, the seasonality of C4 fluorescence (Fig.3) suggests a predominantly terrestrial source.For example,the highest C4 fluorescence occurred during late summer storms and in the fall,which is when terrestrial material inputs to rivers,such as direct plant litter and soil-derived DOM,would be greatest (Shultz et al. 2018);additionally, C4 fluorescence was lowest,on average, in the summer when instream primary production generally dominates over terrestrial inputs in large rivers (Hosen et al. 2O2O). Therefore,the low availability of terrestrially-sourced DOM with a high metal-binding affinity in the summer may drive the,on average,low soluble Fe concentrations and fluxes in the summer (Fig.5a,b).Together, these results demonstrate the importance of terrestrial contributions of DOM in controlling riverine Fe solubility，in line with other work (Powell and WilsonFinelli 2003; Krachler et al. 2005).

# DOM composition and Fe coupling

Using PARAFAC components as opposed to DOC concentrations to evaluate DOM controls on soluble Fe is useful because it accounts for the seasonal and hydrologic variability in DOM type,composition,and source,and therefore, Fe coupling.Many studies have evaluated the relationship between different Fe fractions and DOC,and many have shown differences in these relationships across site,hydrology,or season. For example,in boreal rivers,Tashiro et al.(2O2O) found different slopes in the relationship between dissolved Fe and DOC concentrations for the spring and summer,which is likely due to the seasonality of both DOC composition and source and Fe size or phase.Here,the slope between soluble Fe and C4 fluorescence is the same for all seasons (Fig.3),which accounts for the seasonal and hydrologically-driven sourceswitching of DOM often common in large river systems (Hosen et al. 2O2O),with different sources having vastly different complexation potential.Additionally，here we showed coupling between colloidal Fe and some subsets of DOM, even though there was only a weak relationship between colloidal Fe and DOC concentration. Thus,accounting for the variability in DOM composition and source is important when trying to understand riverine Fe cycling or solubility in relation to DOM,and could explain the observed variability in Fe and DOM coupling across systems.

# Modeled concentrations and fluxes

The modeled average annual fluxes for DOC and dissolved Fe calculated here are similar to previous estimates.Average annual DOC fluxes and the percent allochthonous-like DOC flux calculated here were similar to past estimates in the same system (Hosen et al. 202O);however, the DOC yield,defined as the DOC flux standardized to catchment area,was much lower in the Connecticut River $( 6 2 1 9 \mathrm { ~ g } \mathrm { k m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { ~ d } ^ { - 1 } )$ compared tosome boreal rivers， such asthe Severnaya Dvina $( 1 4 , 3 5 6 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { k m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 } )$ (Pokrovsky et al. 2010). Our dissolved Fe yields were similar to calculated yields in mountainous rivers in Japan in the summer (Endo et al. 2O21),but significantly lower than in a boreal river (Pokrovsky et al. 2010).

We found a decoupling of dissolved Fe fluxes and discharge，contrary to some previous work in boreal rivers (Dahlqvist et al. 20O7；Pokrovsky et al. 2012; Tashiro et al. 2020),mountainous rivers (Endo et al. 2O21),and a temperate watershed (Rosenberg and Schroth 2O17). In the Connecticut River,the spring freshet when discharge was greatest (Fig. 6) resulted in the dilution of both colloidal and soluble Fe (Figs.2，3b).Thus,dissolved Fe fluxes were not particularly high in the spring (Fig.5)，similar to observations from the Mississippi River(Shiller1997)．For colloidal Fe， this decoupling with discharge is explicitly included in the colloidal Fe concentration-discharge curve,which is modulated by temperature (Fig.2)，stressing how consideration of temperature and season may be fundamental to accurately predicting the seasonality of the riverine export of elements like Fe.In fact，temperature-dependent colloidal Fe-discharge relationships in the Connecticut River dampened the seasonal variability in colloidal Fe fluxes downstream.

Seasonal fluctuations in dissolved Fe fluxes in the Connecticut River mainstem were predominantly driven by the large variability in soluble Fe fluxes.While colloidal Fe fluxes did vary significantly across months，the variability was much lower than soluble Fe fluxes (e.g., twofold vs. ninefold). Therefore,although soluble Fe makes up a lower percentage of the total dissolved Fe pool (Table 2),its drivers (i.e.,allochthonouslike DOM and C4 fluorescence） are more important in explaining the total dissolved Fe flux variability seasonally in this system.Soluble Fe is probably the more bioavailable fraction,even when complexed with DOM (Hassler et al. 2009). Further,previous work has shown soluble Fe to be,in part, composed of mononuclear organic complexes(Baalousha et al.2006; Shiller et al. 2006; Joung and Shiller 2016) as also suggested in this study (Fig.3), thereby making soluble Fe more likely to escape the estuarine mixing zone (Herzog et al. 2017, 2020a，2O2Ob) compared to colloidal Fe. Thus,characterizing the seasonality of soluble Fe is important for estimating the effects of the riverine export of Fe on coastal and ocean productivity and Fe uptake. In our system, approximately $4 0 \%$ of the annual soluble Fe flux was delivered in 2 months in the fall, and both the total operationally dissolved and soluble Fe fluxes were the lowest, on average,in the summer, in line with some work in boreal systems (Pokrovsky et al. 2O1O). This low flux of soluble Fe in the summer coincides with a time in which the downstream biological Fe demand would be high，having implications for downstream productivity.

# Conclusions

Our results demonstrate distinct behavior of two operationally dissolved Fe size fractions，which together drive operationally dissolved Fe trends in concentrations and fluxes.We provide seasonal estimates of the downstream flux and concentrations of soluble and colloidal Fe in a large temperate river.These estimates are useful to determine the seasonal and hydrologic constraints on dissolved Fe export to coastal zones, as the dissolved Fe exported by rivers is first and foremost limited by dissolved Fe at the freshwater end-member.As shown here,freshwater dissolved Fe fluxes and concentrations are controlled by the separate cycling of soluble and colloidal Fe, with colloidal Fe concentrations dominating at high discharge at high temperatures (e.g.,during summer storms),and soluble Fe concentrations and fluxes dominating in the late fall and through coupling with pulses of terrestrially-derived DOM.Importantly，mirrored by the low C4 FDOM fluorescence and allochthonous-like DOC fluxes in the summer,soluble Fe fluxes were also lowest,on average,in the summer when downstream biological Fe demand is high.Additional work is needed to estimate the riverine export of soluble and colloidal Fe to the coastal zone by estimating the amount of Fe removal in estuaries,which can reach up to $9 0 \%$ (Boyle et al.1977),depending on Fe phase and complexation with DOM (Herzog et al. 2017, 2020b).While much work has been done on how the size class and speciation of Fe impact its removal (Krachler et al. 2005；Herzog et al. 2017，2020b), explicitly linking Fe reactivity to the hydrologic and biologic factors that drive concentrations of the varying dissolved Fe fractions is needed.
