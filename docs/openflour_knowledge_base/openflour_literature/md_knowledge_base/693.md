Abstract Fluorescence was used to examine the quality of dissolved and particulate organic matter (DOM and POM) exchanging between a tidal creek in a created salt marsh and its adjacent estuary in eastern North Carolina, USA.Samples from the creek were collcted hourly over four tidal cycles in May, July,August,and October 2011.Absorbance and fluorescence of chromophoric DOM (CDOM)and of base-extracted POM (BEPOM) served as the tracers for organic matter quality while dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and base-extracted particulate organic carbon (BEPOC) were used to compute fluxes. Fluorescence was modeled using parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) and principle components analysis (PCA) of the PARAFAC results. Of nine PARAFAC components (C) modeled, C3 represented recalcitrant DOM and C4 represented fresher soil-derived source DOM. Component 1 represented detrital POM, and C6 represented planktonic POM. Based on mass balance,recalcitrant DOC export was $8 6 { \mathfrak { g } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { m } } ^ { - 2 } { \mathsf { y r } } ^ { - 1 }$ and labile DOC export was $4 9 9 \mathsf { C m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ ; no planktonic DOC was exported.The marsh also exported $4 1 \mathsf { g C m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ of detrital terrestrial POC, which likely originated from lands adjacent to the North River estuary. Planktonic POC export from the marsh was $6 9 mathsf { C m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ . Assuming the exported organic matter was oxidized to ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and scaled up to global salt marsh area,respiration of salt marsh DOC and POC transported to estuaries could amount to a global ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ flux of $1 1 \mathsf { T g C y r } ^ { - 1 }$ ,roughly $4 \%$ of the recently estimated ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ release for marshes and estuaries globally.

# 1. Introduction

Salt marshes are transitional ecosystems between the terestrial and marine environments. Along with mangroves and other vegetated coastal habitats,salt marshes rank among the most productive ecosystems on Earth,with critical global importance for the planet's carbon cycle. Recently,the term "blue carbon" has been used to conceptualize the storage of ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ in coastal wetland sediments and soils resulting from their intense primary production,approximately $0 . 5 5 \mathsf { P g } \mathsf { C y r } ^ { - 1 }$ [Bauer et al., 2013] of which approximately $9 0 \mathsf { T g } \mathsf { C y r } ^ { - 1 }$ is buried in soils [Mcleod etal.,2011; Macreadie et al.,2013]. However,substantial knowledge gaps remain on the quality of the dynamic organic carbon pools—dissolved (DOM)and particulate organic matter (POM)—which tidall exchange between salt marshes and estuaries daily [Tobias and Neubauer, 2009; Fagherazzi etal.,2013].

Exchanges of dissolved and particulate organic carbon (DOC and POC) between tidal marshes and estuaries (or coastal waters)are of great interest in order to elucidate current knowledge gaps in the carbon cycle of the coastal ocean.Recent concern about relative sea level rise (RSLR)in part revolves around the ecosystem services of coastal salt marshes and the role of salt marsh biogeochemistry in adjusting to RSLR [Mcleod et al.,2011].This concern raises questions as to how the blue carbon storage of marshes might change in the face of RSLR. One unknown is how the changes in the quality of organic mater (OM) inputs may impact C storage and blue carbon biogeochemistry in these systems. Bianchi [2011] has suggested that labile OM can serve as a "priming" mechanism,stimulating the cometabolism of recalcitrant,terrestrial OM.If RSLR results in the import of more labile planktonic OM to coastal salt marshes connected to productive estuaries,the net result could be a diminishment of blue carbon storage of ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and thus a global ecosystem service provided by salt marshes.The various degrees of lability of numerous carbon sources to and within salt marsh-estuarine continuum require study of the quality of dissolved and particulate organic matter in addition to determining their bulk concentrations.

Marshes are thought to export DOM mostly at ebbing tide on the order of $1 5 { - } 3 3 0 { \mathsf { g C m } } ^ { - 2 } { \mathsf { y r } } ^ { - 1 }$ [Tzortziou et al.,2008; Tobias and Neubauer,2009]. Childers et al.[2002] summarized POM imports and exports in salt marshes and found nearly equal rates between approximately $1 0 { - } 1 4 0 9 \mathsf { C m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ .This suggests on whole a near balance in POM fluxes between marshes and estuaries. However，quantifying carbon exchanges between marshes and their adjacent waters is chalenging because of their enormous variabilitywith respect tohydrologyand geomorphology[Tobiasand Neubauer,2009;Fagherazzietal.,2013].

Over tidal cycles,bidirectionalflow causes autochthonous POM and DOM (e.g.,marsh plants and plankton) to $" \mathsf { m i x ^ { \prime \prime } }$ with estuarine POM and DOM,the latter including algal-based primary production in addition to a variety of alochthonous sources (e.g.，terrestrial runoff，direct riverine inputs,submarine groundwater discharge，precipitation,and other marshes). Compared to terrestrial OM draining into marshes from upstream,largely in dissolved form as DOM,as well as OM formed in the marsh habitat itself,planktonic POM from estuaries is thought to be more labile to degradation [Fogel et al.，1989].Being composed primarily of sugars, N-rich amino acids and proteins, planktonic OM could be rapidly remineralized to $C O _ { 2 } ,$ adding to the carbon flux out of sat marshes to adjacent coastal ocean waters [Cai,2011; Bauer et al, 2013].Thus,adding to the uncertainty in the quantities of OM exchanging between tidal marshes and estuaries is a paucity of information about its quality.

Availablechemical methods (e.g.,biomarkers such as amino acids,lipids,or lignin)are highly informational on OM quality but most are labor intensive and not amenable to high-resolution studies.Others (e.g., atomic elemental ratios such as a C:N) lack source specificity to resolve source changes over temporal (tidal to seasonal to decadal) or spatial (tidal fresh to estuarine) gradients.Importantly,these techniques are difcult to implement into routine or in situ monitoring and can thus lack critical observations that lead to understanding ecosystem function.For example,Birgand et al.[2010] showed distinct differences in materialfluxes from rivers basedon the time basis of observation.Etheridge etal.[2015] found similar diferences in DOC and nutrient fluxes in the tidal marsh in the present study.Mass balance estimates on DOC in this marsh varied by as much as 50-fold depending on sampling frequency.Thus,refined estimates of material fluxes in marshes are a critical need for closing nutrient and carbon balances in these transitional ecosystems.

Chromophoric DOM (CDOM)absorbs and fluoresces light and has been widely used along with multivariate statistical modeling to discern DOM biogeochemistry in most aquatic ecosystems [e.g.,Fellman etal.,2010; Coble,2007]. Recently, CDOM fluorescence and modeling techniques have been applied to POM through a means in which dilute base extraction of POM provides a solution—BEPOM—the optical properties of which can be measured similar to DOM [Osburn et al.,2012; Brym et a.,2014].These works have shown a discrete planktonic signal present in estuarine and coastal BEPOM which can be modeled along with the more terrestrial signals that comprise the majority of DOM.

While prior work on salt marshes has examined OM quality in detail,few studies have computed fluxes of specific types of DOM and POM.The aim of this study was to combine OM fluorescence with hydrologic measurements of stream flow to quantify the tidal exchanges of DOM and POM between a temperate salt marsh and its adjacent estuaryin eastern North Carolina, USA. Calculations similar to Tzortziou etal.[2008] and Etheridge et al.[2015] were combined with PARAFAC model results of OM sources in the marsh to compute fluxes of marsh-derived,terrestrial,and planktonic POCand DOC over four tidal cycles in 2011 [e.g., Osburn and Stedmon 2011].

# 2. Methods

Site Location. Research was conducted on a $1 0 0 0 \mathsf { m }$ tidal creek draining the $1 4 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ of brackish and salt marsh created at North River Farms (NRF),in Carteret County,eastern North Carolina, USA $( 3 4 . 8 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { N } , 7 6 . 6 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { W } )$ The created salt marsh was an extension of salt marsh adjacent to the North River Estuary,itself a tributary to Back Sound,part of the Pamlico Sound,the largest lagoonal estuary in the United States.The vegetation on the marsh platform is dominated by Spartina alterniflora,Spartina patens,and Juncus roemerianus.The created marsh was designed and constructed in a former wetland ecosystem that was drained for agricultural use,to also intercept drainage water generated tothe north byan adjacent large flat agricultural land and a bottomland hardwood forest wetland,before it reaches the estuary (Figure 1).An agricultural drainage system provided a source of freshwater runof to the marsh and was sampled from a water control structure in the drain (DR,Figure 1).A portion of the drainage water was diverted into the tidal creek (CR,labeled as Broome's Branch in Figure 1)by a low rock weir installd in the original main drainage canal that is west of the marsh.Details on the marsh construction and hydrology can be found in Etheridge et al.[2015].

Water Sampling. Water samples taken at CR,DR,and a bridge overpass on the North River estuary (site $" \in { \mathsf { S } } ^ { \prime \prime } )$ were colected during May,July,August,and October 2011.Table 1 presents specific dates and numbers of samples collected for the sampling periods during this study. Unfiltered water samples were collected at the outlet flume installed at the CR site hourly over a $2 4 \mathsf { h }$ period in order to capture a full tidal cycle in the marsh the day before field servicing.Samples were collected in cleaned $1 0 0 0 \mathsf { m L }$ polyethylene bottles using a timeactivated automatic sampler (Teledyne ISCO)[Etheridge, 2013]. Aliquots were poured into cleaned $5 0 0 \mathsf { m L }$ polyethylene botles and stored on ice until return to the laboratory at North Carolina State University (typically storage was $2 4 { - } 4 8 \mathsf { h } _ { \iota }$ .After collection of the tidal cycle samples from the creek,additional samples were collected from the DR and ES sites (Table 1 and Figure 1).The DR site represented the freshwater runoff to the marsh tidal creek,while the ES site represented an estuarine end-member to the marsh.Some of the freshwater from the DR site is diverted into the Drainage Canal along the western border with the remainder entering the headwater of the tidal creek above the CR site.

Samples were filtered through precombusted Whatman GF/F filters using gentle vacuum.The filtrate was further fltered through $0 . 2 \mu \mathsf { m }$ polyethersulfone filters; this was considered the DOM fraction,and the material retained on the GF/F filter was considered the POM fraction.The GF/F filters were stored in at $0 \%$ until the BEPOM was extracted.

BEPOM extraction involved placing the filters in cleaned polypropylene centrifuge tubes containing $1 0 \mathsf { m L }$ of 0.1 N NaOH [Osburn etal.,2012].The tubes were prerinsed with 0.1N NaOH.Each tube was shaken vigorously for 1 min and then placed in the refrigerator to extract at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $2 4 \mathsf { h }$ in the dark.After the base-extraction period,the solutions were neutralized with $3 7 \%$ HCl to pH approximately 7 or close to water sample's ambient pH when known, generally between 7 and 8.This ensured that pH effects on fluorescence were minimized [Brym et al., 2014].After neutralization,the solutions were refiltered to $0 . 2 \mu \mathsf { m }$ to remove pieces of the filter that disintegrated during extraction.The clarified solution was then treated as a DOM sample for further analyses.

Absorbance on DOM and BEPOM solutions was made in 1 or $5 c m$ quartz cells (Starna Cells, Inc.) in a Varian 300UV spectrophotometer using 18.2 MΩ ultrapure water as a reference for DOM and a neutralized $0 . 1 \ N$ NaOH solution as a reference for BEPOM. Napierian absorption coefcients (a) were then computed from the following equation:

$$
a ( \lambda ) = \frac { A \left( \lambda _ { \mathsf { s a m p l e } } \right) - A \left( \lambda _ { \mathsf { b l a n k } } \right) } { L } \times 2 . 3 0 3 ,
$$

where $^ a$ is the absorption coeficient (in $\mathfrak { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ） $\lambda$ is the wavelength in nanometer,A is the optical density of the sample or blank, measured on the spectrophotometer,and $L$ is the pathlength of the quartz cell,in meters [Osburn and Morris, 2003]. Absorption was backcorrected for dilution during sample processing and in case $A _ { 2 4 0 }$ values were greater than 0.4.

Fluorescence of DOM and BEPOM solutions was measured on a Varian Eclipse spectrophotometer either at 800 or $9 5 0 V ,$ depending on sample response.Excitation (ex) wavelengths ranged from 240 to $4 5 0 \mathsf { n m }$ at $5 \mathsf { n m }$ intervals,while emission (em) wavelengths were sampled at $2 \mathsf { n m }$ intervals from 300 to $6 0 0 \mathsf { n m }$ 5 nm slit widths were used in ex and em modes and an integration time of 0.0125 s and a scan rate of $9 6 0 0 \mathsf { n m } \mathsf { m m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ .The fluorescence measurements were corrected for excitation energy and emission detector response using manufacturer-supplied correction functions. Any inner-filter efects were corrected using methods in Tucker et al.[1992].Fluorescence was then calibrated against the instrument's water Raman signal,and standardized to quinine sulfate units (ppb QSU)[Lawaetz and Stedmon,20o9].Fluorescence results were concatenated into the common-used excitation-emision matrices (EMs)and visualized as contour plots.

Dissolved organic carbon (DOC)and base-extracted particulate organic carbon (BEPOC) concentrations were measured on an Ol 1030D TOC analyzer in wet chemical oxidation mode.Samples were acidified to pH 2 with $8 5 \%$ phosphoric acid $( H _ { 3 } { \mathsf { P O } } _ { 4 } )$ and sparged with ultrahigh purity argon prior to analysis.DOC and BEPOC values were blank corrected against Mili-Q water and calibrated against solutions of caffeine at DOC concentrations of 1,5,and $1 0 \mathsf { m g C L } ^ { - 1 }$ .Final BEPOC values accounted for Mill-Q water dilutions and initial filtration volume.

Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) modeling was conducted using MATLAB software (Mathworks,Inc., Natick, MA),including theDOMFluortoolbox[Stedmonnd Bro,2008].Ingeneral,DOMfluorescence intensity isfar greater than BEPOM fluorescence intensity,based onour experimental protocol.Prior to fiting the PARAFAC model,the EEMdata were normalized byscaling each EEM toits totalfluorescence[Murphy etal.,2013].Large variation in sample fluorescence intensity during PARAFAC modeling will result in large leverages for the stronger fluorescent samples and bias the model.PARAFAC decomposes high-order data such as EEMs into underlying components that describe up to $9 8 \%$ of the variation in the data.The components are visually inspected as having characteristics of organic fluorophores (or groups of fluorophores). Maximum fluorescence intensities of each component (FMax) are reported in quinine sulfate units ( $1 \mathsf { Q } \mathsf { S } \mathsf { U } = 1 \mathsf { \mu } \mathsf { g } \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ quinine sulfate in $0 . 1 \mathsf { N } \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S } \mathsf { O } _ { 4 } )$ ：

A typical PARAFAC model explains ${ > } 9 5 \%$ of the variation in the data.An efficient alternating least squares approach is used to minimize residuals between measured and modeled EMs and usually produces robust models with four to nine components. Models were validated first by dividing the data array into two halves and using Tucker's congruence coefficient (TCC,set to $9 5 \%$ similarity) to test separate PARAFAC models fit to each half (split-half analysis). Random initialization was next used on 10 models to find the model having the lowest sum of squared error.The PLS_Toolbox for MATLAB (Eigenvector, Inc.) was used for principal component analysis (PCA) of PARAFAC FMax results.

PARAFAC model results also were queried against approximately 50 PARAFAC models in the OpenFluor database (http://www.openfluor.org).This new database is a clearinghouse for published PARAFAC models on a variety of DOM sources and provides an expedient way to test new PARAFAC models against those in the literature. Models in the database include those fit on lakes, rivers,estuaries,and oceans using several diferent fluorometers.Individual model components are tested against others in the database during the query using TCC (set at $9 5 \%$ ）so that any one query may result in different numbers of matches for a particular component.

# 3.2.Tidal Dynamics of DOM and POM

Tidal exchange of CDOM and BEPOM absorption was evident during all months sampled and followed tidal exchanges of DOMobserved in other marshes [e.g.,Gardner etal.,2005;Tzortziou et al.,2008].During May, July,and August, CDOM $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ values decreased during flood tide,was lowest during high tide (HT),and then increased to maximum values during low tide (LT) (Figures 3a-3c). BEPOM $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ values also followed this pattern.Thus,during these months,the most chromophoric material was in the terrestrial organic matter present in the DR water draining agricultural fields north of the marsh.In contrast, during October, a directly opposite pattern was observed in both DOM and POM (Figure 3d). CDOM absorption and BEPOM absorption both followed the flooding tidal cycle and values of $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ in each pool were greatest during high tide (HT). The total fluorescence $( \bar { F } _ { \mathrm { t o t } } )$ measured for each sample generally followed the patterns for absorption (Table 2).

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Major Variations of Organic Matter Quality During Tides and Over Seasons

Each carbon pool had the presence of all PARAFAC components,underlying the mixing of terrestrial and planktonic organic matter along the marsh-estuary continuum [Childers et al.，2002]. Groupings of PARAFAC components related to OM source and reactivity are common outcomes when they are explored using PCA models [Santin etal.,2009; Osburn etal.,2012; Cawley et al,2014].The PCA separated BEPOM from CDOM but also fresh and detrital sources of OM (Figure 7).The separate PCAs conducted on BEPOM and CDOM individually demonstrated similar qualitative changes in the OM over seasons and tidal cycles, showing the importance of marsh hydrology (Figures 8a and 8b).These OM quality designations are most obvious when considering the trends with tides and contrasting seasonal effects when the marsh was euhaline (Aug) or oligohaline (Oct) (Figure 2).

The magnitude of change in the relative proportions of components (%FMax) between ebb and flood tide during May 2011 was twofold to threefold (Figure 9).This range was similar to the change in $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ of CDOM and BEPOM we measured on the four tidal cycles in this study (Figure 2).A twofold to threefold change in CDOM values over tidal cycles was also observed in U.S.salt marshes in Maryland and in Southern California[Tzortziou etal.,2008; Clark etal.,2008]. However,qualitative differences were found only in the Maryland salt marsh in which synchronous fluorescence values pointed to higher molecular weight material fluxing out of the marsh [Tzortziou et al.，2008]. Our PARAFAC results clearly showed

However， the PCA model suggested that seasonal differences in OM quality appeared more dramatic than those during tides. In August, the low amount of freshwater in the tidal creek was evident because salinities were ${ > } 3 5$ .Monthly averages of South Atlantic Bight coastal waters rarely exceed salinities of 37 [Blanton et al.,2003]. Thus, the CR and perhaps upper reaches of the ES experienced evapoconcentration producing euhaline conditions in the tidal creek in August despite tidal mixing similar to other months we studied (Figure 2d).The changes in tidal stage and flow were not markedly different from other months,but the CDOM concentration was far greater (Figure 3c).

CDOM concentration typically increases with salinity in arid coastal regions and in inverse estuaries [Milbrandt et al., 2010; Catala et al., 2013].During the summer euhaline conditions, DOM seepage into the channel from Spartina substrates in the sediments was possible [Wolaver et al., 1988; Yelverton and Hackney, 1986].DOM seepage would explain our highest $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ measurements during LT in July and August,as common salt marsh plants can produce leachate enriched in both protein and humic fluorescence [Clark et al.,

2008;Shank et al.， 2010]. However，photochemical and/or microbial degradation during euhaline conditions could be such red-shifted DOM fluorescence and enhancing the C3-like fluorescence we observed during August [Scully et al.,2004; Milbrandt et al.,2010]. We speculate that processing by the microbial loop also would generate quinone-like (C6)and microbial humic-like (C8) fluorescence that was enriched in POM due to the milieu of particle-associated bacteria and plankton captured on filters. Those signals were prevalent in BEPOM measurements taken on the nearby Neuse River estuary during low discharge,long residence period in late August 2011 [Osburn et al.,2012; Brym et al., 2014].

In October 2011,the marsh was oligohaline and positive DOM scores on PC2 coincided with loadings for C2, C4, and C9.Each of these components had dual excitation peaks,consistent with fresh humic organic matter in which the secondary maxima coincided with highly photoreactive peaks in marsh CDOM (e.g., ex 325/em 450) [Clark et al.,20o8].Longwave excitationand emisson that would produce these secondary excitation peaks is due to charge transfer reactions in DOM,which can be easily disrupted chemically and photochemically [Ma et al.,2010]. In other words, the presence of photochemicallyand perhaps biologically labile fluorescence at approximately ex $3 2 5 / \mathrm { e m } ~ 4 5 0$ in wetter months would explain why DOM sample scores clustered with loadings for C2 and C4.Fresh (and thus reactive) DOM was mobilized from the marsh pore waters,as a function of hydrology,though apparently distinct from the DOM in seepage during euhaline conditions which was enriched in C3 fluorescence.

Much like CDOM fluorescence, BEPOM fluorescence showed a wider separation in quality over seasons than over tides (Figure 8b).Planktonic POM represented by C6-C8 was present in the marsh during relatively dry months while more sedimentary POM was present during wetter months,presumably due to scouring by overland flow.In the POM,detrital terrestrial C1 decreased while planktonic C6 increased in relative proportion during the flood tide though C1 maintains its relative abundance during the ebb tide into the slack water (Figure 9b). Overall,these results demonstrate the need to capture the quality of DOM and POM in marshes over tidal cycles as well as seasonally as these qualitative changes will influence the C exchanged across the marsh-estuarine continuum.

# 4.2. Variability in Salt Marsh Organic Matter Sources Measured by Fluorescence

The presence of humic fluorescence signals in coastal seawater resembling C3 in the NRF model suggests that this component is a tracer for recalcitrant organic mater escaping degradation acrossthe marsh-estuarine continuum[e.g., Murphyetal.,2008; Kowalczuk etal.,2009;Jorgensenetal,2011].Ourassertion isspported by the decrease of fresh CDOM (C4) fluorescence during flood tide while C3 fluorescence from the estuary increased. Recalcitrance of C3 thus explains why this component matched with components identified as recalcitrant DOM found in coastal ocean models.Likewise, C6-C8 matched with components from models of mesotrophic to eutrophic ecosystems,soil pore waters,etc.,indicated relatively fresh and potentially labile DOM and POM.Notably，Shimotori et al.[2009] documented a "humic-like" EEM fluorescence pattern in bacterial cultures grown on glucose that bear remarkable similarity to C6 and C8—a pattern found in BEPOM from the eutrophic Neuse River Estuary [Osburn et al.,2012].We thus contend that our nine-component PARAFAC model has described a continuum of reactive organic matter pools exchanging between the tidal creek and the estuary.

Component 3 in our PARAFAC model has shown resistance to photochemical and biological degradation [Cawley et al.，2014].Moreover，Boyd and Osburn [2004] found blue-shifted fluorescence occurred in DOM after biodegradation,while Tzortziou et al.[2007] found blue-shifted fluorescence remaining after photodegradation despite loss offuorescence in the region ofC3 [Gonsior et al.,2009; Osburn etal.,2014]. The disruption of the charge-transfer reactions that produce longwave emisson is caused by decreased conjugation and loss of functional groups [Senesi,199o; Sharpless and Blough,2014].

Clark et al. [2008] surveyed several salt marsh plant species and found that in the DOM they produced, C3-typefluorescence was most resistant to photodegradation among the humic peaks they studied. C3 lacked thefluorescence signal at ex 325/em 450 that indicates charge-transfer reactions occurring in fresh humic material due to extensive conjugation. Degradation typically reduces the conjugation-producing longwave fluorescence; therefore, C3 could provide an estimate of the recalcitrant DOM exported from marshes.C3 also was persistent at HT which indicates its predominance in the North River estuary,likely as degraded DOM exported from its fringing salt marshes downstream of North River Farms marsh.The fresh humic fluorescence (C2, C4,and C5) was similar to CDOM in the DR samples,but we note that labile CDOM exhibiting C2-and C5-like fluorescence can be produced rapidly (within 40 days) in salt marshes andin mangrove pore waters [Wang etal.,2007; Tremblayetal.,2007]. However,the PCA scores for CDOM clustered near loadings for C2, C4,and C5 when more freshwater was in the marsh,indicating an important component from land runoff (Figure 8).

Our BEPOM fluorescence results suggested that most detrital POM occurred during October when the marsh was mesohaline with some of the lowest salinities measured (Table 2and Figure 2).Studies of the Saeftinghe salt marsh (SW Netherlands) found that primarily refractory material comprised that marsh's background POM signal [Hemminga et al.，1993; Klap et al.，1996].Using biomarkers such as lignin phenols and carbohydrates as source indicators in Dabob Bay,WA (USA),Hedges et al.[1988] found enrichment in terrestrial woody debris during winter months when primary production was reduced and rainfall was proportionaly greater than during the summer growing season. Enrichment in substances such as lignin might very well produce the long wave emission properties such as we observed in the humic-like components C1-C5 [Ma et al,2010].In Georgia salt marshes (similar to North River marsh in the present study),stable isotopes suggested that most sedimentary OM and humic substances consisted of decayed Spartina tissue. Only $30 \%$ of sedimentary OM in Georgia salt marshes was attributed to phytoplankton sources [Fogel et al.,1989].Thus,the negative BEPOM scores clustering near the detrital C1 fluorescence were found for the October samples when runoff mobilized detrital POM from the marsh surface into the tidal creek.

During summer,enrichment in planktonic POM in the NRF marsh likely influenced its degradation.Hemminga et al.[1993] used estuarine bacteria in assays that showed substantial ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ production from salt marsh detritus enriched in chlorophyll during the spring and summer months—nearly 5 orders of magnitude greater than during winter and autumn.Also,during the summer,labile OM formed from planktonic primary production in estuaries and imported to the Saeftinghe marsh during flood tide lost lipid- and polysaccharide-rich material,implying its rapid consumption by zooplankton，crustaceans,or other grazers in the marsh [Klap et al., 1996].

Thus, marshes are transitory systems whereby ebb tides drain the marsh mostly of relatively recalcitrant DOM solubilized and biogeochemicaly transformed during and after flooding of the marsh surface—probably from sedimentary pore waters [Clark et al.,2014]—mixed with freshwater runof.Note that scores near C3 were mostly for euhaline conditions (Figures 7 and 8).This paradigm fits our observations of POM and DOM quality in the NRF marsh and would partly explain the rather large export of DOM relative to POM from marshes [Tobias and Neubauer,2009].

# 4.3. Fluxes of Organic Carbon in and out of the North River Farms Marsh to North River Estuary

Knowledge gaps in the quantityand quality of OM fluxes are central to the issue of blue carbon losses of DOC and POC from vegetated coastal wetland soilsand sediments [Pendleton et al.,2012; Bauer et al.,2013].The limited studies of DOC and POC exchanges between tidal wetlands and the estuaries they border suggest net exportsofcarbon[Childers etal.,2002;Tobiasand Neubauer,2009].However,lacking stilsinformationon the type of carbon exchanged.Despite our study being done on a created marsh,the marsh hydrology was such that DR was an extension of the existing marsh channel (Figure 1).The advantage of this study is that the water balance was able to be closed allowing for a representative calculation of the fluxes [Etheridge et al., 2015].

The PARAFAC component matches to OpenFluor,along with the PCA results viewed through tides and seasons, were used to select discrete tracers of OM sources exchanging between the NRF marsh and North River Estuary. C3 and C4 were clear tracers for degraded and fresh DOM in the marsh,respectively,and C1 and C6 as tracers for detrital and planktonic POM,respectively. Protein-like C7 was dificult to assign to one OM source because of its prevalence in planktonic and marsh-plant organic matter [Maie et al.,2008; Clark et al.,2008; Brym etal.,2014].

Multivariate regression was used to predict DOC or POC concentrations based on the component FMax values [Osburn and Stedmon,2011]. Total fluorescence $( \bar { F } _ { \mathrm { t o t } } )$ Table 2) for each sample (equivalent to the sum of the FMax scores for each of the nine components in each sample) was correlated with available DOC or BEPOC measurements,so the results are empirical (Table 2).Further,we scaled up our BEPOC results by $5 0 \%$ to represent the mean extraction efficiency of BEPOC compared to total POC [Brym et al., 2014].We used linear interpolation to predict $1 5 \mathsf { m i n }$ time points in between the hourly sampled collected [Etheridge et al.,2015]. Caution on using these results is detailed herein.

We fitone model toallofthe BEPOM data buthadto create individualmodels forthe DOM data from each of the 4 months.In some cases (e.g.,July)this meantthat regresson results were weaker than in other months (Table 3). where $c$ is the 15 min concentration $\mathsf { ( m g C L ^ { - 1 } ) }$ of DOC or POC predicted from the regression equations using the FMax of the appropriate component (Table 3), $q _ { i }$ is the 15 min flow $( \mathsf { m } ^ { 3 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ at time $i ,$ t is time (min),and $k$ is a constant for converting units.

Our mass balance calculations revealed that the marsh exported both DOC and POCin May,July,and August 2011 but imported DOC and POC during October 2011 (Tables S1 and S2).July was the largest export of DOC while May was the largest export of POC.In general, more detrital terrestrial POC was exported than labile planktonic POC.During October, nearly threefold more labile terrestrial DOC than recalcitrant terrestrial DOC was taken up by the marsh.

# 5. Conclusions

PARAFAC modeling of CDOM and BEPOM fluorescence coupled with PCA showed that each carbon pool in the created marsh was dominated by distinctly different components that varied tidally and seasonaly. CDOM fluorescence was dominated by fresh and degraded humic fluorescence components，which matched PARAFAC model components from several wetland and coastal water environments. Estuarine BEPOM fluorescence was dominated by components having strong protein and quinone features indicative of planktonic organic matter as well as detrital organic matter. Overall,the marsh was a net sink for marsh detrital POC,and sources of planktonic POC and recalcitrant DOC to its adjacent estuary.

Ourresults indicate that hydrology exerts large controlon the variations in DOMand POM quality ina created salt marsh. Biological activity which produced strong planktonic signals also seemed shaped by hydrological factors.Fluorescence fingerprinting of surface water and groundwater DOM in marshes may bean important tool for measuring organic carbon exchanges and refining estimates of carbon flows along interacting subsystems of the marsh-estuarine continuum [Childers et al.,20o2; Bauer et al., 2013].

The approach used in this study has important implications for evaluating fluxes of DOC and POC with respect to their reactivities.In fact,our approach could aid in evaluating coastal marsh restoration. Coupling these measurements to studies of photochemical and biological degradation of DOM and POM in tidal creeks and estuaries could lead to a broader understanding of their complex biogeochemistry, especially in the face of SLR,the loss of blue carbon,and general loss of coastal wetlands.

The limitations of upscaling North River Farms,a created marsh extension of existing marsh, in terms of area areas follows:(1)itisa small marsh,(2)it stillmay stillbeattainingadynamic equilibrium,and (3)someof the OM may derive from legacy cropland sediments given the land usage prior to the construction.While small, our study site is comparable to smaller marsh studies reviewed by Childers et al. [2002].

The ability to estimate the export of"labile" OM from salt marshes to estuaries is potentially critical in beter constraining the effects of priming on estuarine respiration [e.g., Bianchi,2011]. Despite theuncertainties and asumptions in our modeling eforts,our calculations show exports of predominantly labile planktonic POC and recalcitrant DOC from a young tidal creek to its estuary. However,one system's recalcitrant material is another system's labile material. Salt marsh and estuarine net fluxes of ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ to the atmosphere are on the order of $2 5 0 \mathsf { T g } \mathsf { C y r } ^ { - 1 }$ and thought to be largely of marsh detritus [Cai,2011].We can use results of this study coupled with estimates of POC and DOC respiration to scale up our results to global marsh area $( 4 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 1 } \mathsf m ^ { 2 } )$ [Duarte et al.,2005]. Using Hemminga et al.'s values from the Saeftinghe marsh,a $5 \%$ oxidation of recalcitrant POC released by salt marshes globally results in a potential ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ flux of approximately $1 \mathsf { T g C y r } ^ { - 1 }$ from this carbon pool alone. Hemminga et al.[1993] estimated $3 5 \%$ lability for planktonic POC; thus,we also estimate approximately $1 \mathsf { T g C y r } ^ { - 1 }$ from its respiration. Recalcitrant POC export of $1 6 \mathsf { T } \mathsf { g y r } ^ { - 1 }$ amounts to about $2 6 \%$ of the global burial rate of $6 0 \mathsf { T g } \mathsf { C y r } ^ { - 1 }$ [Duarte et al., 2005]. Using respiration estimates of $1 0 \%$ and $3 0 \%$ for recalcitrant and labile DOC,respectively, provides potential ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ fluxes of 3 and $6 \mathsf { T g C y r } ^ { - 1 }$ [Muylaert et al.,2005]. In total, $1 1 \mathsf { T g C y r } ^ { - 1 } \_ { 4 \% }$ of the net flux estimated by Cai [2011]—could be atributed to respiration of these transported carbon pools.

Combining high-resolution hydrologic measurements with OM fluorescence provided a key means of quantifying salt marsh organic matter cycling. Quantifying OM sources provides important insight into the question of Odum's “outweling" hypothesis regarding the nature of OM exported by marshes to coastal waters [Childers et al.,20o2].As concern forthe effcacy of the blue carbon sink increases with RSLR and changing land use,the approach presented in this study can assist in evaluating this and other important ecosystem services provided by salt marshes and other vegetated coastal wetlands [Pendleton et al., 2012].
