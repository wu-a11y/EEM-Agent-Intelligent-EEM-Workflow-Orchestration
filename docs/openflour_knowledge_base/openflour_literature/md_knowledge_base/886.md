# Effects of acidification on the optical properties of dissolved organic matter from high and low arsenic groundwater and surface water

Harshad Kulkarni a, ⁎, Natalie Mladenov b , Saugata Datta a

a Department of Geology, Kansas State University, Manhattan, KS, USA b Department of Civil, Construction and Environmental Engineering, San Diego State University, San Diego, CA, USA

# H I G H L I G H T S

• Response of DOM optical properties to acidification varies between surface and groundwater   
• Acidification quenches humic-like as well as protein-like fluorescence intensities in groundwater but not in surface water   
• Fluorescence index and freshness index remain unchanged upon acidification in all samples   
• Acidification by $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ results in the production of a new PARAFAC component

The optical properties of bulk dissolved organic matter (DOM) at ambient pH and upon acidification (pH \~2) by hydrochloric acid (HCl) or nitric acid $\left( \mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 } \right)$ were examined in groundwater and surface water samples from the Bengal Basin. Samples of shallow high arsenic (As) and deep low As groundwaters and surface waters from the same geographic area were collected and preserved with HCl and $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ . The optical properties of groundwater samples responded to acidification differently than those of the surface water samples. The intensity of humiclike and protein-like fluorescence decreased by $4 7 \%$ and $8 0 \%$ , respectively, upon acidification with HCl in groundwater but remained unchanged in surface water samples. Similarly, the humification index (HIX) decreased only in surface waters (from 6.6 to 3.7) and remain unchanged in groundwaters upon HCl acidification. The absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ $( A b s _ { 2 5 4 } )$ was not affected by HCl acidification; however, $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ acidification increased $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ in groundwater (by 9-fold) as well as in surface water samples (by 3-fold), possibly due to inherent absorbance of $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ . Humic- and protein-like fluorescence intensities decreased by $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ acidification by $4 9 \%$ and $7 8 \%$ respectively, which may be attributed to aggregation losses and changes in the protonation states of amines, hydroxyls and carboxylic functional groups. Parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis revealed a unique component that resulted from the acidification of samples with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ . The other fluorescence indices such as fluorescence index (FI) and freshness index (β:α) remained unchanged upon either type of acidification of surface water as well as groundwater samples. These results reflect the effects of pH perturbation in groundwater environments where DOC concentrations may be lower as compared to surface water environments and provide insights into the structural, molecular and reactive properties of DOM in these environments.

The optical properties of dissolved organic matter (DOM) provide valuable insights into the biogeochemical processes in various aquatic environments. The structural bonding arrangements of DOM molecules result in ultra-violet (UV) absorbance, which can be used to characterize different types of DOM (Weishaar et al., 2003). The spectral slopes and their ratios are also used to detect the alteration in the molecular weights of DOM (Moran et al., 2000; Vähätalo and Wetzel, 2004; Helms et al., 2008). Three-dimensional fluorescence excitation emission matrices (EEMs) provide additional information on the source, origin and reactivity of DOM (Coble et al., 2014). The fluorescence of DOM results from its complex structure, consisting of fluorescence-causing molecules (fluorophores) with the different origins, molecular weights and reactivity. Statistical analyses of fluorescence spectra using principle component analysis (Persson and Wedborg, 2001), parallel factor (PARAFAC) analysis (Stedmon and Bro, 2008) and PARAFAC analysis combined with the size exclusion chromatography (Wünsch et al., 2017) can extract further information about the individual fluorophores. While direct measurements of molecular properties and functional groups are possible with the advanced chemical analyses techniques such as gas chromatography mass spectrometry (GC–MS, Rowland et al., 2006; Ghosh et al., 2015) or high performance liquid chromatography size exclusion chromatography (HPLC-SEC, Wu and Tanoue, 2001; Wu et al., 2007) and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR, Weishaar et al., 2003), these methods are time and labor intensive and require detailed sample processing procedures. Simple and rapid measurements with absorbance and fluorescence spectroscopy are therefore being used increasingly in the environmental studies. Optical measurements together with controlled alterations in environmental conditions such as temperature, pH and size fractions can provide additional information regarding molecular properties (Baker et al., 2007). Fluorescence of DOM in the natural waters may originate from a wide range of sources. The source of protein-like fluorescence indicated by peaks B and T (Coble, 1996; Coble et al., 1998) has been shown to be the DOM produced during bacterial production or algal growth and grazing (Cammack et al., 2004; Elliott et al., 2006; Urban-Rich et al., 2006; Baker et al., 2007). The humic-like fluorescence indicated by peaks A, C and M (Coble, 1996; Coble et al., 1998) has been shown to be derived from soils or sediments (allochthonous) as well as from algal or microbial sources (autochthonous) of organic matter (Senesi et al., 1989; Corvasce et al., 2006). Other fluorescence indices such as the fluorescence index (FI, McKnight et al., 2001), freshness index (β:α, Parlanti et al., 2000; Wilson and Xenopoulos, 2008; Fellman et al., 2010) and humification index (HIX, Ohno, 2002; Zsolnay, 2003) provide qualitative information about the source, degree of microbial processing and extent of humification of DOM, respectively. Most studies, including those mentioned above, describe the optical properties of DOM measured in ambient or unpreserved samples (Spencer et al., 2007) although various sample preservation techniques are commonly used for the field samples such as acidification with nitric acid for measurements of major and trace elements (Sankar et al., 2014; Vega et al., 2017), or acidification with hydrochloric acid for measurement of dissolved organic carbon and nitrogen (Mladenov et al., 2010, 2015; Kulkarni et al., 2017, 2018b; Vega et al., 2017).

Previous studies have investigated the effects of pH on optical properties of isolated as well as bulk DOM samples. Chen and Kenny (2007) measured the fluorescence of isolated humic substances at pH (adjusted with HCl) ranging from 2 to 11 at $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and found that the intensity of humic-like fluorescence components increased with the pH while protein-like fluorescence components showed no sensitivity towards pH. It was concluded that the tryptophan in the humic DOM samples was shielded from the bulk aqueous environment by its association with other moieties in the mixture (Chen and Kenny, 2007). In another study (Patel-Sorrentino et al., 2002), the fluorescence of bulk freshwater samples from Amazonian rivers was measured at pH ranging from 2 to 12. The results of this study also suggested that the intensity of humiclike fluorescence increased with the increase in pH, due to the ionization of the fluorescent molecules resulting into alteration of the molecular orbit of the excitable electrons. Spencer et al. (2007) reported a significant increase in the absorbance at $3 4 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , intensities of humic-like peak A and protein-like peak B with an increase in pH from 2 to 10 in bulk freshwater samples, which was probably due to changes in the stabilization of excited states (protonation) of electron donating or accepting functional groups in the DOM.” Several other studies summarized in Coble et al. (2014) have reported varying responses of DOM fluorescence to pH change, but no clear rule could be established as the fluorescence originates from a variety of compounds.

Recently the optical properties of DOM were investigated in arsenic (As)-contaminated groundwater in the Bengal Basin (Mladenov et al., 2010; Kulkarni et al., 2017; Vega et al., 2017; Schittich et al., 2018). It was shown that groundwater DOM plays an important role in the mobilization of As (Mladenov et al., 2015; Kulkarni et al., 2018a) in the reducing groundwater, and the optical properties of DOM have provided significant insights in understanding the biogeochemical cycling of As. According to the previous studies, the DOM in this geological setting is mainly of sedimentary origin (McArthur et al., 2004; Sengupta et al., 2008; Datta et al., 2011) and is highly humic-like (Kulkarni et al., 2017; Vega et al., 2017). To our knowledge, no studies have investigated the influence of pH on the optical properties of DOM in As contaminated reducing groundwaters of the Bengal Basin.

In addition, comparisons between the effects of different acids on DOM optical properties are lacking. For example, unlike hydrochloric acid (HCl), the oxygen in the nitric acid $\mathrm { ( H N O _ { 3 } ) }$ may react differently to the DOM molecules. It has been demonstrated that the reaction between the $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ and Suwanee River Fulvic Acid (SRFA) resulted in nitration (introducing nitro group into an organic molecule) and nitrosation (such as oxidation of hydroxylamine) reactions (Thorn and Cox, 2016). In an earlier paper (Kulkarni et al., 2018b), we described the hydrogeology, groundwater chemistry and the DOM characteristics measured at ambient pH, at a study site in West Bengal (India). These samples represented shallow (high As, Holocene age sediments) and deep (low As, Pleistocene age sediments) groundwater as well as the surface waters from ponds and streams included in the study area. In this present study, the acidified (pH \~2, using HCl or $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ ) groundwater and surface water sub-samples from the same study site as Kulkarni et al. (2018b), were used to investigate the influence of acidification on the absorbance and fluorescence properties of DOM.

# 2. Materials and methods

The detailed description of the study sites and samples is described elsewhere (Kulkarni et al., 2018b). Briefly, shallow $\scriptstyle ( < 4 0 \mathrm { m } )$ groundwater samples (total ${ \mathsf { A } } s > 1 0 { \mathsf { \mu g / L } } )$ were collected from the tube-wells in the villages of Chakudanga $( 2 3 ^ { \circ } 4 ^ { \prime } 1 8 . 4 7 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N }$ , $8 8 ^ { \circ } 3 6 ^ { \prime } 3 4 . 9 6 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E }$ , ${ \sim } 1 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ above sea level, $n = 1 0$ ) and Shahispur $( 2 3 ^ { \circ } 4 ^ { \prime } 1 8 . 4 7 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N }$ , $8 8 ^ { \circ } 3 6 ^ { \prime } 3 4 . 9 6 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E }$ $\mathrm { \sim } 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ above sea level, $\mathrm { n } = 1 0$ ) in Nadia district, West Bengal (India) during May – June 2015. Deep $( 4 0 - 1 5 0 \mathrm { ~ m } )$ ) groundwater samples (total ${ \mathsf { A } } s < 1 0 { \mu \mathrm { g / L } } )$ were collected from Radhanagar $( 2 3 ^ { \circ } 3 ^ { \prime } 1 8 . 2 2 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N } ,$ $8 8 ^ { \circ } 2 0 ^ { \prime } 4 1 . 1 0 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E }$ , $n = 5$ ) and Bele $( 2 3 ^ { \circ } 3 ^ { \prime } 4 0 . 5 5 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N }$ , $8 8 ^ { \circ } 2 1 ^ { \prime } 7 . 8 1 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { E }$ , $\mathrm { n } = 5$ in Hooghly district, West Bengal (India). The surface water samples were also collected from ponds and streams in above mentioned locations $n = 1 0$ ). All the samples were filtered through pre-combusted (4 h, $4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } $ ) and pre-rinsed (with $3 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m L } }$ of sample water) $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ glass fiber filters (GFF) using a handheld vacuum pump in the field. For each sample set, three sub-samples were prepared from the filtrate as unacidified, 2) three were acidified with $0 . 2 \%$ v/v reagent grade HCl to pH \~2, and 3) three were acidified with $0 . 2 \% \mathrm { v / v }$ reagent grade $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ to pH \~2. Visual inspection during experiments indicated that no humic acid precipitation occurred in any of the samples. All the samples were stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (except for the duration of overseas transport, \~ 1 week) and in the dark until absorbance and fluorescence analyses.

Spectral properties of the bulk DOM in unacidified and acidified samples was done by Horiba Aqualog benchtop fluorometer (Dept. of CCEE, SDSU). Instrument settings and methods for the calculation of spectral properties have been described in detail in Kulkarni et al. (2017). In brief, absorbance and fluorescence data were acquired with excitation wavelengths ranging from $2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (at an increment of $3 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ) and excitation wavelengths ranging from $3 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ to $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (at an instrument default increment of $3 . 2 8 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ). Corrections for inner filter effects and Rayleigh scattering were applied to the raw fluorescence spectra, and the spectra were normalized to the water Raman area using $1 8 . 3 ~ \mathrm { M } \Omega \cdot \mathrm { c m }$ Milli-Q ultrapure water. Further, a four-component PARAFAC model was validated for unacidified and acidified groundwater samples $n = 1 1 7$ ) with split-half analysis and random initialization techniques (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Several absorbance $( \mathsf { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 } , \mathsf { S } _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ ), fluorescence parameters (FI, $\beta { : } \alpha ,$ HIX, humiclike peaks A, C, M, and protein-like peaks B and T) and intensities of four PARAFAC components were computed and statistically compared. A paired two-tailed t-test was used to evaluate the statistically significant changes in spectral properties between unacidified and HCl acidified, and between unacidified and $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ acidified samples.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Optical properties of DOM at ambient pH

The optical properties of DOM in our samples measured at ambient pH and associated water chemistry have been discussed in detail in Kulkarni et al. (2018b) and therefore only briefly described here (Table 1). The intensity of $\mathtt { A b } s _ { 2 5 4 }$ in shallow groundwater $( 0 . 0 5 1 \pm$ 0.0120 a.u.) was significantly higher $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ than in deep groundwater $( 0 . 0 2 7 \pm 0 . 0 1 5 )$ ). The ratio of $\beta { : } \alpha$ in shallow groundwater $( 0 . 7 7 \pm$ 0.04) was significantly $( p < 0 . 0 5 ) $ lower than that in deep groundwater

$0 . 9 8 \pm 0 . 0 9$ a.u.). Similarly, the DOM in shallow groundwater was significantly $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ more humified (HIX $5 . 8 \pm 2 . 4$ ) than DOM found in deep groundwater (HIX $1 . 3 \pm 0 . 6$ ). Surface water DOM showed significantly $\left( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ higher $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ intensity $( 0 . 1 5 4 \pm 0 . 0 6$ a.u.), higher $\beta { : } \alpha$ $( 0 . 8 1 \pm 0 . 1 ) $ and higher HIX $( 6 . 6 \pm 2 . 3 ) $ values than those of groundwater DOM.

# 3.2. Absorbance

The acidification of samples using $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ increased the $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ intensities by 8-fold in shallow groundwater from $0 . 0 5 1 \pm 0 . 0 1$ to $0 . 4 2 3 \pm$ 0.1, by 10-fold in deep groundwater from $0 . 0 2 7 \pm 0 . 0 1 5$ to $0 . 2 8 2 \pm$ 0.04 and by 3-fold in surface water from $0 . 1 5 4 \pm 0 . 0 6$ to $0 . 4 5 2 \pm 0 . 1$ (Table 1, Fig. 2a). The increase in $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ (Table 1) in the samples acidified with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ was independent of the DOC concentrations in the samples. In other words, relationships between Abs254 and DOC concentration were not significant in HNO3-acidified samples. By contrast, upon acidification with HCl, the $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ intensities did not increase substantially in the shallow or deep groundwater samples or in the surface water samples (Table 1, Fig. 2). Nitric acid, which has its peak absorbance near $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m } ,$ , is known to absorb in the UV range more than the hydrochloric acid due to the light absorbing properties of nitrate (Jones and Thorn, 1949). Therefore, the increase in $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ intensities was observed only in samples acidified with nitric acid and not in those acidified with hydrochloric acid.

# 3.3. Humic-like fluorescence

The intensities of humic-like peaks A, C and M significantly $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ decreased by ${ > } 4 5 \%$ in both shallow and deep groundwater samples upon HCl acidification (Table 1). Similarly, when acidified with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ the intensities of humic-like peaks A, C and M decreased both in shallow groundwater and deep groundwater. Interestingly, the surface water samples did not show any changes in humic-like fluorescence peaks upon acidification with either HCl or $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ (Table 1). The only statistically significant $\scriptstyle { \overbrace { p } } < 0 . 0 5 { \overrightarrow { \mathrm { \Lambda } } }$ ) decreases occurred in the HIX upon acidification with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ and HCl ( $3 5 \%$ and $4 4 \%$ decrease, respectively).

It should be noted that shallow groundwater samples had low DOC $( < 2 \mathrm { m g / L } )$ , whereas surface water samples had high DOC $\left( 3 { - } 1 1 \ \mathrm { m g / L } \right)$ ). It appears that the higher DOC of the bulk surface waters provided a shielding effect to humic-like fluorophores from the effects of acidification. This shielding effect has been previously attributed to the association of DOM with other moieties in surface water (Chen and Kenny, 2007; Spencer et al., 2007). The decrease in the fluorescence of humic substances upon acidification with HCl occurs due to aggregation losses and changes in the protonation states of amines, hydroxyls and carboxylic functional groups (Mobed et al., 1996; Avena et al., 1999; Chen et al., 2003). These losses and changes in protonation states would act to keep the molecules more tightly coiled and decrease their ability to emit light as fluorescence. The shielding effect imposed by higher concentrations or DOC has been speculated to be due to the association of DOM with other moieties in surface water, which prevent coiling of molecules and subsequent reduction in fluorescence (Chen and Kenny, 2007; Spencer et al., 2007). Compared to lower DOC rivers (1.2 to $6 \mathrm { m g / L }$ in the UK (Baker et al., 2007; Spencer et al., 2007) and in the Amazon (Patel-Sorrentino et al., 2002) where pH perturbation did influence DOM properties, the much higher DOC concentrations (3 to $1 1 \mathrm { \ m g / L }$ ) in the surface waters in the study area (Sengupta et al., 2008) may therefore be responsible for the lack of response to acidification.

Acidification also resulted in some changes in the four PARAFAC components identified by our PARAFAC model for samples (Fig. 2). The spectra of the components validated in our PARAFAC model were compared with the PARAFAC database (OpenFluor) with $5 9 5 \%$ confidence. Component C1 (ex240/em408) was identified as terrestrial humic-like, C2 (ex255/em472) as humic-like impacted by agriculture or wastewater and C4 (ex240/em328) as protein-like. The intensities of humic-like components C1 and C2 in shallow groundwater decreased upon acidification, with substantial decreases for both HCl ( $4 1 \%$ for C1 and $5 2 \%$ for C2) and $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ $9 3 \%$ for C1 and $6 2 \%$ for C2; Fig. 1). By contrast, no change was seen in C1 and C2 for deep groundwater samples, where the DOM is also far less humic-like.

Whereas components C1, C2 and C4 have been previously reported in groundwaters collected from the same study site (Kulkarni et al.,

2018b) and were similar to those found by other studies in Murshidabad district of West Bengal (Kulkarni et al., 2017; Vega et al., 2017), component C3 $\mathrm { ( e x 2 5 0 / e m 4 0 8 }$ ) was not observed in any of these previous studies, conducted at ambient pH, in this geographical area. In our current study, the intensities of component C3 were negligible in samples at ambient pH and in samples acidified with HCl but increased 10-fold (Fig. 2, Table 1) in samples acidified with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ . Although components with spectra similar to C3 have been previously reported and attributed to humic DOM from the natural environment at ambient pH (Amaral et al., 2016; Yamashita et al., 2010; Yamashita et al., 2011; Catalá et al., 2015), C3 in our study is not likely to originate from the natural environment. Instead, the lack of C3 in unacidified samples and samples acidified with HCl suggests that C3 is produced as a result of acidification with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ . We noted that component C3 is very similar to component C1 except for its excitation maxima. The addition of $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ may have shifted the primary excitation maximum of C1 from $2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ to $2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and may have resulted in the new PARAFAC component C3 (Fig. 1). Shifts in emission wavelength up to $1 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ have been observed previously (Chen and Kenny, 2007) for a free tryptophan fluorescence peak when the pH was adjusted to \~ 2 using HCl. However, no such reports are available for humic-like peaks and for shifts in excitation maximum upon acidification with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ . Given that molecules are excited at the wavelength at which they absorb light, one possible explanation is that the shift in excitation peak to $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ may be due to the absorption by $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ , which occurs at $\sim 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Jones and Thorn, 1949). Another potential explanation is that the reaction of DOM with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ may have resulted in formation of organo nitrogen compounds via aromatic nitration and nitrosation (oxidation of hydroxylamine) reactions, as demonstrated by Thorn and Cox (2016). Such newly formed nitro-organic molecules may also cause different fluorescence signatures than the original organic molecule and may have resulted in appearance of component C3. Nitric acid is known to oxidize DOM (Glaser et al., 1998). Dittmar (2008) showed that upon oxidation of IHSS (International Humic Substances Society) Suwanee River Humic Acid (2S101H) by nitric acid, the polyaromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), which are not substituted by oxygen, could be oxidized to benzenepolycarboxylic acids (BPCAs), which absorb strongly at $2 4 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . While in our study, although specific compounds such as BPCA were not measured, increases in $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ by up to 8-fold as well as the appearance of PARAFAC component C3 $( { \mathrm { e x } } 2 5 0 / { \mathrm { e m } } 4 0 8$ ) only in HNO3 acidified samples may be related to these oxidation products of PAHs in DOM in the original solution. Future experimental and analytical studies would be needed to ascertain these possibilities.

The results also showed that the humification index (HIX) of the shallow and deep groundwater samples increased when acidified with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ (by $5 0 \%$ and $7 6 \%$ , in shallow and deep groundwaters, respectively) but did not change when the samples were acidified with HCl (Table 1). Given that the HIX is also calculated at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ , the increase in HIX of groundwater samples upon acidification with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ also may be attributed to the absorbing properties of $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ rather than the compositional changes in the DOM molecules. In surface water samples, however, the HIX decreased upon acidification by both $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ and HCl. This suggests that the response of HIX is contrasting for water samples with different DOC concentrations and compositions. The HIX is essentially the ratio of more humified to less humified moieties. Therefore, the decrease in HIX in surface waters may indicate the degradation of more humified DOM upon acidification.

The results of this study demonstrated that changes in the optical properties of DOM were different for surface and groundwater as well as for type of acid used for pH perturbation. The most pronounced changes due to acidification were the substantial decrease in humic- and protein-like fluorescence intensities upon acidification with either HCl or $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ , and the increase in $\mathtt { A b s } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and HIX upon acidification with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ only. The results in this study are generally consistent with our understanding of differences in the source, degree of processing of DOM, and DOC concentrations in surface and groundwater in the Bengal basin. Since many of the optical indices significantly changed upon acidification, the above results have wide implications for the use of sample preservation techniques in field studies involving optical characterization of DOM.

# 3.4. Protein-like fluorescence

Acidification with both HCl and $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ had the most notable effects on Peaks B and T, significantly $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ decreasing the fluorescence intensities of those protein-like peaks by $> 7 0 \%$ in shallow and deep groundwater. The protein-like PARAFAC component C4, which is similar in position to Peaks B and T, also decreased by $9 9 \%$ in groundwater samples acidified with $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ and by $7 9 \%$ in groundwater samples acidified with HCl (Table 1). By contrast, the intensities of protein-like peaks did not change in surface water samples upon acidification either by $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ or by HCl.

The quenching of the intensities of the tyrosine-like peak B, tryptophan-like peak T and the PARAFAC component C4 in groundwater samples upon acidification have been previously reported by Baker et al. (2007). Our results are consistent with these findings. Proteinlike fluorescence quenching may be attributed to pH-related changes, such as aggregation, increased exposure to the solution phase and folding-unfolding reactions of protein molecules (Lakowicz, 1999). In surface waters however, our study showed that protein-like fluorescence was not quenched by either of the acids. This suggests that the molecules producing protein-like fluorescence in surface water may be more complex structures (and therefore more resistant to structural changes induced by acidification) than in groundwater.
