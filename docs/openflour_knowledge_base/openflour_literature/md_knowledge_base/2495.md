# Dityrosine formation via reactive oxygen consumption yields increasingly recalcitrant humic-like fluorescent organic matter in the ocean

# Abstract

Marine dissolved organic matter (DOM) is a massive elemental pool on Earth and is thought to consist of a chemically complex mixture of molecules. Part of marine DOM is fluorescent (FDOM) and includes humic-like compounds. The chemical composition of, and biochemical pathways that yield, autochthonous humic-like FDOM in the ocean is largely unknown. Inspired by medical and biochemical research detailing the formation of colored and fluorescent dityrosine via peroxidase mediated reactions, we used fluorometry and spectroscopy and found dityrosine exhibits humic-like fluorescence similar to that of marine FDOM. Investigating its recalcitrance, we conducted short-term nutrient-addition and light exposure experiments with dityrosine and found it is resistant to respiration or assimilation by marine microbial communities, yet readily photodegraded. The formation and characteristics of dityrosine newly points to polymerization via reactive oxygen quenching, under aphotic and putatively photic conditions, as an explanation for autochthonous, humic-like, (semi-)recalcitrant DOM in the ocean.

Marine dissolved organic matter (DOM) is a significant elemental pool on Earth, at $6 6 5 \mathrm { ~ P g ~ C } ,$ , equating roughly to $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ in Earth’s atmosphere (Hansell 2013). Correspondingly, marine DOM contains about $1 9 1 \mathrm { P g } \mathrm { N }$ and exists as a major store of $_ \mathrm { N }$ in the ocean, cycled less rapidly than nitrate or ammonium (Letscher et al. 2015). The long-term cycling of these key elements through DOM exerts a key control on climate and ocean productivity (Jiao et al. 2014). The recalcitrant portion of DOM accumulates in the ocean on long time scales $( 1 0 0 { - } 1 0 0 0 ^ { \prime } s$ of years) (Hansell 2013). Reasons for the existence of recalcitrant DOM range from low concentrations of individual DOM molecules to lack of necessary nutrients or enzymes for DOM use, and DOM structural complexity (Jiao et al. 2018 and references therein).

The chemical composition of the DOM pool remains challenging to fully characterize. However, part of it consists of fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM) which can be optically classified based on fluorescence spectroscopy. Among the classifiable groups (peaks) are humic-like compounds, which are often present in estuarine as well as coastal and open ocean waters (Coble 2007; Stedmon and Nelson 2015; Table S1). Their fluorescent properties indicate structural complexity (Sharpless and Blough 2014).

Humic-like FDOM is of broad interest to oceanographers and limnologists, as the compounds impact growth of plankton and biochemical conditions—e.g., humic-like FDOM can alter light quantity and quality available to phototrophs (Nelson and Siegel 2013), reduce deleterious UV exposure (Zepp et al. 2007), and influence availability of trace metals via chelation (Bundy et al. 2015). Terrestrial run-off has been considered a primary source of humic-like FDOM to marine waters (Coble 2007); however, there is mounting evidence that autochthonous production occurs via phytoplankton and bacterioplankton metabolism (Romera-Castillo et al. 2011; Goto et al. 2017; Kinsey et al. 2018). Humic-like FDOM is also of interest as some studies point out its recalcitrance— potentially contributing to organic carbon that is stored in the deep ocean for centuries to millennia (Jiao et al. 2018). On the contrary, other studies suggest that humic-like FDOM from bacterioplankton is much more labile—being produced and then re-used or modified over days in the course of culture growth experiments (Goto et al. 2017). In nature, both labile and recalcitrant humic-like FDOM potentially exists but presently there are few well-characterized model examples of either.

The chemical composition of humic-like FDOM and (bio) chemical synthesis pathways largely remain elusive. Medical and biochemical research literature describes the formation of colored or fluorescent compounds following cellular exposure to reactive oxygen species (ROS), or solar-simulated irradiance (Galeazzi et al. 1990; Malencik and Anderson 2003; Berto et al. 2016). Galeazzi et al. (1990) specifically detailed formation of dityrosine, which exhibits a brown-yellow color, similar to that of humic material (e.g., gelbstoff), via in vitro reactions with tyrosine, hydrogen peroxide $\left( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \right)$ , and horse radish peroxidase (POX). Additional studies describe tyrosine dimerization by diverse peroxidases, but also general amino acid crosslinking via oxidoreductases, in the presence of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ or $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ (Malencik & Anderson 2003; Heck 2013). Given that most of the ocean is $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ -rich and ROS is readily produced via photochemistry as well as cell metabolism/activity, e.g., respiration, photosynthesis, allelopathy, and nutrient scavenging (e.g., amine oxidase; Palenik and Morel 1991; Lesser 2006; Diaz and Plummer 2018; Hansel et al. 2019), there is high potential for oceanic dityrosine formation. Dityrosine is also central to the formation of polymeric organics in animals and synthetic chemistry that are resistant to degradation (Partlow et al. 2016); thus, dityrosine formed in the ocean may become a component of recalcitrant marine DOM.

While photochemical formation of dityrosine is documented (Berto et al. 2016), it is unclear if humic-like FDOM is indeed generated via the tyrosine, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ,$ and POX reaction. Further, the biological and photolytic recalcitrance of dityrosine at environmentally relevant concentrations is unknown. Here, we tested two hypotheses: (1) in vitro reactions of tyrosine $+ \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } + \mathrm { P O X }$ generate humic-like fluorescence matching that of dityrosine, and (2) dityrosine is more recalcitrant than tyrosine with respect to microbial use and photolytic degradation.

# Methods

# In vitro generation of dityrosine

In vitro dityrosine synthesis was performed in $4 \mathrm { m L }$ of $2 . 7 9 \mathrm { m m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ DL-tyrosine (Tyr) (Sigma; ${ \geq } 9 8 \%$ purity) solution in Milli-Q water. Dropwise additions of hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide were added to solubilize tyrosine and then increase the $\mathrm { p H }$ of the solution to ${ \sim } 7$ . $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ (Fisher Scientific; $3 8 \%$ ) at $0 . 0 0 3 \%$ final concentration and type IV horseradish peroxidase (POX; Sigma P8375) at final concentration of 1.2 enzyme units (U) $\mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ were added to the tyrosine solution and incubated in the dark at room temperature for ${ \sim } 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . Control reactions were incubated in parallel, including Tyr only, $\mathrm { T y r + P O X } _ { \mathrm { \Omega } }$ , and $\mathrm { T y r } + \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ .

# Seawater nutrient amendment incubations

Local surface seawater for nutrient amendment experiments was collected from the end of the Oceanana Fishing Pier (off Atlantic Beach, North Carolina) and Pamlico Sound Sta. #5 (35.1225 N, 76.2006 W), in collaboration with the UNC-IMS ModMon program (Paerl et al. 2019), on 01 July 2019 and 18 July 2019, respectively. Oceanana pier water was collected via cleaned plastic bucket and Pamlico Sound water by diaphragm pump. Approximately $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ L ~ } }$ of water was collected in an acid washed $1 0 \%$ HCl) polycarbonate bottle and transported in the dark in a cooler at room temperature to the NC State campus. Seawater was filtered through $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ Nitex mesh (removing large particles and grazers) and dispensed into triplicate acid washed $1 0 \%$ HCl) polycarbonate $1 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ bottles.

All bottles were amended with $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } N O _ { 3 } }$ $( 8 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) and ${ \mathrm { K H } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { P O } } _ { 4 }$ $( 1 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ in order to alleviate nitrogen or phosphorous limitation, and not add excessive concentrations based on historic Pamlico Sound and coastal N. Atlantic nutrient data (Paerl and Fogel 1994; Paerl et al. 2018). Treatment bottles were amended with $6 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of carbon (C) in the form of either DL-tyrosine, dityrosine dihydrochloride (AK Scientific, $9 6 \%$ purity), or D- $( + )$ -glucose (Alfa Aesar, $9 9 \%$ purity), while negative control bottles received no C addition. Afterwards, respiration and bacterial production assays were setup using C amended water or negative controls.

# Respiration and bacterial production measurements

For respiration assays, cleaned glass vials affixed with optical oxygen sensor spots (Pyroscience) were filled with experimental water (treatments, controls) and sealed tightly with Viton septum-lined caps. Vials were incubated in the dark at ambient temperature $( 2 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ . Oxygen measurements were made using a FireSting oxygen meter and vial adaptor after 4 and $2 4 \mathrm { { h } }$ . Using the change in oxygen concentrations, an assumed respiratory quotient of 1, and incubation time, an average respiration rate was calculated for treatments and controls.

Bacterial production rates were determined using the microcentrifuge assay method (Smith and Azam 1992). In brief, after $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of incubation $2 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark, as above) aliquots of treatment and control water were amended with $2 0 \mathrm { n m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of 3H-leucine (ARC). Isotope incorporation was halted after $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ by addition of cold $1 0 0 \%$ trichloroacetic acid. Replicates were setup as described previously (Paerl et al. 2018), with triplicate isotope microcentrifuge tube incubations per bottle alongside one kill (water plus cold $1 0 0 \%$ trichloroacetic acid prior to isotope addition). Isotope incorporation was determined by addition of liquid scintillation cocktail (Ultma Gold; Perkin Elmer) and counting using a Tri-Carb TR4910 TR (Perkin Elmer). Using the specific activity of 3H-Leu $\mathrm { ( 1 0 0 ~ C i ~ m m o l ^ { - 1 } }$ Leu) and the conversion factor of $3 . 1 \mathrm { k g } \mathrm { ~ C ~ } \mathrm { m o l } ^ { - 1 }$ Leu (Simon and Azam 1989), radioisotope activity was converted to units of C production. Paired two-way $t$ -tests comparing control and treatment values were performed in Excel.

# Solar-simulated light exposures

Dityrosine and L-tyrosine was dissolved in synthetic ocean water (SOW) (Price et al. 1989) at a final concentration of $7 5 0 \mathrm { n m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ and the solutions were added using aseptic technique (in laminar flow hood) to pre-combusted quartz vials and capped with acid washed, Milli-Q-rinsed stoppers. Four quartz vials were filled with each solution type (eight total vials). Vials were evenly spaced within a light exposure chamber (Suntest $\mathrm { C P S + , }$ Atlas) equipped with a full spectrum (UV and visible light) bulb and exposed at $6 4 , 8 0 0 \mathrm { k J } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 }$ . Single vials of each solution type were removed from the chamber at 1, 3, and $^ { 1 8 \mathrm { h } }$ and stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Control vials were stored immediately at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ without solar-simulated light exposure. Approximately one week later, vials were brought to room temperature and the solutions were scanned for absorbance and fluorescence as described above.

# Optical analyses

DOM absorption was measured from 200 to $8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ on a Varian Cary 300 UV spectrophotometer against an ultrapure Milli-Q water blank. Samples were diluted such that optical density at $2 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ was $< 0 . 4$ and corrected for dilution postmeasurement. FDOM was measured on similarly diluted samples using a Cary Eclipse spectrofluorometer from 240 to $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ increments) in excitation mode and from 300 to $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (at $2 \mathrm { n m }$ increments) in emission mode. Variable voltages were used to optimize fluorescence emission peaks at a scan rate of $2 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ , with excitation and emission slit widths each set to $5 \mathrm { n m }$ . After application of instrument corrections for lamp intensity (excitation) and emission detector response, the water Raman signal was used to calibrate the fluorometer internally and then standardized to quinine sulfate units (QSU) (Lawaetz and Stedmon 2009). Limit of detection via this method is 0.01 QSU. As needed, final QSU values were rescaled based on sample dilution.

The resulting 43 corrected emission spectra were concatenated into excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) and processed using Matlab and custom scripts. EEMs were modeled with parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC). A twocomponent model was validated using evaluation of loadings and leverages for coherence with organic fluorophores, splithalf analysis (four splits), and random initialization, with convergence criteria set to $1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ (Stedmon and Bro 2008). Each component was compared to the OpenFluor database using a matching criteria of ${ > } 0 . 9 5$ similarity of excitation and emission spectra (Murphy et al. 2014).

# Results and discussion

# In vitro production of dityrosine and fluorescence properties of tyrosine and dityrosine solutions

In vitro reactions of tyrosine, POX, and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ in Milli-Q water yielded a yellow-brown solution following ${ \sim } 3 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ of incubation at room temperature (Fig. S1; Galeazzi et al. 1990). EEM fluorescence of the yellow-brown reaction product revealed high fluorescence in the regions of peaks A, B, and M (Fig. 1), which are attributed to humic-like fluorescence commonly observed in fresh and marine waters (Table S1; Coble 2007; Stedmon and Nelson 2015), as well as FDOM generated by plankton (Zhao et al. 2017; Kinsey et al. 2018). EEM fluorescence of tyrosine solution was spectrally consistent with peak B found widely in seawater (Coble 2007; Stedmon and Nelson 2015; Fig. 1A and Table S1). EEMs of dityrosine solution contained prominent fluorescence in the regions of peaks A and M (Fig. 1B and Table S1).

PARAFAC modeling of EEMs from these experiments produced two components (Fig. S2) that were then tested against the OpenFluor database for spectral similarity with other PARAFAC models. We found that C1 matched with tyrosine (Wünsch et al. 2015) and components from 12 PARAFAC models. C2 fluoresced in the region of peak A and M $\operatorname { ( E x / E m ) }$ , was spectrally similar to dityrosine (Fig. 1B), and matched with components from 33 PARAFAC models from widely diverse ecosystems. In particular, C2 matched a component from Pony Lake Antarctic, where DOM is thought to be entirely microbial in origin (Wünsch et al. 2017).

Strikingly, this simple tyrosine-dityrosine model system (Fig. 2), mediated by common enzyme activity (peroxidase) (Malencik and Anderson 2003), produces signals statistically verified in several published environmental studies. Our experiments involved free tyrosine/dityrosine; however, intramolecular crosslinks of tyrosine are also expected within peptides and proteins, possibly leading to charge transfer, as has been observed with addition of amino acid residues to free dityrosine (Kikugawa et al. 1991; Michon et al. 1997; Malencik and Anderson 2003). This optical complexity is impressive as it fills signals between peaks A, M, and C linked to humic-like fluorescence in EEM space, corresponding to the complex humic-like DOM fluorescence in the ocean (Stedmon and Nelson 2015).

# Biological recalcitrance of dityrosine

Additions of dityrosine $( 6 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { C }$ final concentration) to natural estuarine (Pamlico Sound) and marine (coastal N. Atlantic Ocean) microbial communities led to no significant change in respiration or bacterial production rates, relative to no amendment controls in our nutrient amendment experiments (Fig. 3), indicative of short-term biological recalcitrance. In contrast, additions of tyrosine or glucose at equimolar C concentrations stimulated rates of respiration or bacterial production, in some cases ${ \sim } 4 \times$ higher than the mean rate of controls (Fig. 4D).

The inability of bacterioplankton to use dityrosine as a C source suggests it is structurally too complex for efficient uptake or incorporation into peptides by bacterioplankton at micromolar concentrations $( \sim 3 . 3 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ dityrosine). Concentrations of dityrosine in the ocean are unknown but tyrosine concentrations are in the nanomolar range and exist along with most amino acids in the high molecular mass fraction of deep ocean organic matter as combined or associated with other molecules (Yamashita and Tanoue 2004). While concentration-dependent use of dityrosine could factor into its utility (Arrieta et al. 2015), the micromolar concentrations in this study likely are a high-end concentration for most free-living microplankton in the ocean, yet dityrosine is clearly a sub-optimal fuel for metabolism (Fig. 3). At the core of dityrosine is a biphenyl bond linking two tyrosine molecules, which is chemically quite stable and enzymatically degraded by only select bacterial populations, e.g., isolates from hydrocarbon-rich environments (Pieper and Seeger 2008). Accordingly, biphenyl degradation pathways may not be commonly expressed by aerobic marine surface water communities. Thus, dimerization processes may be important in building structurally complex DOM found in the ocean.

# Photochemical lability of dityrosine

After $^ { 1 9 \mathrm { h } }$ of solar-simulated light exposure, dityrosine dissolved in seawater lost fluorescence, indicating rapid photodegradation causing scission of the biphenyl bond and liberating tyrosine. After $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of exposure, peaks A and M observed in time zero samples decreased five-fold, while peak B (tyrosine), became more prominent (Fig. 3A). Comparable solar-simulated light exposure of tyrosine seawater solutions also caused rapid change in fluorescence properties, specifically reduced peak B (tyrosine-like) fluorescence (Fig. 3A). Thus, while biologically recalcitrant on short $( 2 4 \mathrm { h } )$ times scales, dityrosine could be rapidly photolyzed back to tyrosine in the sunlit ocean and readily used by microbes (Fig. 4).

Dimerization of tyrosine was expected based on prior research (Malencik and Anderson 2003; Bianco et al. 2014; Berto et al. 2016). However, we did not observe production of humic-like fluorescent material, or dityrosine-like fluorescence signatures, when exposing nanomolar concentrations of tyrosine in seawater. While C1 fluorescence intensity decreased during the photoexposure (Fig. 4C), the dominant loss signal for dityrosine was C2 (Fig. 4D). Experimental design differences possibly explain the divergence in results relative to prior studies (Bianco et al. 2014; Berto et al. 2016). Compared to these studies, concentrations of tyrosine solutions were markedly lower in our experiments $7 5 0 \mathrm { n M }$ vs. $1 \mathrm { m M } )$ , and the exposure light intensity was slightly higher $( 6 4 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathrm { J } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 }$ vs. $7 . 3 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathrm { J } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } )$ . Arguably, the results presented here provide a more relevant perspective of dissolved tyrosine degradation in the upper euphotic ocean.

# Dityrosine and its formation in the ocean

Placing our results in an environmental context, the formation of dityrosine via ROS quenching pathways offers new insight on how labile organic carbon and nitrogen (e.g., tyrosine) can be transformed into more recalcitrant organic matter, including humic-like FDOM, in the ocean. First, abundant supply of tyrosine (required for dityrosine formation) occurs within the dissolved and particulate organic matter pools in the ocean (Yamashita and Tanoue 2004). Second, in situ dityrosine production via ROS-facilitated reactions readily occurs in the ocean via diverse marine bacterioplankton and algae which generate ROS, dismutases, and peroxidases (Diaz et al. 2013; Diaz and Plummer 2018; Wolfe-Simon et al. 2005; Hansel et al. 2019). Notably, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ generation occurs both in euphotic and aphotic marine waters (Yuan and Shiller 2004; Wolfe-Simon et al. 2005) emphasizing pathways separate from reported photo-generation (Bianco et al. 2014; Berto et al. 2016). Third, by characterizing the photolysis of dityrosine, we also pinpoint a mechanism in which labile DOM (tyrosine) can be recovered from more recalcitrant DOM (dityrosine)—specifically through upwelling (coastal and more diffuse thermohaline circulation driven [Munk and Wunsch 1998]) and subsequent exposure to solar irradiance in the surface ocean.

Though we did not directly measure dityrosine in seawater samples, we found its formula $\left( \mathrm { C _ { 1 8 } H _ { 2 0 } N _ { 2 } O _ { 6 } } \right)$ in recent metabolomic datasets (obtained by Fourier-transform ion cyclotron resonance [FT-ICR] mass spectroscopy) from deep ocean water and picocyanobacterial (Synechococcus) biomass (Stubbins and Dittmar 2015; Zhao et al. 2017). In the latter case, dityrosine was the second-most abundant formula recovered from Synechococcus biomass—suggesting that dityrosine may be formed in ubiquitous marine phytoplankton and transported to the aphotic ocean interior via aggregation, e.g., clumping or grazing (Richardson and Jackson 2007). Together the evidence indicates dityrosine is present in seawater and marine biomass (cyanobacteria).

Overall, our findings highlight polymerization as a means to generate increasingly recalcitrant DOM and humic-like FDOM in the ocean. Dityrosine and its formation serve as useful compound and reaction “models” for studying generation and cycling of humic-like FDOM and relatively recalcitrant DOM in the ocean. The linkage between dityrosine formation and ROS helps in part to explain why humic-like FDOM and biologically recalcitrant DOM is formed by marine microbes—to cope with, or as a consequence of, life in an oxic environment where ROS is a perpetual problem (Imlay 2019). More broadly, other tyrosine oligomers (e.g., trityrosine) and cross-linked low molecular weight peptides and proteins, capable of exhibiting charge transfer, may also form via reactions involving ROS. Clearly, the fluorescent signatures of dityrosine do not explain all marine FDOM (Coble 2007). However, future investigations into the formation of other oligomers and polymers through ROS-affiliated reactions, especially those containing aromatics and hydroxyl groups facilitating charge transfer (Sharpless and Blough 2014), may fill in more gaps in knowledge about the compounds within and origins of marine FDOM.
