The Agrio-Caviahue is a natural stream-lake system composed of the Upper Agrio River (UAR) -Lake CaviahueLower Agrio River (LAR). The system has a broad pH gradient from extremely acid in the hot spring to almost neutral pH only $6 0 \mathrm { k m }$ downstream the source, and varies as a function of the distance. The aim of this study was to analyze the dynamics of the dissolved organic matter (DOM) of this extreme system. For that matter, an absorbance and fluorescence-based characterization was performed on natural water samples and hydrophobic resin DOM extracts during different seasons in nine sampling stations. Between August and December, the hydrological connectivity is maximum due to precipitation and snowmelt, respectively. During these months, the stream that exits the lake governs the chemical characteristics downstream mainly in the period of high connectivity. In contrast, in the dry season when the flow of LAR is minimum, the two major affluents of this river influence its pH and DOM features, and deliver inputs of humified DOM from the wetlands where the tributaries are born. DOM was characterized by having low absorbance. The PARAFAC analysis of the fluorescent DOM (FDOM) validated three fluorescent components in the natural samples (with humic and nonhumic features), two of which were also recorded in the extracts, meaning that no additional hydrophobic components were missed under the limit of detection of the fluorometer. The unique features of AgrioCaviahue DOM resemble those found in DOM from Yellowstone hot springs, both acid and alkaline.

# 1. Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is a dynamic heterogeneous pool of compounds that are ubiquitous in natural waters, influencing the physical, chemical and biological properties of the aquatic environments [1–4]. Within DOM, the fraction that absorbs light in the ultraviolet (UV) and visible regions is known as chromophoric dissolved organic matter (CDOM) [5–7]. A portion of the CDOM is also fluorescent, and is named fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM) [8–10].

Both CDOM and FDOM can provide information about the source, the origin and the reactivity of DOM [7,11–14]. The main source of DOM in most aquatic environments are the allochthonous input, which is leached from terrestrial soil and vegetation [15,16], while the autochthonous DOM is produced by phytoplankton and other organisms such as macrophytes, bacteria among others within the aquatic ecosystem [17,18]. The allochthonous DOM fraction is considered to be more susceptible to photo-degradation because it is composed by relatively large molecules with high degree of conjugation that absorb in the UV region [7,19]. In contrast, autochthonous DOM consists of simpler molecules of low molecular weight and typically more susceptible to microbial degradation [10,20].

One natural source of extremely acid aquatic environments $\left( \mathrm { p H } < 4 \right)$ is volcanic springs [21]. These ecosystems are characterized by low pH concomitantly with high concentrations of a wide variety of elements, particularly sulfur, chloride, iron and aluminum [22–24]. In acid streams the precipitation of oxides can form abundant deposits that cover the streambed with red/orange coatings if the metal is Fe and white if the metal is Al [24,25]. The interaction between the stream water and the metals can significantly modify the concentration and composition of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) [25].

One particularly extreme environment is the Agrio-Caviahue catchment located in North Patagonia. This is a natural acidic streamlake system with low algal diversity [26,27], characterized by high metal concentrations, particularly iron [23]. Previous reports in Caviahue lake suggest a very high light attenuation of the ultraviolet radiation (UVR) mainly due to the presence of iron that absorbs around $3 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ [23].

The aim of the present study is to characterize and analyze the dynamics of DOM in the natural pH gradient of the Agrio River, in terms of quality and quantity, assessed through the variation in the

CDOM and FDOM fractions along the Agrio-Caviahue catchment. While the vast majority of the publications are focus on aquatic environments with a homogeneous pH and dissolved solids, in the present work we studied the transformation of FDOM along a natural pH gradient, from extremely acidic to alkaline and possible controlling factors such as the changes in the chemistry of the water at the different pH values.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Study area

The Agrio River and Lake Caviahue are part of a natural and extremely acid to neutral water system in Neuquén Province, Argentina $( 3 7 ^ { \circ } \ 5 2 ^ { \prime } \ 1 8 . 2 ^ { \prime \prime } \ 5 ;$ ： $7 1 ^ { \circ } 0 1 ^ { \prime } 2 4 . 0 ^ { \prime \prime }$ W). The Agrio River constitutes actually two different water courses, the Upper Agrio River (UAR) $1 2 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ long) and the Lower Agrio River (LAR) $( 2 0 0 \ \mathrm { k m \ l o n g } )$ ). The former is born as a hot-spring on the eastern slope of the Copahue Volcano caldera at $2 5 3 0 \mathrm { m }$ a.s.l. $( 3 7 ^ { \circ } 5 1 ^ { \prime } 2 0 . 4 ^ { \prime \prime }$ S; $7 1 ^ { \circ } \ 0 8 ^ { \prime } \ 5 0 . 1 ^ { \prime \prime } \ \mathsf { W }$ ), where Galdieria sulfuraria algal mats were observed growing on the rocks. It has an initial range of $\mathsf { p H }$ from 0.8 to 1.6, a temperature of 56 to $8 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ a conductivity of $3 6 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m S } ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ and has three major affluents, the Rojo, Blanco and Jara Rivers (see Fig. 1). It flows into Lake Caviahue with a pH of 1.9 to 2.9, with a conductivity of $2 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m S } ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ , $1 2 \mathrm { k m }$ downstream the hot spring. The LAR exits the lake at a $\mathsf { p H }$ of 3.2 to 3.4 $( 0 . 6 6 ~ \mathrm { m S } ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ ) and neutralizes progressively to a pH of 4.1 to 7.5 after receiving major affluents such as the rivers Trolope $\mathrm { k m } 2 9 .$ ) and Ñorquin (km 60; 37° 45′ $4 0 . 7 ^ { \prime \prime }$ S; $7 1 ^ { \circ } 3 9 ^ { \prime } 0 6 . 6 ^ { \prime \prime }$ W; Fig. 1) $4 0 \mathrm { k m }$ down the lake. The pH of LAR depends strongly on the season and the influence of the lake waters on its chemical characteristics. Lake Caviahue is located $1 6 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ a.s.l. in the Copahue–Caviahue Provincial Park (Fig. 1). The lake is a naturally–occurring glaciated water body that has a horseshoe shape with a maximum length of $9 . 7 \mathrm { k m }$ , maximum width of $4 . 7 \mathrm { k m }$ , coastline of $2 2 . 3 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ , total area of $9 . 2 ~ \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ and total volume of $0 . 4 7 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 3 }$ . The basins are referred to as North Arm and South Arm (Fig. 1). The lake pH has been rising since 1998 from 2.8 to 3.4, approximately, its conductivity is $0 . 7 3 ~ \mathrm { m S ~ c m } ^ { - 1 }$ and it has very high concentrations of nutrients and metals compared to other ultra-oligotrophic Andean Patagonian lakes, which makes it unique in South America [22,24,28].

Located on the west side of the lake is the town of Caviahue. It has approximately 900 permanent inhabitants, although the population varies throughout the year due to incoming tourists that visit the thermal bath complex and the ski resort located on the Copahue Volcano. The effluents from the wastewater treatment plant are discharged on the north side of the North Arm of the lake.

# 2.2. Sampling and field measurements

Sampling and flow measurements were performed in March 2015, December 2015 and August 2016. The sites chosen for the analysis were: the hot spring $\tan 0$ ) and then 8, 11, 15, 24, 29, 59 and $6 0 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ downstream, corresponding to the UAR (the first three sites); to Lake Caviahue (two sites at $\mathrm { k m } 1 5$ ), and to the LAT (last four sites) (Fig. 1). Flow measurements from UAR (inflow) and LAR (outflow) were performed using a flowmeter to determine water velocity, and river width and depths that were determined at each site. Flow was integrated to the section area of the river [22]. Samples were collected for two different assays in: 1) $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ caramel glass bottles (Natural Samples), and 2) $^ \mathrm { ~ 5 ~ L ~ }$ polyethylene containers (Extract Samples). All the material was rinsed with $1 0 \%$ NaOH, $1 0 \%$ HCl, MilliQ $. 1 8 \ \mathrm { M } \Omega )$ and five times with water from the sampling site. All reagents were of analytical grade. All containers were stored in the dark at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } C .$ . Locations were chosen to study the differences along the natural $\mathsf { p H }$ gradient (Fig. 1). The pH (Orion 920A) measurements, adjusted for temperature, were taken at each site. Sampling and field measurements were done in collaboration with Temporetti et al. [29].

In the field laboratory, samples were filtered through $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ nylon membranes (Millipore) within $^ { 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after sampling. The containers for the filtered samples were rinsed as stated before but the last step was made with filtered water from the site. Samples were stored in the dark at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ until further analysis.

# 2.3. Water analysis

Water pH of each sample was adjusted to 7.0 to precipitate excess of dissolved iron and aluminum ( $2 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of each metal at the hot spring) and iron colloids that pass the filters used to pretreat the samples. The presence of high concentrations of these metals interferes with DOM absorbance and fluorescence measurements. Disproportionate Rayleigh signal and iron absorption (in the UV region) masked the signals of DOM in both cases, fluorescence and absorbance [23].

DOC determination and optical characterization of DOM were performed on filtered natural water samples. DOC concentration was measured as non-purgeable organic carbon (NPOC) (Shimadzu TOC-L).

Absorbance spectra were obtained from 200 to $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals with a UV/Vis spectrophotometer (Hewlett-Packard 8453), using a quartz cuvette ( $1 0 \mathrm { m m }$ path length). The collected absorbance data was converted to absorption coefficients $\left( \mathsf { a } _ { \lambda } \right)$ following Helms et al. [7]. Different CDOM parameters were calculated: the absorption coefficient at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (a254) was used a proxy for CDOM [30], the spectral slope for the interval 275–295 nm (S275–295) was calculated by fitting the log-transformed spectral data to linear models and used as proxies of molecular weight/size. The slope ratio $( S _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ was calculated as the ratio of $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ to $\mathsf { S } _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ and used as a proxy of the relative importance of autochthonous DOM [7,14].

FDOM analysis of filtered water samples was performed in a PerkinElmer 55B spectrofluorometer equipped with a 150-W Xenon arc lamp and a Peltier temperature controller, using a $1 \ \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cell. The excitation-emission matrices (EEMs) of FDOM obtained by scanning the water samples in a spectrofluorometer Perkin Elmer LS55, were corrected for the inner filter effect and normalized to the area under the Raman peak of the blank (MilliQ water) at $3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ using the DrEEM toolbox [31]. The resulting data was expressed as Raman units.

Three fluorescent indexes were calculated to further characterize the FDOM fraction. The humification index (HIX) was computed as the sum of emission intensities between 435 and $4 8 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ divided by the sum of emission intensities between 300 and $3 4 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ excitation and used as a proxy of humic content [32]. The biological index (BIX) was calculated as the ratio of the emission intensity at $3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ divided by the maximum emission intensity between 420 and $4 3 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , at $3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ excitation and applied to detect recently produced dissolved organic compounds [33,34]. The fluorescence index (FI) was calculated as the emission ratio of 470 and $5 2 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ at excitation $3 7 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ [35], and applied to describe the relative contribution of terrestrial and microbial sources.

# 2.4. DOM extractions (extract samples)

DOM content along the watershed was low (below $0 . 5 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 } .$ ) and fluorescence intensity of the natural samples was also low. To be certain that no DOM component was missed below the limit of detection of the fluorescence analysis, DOM from the natural samples was concentrated from five liters of water from each site using a DAX–8 resin after Santos et al. [36] and Liu et al. [37]. The resin was rinsed extensively with $^ { 0 , 1 \mathrm { { M } } }$ NaOH and methanol before the concentration procedure and it was discarded after passing each sample. Briefly, water $\mathsf { p H }$ was adjusted at 2.0 with HCl and passed through a XAD–8 resin column with a flux of $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m L } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ , then it was rinsed with 10 bed volumes with MilliQ water at $\mathsf { p H } 2 . 0$ to remove the salts and finally eluted in the reverse direction with methanol with a flux of $0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m L } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . The solvent of extraction was evaporated in a rotatory evaporator at room temperature. For the analysis, the extract samples were re-dissolved in $2 \ \mathrm { m L }$ of methanol and a $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ aliquot was then diluted in $2 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of MilliQ water before recording the EEMs.

# 2.5. Data analysis

Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was performed on 28 EEMs of natural samples in order to identify underlying fluorophores present in DOM samples using the DrEEM toolbox [31]. Another independent PARAFAC analysis was performed in the nine extracts. In both cases, the Raman and Rayleigh scatter were removed from the analysis according to Stedmon and Bro [38]. An exploratory analysis using non-negative constraints was performed in order to identify outliers (leverage $< 0 . 5$ ). Model components were validated using split-half and the best model fit was obtained using 10 random initializations [31,38]. EEMs processing and the PARAFAC analysis were performed using the software MATLAB (MATLAB R 2014a). Each PARAFAC component was named based on the maximum of excitation and emission values. In addition, linear regression and principal component analysis (PCA) were performed on the standardized PARAFAC components, HIX, BIX, absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , spectral slope S275–295 and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ parameters.

A Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was applied to analyze the relationship between optical and fluorescent DOM quality parameters in the different sampling sites. Variables were centered and standardized for the analysis. The PCA was performed with the Rstudio 3.3.3 open software (Boston, USA) using the FactoMineR package.

# 4. Discussion

The Agrio-Caviahue watershed is an extreme environment because of its natural pH gradient, the very high concentrations of iron and aluminum and the simple character of its DOM. The system can be partitioned if three sections, the Upper Agrio River, the lake Caviahue and the Lower Agrio River. The most distinctive variable in the system is pH because it varies from extreme values such as 0.8 to 1.6 (registered during the last 20 years) at the Hot-spring to at least $7 . 5 ~ 4 0 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ downstream. Upstream Lake Caviahue, pH is mostly stable, reaching values higher than 3.0 only since 2015, whereas downstream the lake there is a dependence with the lakes level and the tributaries load. There, sensitive differences in pH were registered between seasons, and they occur because the flow of LAR in March is minimum and the inflow of the major tributaries (Trolope and Ñorquin among others; Fig. 1) is enough to dilute it and neutralize the acid pH at Lake Caviahue outflow [22–24]. Therefore, LAR pH and other chemical characteristics such as dissolved solids are primarily influenced by the river flow out of the lake. In other Andean Patagonia watersheds, the maximum hydrological connectivity is observed during the wet season [40]. It means that chemical variables such as pH, DOM concentration or the spectral indicators become more homogeneous than throughout the rest of the year. In Caviahue, the same pattern was observed during August and December, with maximum homogeneity of chemical variables at LAR. Albeit the reason in this particular environment is the higher flow of LAR with the extreme characteristics of the lake. That is why these characteristics were measured further down the river, in contrast with March.

With regard to DOM characteristics, the most relevant spectral indicators evaluated were CDOM and $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ . The former variable did not have a unique trend throughout the river, although it was consistently low during the warmer season. The reason why it was highest in August could be related to the higher input of LAR tributaries with DOM of terrestrial or wetland origin (see $\mathrm { k m } 6 0$ value in Fig. 3B).

Likewise, molecular size (MS) was maximum during the wet seasons indicating the presence of allochthonous DOM and contrasting with March [7]. At the Hot-spring the MS values were the lowest in March and they increased towards the lake. Because of the water temperatures that undergoes inside the volcano, if present, the DOM is expected to be simple in character and of low MS and weight. In addition, the high variability observed within the lake in March and December is probably due to the existence of a thermocline, which acts as a barrier preventing the mixing of top and bottom waters. In contrast, the water column temperature in winter was $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and therefore it was fully mixed, which was reflected by the low variation of CDOM. Absorbance-based parameters are highly influenced by Fe [11,23]. It is well known that this metal absorbs strongly in the UV range, around $3 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ [11]. However, absorption coefficients at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ were very low, reflecting the oligotrophic condition of the catchment and the efficient precipitation of the dissolved iron during sample preparation [24]. Overall, the DOM characterization absorbance and fluorescence-based highlight the idea of the hydrologic disconnection and the stronger effect of tributaries downstream the watershed. The hydrological connectivity in the Agrio-Caviahue watershed is ruled out by the strong influence of the Lake Caviahue.

The low BIX values $( < 1 )$ recorded in this study suggest low fresh DOM, as the low HIX values imply weak humification (Fig. 6A) [41]. In this sense, it was reported that an increase in pH can cause a red shift and at $\mathrm { p H } < 4 . 0$ , humification rates were low (at least for peat humic acids) [42,43]. However, the humic components $C _ { ( 2 4 0 ) 3 0 5 / 4 3 0 }$ raised towards $\mathrm { k m } \ 5 9$ indicating higher humic character of DOM in March and August. On the other hand, $C _ { 2 4 0 / 3 6 4 }$ had a greater preponderance at the lake and upstream thus indicating mainly autochthonous DOM or of volcanic origin as it was expected.

Fluorescence of organic matter has been frequently used for the past two decades and it is an important tool to characterize the origin and diagenetic state of the DOM [44]. This is the first study of FDOM in a watershed of volcanic origin with a natural pH gradient from extremely acidic to neutral. The means of analysis of FDOM was PARAFAC and it validated three components for the natural samples. Component $C _ { ( 2 4 0 ) 3 0 5 / 4 3 0 }$ matches with C1 Garcia et al. [45] using Openfluor database [46], whereas the second component $C _ { 2 4 0 / 3 6 4 }$ was difficult to classify solely as an amino acid like or humic-like fluorophore because it has similar characteristics of both groups. This component matched with a similar component that likely comprises compounds with low molecular weight and relatively high aromaticity [47,48]. Finally, component $C _ { ( 2 4 0 ) 2 7 0 / 3 3 9 }$ is a non-humic component that in this case seems to result as the combination of the non-humic peaks B and T (Table S1) [49,50]. On the other hand, the extract samples had an infrequent humic-like component $\textsf { C } _ { 2 4 0 / 3 8 8 }$ that resembles $( C _ { ( 2 4 0 ) 3 6 4 } )$ of the natural samples with a red shift in the emission. This component matched with two humic-like components in the bibliography [51,52]. In addition, the humic component $\mathsf { C } _ { ( 2 4 0 ) 3 1 5 / }$ $4 3 1$ matches with the C1 $( \mathsf { A } + \mathsf { M } )$ , usually found in neutral streams located in North Patagonia (Table S1) [45]. Small changes in the fluorescence pattern were expected because the resin may have differential affinity for the different organic components found in FDOM. Moreover, the non-humic component of the natural samples was not found in the extracts. That component is usually related to compounds of low molecular weight, aromatic and hydrophilic, and therefore not retained in the DAX–8 resin. Peak T (the protein-like component) is an example of such substances and at the pH of the isolation procedure should have net electrical charge.

The FDOM of this system is not complex, with a predominance of humic and non-humic components, similar to other ultra-oligotrophic water bodies studied in Patagonia [45,53]. For instance, Modenutti et al. [54] found FDOM with two components in a mountain stream originated in a glacier. In addition, two of the fluorophores found in this system are not well known, probably due to the lack of information about the fluorescent DOM of aquatic ecosystems associated to volcanoes.

The presence of FDOM in waters from hot springs was recently described by Gonsior et al. [55] in four hot springs (pH 3.5, 7.1, 7.3 and 7.9) from Yellowstone. They likewise found FDOM with unique signals and two of them had great resemblance with two of the components found in the Agrio River waters, the $C _ { 2 4 0 / 3 6 4 }$ from an alkaline spot and C (240)305/430 from the acid spring. The former was described, in our work, as an intermediate component and the latter as humic. The Copahue-Caviahue Volcanic Complex (CCVC) has a hydrothermal system which is fed mainly by meteoric water, although some indicators suggest the presence of magmatic waters as well [56]. Two plausible hypothesis for the presence of FDOM in the hot spring waters, or their combination, are: i) the presence of Galdieria sulphuraria and Cyanidium caldarium algal mats (Cabrera, personal observation) on the wall where the spring is located; there the water emerges through the mats; ii) Tassi et al. [57] found $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 } , \mathrm { C } _ { 3 }$ and $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ alkanes and ${ \mathrm { C } } _ { 6 } { \mathrm { H } } _ { 6 }$ and sulfur heterocycles in the volcanic fluids in Copahue Volcano fumaroles. They present this evidence to support the fact that there are both, abiogenic OM decomposition and thermogenic OM, respectively. Part of the abiotic OM could be incorporated by the meteoric water when it infiltrates through different soil strata into the hydrothermal aquifer or when the water vapor exits the system through the hot-springs (Augusto, personal communication) [57]. In both scenarios, the temperature of the fluids in the hydrothermal system is between $1 7 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ therefore DOM drastic transformations could be expected.

# 5. Conclusions

As a first approach in characterizing the fluorescent dissolved organic matter of the acid Agrio-Caviahue catchment, this extreme ecosystem displayed low fluorescence intensity. The FDOM is simple and has humic and non-humic components.

As a function of the distance from the hot spring, we found a natural variation in the optical and fluorescent properties of the natural DOM of the watershed. This variation had a dependence with the season and the flow of the river that exits Lake Caviahue (LAR).

In August and December, the hydrological connectivity is maximum due to precipitation and snowmelt respectively, although this watershed is greatly influenced by the flow of LAR, the stream that drags out and governs the chemical characteristics downstream Lake Caviahue.

In addition, we registered that in March, when the flow was lowest, Trolope and Ñorquin rivers notoriously influenced LAR CDOM and molecular size.

The fluctuations on some of the variables such as the humic component (C(240)305/430) or CDOM were most likely due to the difference in the flow of LAR.

The most variable season was the summer, or the dry season, when the Agrio-Caviahue catchment is hydrologically less connected and the tributaries deliver inputs of humified DOM from the wetlands where the tributaries are born. No effect could be attributed to the variations of pH, although Lake Caviahue has a disruptive role on the chemical river characteristics.

Future investigations on this remarkable extreme environment should increase the sampling frequency as well as the sampling of the tributaries, to better understand the natural variation and to evaluate the effect of climate on the DOM variation in this extremely acidic watershed.
