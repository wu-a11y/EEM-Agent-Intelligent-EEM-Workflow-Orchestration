# ABSTRACT

Priming effect (PE) is increasingly recognized as an important mechanism in the microbial degradation of dissolved organic matter (DOM) from freshwater to the ocean. However, potential PE during the mixing of DOM from different sources and the effects on different DOM constituents are still largely unknown. This study examined the PE after adding DOM from typical natural and anthropogenic sources (rainwater, fresh plant, leaf litter, and wastewater) into pre-aged river DOM, using dissolved organic carbon (DOC) measurement, absorption spectroscopy, and fluorescence excitation-emission matrices-parallel factor analysis (EEMs-PARAFAC). The plant-derived DOM had a low humic content and was dominated by benzoic acid-like and tyrosine-like fluorescent components (C4 and C5), which showed a high DOC bioavailability of $8 0 \%$ . DOC in rainwater and wastewater also had high bioavailabilities $( 4 5 \% - 5 0 \% )$ , while DOM in the leaf litter leachate showed high aromaticity, average molecular weight, and humic content but low DOC bioavailability $( 1 2 \% )$ . There was generally limited PE ( $- 5 \%$ of the initial values) on the degradation of DOC and chromophoric DOM (CDOM) for most samples. Two humic-like components (C2 and C3) showed little PE, while the humic-like C1 and C6, tyrosine-like C5, and tryptophan-like C7 showed variable PE after adding rainwater, wastewater, and plant leachate. Overall, the results revealed that the DOM from typical natural and anthropogenic sources had different composition and bioavailability, and their inputs to aquatic environments would result in variable PE on the bulk DOC and different DOM components.

# 1. Introduction

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) plays important roles in the biogeochemistry and ecosystem function of aquatic environments, such as carbon cycle, optical properties, primary and secondary productions, speciation and toxicity of heavy metals, and the formation of disinfection byproducts in drinking water treatments (Hood et al., 2009; Jiao et al., 2010; Hansell and Carlson, 2015). For example, the carbon bound within DOM (DOC) represents the largest pool of reduced carbon in aquatic environments, which is comparable to the atmospheric carbon pool and is important in the global carbon cycle (Hansell and Carlson, 2015). DOM is a complex mixture of thousands of organic compounds which are different in chemical composition and reactivity (Flerus et al., 2012; Catala et al., 2015). The biologically labile constituents (e.g., carbohydrates, amino acids, and low molecular weight organic acids) can be degraded by microbes, producing ${ \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and affecting the carbon exchange across the air-water interface (Battin et al., 2008; Raymond et al., 2013). In contrast, the stable constituents (e.g., lignin, humic substance, and black carbon) represent an important carbon pool that can contribute to the carbon storage, which can be in part produced through microbial transformation (i.e., microbial carbon pump, Jiao et al., 2010; Hansell, 2013). Therefore, it is important to study the microbial utilization and transformation of DOM for better understanding the biogeochemical processes and ecosystem function in the river, estuary, and ocean.

In the last decade, priming effect (PE) is increasingly recognized as a potentially important mechanism underlying the microbial degradation of DOM in aquatic environments, which refers to the changes in the degradation of stable DOM by the addition of labile DOM (Guenet et al., 2010; Bianchi, 2011). Positive PE occurs when the degradation of stable DOM is enhanced by the enzymes of the microbes degrading labile DOM and/or the energy released from the degradation of labile DOM (Hotchkiss et al., 2014; Bianchi et al., 2015; Ward et al., 2016, 2019; Morling et al., 2017; Begum et al., 2019). The PE may be a common process in a variety of water bodies from freshwater to the ocean, although some studies identify no significant signals of PE (Catalan et al., 2015 ; Dorado-García et al., 2016; Hofmann and Griebler, 2018; Textor et al., 2019). There may be even negative PE if microbes preferentially utilized the added labile DOM and the degradation of stable DOM is inhibited (Hotchkiss et al., 2014; Steen et al., 2016). There is no consensus on the role of PE in aquatic environments, which highlights the importance of testing the PE of DOM from different sources and the effect on different DOM components.

In particular, DOM in aquatic environments can be derived from multiple natural and anthropogenic sources such as rainwater, plant leaching, soil, wastewater, and algal production (Inamdar et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2015). The DOM from different sources may show a notable difference in the chemical composition and bioavailability, and their mixing may result in potential PE. For example, precipitation events can bring rainwater DOM and leachates of plant and leaf litter on the land surface, which may contain abundant labile constituents (Bao et al., 2018; Textor et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2019). Municipal wastewater is another important source of labile DOM constituents in polluted water bodies (Parr et al., 2015; Begum et al., 2019). The input of these DOM sources may exert PE on the degradation of stable constituents present in aquatic environments. Previous studies on the PE of labile DOM were mainly focused on simple compounds (glucose, sucrose, cellobiose, acetate, and amino acids) and algae exudates (Hotchkiss et al., 2014; Catalan et al., 2015 ; Ward et al., 2016; Morling et al., 2017; Textor et al., 2019), and a few studies tested the PE in the mixing of tributary and mainstem of rivers (Ward et al., 2019; Begum et al., 2019; Lambert and Perga, 2019). Potential PE of inputs from typical sources (rainwater, plant leachate, leaf litter leachate, and wastewater) on stable constituents in aquatic environments remains largely unclear. Furthermore, previous studies mainly applied measurements of ${ { 0 } _ { 2 } }$ , DOC, isotopic labeling, $\mathbf { C O } _ { 2 }$ , ammonium, microbial abundance and activity for studying PE (Catalan et al., 2015; Bianchi et al., 2015; Dorado-García et al., 2016; Textor et al., 2019; Ward et al., 2019). These techniques generally targeted on the consumption of bulk DOC and could not provide more detailed information for the response of different DOM components to the PE. Absorption and fluorescence spectroscopy are popular tools for studying the chromophoric and fluorescent components of DOM in aquatic environments (Helms et al., 2008; Huguet et al., 2009; Stedmon and Bro, 2008; Murphy et al., 2014). In particular, fluorescence excitation-emission matrices-parallel factor analysis (EEMs-PARAFAC) is powerful for identifying different fluorescent components and tracking their environmental behaviors (Ishii and Boyer, 2012; Gueguen et al., 2014  ; Osburn et al., 2015; Wünsch et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2017). However, few studies have applied these techniques in the PE study, which may provide insightful information for PE on different fluorescent components (Lambert and Perga, 2019; Begum et al., 2019).

Therefore, this study aimed to: (1) compare the chemical composition and bioavailability of DOM from different sources; and (2) examine the PE of DOM from typical sources (rainwater, plant, leaf litter, and wastewater) on the biodegradation of stable DOM in pre-aged river water. In particular, the PE will be tested for bulk DOC, CDOM, and fluorescence components identified using EEMsPARAFAC. The results would have implications for better understanding the dynamics of DOM and the carbon cycle in the riverestuary-ocean continuum.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Sample collection and PE experiments

Samples were collected from the subtropical Minjiang Watershed, southeast China, including rainwater, fresh plant, leaf litter, wastewater, and river samples. Rainwater was collected following the method described previously (Yang et al., 2019). Briefly, it was collected using pre-cleaned stainless steel round pots in a rainfall event from January 5th - 7th, 2018 (rainfall amount: $3 9 . 9 \ \mathrm { m m }$ ), on the roof of the College of Environment and Resources, Fuzhou University. The air mass of this event originated from mixing of north and west terrestrial winds and east marine winds based on air-mass back-trajectory analysis using the HYSPLIT model of Air Resources Laboratory, National Oceanic Atmospheric Administration (Stein et al., 2015; Rolph et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019). The collected rainwater was filtered through pre-combusted GF/F filters (pore size: $0 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m } \cdot$ and stored in glass bottles in frozen and dark before the PE experiment. Typical herbaceous plants (Pteridophyta, commonly present in the watershed) and the corresponding leaf litter on the soil surface were collected from the watershed in December 2018, and were leached separately following the methods described in Yang et al. (2015). Briefly, they were shredded into about $1 \mathrm { \ c m }$ , and DOM was leached with Milli-Q water (solidto-solution ratio: 1:10 for plant and 1:5 for leaf litter) at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . The leachates were centrifuged at $1 0 , 0 0 0 { \mathrm { ~ r p m } }$ for $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , filtered sequentially through $\mathrm { G F } / \mathrm { F }$ and $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate filters, and stored in frozen and dark. Municipal wastewater was collected from the influent of a local wastewater treatment plant of Fuzhou City, filtered through $\mathrm { G F / F }$ and $0 . 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ polycarbonate filters, and stored in frozen and dark before the PE experiment. River water samples were collected from the mainstem of Minjiang River and pre-aged by storing at room temperature in dark for over 28 days to remove biologically labile constituents. The pre-aged river samples were filtered through GF/F filters for the PE experiment of rainwater, and through GF/F and $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ polycarbonate filters for the PE experiments of other DOM sources. Microbial inocula were prepared by collecting river water from the Minjiang River and filtered through $2 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m } \ \mathrm { G F / D }$ filters at the beginning of PE experiments (Hood et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2017).

The bioavailability of DOM was examined for pure samples (river water, rainwater, plant leachate, leaf litter leachate, and wastewater) by the incubation after adding microbial inocula, following the procedure described previously (Hood et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2017, 2019). PE experiments were carried out by incubating the pre-aged river DOM after adding microbial inocula and a series of concentrations of DOC from rainwater (0.9, 4.6, 9.1, 18.2, and $4 5 . 5 \mu \mathrm { M } _ { \cdot } ^ { \cdot }$ ), plant leachate (4.2, 7.0, 13.9, 29.3, and $4 7 . 4 \mu \mathrm { M } \dot { }$ ), leaf litter leachate (1.8, 4.3, 8.5, and $1 8 . 2 \mu \mathrm { M } ^ { \cdot }$ ), and wastewater (1.8, 4.5, 8.9, 17.9, and $3 1 . 3 \mu \mathrm { M } \dot { }$ ). All incubation was performed in triplicate in dark at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 14 days, and the average analytical precision was $1 . 9 \pm 2 . 0 \%$ , $1 . 2 \pm 1 . 3 \%$ , and $1 . 5 \pm 2 . 9 \% - 3 . 3 \pm 2 . 9 \%$ for DOC, absorption coefficient, and different fluorescence components, respectively. DOC, absorption, and fluorescence were measured at the beginning and the end of the incubation, to assess their changes by microbial degradation (DCMeasured). The bioavailability of DOM in pure samples was assessed with the percentages of $\Delta { \sf C }$ in the initial values. Potential PE in the mixed samples was assessed by comparing DCMeasured with the expected theoretical values under no PE (DCTheoretical) as:

$$
\begin{array} { r l } & { \Delta C _ { \mathrm { T h e o r e t i c a l } } = \left( \Delta C _ { \mathrm { R } } * V _ { \mathrm { R } } + \Delta C _ { \mathrm { P } } * V _ { \mathrm { P } } \right) / \left( V _ { \mathrm { R } } + V _ { \mathrm { P } } \right) } \\ & { } \\ & { { \mathcal { J } _ { \mathrm { o P E } } } = \left( \Delta C _ { \mathrm { M e a s u r e d } } - \Delta C _ { \mathrm { T h e o r e t i c a l } } \right) / \ : C _ { 0 } * 1 0 0 \% } \end{array}
$$

where $\Delta C _ { \mathrm { R } }$ was the changes in the pure river sample after biodegradation; ${ \sf V } _ { \sf R }$ was the volume of river water in the mixed sample; $\Delta C _ { \mathrm { P } }$ was the changes in the pure priming DOM from different sources (rainwater, plant leachate, leaf litter leachate, and wastewater); $\mathsf { V } _ { \mathrm { P } }$ was the volume of priming DOM in the mixed sample; ${ \sf C } _ { 0 }$ was the initial value of mixed samples before degradation; $\% \mathrm { P E }$ was the percentage of the difference between measured and theoretical $\Delta \mathsfit { C }$ in the initial ${ \sf C } _ { 0 }$ . A positive $\% \mathrm { P E }$ indicated enhanced degradation, while a negative value indicated weakened degradation.

# 2.2. DOC, absorption and fluorescence measurements

Inorganic carbon in the DOM samples was removed by oxygen purging after acidification with HCl to $\mathrm { p H } < 2$ , and the DOC concentration was measured with the high-temperature catalytic oxidation method using a Shimadzu TOC-L analyzer (Yang et al., 2017). Absorption spectra of CDOM at the wavelength of $2 4 0 { - } 8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ were scanned using a Techcomp 1780 UVeVis spectrometer, and the Milli-Q water was used as the blank (Yang et al., 2019). All absorption spectra were baseline-corrected by subtracting the average absorbance over $7 0 0 { - } 8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . Then, the absorption coefficient at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m } \ ( a _ { 2 5 4 } )$ , an index for the level of CDOM, was calculated as:

$$
a _ { 2 5 4 } = 2 . 3 0 3 A _ { 2 5 4 } / l
$$

where $A _ { 2 5 4 }$ was the absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and l was the path length. The absorption spectral slope over $2 7 5 { - } 2 9 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (S275-295) was calculated following the procedure described previously, which decreased with the increasing average molecular weight of DOM (Helms et al., 2008). DOC-specific UV absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ (SUVA254) was calculated as an index for the aromaticity of DOM (Weishaar et al., 2003).

Fluorescence EEMs of DOM were scanned at excitation $\left( \operatorname { E x } \right)$ wavelengths of $2 4 0 { - } 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and emission (Em) wavelengths of $2 8 0 { - } 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , using a Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer, following the procedure described previously (Yang et al., 2017,

2019). Inner-filter effects of fluorescence were corrected based on absorption spectra (McKnight et al., 2001), and the blank signals of Milli-Q water were subtracted. The corrected EEMs were modeled with PARAFAC using the DOMFluor toolbox 1.7 (Stedmon and Bro, 2008; Yang et al., 2017). The number of PARAFAC components was determined with the split-half validation, and the fluorescence intensity $\left( F _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } \right)$ of each component was used to represent its level (in Raman Unit, RU; Stedmon and Bro, 2008). Humification index (HIX) was calculated as the ratio of fluorescence over Em $4 3 4 { - } 4 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ to that over $3 0 0 { - } 3 4 6 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (at $\operatorname { E x } 2 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , as an index for the humic content or humification degree of DOM (Zsolnay et al., 1999). Biological index (BIX) was calculated as the ratio of fluorescence at $\mathrm { E m } 3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to that at $4 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (at $\operatorname { E x } 3 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ), which was traditionally used for indicating the source of DOM (Huguet et al., 2009).

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Composition and bioavailability of DOM from different sources

The value of ${ \tt S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ was highest for the leaf litter leachate $5 . 0 ~ \mathrm { \ L } ^ { - 1 } ~ \mathrm { \ m g } ~ \mathrm { \ m } ^ { - 1 } ;$ , while it was lowest for rainwater DOM $( 0 . 7 \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \operatorname* { m g } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , Fig. 1A). The $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ ranged between $0 . 1 2 1 ~ \mathrm { { n m } } ^ { - 1 }$ and $0 . 3 8 3 \ \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ , which was highest for the plant leachate and was lowest for the leaf litter leachate. The HIX was highest for the leaf litter leachate and lowest for the plant leachate (12.3 vs. 0), while BIX was lower for the leaf litter leachate and rainwater DOM (0.50e0.54) than for the DOM from the plant leachate, river, and wastewater (0.94e1.09, Fig. 1B).

Seven fluorescence components were identified with EEMPARAFAC in this study (C1eC7, Fig. 2), which were compared with those in the OpenFluor database (Murphy et al., 2014). C1 showed two Ex maxima at 245 and $3 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and one Em maximum at $4 4 1 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , similar to a commonly-identified terrestrial humic-like component (C1 in Gueguen et al., 2014  ; Derrien et al., 2018). The $\operatorname { E x } /$ Em maxima of C2 were (250, 395)/490 nm, resembling another terrestrial humic-like component at the longest emission wavelength in freshwater (C4 in Kida et al., 2019) and the ocean (C3 in Murphy et al., 2006). C3 had Ex/Em maxima at (240, 310)/383 nm, similar to the microbial humic-like component in drinking water treatments (C3 in Shutova et al., 2014) and inland waters (C4 in Groeneveld et al., 2020). C4 displayed $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maxima at 245/ $3 2 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , which resembled benzoic acid in Wünsch et al. (2015). C5 showed $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maxima at $2 7 5 / 3 1 4 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , similar to the tyrosine-like component in freshwater (C1 in Kida et al., 2019) and seawater (C4 in Dainard et al., 2015). C6 displayed $\operatorname { E x } / \operatorname { E m }$ maxima ((240, 345)/ $4 3 6 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ) similar to a humic-like component (C2 in Osburn et al., 2015; C3 in Retelletti Brogi et al., 2020). C7 (Ex/Em: (240, 290)/ $3 5 3 \mathrm { n m }$ ) resembled the tryptophan-like component (C7 in Murphy et al., 2008; C1 in Wünsch et al., 2017). In the rainwater, $\% C 1$ was highest $( 3 0 \% )$ , followed by %C7, %C6, %C5, %C3, and $\% C 4$ , while C2 at the longest emission wavelength was not present (Fig. 1C). The plant leachate was dominated by C4 $( 6 8 \% )$ and C5 $( 3 0 \% )$ at short emission wavelengths, while it contained little other fluorescent components. The leaf litter leachate mainly contained humic-like components C1 $( 4 7 \% )$ , C2 $( 2 7 \% )$ , and C3 $( 1 2 \% )$ . The wastewater and river DOM contained most fluorescent components $( 1 1 \% - 2 3 \%$ and $1 1 \% { - } 2 7 \%$ , respectively) except for C4.

DOC decreased by up to $8 0 \%$ in the plant leachate after 14-day bio-degradation, and it decreased by $4 5 \% - 5 0 \%$ in rainwater and wastewater (Fig. 1D). The bioavailability of DOC was low for the leaf litter leachate $( 1 2 \% )$ and for the pre-aged river samples $( 7 \% )$ . The value of $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ decreased by $4 3 \%$ for the plant leachate and changed little $( 1 \% - 7 \% )$ for other DOM sources after bio-degradation (Table 1). The two dominant components in the plant leachate (C4 and C5) were almost removed completely after bio-degradation $( 9 6 \% - 1 0 0 \% )$ . C5 decreased notably in rainwater $( 3 6 \% )$ and wastewater $( 2 4 \% )$ , and C7 showed significant removals in wastewater $( 3 9 \% )$ , leaf litter leachate $( 2 6 \% )$ , and river $( 1 5 \% )$ . There were also significant decreases of $12 \% { - } 3 6 \%$ for C1 and C3 in rainwater, C6 in the leaf litter leachate, and C3 in wastewater. The value of $\mathsf { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ increased largely $( 7 9 \% - 1 8 7 \% )$ for rainwater, plant leachate, and wastewater, and $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ decreased by $1 3 \% { - } 5 0 \%$ for rainwater, plant leachate, and wastewater after bio-degradation. The HIX value increased for all DOM sources after bio-degradation, while BIX was generally less affected by bio-degradation $( - 1 8 \% - 2 3 \% )$ and did not show a consistent response to bio-degradation for different sources (Table 1).

# 3.2. Priming effects of DOC, CDOM, and fluorescent components

The measured decreases of DOC after bio-degradation for river samples added with rainwater, plant leachate, leaf litter leachate, and wastewater were compared with the theoretical values in Fig. 3A. The percentage of the difference between measured and theoretical DDOC in the initial DOC (%PE) was shown in Table 2. Generally, most measured DDOC values were close to the theoretical values, following the 1:1 line (Fig. 3A). Correspondingly, most $\%$ PE values were $- 5 \%$ for DOC (Table 2). A positive PE of $7 . 2 \%$ was observed in the river sample with the highest addition amount of rainwater DOC $( 4 5 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , while negative %PE values of $- 8 . 9 \%$ to $- 1 1 . 3 \%$ were observed in the samples added with wastewater at three addition concentrations of $4 . 5 \mathrm { - } 1 7 . 9 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ .

The results of potential PE on CDOM and different fluorescent components (except for C4 which was not present in the pre-aged river sample) were shown in Figs. 3Be4 and Table 2. Most measured $\Delta a _ { 2 5 4 }$ after bio-degradation were close to the theoretical values. The percentage of their difference in the initial $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ $( \% \mathrm { P E } )$ was $- 5 \%$ , except for one value of $5 . 3 \%$ in the river sample added with $8 . 9 \mu \mathrm { M }$ wastewater DOC. The %PE of $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ was positive for most samples with the addition of rainwater, plant leachate, and wastewater (Fig. 3B). C2 and C3 both showed little PE of $\_ 4 . 6 \%$ $5 . 1 \%$ in all groups (Fig. 4B and C, Table 2). C1 and C6 also showed little PE of $- 3 . 5 \% - 2 . 7 \%$ with the addition of leaf litter leachate and wastewater, except for a negative PE of $- 8 . 3 \%$ for C6 at the addition concentration of $1 8 . 2 \mu \mathrm { M }$ leaf litter DOC (Fig. 4A and E, Table 2). C1 and C6 showed positive PE of $5 . 0 \% { - 2 9 . 6 \% }$ with the addition of 4.6 and $9 . 1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ rainwater DOC, but negative PE of $- 1 6 . 6 \%$ and $- 4 9 . 1 \%$ at the highest load of rainwater DOC. C1 showed negative PE of $- 2 . 7 \%$ to $- 1 0 \%$ while C6 showed positive PE of $1 . 0 \% { - } 7 . 8 \%$ with the addition of plant leachate. C5 and C7 both showed little PE with the addition of rainwater and leaf litter leachate $( < 5 \% )$ , except for a positive PE of $7 . 9 \%$ for C7 at the highest addition of rainwater DOM (Fig. 4D and F, Table 2). They showed negative PE of up to $- 9 . 0 \%$ with the addition of plant leachate, but mainly positive PE of up to $1 1 . 3 \%$ with the addition of wastewater.

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Variability of chemical composition and bioavailability of DOM from different sources

Absorption and fluorescence indices changed greatly for DOM from different sources, indicating notable differences in the chemical composition of DOM (Fig. 1). The leaf litter leachate had the highest ${ \mathsf { S U V } } { \mathsf { A } } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , indicating the highest aromaticity due to the humification process by microbial transformation (Weishaar et al., 2003). In contrast, ${ \mathsf { S U V A } } _ { 2 5 4 }$ was lowest for rainwater DOM, which was probably related to the decrease of aromaticity by photodegradation in the atmosphere (Yang et al., 2019) and the contribution of low molecular weight organic acids which have very low light absorption in the UV region (Willey et al., 2011). The $S _ { 2 7 5 }$ -295 was highest for the plant leachate and was lowest for the leaf litter leachate, while HIX was lower for the plant leachate than the leaf litter leachate. This suggested much lower average molecular weight and humic content of DOM derived from the plant than that from leaf litter, which might be resulted from the microbial degradation of low molecular weight constituents and the production of high molecular weight humic substances in the soil humification process (Zsolnay et al., 1999; Helms et al., 2008). The chemical diversity of DOM was further indicated by the notable variability of fluorescent components for different sources (Fig. 1C). In the rainwater, $\% C 1$ was highest while C2 at the longest emission wavelength (i.e., highest conjugation) was not present due to strong photo-degradation in the atmosphere. The plant leachate was dominated by C4 and C5 at short emission wavelengths and contained little other fluorescent components, which indicated a low molecular weight of DOM and almost no humic substance. In contrast, the leaf litter leachate mainly contained humic-like components C1eC3. The wastewater and river DOM contained most fluorescent components except for C4. It is noteworthy that C4 was the most abundant component in the plant leachate, while it was not present in the leaf litter leachate, wastewater, and river. Therefore, C4 mainly originated from the plant materials, and the minor proportion of C4 $( 7 \% )$ in rainwater might be related to the contribution of plant materials (Yang et al., 2019).

The bioavailability of DOC also showed notable variability for different sources (Fig. 1D), since it was largely dependent on the chemical composition of DOM (Hood et al., 2009; Parr et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2019). It was up to $8 0 \%$ for the plant leachate, indicating that the DOC from the fresh plant was highly degradable by microbes. Similarly, Textor et al. (2019) found that the average bioavailability of DOC for the leachates of fresh vegetation was up to $6 4 . 1 \%$ . The bioavailability of DOC from rainwater and wastewater were also high $( 4 5 \% - 5 0 \% )$ , which might be resulted from the photochemical processing of rainwater DOM in the atmosphere, the contribution of labile low molecular weight organic acids to rainwater DOC, and the abundant labile DOM constituents in wastewater (e.g., carbohydrates and proteins), respectively (Willey et al., 2011; Parr et al., 2015). Our results are consistent with the high DOC lability previously reported for rainwater $4 6 \pm 1 7 \%$ in Bao et al., 2018; $5 0 \% \pm 1 9 \%$ in Yang et al., 2019). The wastewater DOC is also generally highly labile, which can be removed efficiently by biological treatments $8 5 \% \pm 1 0 \%$ in Yang et al., 2014) and contributes to the elevated DOC lability in urbanized rivers (Parr et al., 2015). In contrast, the bioavailability of DOC in the leaf litter leachate was low $( 1 2 \% )$ , since labile DOM constituents had undergone microbial degradation in soils. The lowest bioavailability of DOC in the pre-aged river samples $( 7 \% )$ indicated a dominance of stable constituents, due to the generally low content of labile DOC in the Minjiang River and the removal of labile DOC by microbes during the pre-aging process. These results are consistent with the low bioavailability of DOC in a number of rivers $( 0 - 2 5 \%$ , Wiegner et al., 2009; Mann et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2017).

The bioavailability of CDOM and different fluorescent components were also tested for different sources in this study (Table 1). The value of $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ , which was used as an indicator for the CDOM level, decreased by $4 3 \%$ for the plant leachate and changed little $( 1 \% - 7 \% )$ for other DOM sources after bio-degradation. This suggested that CDOM was generally less biologically labile than nonchromophoric components and supported that DOM in the plant leachate had the highest lability. The degradability changed notably for fluorescent different components in different sources. The two dominant components in the plant leachate (benzoic acid-like C4 and tyrosine-like C5) were almost removed completely, indicating the highest bio-degradability. The tyrosine-like C5 decreased notably in rainwater and wastewater, and the tryptophan-like C7 was removed significantly in wastewater, leaf litter leachate, and river. These results are consistent with previous studies showing that the protein-like components are biologically labile (Hood et al., 2009; Parr et al., 2015; Begum et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2019). Some humic-like components also decreased after bio-degradation (C1 and C3 in rainwater, C6 in the leaf litter leachate, and C3 in wastewater), while other humic-like components showed limited changes of $- 1 0 \%$ . The humic-like components are generally less labile to microbes and can be produced from the microbial transformation of other organic compounds (Lønborg et al., 2010; Yang et al., 2017, 2019). The selectivity of bio-degradation was also reflected in the changes of DOM composition indices (Table 1). The increasing $\mathsf { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and decreasing S275-295 for rainwater, plant leachate, and wastewater suggested notable elevations of aromaticity and average molecular weight of DOM after bio-degradation (Weishaar et al., 2003; Helms et al., 2008). The increase of HIX for all DOM sources after bio-degradation indicated an increase in the humic content, which was most evident for the plant leachate containing little humic substance before degradation (Zsolnay et al., 1999).

In summary, the comparison of DOM from different sources showed highly variable chemical composition and bioavailability. Therefore, it is important to test if there is a notable priming effect on the microbial degradation of stable constituents in the river by the input of DOM from different sources, which occurs under precipitation (rainwater, plant leachate, and leaf litter leachate) and pollution (wastewater).

# 4.2. Priming effects by the addition of different sources and the effects on different DOM pools

Generally, most measured decreases of DOC were close to the theoretical values (Fig. 3A), and most %PE values were $- 5 \%$ for DOC in the pre-aged river samples added with rainwater, plant leachate, leaf litter leachate, and wastewater (Table 3). These results suggested that there was generally no strong PE on the microbial degradation of bulk DOC, even though the added DOC covered typical natural and anthropogenic sources. Therefore, the degradation of DOC in the mixed samples was largely dependent on the addition amount of labile DOC. The degradation of labile DOC from rainfall and plant leaching in precipitation events and from wastewater at polluted locations would release ${ \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ to the water body which might further escape to the atmosphere (Battin et al., 2008; Raymond et al., 2013). In addition, the positive $\% \mathrm { P E }$ of $7 . 2 \%$ at the highest addition amount of rainwater DOC indicated the potential occurrence of enhanced DOC degradation (e.g., during storm events). In contrast, negative PE $( - 8 . 9 \%$ to $- 1 1 . 3 \% )$ was observed at three addition concentrations of wastewater DOC, suggesting weakened DOC degradation which might be related to preferential degradation of labile DOC from wastewater (Hotchkiss et al., 2014; Steen et al., 2016). Similarly, previous studies show that PE is in part dependent on the concentration of priming DOC (Ward et al., 2019). Most measured $\Delta a _ { 2 5 4 }$ after bio-degradation were also close to the theoretical values (Fig. 3B), and the $\% \mathrm { P E }$ of $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ were $- 5 \%$ except for one value of $5 . 3 \%$ in the sample added with $8 . 9 \mu \mathrm { M }$ wastewater DOC (Table 3). These results indicated an overall limited role of PE on the degradation of CDOM. However, it is noteworthy that the %PE value of $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ was positive for most samples with the addition of rainwater, plant leachate, and wastewater (Fig. 3B, Table 2), suggesting that there was probably a weak but consistent intensification of CDOM degradation.

There were notable differences in the PE results for different fluorescent components with the addition of different DOM sources (Fig. 4, Tables 2 and 3). The terrestrial humic-like C2 and microbial humic-like C3 both showed limited PE in all samples. The other two humic-like components C1 and C6 also showed little PE with the addition of leaf litter leachate and wastewater, except for a negative PE of $- 8 . 3 \%$ for C6 at the addition of leaf litter leachate. However, C1 and C6 were more sensitive to the addition of rainwater and plant leachate. On one hand, they both showed positive PE with the addition of 4.6 and $9 . 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ rainwater DOC, but negative PE at the highest load of rainwater. This suggested a transition of positive PE to negative PE on humic-like components C1 and C6 with increasing rainwater DOM. On the other hand, C1 showed negative PE while C6 showed positive PE in the samples added with the plant leachate, indicating different PE on different humic-like components. It is noteworthy that positive values of DC1, DC2, DC3, and $\Delta C 6$ indicated net production of humic-like components by microbial transformation while negative values indicated net decreases by microbial degradation (Fig. 4AeC and E). Thus, positive PE indicated enhanced net decreases of humic-like components which might be caused by enhanced degradation and/or weakened production. In contrast, DC5 and DC7 were always negative in the PE experiments, indicating a dominant removal of the more labile tyrosine-like and tryptophan-like components after bio-degradation (Fig. 4D and F). C5 and C7 both showed little PE with the addition of rainwater and leaf litter leachate, except for a positive PE of $7 . 9 \%$ for C7 at the highest addition of rainwater DOM. They showed positive PE with the addition of wastewater, indicating enhanced degradation of protein-like components. However, they showed negative PE with the addition of plant leachate, indicating they were less degraded due to preferential degradation of more labile plant constituents and/or were released as byproducts of microbial activity (Lambert and Perga, 2019).

To our knowledge, there is little information on the response of different PARAFAC components to PE (Begum et al., 2019; Lambert and Perga, 2019). Begum et al. (2019) find an increase in the humiclike component and a notable consumption of the protein-like component by bio-degradation in the river mainstem, urban tributary, and their mixture. Lambert and Perga (2019) identify no significant PE for humic-like components but enhanced production of tryptophan-like and microbial protein-like components in the mixtures of the lake and river waters. Our study is likely the first to apply fluorescence EEMs-PARAFAC to study PE of DOM from typical allochthonous sources (rainwater, plant, leaf litter, and wastewater) on the degradation of riverine stable constituents. The responses of DOC, CDOM, and C1eC7 are summarized in Table 3. The addition of plant leachate to pre-aged river DOM resulted in negative PE for C1, C5, and C7 (Table 3). This was probably related to preferential utilization of more labile DOM constituents from the plant leachate which had the highest bioavailability. In contrast, the addition of leaf litter leachate had little PE on all DOM indices, which was probably due to the low bioavailability of leaf litter leachate. The addition of rainwater DOM mainly resulted in positive PE on bulk DOC, CDOM, and fluorescent components, although negative PE was evident for C1 and C6 at the highest addition of rainwater DOM. The addition of wastewater DOM mainly resulted in negative PE on DOC, but positive PE on CDOM, C5, and C7 (Table 3). These results suggested that the PE was probably dependent on a series of factors such as the concentration and composition of labile DOM and the environmental conditions such as inorganic compounds. First, the additions of DOM from the plant, rainwater, and wastewater which contain more labile constituents are more likely to result in PE than the addition of leaf litter leachate which contains more stable constituents. Second, the variability of PE within each experiment group for different addition concentrations of priming DOM indicates an effect of primer concentration, which is consistent with previous studies (Ward et al., 2019). Finally, the concentrations of inorganic compounds are likely variable for different DOM sources, and their potential influences on PE warrant further studies (Hotchkiss et al., 2014; Catalan et al., 2015 ; Dorado-García et al., 2016). Overall, our results show that EEMs-PARAFAC is an effective tool for identifying potential PE of different fluorescent components in the mixing of diverse DOM sources. This technique has evident advantages over the measurements of bulk parameters. For example, the fluorescence measurement generally has a high sensitivity, which allows detection at a low concentration. The EEMs-PARAFAC further provides more detailed information on the degradation behaviors and variable responses of different DOM components to potential PE.

# 5. Conclusions

(1) The plant-derived DOM had a low humic content and was dominated by benzoic acid-like C4 and tyrosine-like C5, while DOM in the leaf litter leachate showed high aromaticity, average molecular weight, and humic content due to the soil humification process. Rainwater DOM showed low aromaticity and did not contain C2 at the longest emission wavelength due to photodegradation in the atmosphere and the contribution of low molecular weight organic compounds. Wastewater had low aromaticity and contained most fluorescent components, except for C4. These results indicated a high diversity in the chemical composition of DOM from different natural and anthropogenic sources.

(2) The bioavailability of DOC was highest for the plant-derived DOM, followed by rainwater and wastewater, while it was low for the leaf litter leachate and pre-aged river water. CDOM was generally less biologically labile, which decreased more for the plant leachate than for other sources. The dominant fluorescent components in the plant leachate (C4 and C5) were highly labile, while humic-like fluorescent components in other sources were generally less labile. Therefore, the inputs of multiple sources to aquatic environments introduce different DOM pools of variable lability, which probably play different roles in aquatic biogeochemistry and ecosystem function.

(3) The addition of DOM from different sources to pre-aged river DOM resulted in different PE results, which was less significant for the less labile leaf litter leachate than other sources. There was generally limited PE on the degradation of DOC and CDOM for most samples, while the responses of fluorescent components were more variable and changes with different components and with the additions of DOM from different sources. The results provided useful information for assessing the priming effect by the inputs of different sources, which had implications for better understanding the microbial degradation of DOM in aquatic environments.
