# 1. Introduction

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) are important oxidative agents that can mediate redox transformations in different environments (Copper et al., 1989; Scully et al., 1996; Abele et al, 1999; Mostofa and Sakugawa, 2009; Cory et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2016). Hydrogen peroxide $\left( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \right)$ is a long-lived ROS with a half-life ranging from several to a hundred hours, which makes it a useful indicator of photochemical activity in aquatic ecosystems (Szymczak and Waite, 1991; Mostofa et al., 2013). In these environments, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ can be produced by photochemical reactions involving the humic fraction of chromophoric dissolved organic mater (CDOM) (Cooper et al.， 1994; Mostofa and Sakuguwa, 2009; Clark et al.,2009; Mostofa et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2016). Also, heterotrophic bacteria, fungi and phytoplankton can release ROS in light-independent processes, with greater production in the aphotic zone (Kim et al., 2004; Aguirre et al., 2005; Diaz et al., 2013). The fact that $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ can be generated in dark and sunlit marine, brackish and fresh waters extends the significance of this reactive molecule production to all oxygenated habitats (Zhang et al., 2016). 7

The absorption of ultraviolet radiation (UVR, 290-400 nm) by CDOM leads to an excited state. In order to return to the ground state, an electron transfer to dissolved oxygen $( \mathbf { O } _ { 2 } )$ is needed, forming superoxide $( \mathbf { O } _ { 2 } \mathbf { \bar { ) } }$ ，which in turn dismutates， generating $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ (O'Sullivan et al., 2005; Clark et al., 2009; Zhang et al., 2012; Mostofa et al., 2013). The formation of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ is related to changes in DOM features due to abiotic factors, thus variation in DOM quality and/or quantity may affect the production of this compound.

The Andean sector of northwestern Patagonia includes one of the most important headwater catchments of the region, and is composed of a network of mountain streams and lakes, shallow and deep piedmont lakes and rivers of glacial origin. In Northwestern Patagonia the combination of high altitude ( $> 7 0 0$ m.a.s.l.), low zenith angles during spring and summer (Villafane et al., 2001; Diaz et al., 2006) and optically-thin water columns (low DOC) create a high-radiation underwater climate (Morris et al., 1995; Garcia et al., 2015a). The majority of these systems are low nutrient and high light environments, variables that shape ecosystemic metabolism and trophic pathways (Diaz et al., 2007; Pérez et al., 2010). In low order streams and rivers, the natural DOM pool is mostly terrestrially derived, with a high proportion of humic compounds with high molecular weight/size, closely resembling soil DOM (Garcia et al.， 2015a; 2018). Terrestrial DOM inputs are also a very important fraction of the DOM pool present in mountain and piedmont lakes; however, lake DOM shows clear signs of photochemical and biological processing (Queimalinos et al., 2012; Garcia et al.， 2015b; Garcia et al.， submitted). CDOM and fluorescent dissolved organic matter (FDOM) analysis of natural DOM from different aquatic systems of Andean Patagonia showed seasonal allochthonous inputs tied to autumn-winter precipitation, and also to the contribution of protein-like compounds from biological production (Garcia et al., 2015 a, b; Soto Cardenas et al., 2017; Garcia et al., 2018). In addition, lake DOM pools show clear signs of photochemical processng, particularly in the upper layers (Queimalinos et al., 2012; Garcia et al., 2015 a; Gerea et al., 2016; Soto Cardenas et al.,2017; 2018). The available information about natural occurrence of ROS in Patagonian aquatic systems is indirect, mostly dealing with their effect on planktonic organisms and enzymatic activity (Souza et al., 2007, 2010; Wolinski et al., 2016). Aquatic communities in North Patagonia display a suite of chemical and behavioral traits that enables them to endure the high underwater radiation levels and naturally elevated oxidative stress (Alonso et al., 2004; Marinone et al., 2006; Garcia et al.， 2010). ROS have been shown to induce chemical responses in different zooplankton species of Andean lakes including the accumulation of antioxidants such as carotenoids (Garcia et al., 2O08) and the production of the enzymes catalase (CAT), superoxide dismutase and Glutathione S-transferase (GST), which take part in major antioxidant mechanisms (Souza et al., 2007,2010, 2012; Wolinski et al., 2016). This study addresses, for the first time, the photochemical formation of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ in natural water from streams and lakes of Andean Patagonia, spanning a range of DOM conditions. The analysis comprises field measurements and laboratory experiments designed to analyze the role of DOM in the photo-chemical production of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ in these high light, oligotrophic waters. The central hypothesis of this work is that the photo-chemical production of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ relates to CDOM concentration and to the reactivity of the natural DOM pools. In this context， we expect that the natural gradients of CDOM concentrations and quality of streams and shallow and deep lakes drive their potential to produce $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ ：

# 2. Materials and Methods

# 2.1. Study Area

Water samples for this study were colected in streams, shallow lakes and deep lakes located along a W-E gradient inside Nahuel Huapi National Park (NHNP; 41.01-41.36 S; 71.14-71.56 oW), in northwestern Patagonia (Argentina) (Table S1). The western streams Casa de Piedra (CDP) and De la Virgen (DLV) are low order systems surrounded by a mixed evergreen forest, while Nirihuau (NHU) and Chacabuco (CHA) are situated in the

East, and run through a steppe with shrubs and grasses (Mermoz et al., 2009). The shallow lakes Escondido (ESC), Morenito (MITO), Trebol (TRE) and Juventus (JUV),and the dep lakes Gutiérrez (GUT), Moreno Este (ME), Moreno Oeste (MO) and Nahuel Huapi (NH, Bahia Serena) are surrounded by mixed Nothofagus forest (Table S1, Fig. S1). 人 Surface samples were collected between January and February 2018 (austral summer) from 8 to $1 0 \ \mathrm { a m }$ . The water was collected using a Kemmerer bottle, poured into acid-cleaned polycarbonate carboys and transported to the laboratory in darkness until processed within 2-3 h of collection.

In the laboratory，water samples were sterilized by filtration through polyvinylidene difluoride membranes (PVDF Millipore, $0 . 2 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } } )$ allowing the removal of bacteria with low protein binding what ensures that all dissolved substances including enzymes pass to the filtrates. This method has been reported as the most appropriate to sterilize and preserve DOC concentration and quality (Andersson, 2007). An aliquot of $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ was taken from each filtered sample to perform the characterization of CDOM, FDOM and measure the DOC concentration. Filtered samples were kept in darkness at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until the experiment was set up.

# 2.2. Water analysis

DOC determination and optical characterization of DOM were performed on filtered natural water samples. DOC concentration was measured as non-purgeable organic carbon (NPOC) with a Shimadzu TOC-L high temperature analyzer equipped with a high sensitivity catalyzer. Absorbance spectra were obtained from 200 to $8 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ at $1 \ \mathrm { n m }$ intervals with a UV/Vis spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, UV1800), using a quartz cuvete （ $1 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ path length). FDOM analysis of filtered water samples was performed in a PerkinElmer 55B spectrofluorometer equipped with a 150-W Xenon arc lamp and a Peltier temperature controller, using a 1 cm quartz cell. 人 $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ concentration was measured in filtered natural water samples, within 2-3 hours after collection, following the standard addition method (Zika and Saltzman, 1982; Donahue, 1998). $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ analysis involved a phosphate buffer $( 0 . 5 \mathrm { ~ M } )$ $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ $( 1 \mathrm { { x } } 1 0 \mathrm { \Omega } ^ { - 5 } \mathrm { { \ m } }$ SigmaAldrich), scopoletin $( 9 \mathrm { x } 1 0 ^ { - 5 } \ \mathrm { M } .$ ，Sigma-Aldrich) and horseradish peroxidase (HRP, $1 \mathrm { x } 1 0 ^ { - 4 }$ M, Sigma-Aldrich) as the main reagents. A volume of $0 . 1 \ \mathrm { m L }$ of phosphate buffer was added to a $1 0 \ \mathrm { m L }$ water sample and its fluorescence measured in a spectrofluorometer (Perkin-Elmer 55B) set with the excitation centered at $3 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and emission at $4 9 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ Following the addition of $4 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of scopoletin $9 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ a new measurement was performed and considered as $100 \%$ fluorescence intensity. Following this, $7 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ HRP were added to the sample, and after l minute a new fluorescence assessment was carried out. The decrease in fluorescence intensity was interpreted as the concentration of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ (nM) in the sample (Febria et al., 2006; Clark et al., 2009). Afterwards, the sample was spiked with four serial volumes of a verified $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ standard ( $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ each) at 2 minutes intervals,and measured each time to obtain a standard curve.

For each water type, 12 replicates were set up, 3 for each light treatment and time of exposure (4 and $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ ). The experiment was run at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a test chamber (Sanyo ML 500). 人

# 2.4. Analytical procedure

CDOM and FDOM proxies were used to characterize the DOM pool of water samples (Table S2). Absorbance and fluorescence data preprocessing methodology can be found in thesupplementary methods (Supplementary information). The averaged UV-visible absorbance in the range of $7 0 0 { - } 8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ was subtracted from each spectrum to correct for the instrument baseline effect (Helms et al., 2Oo8). Absorbance data was used to calculate the spectral slopes for the intervals 275-295 nm (S275-29s) and $3 5 0 – 4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (S350-400), and the slope ratio $( \mathbf { S } _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ as a proxy for photo-bleaching and molecular weight/size (Table S2). Also， the fluorescence index (FI)， the freshness index $\left( \mathsf { a } \colon \mathsf { \beta } \right)$ and the humification index (HIX) were calculated from the EEMs (Table S2).

$\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation rate $\mathrm { ( n M ~ h ^ { - 1 } ) } = \left( \mathrm { [ H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 } ] _ { \ t = f i n a l ^ { - } } [ H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 } ] _ { \ t = i n i t i a l } ) / \Delta t = f i n a l } \right.$

# 2.5.Data analysis and statistics

Paralel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was performed with 152 EEMs in order to identify underlying fluorophores present in DOM samples, using DrEEM toolbox (Supplementary methods).   
A two-way ANOVA was applied to study the effect of the radiation treatment (light PAR+UVR and dark) and incubation time (4 and $2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ ）in the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation rate. Normality and homoscedasticity were previously verified. The relationship between DOC concentration and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ photo-production was fited to a linear model using simple liear regression.   
A Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was applied to analyze the relationship between DOC concentration and DOM quality parameters and the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation rate. Variables were centered and standardized for the analysis. The PCA was performed with the Rstudio 3.3.3 open software (Boston, USA) using the FactoMiner package.

# 4. Discussion

This investigation shows natural levels of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ as well as the potential of photo-production of this ROS in water from streams and lakes of northwestern Patagonia. The natural $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ concentrations found were higher in deep lakes, followed by shallow lakes and streams (Table 2). Noteworthy, in the deep lake Gutierrez an exceptionally high $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ concentration was ，although we cannot provide a mechanistic explanation for this $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ level. Previous investigations evidenced that $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ concentrations in natural environments fluctuate daily; with maximum values occurring immediately after the highest radiation level is attained, and decreasing towards nightfall(Mostofa et al., 2013 and references therein). In this study, water samples were collected during the morning, so are likely to have experienced low cumulative irradiation. Therefore, the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ concentrations measured could be considered baseline levels and are comparable between the diferent ecosystems studied. The natural $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels recorded in our study are low compared with values cited in the literature for different environments showing natural $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels ranging from ${ \sim } 1 0 \mathrm { n M }$ to ${ \sim } 2 \mu \mathrm { M }$ in lakes (Cooper and Zika,1983；Scully et al., 1996; Hakkinen et al.,2004,Febria et al., 2006; Mostofa et al., 2013; Cory et al., 2016) and from 0 to $2 0 0 \mathrm { n M }$ in running water (Scott et al., 2003; Mostofa and Sakugawa, 2009).

Higher levels of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation were found in all water samples in the light treatment, probably due to photolysis of light-absorbing organic substances present in the natural DOM pools. Interestingly, a strong positive relationship between $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ photo-production rate and DOC concentration was found in shallow lakes and streams, whereas in deep lakes this relationship was negative. This $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ -DOC pattern has previously been observed in different environments around the world (Scully et al.， 1996; Mostofa and Sakugawa, 2009). In this work, the positive $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ -DOC trend could be related to the higher levels of humic compounds in natural DOM pools of the shallow lakes and streams studied, compared to the DOM present in deep lakes, which displays photochemical weathering and biological processing (Table 1). This also indicates that the DOM pools of streams and shallow lakes are more photo-reactive substrates than those of deep lakes, where higher internal processing reduces DOM reactivity.

Different investigations in aquatic systems of northwestern Patagonia have indicated differences in the concentration and quality of natural DOM pools (Queimalinos et al., 2012; Zagarese et al., 2017; Garcia et al., 2015a; 2018). In general, mountain streams and deep piedmont lakes show extremely low DOC concentrations typical of ultraoligotrophy and oligotrophy (Morris et al., 1995; Garcia et al., 2015a). In contrast, the DOC level in shallow lakes span a wider range， with high mountain lakes showing low DOC concentrations comparable to deep piedmont lakes and streams $( < 1 \ \mathrm { m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ ） (Garcia et al., 2015a,b; Queimalinos et al., 2012), and shallow piedmont lakes with notably higher DOC (up to $5 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ )(Gerea et al., 2016; Soto Cardenas et al.,2017). In the deep lakes studied, surface water samples showed a decoupling between DOM concentration and CDOM, indicating that colorless(non-chromophoric） compounds， probably productsof photochemical weathering, partly contribute to DOC. A similar patern has been found in Lake Superior, where the long water retention time (WRT） explained the extensive photochemical reprocessing in the water column, resulting in DOC-CDOM decoupling (Zhou et al.， 2016). In contrast, CDOM-DOC coupling was observed in streams and shallow lakes, reflecting their lower processing. In fact in shallow lakes the WRT is comparatively much lower, 0.50 years in Lake Escondido and 0.56 years in Lake Morenito, and the extent of photo and biological degradation has been shown to be limited compared with deep lakes in the area (Gerea et al., 2016; Soto Cardenas et al., 2017). In turn, low order Andean streams have been described to act as pipes, draining terrestrially-derived

DOM with negligible internal processing. For example, in Casa de Piedra and De la Virgen streams， the extremely low WRT (hours） due to their high slopes prevents internal processing, resulting in similar DOM pools along their course (Garcia et al., 2015b; 2018). In contrast, Andean deep lakes show higher WRT, which enhances exposure to the high solar radiation levels and promotes photochemical processng. In the deep lakes Moreno Este (ME), and Moreno Oeste (MO) the WRT has been estimated at ${ \sim } 2 . 2 9$ and ${ \sim } 0 . 9 8$ years, respectively, and strong signs of photochemical weathering in their DOM pools have been detected, particularly in the upper layers (Queimalinos et al., 2012; Soto Cardenas et al., 2018). In the case of Lake Nahuel Huapi, this processing may be even stronger than in any of the deep lakes of the area, given its greater WRT, calculated as 11.6 years( Pedrozo et al., 1997). Moreover, in deep lakes the S275-295, a proxy for processing, was up to twice as high as in streams (Table 1), indicating the prevalence of low molecular weight/size compounds, likely products of photochemical degradation. Also, in deep lakes biological processing contributes low molecular weight/size compounds, as indicated by the freshness index values $( > 0 . 7 )$ , overall higher than in streams and shallow lakes (Table 1).

Our experiments showed that $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ production was highly dependent on light. In fact, final $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ concentrations were up to twice as high in the light compared with the dark treatment, which showed negligible concentration changes throughout incubation. We cannot rule out the potential effect of extracellular enzymes on the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation or decay rate, because water samples were filtered to remove bacteria but colloids and dissolved substances $< 0 . 2 2$ $\mu \mathrm { m }$ remained in the filtrate.

Interestingly, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ production was negatively related to DOC in deep Andean lakes. In these low-DOC systems, the CDOM, reprocessed substances with low reactivity, appear to condition $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ production. In contrast, the streams studied showed higher $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ production at similarly low DOC concentrations $( < 1 \ \mathrm { m g \ L ^ { - 1 } } )$ )， probably due to the higher content of humic reactive compounds in their CDOM pools. Other investigations have also indicated that DOM quality can determine the photo-production of this ROS,with high levels of light-absorbing organic substances promoting $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation (Cooper et al., 1988; Hakkinen et al., 2004; Richard et al.，2007 Clark et al., 2009; Rusak et al.,2010). The exposure of fulvic and humic acids to solar radiation can increase $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ production, particularly in the upper layers of aquatic systems (Mostofa et al., 2013). Future investigations should address the role of the substrate quality in the production of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ in natural environments,also taking into account the contribution of photo and biological generation. In natural systems, DOM concentration and quality vary spatially (between lakes and in the vertical profile of lakes)and temporally (daily and seasonally), depending largely on allochthonous inputs and internal processes. In our study, aquatic environments in the same area, and some belonging to the same watershed, show distinct DOM pools that could be related to their diferent potential for $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ photo-production. The CDOM fraction regulates the underwater light climate and nutrient availability, which in turn determines primary and microbial production. Microbial and phytoplankton assemblages can contribute ROS directly to the water (Zhang et al., 2016) and indirectly through autochthonous DOM, adding to the potential of the DOM pool to generate ROS (Mostofa et al., 2013). ROS can be produced by algae through different pathways, and these compounds may provide physiological benefits that vary according to the species and 15 environmental conditions (Cory et al., 2016; Schneider et al., 2016). On the other hand, it has been shown that low levels of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ can impact microbial communities by affecting, for instance, bacterial carbon and protein production and cell survivorship (Xenopoulus and Bird,1997; Maranger et al. 2002; Anesio et al., 2005). 人

In deep Andean lakes, natural UVR levels have been found to induce morphological, physiological and behavioral changes in different organisms of pelagic food webs (Alonso et al., 2004; Corno et al., 2009; Souza et al., 2007; 2010; Garcia et al., 2008; 2010). These adjustments may respond to the direct impact of UVR and also to indirect effects related to its interaction with DOC which leads to the formation of ROS (Souza et al., 2007). Thus, the environmental potential for ROS production, including $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ may be determined by the interplay between physical, chemical and biological factors that overall shape the DOM pool. The marked seasonality of the Andean Patagonian climate may drive the production of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ differentially throughout the year, due to seasonal contrasts in solar radiation levels and allochthonous DOM inputs， that are tied to fall-winter precipitation. This environmental scenario is appropriate to assess the effect of substrate quality on $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels,contributing overal to understand temporal and spatial fluctuations of this ROS in oligotrophic clear systems where photochemical weathering strongly influences the dynamics of water chemistry.

# 5. Conclusions

Our results let us propose that lakes showing strong signs of photo-bleaching bear naturally high levels of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ ，in contrast with shallow lakes and streams in which signs of photochemical weathering and natural $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels are lower. Interestingly,a distinct pattern was found in the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation rate, which was higher in streams and shallow lakes than in deep lake water, where substrate could have limited the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation during incubation.

The natural potential for $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation of the different natural water studied appears to relate mostly to the natural levels of DOC and the quality of the DOM pool. At higher DOC levels, such as in shallow lakes, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ production was directly related to DOC. In contrast, at the low DOC levels present in deep lakes and mountain streams, two patterns were distinguished through DOM quality properties. In streams, the CDOM is composed of high molecular weight/size humic compounds that promote higher $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation rate, suggesting a higher photo-reactivity of the substrate. In contrast, the low $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ formation rate recorded in the surface water of deep lakes likely relates to the lower DOM reactivity resulting from the higher relative contribution of small and highly processed compounds to the DOM pool.
