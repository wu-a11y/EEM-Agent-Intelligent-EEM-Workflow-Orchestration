The ecological risks of biodegradable microplastics (BMPs) to soil ecosystems have received increasing attention. This study investigates the impacts of polylactic acid microplastics (PLA-MPs) and polybutylene adipate terephthalate microplastics (PBAT-MPs) on soil properties of black soil (BS) and fluvo-aquic soil (FS) under three water conditions including dry (Dry), flooded (FL), and alternate wetting and drying (AWD). The results show that BMPs exhibited more evident aging under Dry and AWD conditions compared to FL condition. However, BMPs aging under FL condition induced more substantial changes in soil properties, especially dissolved organic carbon (DOC) concentrations, than under Dry and AWD conditions. BMPs also increased the humification degree of soil dissolved organic matter (DOM), particularly in BS. Metagenomic analysis of PBAT-MPs treatments showed different changes in microbial community structure depending on soil moisture. Under Dry conditions, PBAT-MPs enhance the ammonium-producing process of soil microbial communities. Genes related to N nitrification and benzene degradation were enriched under AWD conditions. In contrast, PBAT-MPs do not change the abundance of genes related to the N cycle under FL conditions but significantly reduce genes related to benzene degradation. This reduction in benzene degradation genes under FL condition might potentially slow down the degradation of PBAT-MPs, and could lead to temporary accumulation of benzene-related intermediates. These findings highlight the complex interactions between BMPs, soil properties, and microbial communities, emphasizing the need for comprehensive evaluations of BMPs’ environmental impacts under varying soil water conditions.

# 1. Introduction

The widespread use of plastic products, coupled with poor management of plastic waste, has led to substantial accumulation of plastic debris in the environment [1]. These waste plastics are resistant to degradation under natural conditions; however, they can still break down into smaller particles through processes such as physical abrasion, photodegradation, oxidative degradation, and biodegradation [2]. Plastic debris smaller than $5 \mathrm { m m }$ is referred to as microplastics (MPs) [3, 4]. MPs are not only widely distributed globally but have also been proven to affect the growth and reproduction of animals and plants [5, 6]. Recent advancements have led to the development of biodegradable plastics (BPs), designed to mitigate the long-term environmental impacts of conventional plastics [7]. Although BPs are designed to degrade faster, they can still persist in the environment long enough to form biodegradable microplastics (BMPs) more quickly [8]. Thus, understanding the environmental risks associated with BMPs is crucial for assessing BPs’ viability as a sustainable alternative to conventional plastics.

BMPs in the environment undergo aging processes due to mechanical forces, sunlight radiation, thermal radiation, and biodegradation [9]. BMPs generally exhibit more significant aging than non-biodegradable MPs. For example, BMPs such as polyhydroxyalkanoates and polycaprolactones demonstrate faster photoaging rates and better fragmentation compared to non-biodegradable MPs like polystyrene and polyurethane [10]. In soil environment, the degradation of BMPs by microorganisms is particularly important. Microorganisms initially colonize the surface of BMPs and subsequently secrete extracellular enzymes, which facilitate the depolymerization of the polymers into low molecular weight compounds [11]. The degradation rate of BMPs in soil is influenced by soil conditions, which affects the microbial activity and the efficiency of polymer-degrading enzymes [8,12]. For instance, the degradation rate of polybutylene adipate terephthalate (PBAT) film is linked to soil type [12]. Similarly, Huang et al. [13] found that adding wheat straw reduced pH, increased dissolved organic matter (DOM) content and enhanced protease activity of soil, leading to greater chrominance, hydrophilicity, and roughness of polylactic acid MPs (PLA-MPs). These studies emphasize the necessity of studying $\mathbf { B M P s } ^ { \prime }$ aging characteristics under diverse soil conditions.

The pervasive presence of MPs in soils has raised concerns due to their potential to change soil properties and affect microbial communities [14,15]. Compared with non-biodegradable MPs (such as polyethylene and polypropylene), BMPs change more actively in soil and may have greater impacts on soil ecosystems. In the process of degradation, BMPs release organic carbon intermediates faster, significantly increasing the content of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) in soil compared to traditional MPs [16]. The degradation of BMPs in soil is mainly carried out by soil microorganisms, which in turn alters the structure of microbial communities in the surrounding soil [17]. The addition of BMPs transforms the microplastisphere (the interface between soil and microplastic particles) into a soil hotspot where carbon and nutrient turnover is greatly enhanced, mainly due to the acceleration of microbial biomass and activity [18]. Sun et al. [19] found that BMPs significantly changed the composition of microbial communities, with DOC derived from PLA-MPs and polybutylene succinate MPs (PBS-MPs) increasing the proportion of eutrophic bacteria. However,

BMPs may have different effects under varying soil and natural conditions, necessitating broader evaluations of their impacts on soil and microbial communities.

Soil moisture is a critical factor influencing soil properties and microbial activity. Changes in soil moisture can significantly impact soil structure, nutrient availability, and microbial community composition [20]. Moisture levels affect the diffusion of oxygen and nutrients, essential for microbial metabolism. In Dry conditions, microbial activity tends to decrease due to limited water availability, whereas in saturated soils, anaerobic conditions can prevail, leading to different microbial processes and community shifts [21]. Additionally, soil moisture influences the physical properties of the soil, such as porosity and aggregation, which in turn affect root growth and soil aeration [22]. However, most studies examining the effects of BMPs on soil have been conducted under fixed moisture conditions. Rauscher et al. [23] and Liu et al. [24] studied the effects of BMPs on soil properties and microbial communities under conditions of $4 0 \%$ soil water holding capacity and $1 8 - 2 0 \%$ soil moisture content, respectively. Inubushi et al. [25] investigated the effect of BPs on greenhouse gas emission and paddy rice growth under flooding condition. Few studies have compared the effects of BMPs on soil properties and microbial communities across different moisture conditions.

In this study, we hypothesized that (1) BMPs would age more significantly under unsaturated conditions due to higher biological activity and resource utilization efficiency of soil microorganisms under aerobic conditions; (2) BMPs would have a greater impact on soil properties under flooded conditions, as BMPs degradation products are not easily utilized by microorganisms in time under anaerobic conditions and accumulate; and (3) BMPs would recruit different degrading bacteria in the soil under different water conditions, thereby changing the structure and function of the soil bacterial community. To verify these hypotheses, we compared the aging characteristics of PLA-MPs and PBAT MPs (PBAT-MPs) in soil under different moisture conditions and their effects on soil properties and microbial communities. The study was conducted using two types of soil — black soil (BS) and fluvoaquic soil (FS) — under three moisture conditions: two unsaturated (60 $\%$ maximum water holding capacity (MWHC) (Dry) and alternate wetting and drying (AWD)) and one saturated (flooding (FL)). PLA-MPs and PBAT-MPs were selected as typical BMPs since they accounted for 59.3 $\%$ of the total biodegradable plastic production in 2021 [26]. While mixtures of PLA and PBAT are common, studying PLA and PBAT individually allows for a clearer understanding of their distinct impacts and behaviors. We selected BS and FS due to their significantly different organic matter contents and pH values, which can provide comprehensive insights into the aging of BMPs across various soil environments. The Dry condition is common during farmland farming, while the FL and AWD conditions usually occur in paddy fields and farmlands with frequent irrigation or rainfall [20]. This research aims to provide new insights into the effects of BMPs on soils under a wider range of natural conditions.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Experimental soil and BMPs

The size of PLA-MPs and PBAT-MPs obtained by SEM were $1 4 6 . 7 4 \pm$

65.18 and $1 6 9 . 9 9 \pm 8 1 . 7 1 \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ respectively, as described in our previous study [27]. The preparation of BMPs and soil (BS and FS) are described in the Supplementary Information (Text S1). The physicochemical properties of the BS and FS are shown in Table S1.

# 2.2. Soil incubation

In the incubation experiment, BMPs-polluted soil (PLA-MPs or PBATMPs) was prepared using a step-by-step amplification method described in our previous research [28]. The group without BMPs addition was designated as the control (labeled as BLK), while the groups with the addition of PLA-MPs and PBAT-MPs were labeled PLA and PBAT, respectively. The dosage of BMPs was set at $2 \%$ (w/w), considering the field concentrations of plastic debris in agricultural soils and the dosages used in similar incubation studies [29,30]. This concentration was also selected to model potential future conditions, taking into account the projected long-term accumulation of BMPs in soil environments [31]. In a worst-case scenario, where the annual use of mulch in surface-cultivated soils (greater than $2 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ deep) can reach up to $0 . 0 1 6 2 \%$ (w/w) [32] and the degradation rate of some biodegradable mulch is less than $0 . 5 ~ \%$ per year [33], it is estimated that BMPs in the soil will reach a concentration of $2 \% \mathrm { ( w / w ) }$ in approximately 195 years. Three water conditions (Dry, AWD, and FL) were set up. Briefly, soil moisture under Dry condition was maintained at $6 0 ~ \%$ MWHC; under AWD condition, soil was first flooded, allowed to dry naturally for seven days, and then re-flooded; and under FL condition, soil was kept flooded. Each treatment was replicated three times. All soil samples were incubated at room temperature for 84 days. More details about the incubation experiment are described in Text S2. After incubation, $3 0 ~ g$ of fresh soil samples were collected and freeze-dried to analyze soil physicochemical properties and BMPs, and $_ { 5 } \mathrm { ~ g ~ }$ of fresh soil was collected and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until DNA extraction.

# 2.3. Extraction and characterization of BMPs

BMPs were extracted from soil samples using an improved method by Li et al. [34]. Detailed steps for BMPs extraction are described in Text S3. Detailed steps for BMPs extraction are described in Text S3. The morphology of the BMPs was identified by scanning electron microscopy (SEM, GeminiSEM 300, ZEISS, Germany). The surface chemical composition was analyzed by attenuated total reflection Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (ATR-FTIR, Nicolet 6700, Thermo Fisher, USA) and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Thermo Scientific K-Alpha, USA). The molar mass of BMPs were measured by using gel permeation chromatography (GPC, Waters ACQUITY APCTM System, Singapore) (Text S4).

# 2.4. Analysis of physicochemical properties of soil

Soil pH was determined at a soil: water ratio of 1:2.5 using a pH electrode [35]. Soil organic matter (SOM) content was measured using the external-heat potassium dichromate oxidation colorimetric method [35]. DOC was extracted by shaking for $4 \mathrm { h }$ at a soil: water ratio of 1:10 [36]. The extract was centrifuged at $2 8 0 0 ~ g$ for $2 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , filtered with a $0 . 4 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ polyethersulfone filter, and analyzed using a TOC analyzer (TOC-V CPN, Shimadzu, Japan).

# 2.5. DOM spectroscopic characterization

The excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence spectra of the DOM were measured with an Agilent Cary Eclipse spectrophotometer. The scan program was configured as follows: increasing excitation wavelength from 250 to $5 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ at $5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ increments and emission spectra from 280 to $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ at $2 \ \mathrm { n m }$ increments. Ultrapure water was used as a blank to eliminate the impacts of Raman scatter [37]. The DOC sample was diluted to $< 1 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ to minimize inner filtering effects [38].

The R package "staRdom" [39] was used to perform blank correction, remove and interpolate scattering, Raman normalization, smoothing, calculation of fluorescent indices (fluorescent index (FI) [40], humic index (HIX) [41], and biological index (BIX) [42]), and PARAFAC modeling for EEM fluorescence spectra. The matching of PARAFAC modeling results was done on the OpenFluor website (https://openfluor. lablicate.com/).

# 2.6. Metagenomic sequencing and bioinformatic analysis

Detailed steps for metagenomic sequencing and bioinformatics analysis are provided in Text S5. The sequencing data were analyzed on the free online platform of Majorbio Cloud Platform (www.majorbio. com). Metagenomic assembly analysis was carried out on the Kbase platform (https://www.kbase.us/) [43], and the processing flow can be viewed with Narrative number 155584. The detailed process of assembly analysis is provided in Text S6.

# 2.7. Data analysis

Two-dimensional correlation spectroscopy (2D-COS) analysis of the FTIR spectrum was performed using the corr2D package in R. The assumptions of normal distribution and homogeneity of variance were tested by Shapiro-Wilk and Levene tests for parameters including soil pH, SOM, DOC, FI, BIX, HIX, and the content of DOM forms produced by PARAFAC modeling prior to further statistical analysis. One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Tukey post-hoc multiple comparisons was employed to compare the significance of differences among treatments (PLA, PBAT, and BLK) under various soil and water conditions when data met the assumptions; otherwise, the Kruskal-Wallis test and post-hoc Dunn test were used. A three-way ANOVA was used to investigate the interactive effects of soil type, water condition, and BMPs type on pH, SOM, DOC, FI, BIX, and HIX. The principal component analysis (PCA) was performed using the R package “FactoMineR.” Additionally, a two-way ANOVA was utilized to examine the interactive effects of water condition and BMPs type on the ACE and Shannon indices. The t-test was used to compare the difference in ACE index, Shannon index, and the phylum abundance of microorganisms between treatments. Nonmetric Multidimensional Scaling (NMDS) based on the Bray-Curtis distance of Phylum was used to evaluate the clustering of microbial communities. The co-occurrence network of microbial communities based on Spearman correlation analysis was visualized using Gephi (v. 0.1). The significantly different functional genes and metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs) abundances between the group without BMPs (marked as BLK) and the group with PBAT-MPs addition (marked as PBAT) under each water condition (Dry, AWD, and FL) were assessed by log2-fold changes (LCFs) $( p < 0 . 5 )$ using the DESeq2 package in R. The R package “plspm” was used to construct a partial least squares path model (PLS-PM). The hierarchies of bins was visualized using Krona [44]. All statistical analysis and image production without description were conducted in R (v. 4.3.3) and Adobe Illustrator 2022.

# 3.1. Aging of BMPs in soil

After incubation, holes or cracks appeared on the surface of PLAMPs, whereas the surface of PBAT-MPs remained relatively smooth (Fig. 1A). Previous studies have also observed more pronounced morphological changes in PLA films in sediments compared to PBAT, which is attributed to the faster decrease in the molecular weight of PLA [45]. However, further analysis revealed changes in the surface chemical structures of both PLA-MPs and PBAT-MPs (Fig. 1B and C and Fig. 2). The ATR− FTIR spectra of BMPs before and after aging are shown in Fig. S2. Further 2D-COS analysis shows that the autopeaks along the diagonals in the synchronous map of PLA-MPs are located at 1039 − 1180 and $1 7 4 9 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Fig. 1B), while the autopeaks in the synchronous map of PBAT-MPs are located at 728, 1017, 1101, 1249 and $1 7 1 3 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ (Fig. 1C). The appearance of these autopeaks indicates significant changes in the $_ { \mathrm { c - O } }$ and $\scriptstyle \mathrm { C = } 0$ groups on the PLA-MPs surface, and in the aromatic $\mathrm { C - H }$ groups, C− O, and $\scriptstyle { \mathrm { C } } = 0$ groups on the PBAT-MPs surface [48].

# 3.2. Effects of BMPs on soil properties

The effects of BMPs on soil pH and DOC varied depending on both soil types and water conditions, as indicated by the highly significant three-way interaction effects of soil type, water condition, and BMPs type (Table S2). Additionally, there were significant two-way interaction effects among soil type, water condition, and BMPs type (Table S2). Under varying water regimes, BMPs significantly impacted the pH of BS (Fig. 3A and D). PLA-MPs and PBAT-MPs significantly reduced the pH of BS from 5.45 to 4.94 and 5.15, respectively, under Dry condition. PLAMPs significantly elevated the pH of BS from 6.32 to 6.51 under FL condition. Previous studies have shown that BMPs can increase [54-56], decrease [24,57], or not change soil pH [55,58], depending on factors like soil type, MPs concentration, and size. Our research not only found that BMPs alter soil pH based on soil type, but also found that soil water conditions also play an important role. The assumption that BMPs-induced pH changes result solely from the acids produced during BMPs aging does not explain the observed $\mathsf { p H }$ increase caused by PLA-MPs under FL conditions [24]. BMPs may also modify soil pH by altering microbial communities and selectively adsorbing negatively or positively charged substances [59], which leads to different outcomes based on soil type and water conditions.

For SOM, BMPs addition significantly increased its content under varying water regimes, with PBAT-MPs having a more pronounced effect (increasing BS and FS by $1 1 . 3 3 \%$ and $4 0 . 0 7 \%$ on average, respectively) compared to PLA-MPs (increasing BS and FS by $6 . 2 3 \%$ and $1 6 . 2 0 \%$ on average, respectively) (Fig. 3B and E). Strong oxidizing agents used to measure SOM may release organic compounds from BMPs, potentially overestimating SOM values [60]. At present, it remains controversial whether BMPs-derived SOM should be included in soil SOM measurements [61]. Hence, we further analyzed soil DOM, a crucial component of SOM.

# 3.3. Effects of BMPs on soil DOM composition

To further understand the impact of BMPs on soil DOM composition under varying water conditions, we analyzed EEM spectral data from extracted samples. FI, BIX and HIX are indicators describing the origin, autotrophic productivity and humification of DOM, respectively [66]. Based on the results of a three-way ANOVA, soil type, water condition, and BMPs type had no significant impact on FI (Table S3). BIX was primarily influenced by soil type, with a significant interaction between soil type and water condition (Table S3). The FI of all samples being lower than 1.5 and the stable BIX index under varying water conditions (Fig. 4A, D, B, and E) suggest that DOM in BS and FS is predominantly terrestrial, with microbially derived organics unchanged by BMPs [16]. The effects of BMPs on HIX varied depending on both soil type and water condition, as indicated by the significant three-way interaction effects among soil type, water condition, and BMPs type (Table S3). PLA-MPs and PBAT-MPs significantly increased the HIX index of BS DOM by $1 . 4 9 \%$ and $4 . 3 2 \%$ , respectively, under Dry conditions, and by $5 . 0 4 \%$ and $7 . 0 7 \%$ , respectively, under AWD conditions. However, PLA-MPs significantly reduced BS DOM HIX under FL conditions by $3 . 0 3 \%$ , and FS DOM HIX under AWD and FL conditions by $1 . 3 6 \%$ and $0 . 8 8 \%$ , respectively (Fig. 4C and F).

The PARAFAC model generates seven components (Fig. S5) from the EEM database of all samples, detailed in Table S4. C1 and C2 are terrestrial humic-like components, C3 is related to fulvic acid extracted from soil, C4 belongs to microbial humic-like components, and C7 is a protein-like component. C5 and C6 have not been previously reported. PLA-MPs and PBAT-MPs significantly increased the relative abundance of C1 and C2 (Fig. 4G), consistent with the increased humification of BS DOM under Dry and AWD conditions (Fig. 4C). The increase in C1 and C2 proportions can be directly attributed to DOM produced by BMPs, with previous studies confirming that a significant portion of DOM from PLA-MPs and PBAT-MPs is classified as terrestrial humic-like components [67,68]. Compared to PLA-MPs, PBAT-MPs not only increased BS DOM humification more but also significantly reduced the protein-like component C7 under Dry conditions (Fig. 4G). Previous studies also reported similar conclusions, and they attributed this to BMPs-induced microbial transformation of unstable organic matter [66,69]. Additionally, BMPs-derived DOM is more unstable and bioavailable than natural DOM, enhancing the electron transport capacity of soil DOM and potentially stimulating its mineralization [68]. Du et al. [70] found that aged PBAT-MPs can strongly adsorb microbial humic-like components like C4, which may explain the significant decrease in the C4 proportion in BS DOM under BMPs action (Fig. 4G). Interestingly, BMPs’ influence on FS DOM components under different water conditions shows an opposite trend to that of BS. For FS, the increase in C1 and C2 proportions and the decrease in C7 proportions did not appear under Dry and AWD conditions but occurred under FL conditions (Fig. 4G). This opposite phenomenon in FS may be due to its low content and high humification of DOM. Stable organic matter components were completely converted in FS, and DOM produced by BMPs was rapidly mineralized under Dry and AWD conditions, while weak microbial activity under FL conditions allowed the accumulation of DOM produced by BMPs. We also found that the significant increase of DOC content in BS under FL conditions (Fig. 3C) did not change the DOM composition as drastically as under Dry and AWD conditions (Fig. 4G). This can be attributed to the limited aging of BMPs (Fig. 2) and the high concentration of DOM in BS under FL conditions (Fig. 3C).

# 3.4. Effects of BMPs on soil microbial community

We investigated the effects of PBAT and PLA treatments on soil physicochemical properties and DOM components using PCA. The PCA revealed that the first two components accounted for $6 8 . 5 ~ \%$ of the variance, the first principal component explaining $5 0 \%$ and the second principal component explaining $1 8 . 5 ~ \%$ (Fig. S6). PBAT-MPs treatment exhibited a more significant deviation from the treatment without BMPs, as evidenced by a Euclidean distance of 1.38 in comparison to PLA-MPs treatment, which had a distance of 0.80 (Fig. S6). Consequently, we examined the influence of PBAT-MPs on the soil microbial community in different water conditions due to their more pronounced effect on soil properties and DOM composition. The BS microbial community richness (ACE index) was significantly affected by both water condition and PBAT-MPs treatment, whereas the microbial community diversity (Shannon index) was significantly influenced solely by water condition (Table S5). No interaction effects between water condition and PBATMPs treatment on either the ACE or Shannon indices were observed (Table S5). PBAT-MPs significantly decreased BS ACE index under AWD conditions but had no significant effect under Dry and FL conditions (Fig. 5A). PBAT-MPs did not significantly affect Shannon index under the three water conditions (Fig. S7). Previous studies have shown that excessive $( 5 \% )$ PBAT-MPs reduce soil bacterial community richness and diversity [71]. We found that $2 \%$ PBAT-MPs only negatively affected BS richness under AWD conditions, indicating the dose-dependent effect of BMPs on microbial community richness and diversity.

NMDS analysis showed the six treatments were clearly distinguished by different water conditions, with PBAT causing greater changes to the BS microbial community under FL conditions compared to Dry and AWD conditions (Fig. 5B). The most dominant microorganisms at the phylum level in BS are Proteobacteria, Actinobacteria, Acidobacteria, Bacteroidota, Gemmatimonadetes, Firmicutes, Verrucomicrobia, Chloroflexi, Ignavibacteriae, and Thaumarchaeota (Fig. 5C). Under Dry conditions, PBATMPs significantly reduced Acidobacteria abundance by $1 0 . 3 8 ~ \%$ . Under AWD conditions, PBAT-MPs significantly increased Bacteroidota abundance by $3 0 . 0 7 \%$ and significantly decreased Ignavibacteriae abundance by $5 3 . 8 5 \%$ . Under FL conditions, PBAT-MPs significantly increased Bacteroidota and Firmicutes by $2 1 3 . 0 5 \%$ and $4 5 . 7 7 \%$ , respectively, while significantly reducing Proteobacteria and Gemmatimonadetes by $2 9 . 6 7 \%$ and $2 1 . 5 0 \%$ , respectively (Fig. 5C and Table S6). These results indicate that PBAT-MPs affect microbial community composition more severely under FL conditions. Wang et al. [2] also found that PLA-MPs caused more significant changes in microbial community composition in flooded soils than in non-flooded soils, likely due to the enrichment of degrading bacteria and the production of more unstable carbon by BMPs under flooded conditions. Bacteroidota and Firmicutes have been proven to degrade biodegradable plastics (PLA/PBAT) under anaerobic conditions [72,73], and their significant increase under FL conditions indicates their important role in PBAT-MPs degradation in flooded soil. Notably, previous studies have shown that Proteobacteria and Gemmatimonadetes play a significant role in PBAT degradation in soil [74,75]. However, most previous studies were conducted under non-flooded conditions, and our results suggest that Proteobacteria and Gemmatimonadetes cannot reproduce and degrade PBAT-MPs well under FL conditions.

The co-occurrence network of microbial communities at the class level is shown in Fig. 5D. After adding PBAT-MPs, the average degree (reflecting the complexity of the bacterial community network) decreased from 17.52 to 16.16, 18.56 to 17.44, and 17.96 to 16.88 under Dry, AWD, and FL conditions, respectively. Our results are similar to previous reports that $2 . 0 \%$ PBAT film residues significantly reduce soil bacterial community complexity and stability [76]. The proportion of positive correlations between edges decreased from $5 6 . 4 6 \%$ to $4 8 . 3 9 \%$ under AWD conditions and increased from $4 8 . 1 1 \ \%$ to $5 1 . 0 8 \%$ under FL. This may be due to PBAT-MPs degradation enriching the flooded soil into a resource-rich region, transforming the microbiome into a more mutually beneficial symbiotic relationship [77].

Numerous studies have reported the significant effect of BMPs on the soil N cycle [78,79] and the production of terephthalic acid after PBAT degradation [80]. We further analyzed changes in microbial community N cycle and benzene degradation-related functional genes. Fig. 5E shows the effect of PBAT-MPs on the expression of functional genes involved in N cycling (N fixation and degradation, nitrification and denitrification, assimilatory nitrate reduction (ANRA), and dissimilatory nitrate reduction (DNRA)) and benzene degradation. Three genes related to N fixation (nifS), degradation (GDH2) and ANRA (nirB) significantly increased under Dry conditions due to PBAT-MPs. The increase in labile carbon, including PBAT monomers (such as adipic acid, 1,4-butanediol, and terephthalic acid) and oligomers, produced by PBAT-MPs degradation makes microorganisms need rich ammonium as an N source for biomass assimilation, promoting growth and thus N fixation, degradation, and ANRA [81]. Seven genes related to N nitrification (amoA, amoB, and amoC) and benzene degradation (dmpN, dmpL, dmpO, and dmpK) significantly increased under AWD conditions. This may be due to PBAT-MPs inducing PBAT-degrading bacteria (Bacteroidota) reproduction (Fig. 5C). Bacteroidota plays a crucial role in decomposing organic matter and releasing ammonium, providing substrates for nitrifying bacteria, indirectly promoting N nitrification [82]. Four genes related to N fixation (nifS), degradation (ureC, GDH2), and denitrification (narG) significantly decreased under AWD conditions due to ammonium accumulation inhibiting ammonium production [2]. Unlike AWD conditions, PBAT-MPs significantly reduced one gene (dmpP) and four genes (dmpN, dmpO, dmpP, and dmpK) related to benzene degradation under Dry and FL conditions, respectively. The degradation of PBAT-MPs in soil primarily produces three monomers including adipic acid, 1,4-butanediol, and terephthalic acid [74,83]. Thereinto, terephthalic acid is more resistant to microbial mineralization [84]. Ma et al. [80] observed the accumulation of terephthalic acid in localized soil areas after 180 days of PBAT film burial. The significant suppression of benzene-degrading functional genes (Fig. 5E) may lead to the continuous accumulation of benzene-containing oligomers and monomers (terephthalic acid) derived from PBAT-MPs under FL condition. Given that terephthalic acid exhibits higher biotoxicity [83,85], its long-term accumulation under FL condition could pose a greater risk to crops such as rice.

A PLS-PM was constructed to further explore the effects of PBAT-MPs aging on soil properties and microbial communities under different moisture conditions (Fig. 6). The model’s manifest and latent variables are detailed in Table S7. The aging of PBAT-MPs exhibited significant effects on soil pH, which were negative under Dry and FL conditions, but positive under AWD conditions (Fig. 6). Conversely, PBAT-MPs’ aging significantly increased soil DOC under Dry and FL conditions, while decreasing that under AWD conditions. Notably, the HIX of soil DOM was significantly and positively affected by PBAT-MPs’ aging across all moisture regimes. Under Dry and FL conditions, microbial community structure was primarily impeded by increases in soil DOC and HIX. In AWD conditions, microbial community structure was primarily impeded by increases in soil pH and HIX. Changes in soil properties did not significantly affect benzene degradation under Dry condition. However, benzene degradation was adversely affected under both AWD and FL conditions. Specifically, in AWD condition, the degradation was hindered by changes in the HIX, while in FL condition, soil pH and DOC posed significant obstacles.

# 3.5. Effects of BMPs on individual microbial species in soil

To explore individual microbes in soil affected by PBAT-MPs, we carried out metagenomic assembly analysis. Thirty high-quality MAGs were reconstructed using metagenomic assembly, with twenty-eight classified as bacteria and the remaining two as archaea (Fig. 7A and Table S8). Bin2, classified as Rhodococcus qingshengii (Table S8), reportedly secretes carboxylesterase and degrades PBAT [86]. Bin2′s abundance significantly increased under Dry conditions (Fig. 7B and S8), indicating Rhodococcus qingshengii’s crucial role in PBAT-MPs degradation under Dry conditions. Under AWD conditions, bin8, bin19, bin20, and bin21 significantly increased with PBAT-MPs addition (Fig. 7B and S8). Bin8, classified as Geothrix (Table S8), consists of iron-reducing bacteria widely distributed in anaerobic environments [87]. Bin8′s significant increase under AWD conditions indicates that soil Fe reduction processes are promoted, likely due to the unstable carbon source generated by PBAT-MPs degradation. Bin19 and bin21 belong to Luteimonas and UBA2475 (Table S8), respectively, with reported potential to degrade PBAT and polyester [88,89]. Bin20 belongs to Nevskia (Table S8), which shows the ability to degrade organic pollutants, suggesting bin20′s role in decomposing PBAT depolymerization products. Bin28, bin29, and bin30, all belonging to Bacteroidales (Table S8), significantly increased under FL conditions (Fig. 7B and S8), indicating their role in PBAT degradation under flooded conditions [90]. Other MAGs, including bin7, bin9, bin10, bin11, bin14, and bin27, were significantly reduced by PBAT-MPs (Fig. 7B and S8), indicating their disadvantage in competing with PBAT-degrading bacteria and those benefiting from PBAT aging.

# 4. Conclusion

Understanding the environmental fate of BMPs is critical as society transitions toward using biodegradable plastics to mitigate plastic pollution. This study provides a comprehensive analysis of BMPs (PLAMPs and PBAT-MPs), and their effects on soil properties under different water conditions. As anticipated, BMPs aged more significantly in unsaturated soils, but this does not significantly impact soil properties. Conversely, the properties of flooded soils are more affected by BMPs due to the inability of degradation products to mineralize promptly under anaerobic conditions. Furthermore, BMPs increase the humification degree of soil DOM, especially in soil rich in organic matter. Further metagenomic analysis of PBAT-MPs revealed their significant impact on microbial communities. PBAT-MPs enrich the corresponding aerobic or anaerobic bacteria suitable for its degradation under different water conditions, further changing the soil microbial community’s function. The microbial community’s function benefits the mineralization of PBAT degradation products under AWD conditions, but not under FL conditions. Our study highlights the crucial role of water conditions in the impact of BMPs on soil properties and microbial communities. However, it’s important to note that real-world conditions are more variable and complex than our controlled experiments. Factors such as the frequency of moisture fluctuations, the depth of flooding, the severity of drought, and the duration of varying moisture conditions could all modulate the effects we observed. Future field studies across different precipitation conditions and cultivation practices are essential to validate and extend our findings to more diverse real-world scenarios.

Furthermore, this study only conducted a semi-quantitative analysis of the aging of BMPs, and we do not know the extent of BMPs degradation under different water conditions. Additionally, it is unclear whether changes in soil DOM components are directly caused by the degradation products of BMPs or indirectly caused by priming effects. Future research could utilize pyrolysis− gas chromatography− mass spectrometry (Pyr-GC/MS), quantitative protonnuclear magnetic resonance (q $\mathbf { \Gamma } ^ { 1 } \mathrm { H N M R } )$ , or isotope labeling techniques to quantify BMPs, and Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometry (FT-ICRMS) for high-resolution analysis of soil DOM.

# Environmental implication

BMPs pose emerging ecological risks due to their complex interactions with soil properties and microbial communities. This study reveals that BMPs significantly altered soil pH, SOM, and DOC levels, which was also controlled by soil moisture conditions. Moreover, BMPs enhanced the humification of soil DOM. The influence of PBAT-MPs on nitrogen and benzene degradation processes was mediated by moisturedependent alterations in microbial communities. These findings are crucial for developing BMPs management strategies, ensuring they provide a sustainable alternative to conventional plastics while mitigating potential environmental harm, especially under varying water regimes.
