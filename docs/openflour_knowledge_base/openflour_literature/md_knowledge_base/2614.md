# DOM degradation by light and microbes along theYukon River‑coastal ocean continuum

Brice K. Grunert1\*, Maria Tzortziou1 , Patrick Neale2 , Alana Menendez1 & Peter Hernes3

The Arctic is experiencing rapid warming, resulting in fundamental shifts in hydrologic connectivity and carbon cycling. Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is a signifcant component of the Arctic and global carbon cycle, and signifcant perturbations to DOM cycling are expected with Arctic warming. The impact of photochemical and microbial degradation, and their interactive efects, on DOM composition and remineralization have been documented in Arctic soils and rivers. However, the role of microbes, sunlight and their interactions on Arctic DOM alteration and remineralization in the coastal ocean has not been considered, particularly during the spring freshet when DOM loads are high, photoexposure can be quite limited and residence time within river networks is low. Here, we collected DOM samples along a salinity gradient in the Yukon River delta, plume and coastal ocean during peak river discharge immediately after spring freshet and explored the role of UV exposure, microbial transformations and interactive efects on DOM quantity and composition. Our results show: (1) photochemical alteration of DOM signifcantly shifts processing pathways of terrestrial DOM, including increasing relative humifcation of DOM by microbes by ${ \bf > 1 0 \% }$ ; (2) microbes produce humic like material that is not optically distinguishable from terrestrial humics; and (3) size-fractionation of the microbial community indicates a size-dependent role for DOM remineralization and humifcation of DOM observed through modeled PARAFAC components of fuorescent DOM, either through direct or community efects. Field observations indicate apparent conservative mixing along the salinity gradient; however, changing photochemical and microbial alteration of DOM with increasing salinity indicate changing DOM composition likely due to microbial activity. Finally, our fndings show potential for rapid transformation of DOM in the coastal ocean from photochemical and microbial alteration, with microbes responsible for the majority of dissolved organic matter remineralization.

Te major Arctic Rivers—Ob’, Yenisey, Lena, Kolyma, Yukon and Mackenzie—deliver over $1 8 \mathrm { T g }$ of terrestriallyderived dissolved organic carbon (DOC) per year to the coastal ocean, with nearly half of this material delivered during the spring freshet1 . Long-term observations in Arctic rivers have confrmed increases and a recent acceleration in total discharge accompanying rapid warming in the Arctic2,3 with concurrent increases in total DOC export1 . Tis intensifcation of the hydrologic cycle and altered fow paths from thawing permafrost afects both the timing and composition of organic matter delivered to aquatic systems4,5 ; however, how these factors will impact dissolved organic matter (DOM) cycling in Arctic rivers and the coastal ocean is not known. DOM is an important source of carbon and nutrients for microbes in Arctic ecosystems6,7 , with use and remineralization of DOM dependent on its composition8,9 . Interactive exposure to sunlight and processing by the microbial community can alter DOM composition and energy content, with continued re-working of the DOM pool contingent on microbial community composition and enzymatic capabilities10,11. Studies in Arctic soils and rivers have shown competitive degradation of high molecular weight, aromatic molecules between sunlight and microbes12,13, signifcant contributions of microbial metabolic byproducts to total $\mathrm { D O C ^ { 1 4 } }$ , and increasing DOM bioavailability during transport through river networks15. However, what drives transformation and remineralization of DOM from Arctic rivers in the coastal ocean is not well understood, particularly during the spring freshet when DOM loads are high and residence time across aquatic systems is low.

DOM, and particularly the chromophoric fraction, CDOM, is the dominant bio-optical constituent of Arctic and sub-arctic surface waters, impacting light and nutrient availability, sea ice cover and ecosystem productivity16–19. In Arctic Rivers, terrestrial material, including lignin and hydroxy-benzene biomarkers, increases as a DOM source during the spring freshet7,20. Lignin concentrations correlate strongly with total DOC and CDOM absorption $( \mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( \lambda )$ , λ denotes light wavelength) across the major Arctic Rivers21,22, and tannic compounds constitute a signifcant carbon pool available for photo- and microbial alteration12,13. Lignin and partially oxidized lignin derivatives are traditionally seen as relatively refractory DOM, with North Atlantic Deep Water sourced from the Arctic Ocean still exhibiting elevated concentrations of lignin and optical properties consistent with terrestrial $\mathrm { C D O M } ^ { 2 3 , 2 4 }$ . Processing of high molecular weight terrestrial material, including lignin, is tied to proximity to source, specifc enzymes, and microbial cell size25,26. Te dependence of DOM processing and composition on larger cell-sized microbes has been documented, with high nucleic acid and larger cell sized microbes from an Arctic ford responsible for the majority of terrestrial DOM processing26. Te size-fractionation of microbes has also been shown to impact DOM composition, with a larger size fraction encompassing the entire microbial community in mesocosm experiments resulting in DOM composition more representative of that observed in the environment27. Tese efects point to the role of specifc microbial functional traits, clades, and community efects, that ultimately shape the transformation of DOM and highlight the role of larger microbes in DOM processing9,28.

Microbial community composition and group abundance have explained the majority of variability in DOM assimilation in Arctic waters, indicating the importance of the entire microbial community in DOM degradation. Spatial variability in DOM processing in the coastal ocean is expected, as microbial community composition can vary strongly along shelf to open ocean transects in the Arctic29,30. Prior work has shown that environmental conditions, including temperature and salinity, strongly regulate microbial structure and function31,32. DOM source, as well as the metabolic capability of distinct microbial groups and interactive efects within the microbial community and with sunlight also play a strong role in terrestrial DOM use and remineralization across aquatic environments11,33.

Building on this past work, we hypothesized that: (1) photochemical exposure would increase net remineralization of DOC and expand available pathways for degradation of DOM by microbes; and (2) larger cell size bacteria (those uniquely present in the $0 . 7 \substack { - 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m } }$ fraction here) would more readily degrade high molecular weight (HMW) terrestrial DOM. We tested these hypotheses by observing CDOM and DOC variability in situ and assessing the impact of photochemical transformation, microbial processing and their interplay on CDOM and DOC amount and quality along the Yukon River freshwater-marine continuum—one of the six major rivers draining the terrestrial Arctic—during peak spring discharge (Fig. S1). We manipulated DOM using three experiments explicitly characterizing photochemical, microbial, and microbial afer photochemical transformation of DOM, with DOM compositional shifs assessed via changes in DOM optical properties (CDOM and fuorescent DOM) and DOM concentration assessed through changes in DOC. Dark microbial incubation experiments were treated with $< 0 . 7$ and $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ size-fractionated inocula to explore the role of diferent microbial size classes on DOM transformations, following the hypothesis that the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ inoculum would more readily degrade HMW terrestrial DOM due to the inclusion of larger microbes as well as microbes present in all other inocula26.

# Results and discussion

Photochemistry alters microbial bioavailability of DOM. Photochemical processing signifcantly altered the molecular structure of DOM and increased available microbial processing pathways, with a far greater impact on DOM composition $( > 4 0 \%$ loss of CDOM across the Yukon salinity gradient) than on DOM concentration (represented by $1 6 . 9 \mathrm { - } 2 1 . 7 \%$ DOC loss). CDOM also displayed two distinct photoreactivities across most stations, as river and plume stations (lower salinities) were consistently well-modeled with a double exponential decay function (excluding St 10, the main river mouth) and oceanic (higher salinity) stations were well-modeled with a single exponential decay function (Fig. 1; Table S1). Cumulative UV exposure during the course of the experiments ranged from 11.61 MJ $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 }$ for St 6 (salinity $= 0 . 1$ psu) to $1 5 . 0 8 \mathrm { M J } \mathrm { \ m } ^ { - 2 }$ for our oceanic end-member $\mathrm { S t } 1 2$ (salinity $= 3 1 . 2 $ psu). Not accounting for self-shading, this exposure ranged from 61 to $6 9 \%$ of incident solar irradiance, consistent with average UV exposure in the frst optical depth for clear water conditions (see “Supplementary Methods”). Afer 24 days of UV exposure, average DOC change in the $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate was $- 2 1 . 7 \pm 6 . 6 \%$ for river stations and $- 1 6 . 9 \pm 1 . 5 \%$ for plume stations (Table 1). Our photochemical incubations included the infuence of ultra-microbacteria that passed through a $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ flter (see “Methods”). Afer accounting for observed changes to $\mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ and DOC in the $< 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ dark treatment (losses of $1 - 9 . 8 \%$ and $1 1 . 3 \mathrm { - } 1 7 . 0 \%$ , respectively; Table 1), photochemical processing infuenced DOM composition $( 3 9 . 1 \substack { - 6 5 . 7 \% }$ loss in CDOM across sites) far more than concentration (DOC removal of $4 . 7 { - } 5 . 6 \%$ ). Although microbial activity can be inhibited by UV exposure and formation of reactive oxygen species13,34, our experiments used a low UV irradiance $( 5 . 6 \mathrm { W } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } )$ ) to limit such efects (see “Methods”). Te net remineralization of DOC was not signifcantly diferent between the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ dark treatment and the $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment, further indicating that some of the DOC loss in the PB experiments was due to microbial activity. DOC loss over our 24 day incubations was consistent with short-term photoincubation experiments in the coastal Arctic35, suggesting that much of the DOC removal occurred early in our experiments.

Our fuorescence results further supported our hypothesis that photochemical alteration increases microbial transformation pathways of DOM, primarily through photodegradation of humic DOM and formation of terrestrial derivatives (e.g., lignin depolymerization products). PARAFAC modeled fuorescent components displayed a consistent photochemical response across stations (Figs. 2; S2). We designated our six modeled components as visible humic (C1), long-wavelength humic (C2), UV humic (C3), degraded humic (C4), terrestrial derivatives (C5) and protein-like (C6). Increasing UV exposure led to decreasing contributions to total fuorescence of visible and UV humic components. Long-wavelength humics maintained relative contributions to total fuorescence in river stations (0 salinity) and increased in higher salinity samples, while degraded humic, terrestrial derivatives and protein-like contributions to total fuorescence increased (Fig. 2a). Visible, long-wavelength, UV and degraded humic fuorescence intensity decreased over time, relative to initial fuorescence intensity (Fig. 2c). Terrestrial derivative fuorescence intensity increased initially, then decreased, suggesting formation of this material followed by photodegradation. Protein-like DOM displayed increasing fuorescence intensity over time approaching a maximum, indicating this material is photochemically produced from a limited substrate pool (Fig. 2c). Photochemical production of this component has been observed in Arctic river DOM samples, DOM produced from photodegradation of terrestrial foc, as well as in open ocean samples, indicating a diversity of potential sources for this material7,36,37.

Te uneven decrease of degraded humics (C4) suggests that this is the most heterogeneous component. C4 matched with 14 modeled PARAFAC components on OpenFluor and is consistent with Peak A described in the literature38. C4 was previously found to positively correlate with climate (mean annual temperature and length of growing season) and nutrients at high latitudes and is typically afliated with degraded, terrestrially sourced $\mathrm { D O M } ^ { 2 2 , 3 9 }$ . C4, C5 and C6 are all consistent with molecules derived from photochemical degradation of larger molecules, as evidenced by temporary (C4, C5) and consistent (C6) increases in fuorescence intensity over the course of the photobleaching experiment. C5 and C6, however, were more consistent with small aromatic molecules, with a defned fuorescent structure consistent with tryptophan and tyrosine fuorescence. C5 matched with 36 modeled components on OpenFluor and C6 matched with seven models, including a tyrosine standard40. Considering the high concentrations of lignin in the Yukon River21,41 and the disproportionate role of lignin, tannin and depolymerization products in CDOM optics, C5 and C6 are very likely reactive intermediaries from degradation of lignin and tannin42–45. Based on results in the microbial degradation experiments, formation of these compounds in photoexposure incubations can fundamentally alter microbial use of terrestrial DOM, efectively bypassing the necessity for creation of these materials in dark incubations.

Microbial size dependency of DOM diagenesis. Microbial transformations of DOM resulted in three predominant trends (Table 1): (1) Te $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial size class removed more DOC in river and plume samples, indicating large microbes increase terrestrial DOC remineralization; (2) Relative bioavailability of DOM decreased with increasing salinity across all microbial size class inocula; and (3) Transformations of photoexposed DOM showed a distinct shif in how DOM is used by microbes afer exposure to light. Both $\bar { \mathsf { a } } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( \lambda )$ and modeled PARAFAC components displayed a drawdown from day 0 to day 3, indicative of rapid removal of biologically labile humic DOM (Figs. 3, 4).

Changes to $\mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( \lambda )$ were not consistent across or within treatments, characteristic of competing processes of consumption and production of CDOM by the microbial community (Fig. 3). Te $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial size class displayed an eventual net humifcation of CDOM (increases in C1, C2 and C3), especially in the $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment (Fig. 4). All humic components showed similar kinetics during the incubations within treatments (Fig. 4b) despite visible and long-wavelength humics being attributed to terrestrial sources38,46 and UV humics traditionally being afliated with microbes38. Ultimately, microbes break down river DOM, shuttling compounds between optically distinct components with a net humifcation of $\mathrm { D O M ^ { 7 } }$ .

In the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ treatment, gradual increases in CDOM and humic-like DOM over the 24-day incubations indicated the potential for larger bacteria to process a broader variety of DOM, including aromatic terrestrial material, either directly or through community efects. Te $< 0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ treatment fuctuated considerably between time points, suggesting that changing DOM composition and microbial community succession play a large role in production/consumption dynamics for this treatment, with diferences in change through time relative to the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ treatment indicating distinct processing.

Microbial transformation of fuorescent components was typically an order of magnitude less than change due to photobleaching. In tandem, however, photobleaching signifcantly altered trends in microbial transformation and production of fuorophores. CDOM was removed to a greater extent at day 3 and day 24 in the $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment relative to the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ dark treatment. At day 10 in the $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment, CDOM was produced (Fig. 3), suggesting photochemical breakdown of aromatic terrestrial molecules into reactive intermediaries that resulted in a short-term shif in microbial response to diversifcation of DOM composition, consistent with microbial community succession and a distinct role for larger microbes as stated in our second hypothesis47.

Te $< 0 . 2$ and $< 0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ size classes showed limited production and consumption of degraded humics (C4). Te $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ size class, both in the dark and previously photoexposed treatments, showed signifcant consumption of C4 from day 0 to day 3. However, afer 3 days, two diverging patterns emerged: (1) In the dark treatment, C4 was produced to a greater extent than humics (C1–C3), while (2) previous exposure to light resulted in stable concentrations of C4 afer day 3, suggesting either inactivity or static processing. From this, it is likely that relatively complex, photo- and biologically labile molecules contribute to C4 that require functional traits and/or community interactions unique to the $< 3 . 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ treatment (present in the $0 . 7 \substack { - 3 . 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m } }$ microbial size class) to degrade in the absence of UV exposure. In the $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment, photolabile compounds that served as precursors to C4 were photochemically altered and unavailable, while in the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ dark treatment, microbial degradation of these compounds produced C4. Tese results indicate competitive degradation of these precursor compounds by sunlight and microbes, consistent with prior studies of permafrost-derived $\mathrm { D O M } ^ { 1 3 }$ . Our results show C4 includes functionally heterogeneous fuorophores, including compounds labile to the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial community, photolabile compounds and compounds contributed by photodegradation of HMW terrestrial material. Tis is consistent with C4 constituting a more degraded component of the DOM pool, evident by C4 fuorescence dominating total fuorescence in oceanic samples. Our results are consistent with production of humics observed by Mann et al.7 , although our results also highlight how UV exposure can disrupt patterns of processing by altering precursor DOM.

Te $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment displayed the largest increase in humics over the 24-day incubation (fuorescence intensity increases of $1 9 . 5 \%$ , $1 1 . 4 \%$ and $1 8 . 6 \%$ for C1, C2 and C3, respectively, relative to initial fuorescence intensity), while all other treatments had a net increase of $\leq 5 \%$ (Fig. 4b). As seen in absorption losses, photodegradation enhanced short-term lability, resulting in enhanced DOM consumption and production of humics afer an initial drawdown (Table 1). While many studies of fuorescent components attribute C1 and C2 to terrestrial sources, our results show river and coastal ocean microbes increase individual humic contributions by $\sim 5 \%$ in dark incubations and $> 1 5 \%$ in our $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment. C2 was linked to terrestrial material in North Atlantic Deep Water, a water mass sourced from the Arctic Ocean, due to corresponding observations of elevated concentrations of lignin phenols23,24. However, C2 has also been linked to in situ production in North Atlantic waters48. Our results show that microbial reworking of DOM, especially afer exposure to light, can result in humifcation that is optically similar to fuorescent DOM (peaks A and C) traditionally viewed as terrestrial, in agreement with prior Arctic studies7 . In this context, elevated concentrations of lignin phenols that still fall below a concentration threshold for microbial use could be observed alongside visible humic material derived from more accessible, potentially non-colored, terrestrial DOM, although the necessity of terrestrial source material is not clear from our dataset25,49. Our observations of an increasingly humic signal over time are consistent with observations of successive microbial processing to a diverse, “universal” aquatic DOM pool50,51 as well as “universal” fuorescent signatures, consistent with similar functional processing of material by microbes to a convergent optical signature regardless of source52. Microbial community structure and function appear to play a role in this process, illustrated by the diferences in production of humic fuorescence between size-fractionated inocula and the role of UV exposure in accelerating production of humic material9,53. Our results illustrate that optical signatures for DOM carry a microbial processing signature that skews toward humic-like fuorescence that is more distributed in excitation-emission space than precursor DOM, consistent with a DOM pool diversifed by microbes, where structurally diverse fuorescent molecules do not directly overlap.

Relative to C1-C4, C5 and C6 were the most variable across treatments and stations (Figs. 4b, S2). Tese components are typically attributed to protein-like fuorescence (tryptophan and tyrosine-like for C5 and C6, respectively). However, other compounds with similar molecular structures have been nominated as sources of these signals in aquatic environments and are known degradation products from terrestrial vegetation43,44. Microbes, including members of the globally abundant SAR202 clade, produce enzymes that break down lignin derivatives and catechols into intermediates used in the Krebs cycle54. Catechol, which displays very similar fuorescence to our $\mathrm { C } 5 ^ { 5 5 }$ , is a reactionary intermediate in the microbial metabolism of phenols and other compounds56, as well as a structural component of siderophores used to acquire trace metals57. Tis diversity of roles lends itself to a number of structurally similar compounds contributing to C5 that would, in turn, exhibit variable behavior over time and across treatments. C5 was initially photoproduced and then subsequently photooxidized in PB experiments, with some stations showing losses of $> 2 0 \%$ (Fig. 4b). Te $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment showed initial production of C5 from day 0 to day 3, followed by consumption. It appears photodegraded compounds were further metabolized by microbes into C5 at day 3, and these aromatic intermediaries were further degraded for a net removal of C5. Across all microbial experiments, C5 was net removed over time, indicative of wide use of this aromatic material across microbial groups.

C6 was produced across all dark incubation treatments, with moderate consumption occurring for some stations and time points. Tese trends are in strong contrast to the $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment, which showed that compounds were net consumed. Tis indicates that photodegradation of complex molecules led to release of molecules with a similar structure to tyrosine. Tis material was rapidly drawn down and then stabilized, suggesting that photobleaching cleaved larger compounds into small aromatic compounds that were more bioavailable (e.g., dityrosine to tyrosine40). Overall, our fndings strongly support the hypothesis that photoexposure increases the available pathways for DOM degradation.

Spatial patterns and fate of freshet DOM in coastal waters. Results from our incubation experiments show that Yukon River CDOM is susceptible to rapid photodegradation, with subtle reworking by microbes. Historic observations in the Yukon River, including near our delta sites41 and at Pilot Station, AK $\sim 2 0 0 \mathrm { k m }$ upstream) displayed similar spring DOC, $\mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 5 0 )$ and $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values as those observed here21,22. Tese parameters largely follow established conservative mixing relationships58 (Figs. 5, S4). Relative contributions of fuorescent components were mostly stable until higher salinities $( \sim 1 6 . 2 \mathrm { p s u ) }$ , at which point the relative contribution of C1 and C2 decreased and contributions of C5 and C6 increased dramatically (Fig. 6). Relative contributions of C3 and C4 also tended to increase with salinity. Within the plume, solar exposure was quite limited (secchi disk depths $\leq 0 . 5 \ \mathrm { m }$ ). Te change in relative contributions of fuorescent components along the salinity gradient are consistent with our PB experiment results. Tese results suggest that photochemical processing of CDOM is minimal within the plume while turbidity is high; however, as material is photoexposed, DOM composition is altered in ways that enhance transformation pathways and bioavailability to microbes.

Microbial alteration of DOM subtly shifed within turbid river and plume stations despite apparently conservative relationships between salinity and DOM metrics (Table 1; Fig. S4). Once turbidity decreases, our experimental results show rapid photodegradation of aromatic, terrestrial DOM to biologically labile terrestrial derivatives that were quickly removed by microbes, indicating that the coastal ocean is a hot spot for DOM utilization in the Arctic. Timing of alteration within the plume afected both subsequent photochemical and microbial transformations, showing that timing of exposure to microbes and sunlight can afect net transformation of DOM. Diferences in consumption and production of aromatic terrestrial compounds and reactive intermediaries in our incubations, and changes in photochemical response of DOM, indicate subtle shifs in DOM composition through the river and plume (Table 1; Fig. S4). Photochemical losses of C5 were higher in the plume samples $( 2 1 . 8 \%$ on average) than in the freshwater river samples $9 \%$ on average), with diferences in rate not readily accounted for by diferences in cumulative exposure between samples (Table S2). In contrast, the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial community removed more DOC from photoexposed media in river samples than in plume samples, consistent with a loss of photochemically and biologically labile terrestrial DOM in plume samples relative to river samples. River samples displayed more production of C6 than plume samples in the PB experiment, and also displayed higher consumption rates of C6 than plume samples in the $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment. Tese fndings all support microbial alteration of this material through the river-ocean continuum and a microbial response tailored to changing DOM composition.

Overall losses in DOC were not signifcantly diferent between microbial processing of photoexposed and non-photoexposed DOM. Tus, while photochemistry alters the timing of DOM use by specifc microbial groups, including removal of biologically and photo-labile compounds, it does not necessarily alter net DOC remineralization, contrary to our hypothesis. Our results showed up to $2 0 \%$ loss of DOC during microbial processing, with the highest utilization of river and plume DOC by the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial community, relative to $< 6 \%$ change in $\mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( 3 0 0 )$ . Sunlight strongly regulates DOM composition and timing of consumption for specifc compounds; however, microbes play a critical role in DOM remineralization, with higher DOC remineralization rates by the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial community and in river samples. Prior work has indicated that larger microbes are more productive and more abundant in samples enriched with terrestrial $\mathrm { D O M } ^ { 2 6 , 5 9 }$ ; trends in consumption of C4 observed in our $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ treatments support a unique role for larger microbes either through direct processing and/or community efects. Notably, our results show compositional shifs in DOM that suggest bioavailability is linked to microbial functional traits unique to the inclusion of the $0 . 7 \substack { - 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m } }$ microbial community, which leads to higher carbon remineralization rates. Tus, our results indicate large microbes play a key role in degradation of terrestrial material in coastal Arctic waters, including competition with photochemical transformation pathways60,61, and point towards new areas of research to further elucidate the role of microbial size. A deeper understanding of how environmental conditions and changing DOM composition impact microbial community structure and function, along with knowledge on specifc degradation pathways of photosensitive aromatic compounds, will help address sensitivity of freshet DOM to remineralization in coastal Arctic waters, and residence time within the global carbon cycle.

# Methods

Study site. Sampling of the Yukon River delta, plume and adjacent coastal waters was conducted as part of a larger Arctic remote sensing of water quality feld campaign, with aims of capturing seasonal and spatial biogeochemical variability along a large Arctic river-coastal ocean salinity gradient. 28 stations were sampled immediately afer spring freshet and during peak Yukon River discharge, to assess in situ conditions. Of these 28 stations, ten were selected to assess the impact of sunlight and microbes on DOM processing across the salinity gradient (Fig. S1). Stations were chosen based on location along a direct salinity gradient (Stations 2, 4 10, salinity $= 0 . 1 \mathrm { - } 1 3 . 9 \mathrm { p s u }$ ), within the Yukon delta and major river mouths (Stations 6, 7 and 10, salinity $= 0 . 1$ psu), across anticipated within-plume salinity-induced focculation gradients (Stations 2, 4, 17, 20 and 23, salinit $\it { \Delta } \tau = 2 . 7 - 1 3 . 9 \ p s u _ { \mathrm { { . } } }$ ), immediately outside the plume (Station 24, salinity $= 2 7 . 8$ psu) and a high salinity, oceanic end-member station (Station 12, salinity $= 3 1 . 2 $ psu). Tese samples were collected on two separate legs of the spring freshet feld campaign: Yukon delta sampling was conducted out of a small vessel $( \sim 2 0 ~ \mathrm { f t } )$ , based from Alakanuk, AK from May 30 to June 3, 2019 and Yukon plume and Norton Sound sampling conducted out of Nome, AK aboard a coastal class vessel ( $\mathord { \sim } 7 0$ f) from June 9 to 11, 2019.

Sample collection. Temperature, salinity, dissolved oxygen, turbidity, chlorophyll and DOM fuorescence for each station were determined immediately using a YSI ExO2 Sonde and used as the basis for determining which stations to sample for lab incubations. Water was acquired using either a bottle grab or a peristaltic pump sampling within $1 \textrm { m }$ of the water surface, with sample bottles pre-cleaned and triple rinsed with sample water prior to collection. Samples were fltered using a Geotech $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ Medium Capacity dispos-a-flter (nonwoven substrate) either in the feld or in the lab, within $1 0 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ of sampling, and stored in amber glass bottles for CDOM/DOC analysis or 2L brown plastic Nalgene bottles for incubation media until further processing (see “Photobleaching and microbial incubations”). A total of $4 \mathrm { L }$ of water was collected at each station for incubation experiments. Inocula used for microbial degradation experiments were fltered on station through either a $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ or $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ nylon syringe flter into a sterile $1 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ brown plastic Nalgene bottle, pre-rinsed three times with sample. Water samples (including inocula) were stored at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ while on site in Alakanuk and Nome. All samples were shipped and received within $4 8 \ \mathrm { h } ,$ , with sample analysis occurring within 1–3 weeks of sampling. All incubation experiments began within 1 month of sampling.

Photobleaching and microbial incubations. Incubation media initially fltered through $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ (see “Sample collection” above) were fltered again through an $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ PES capsule flter within 24 and 10 days of storage (Alakanuk and Nome, respectively). Tere were three distinct incubation experiments: (1) 24-day photobleaching experiments of $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate (herein referred to as "PB"); (2) 24-day dark incubations of $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate incubated with no inoculum added, $< 0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ and $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ inoculum sampled from the respective station (herein referred to as 0.2, 0.7 and $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ treatments, respectively, or MD dark experiments collectively); and (3) 24-day dark incubation of $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate that was previously exposed to light (for 7 days) and incubated with $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ inoculum sampled from the respective station (herein referred to as $" 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB"). Size-fractionation was used as a simplifed proxy of microbial capability to degrade high molecular weight, aromatic terrestrial DOM. We assumed a gradient efect, where some microbes present in the $< 0 . 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ fraction would grow to become larger than this initial size threshold. However, within this approach, we also assumed that there would be microbes that even at their smallest size would still be larger than the $< 0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ threshold, and thus would be uniquely present in only the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ inoculum throughout the course of the experiment62.

Incubation experiments began with a 7 day photoexposure experiment run to acquire the 7 day timepoint used in all photobleaching results, and to provide photoexposed media for the $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB experiment.

For the photoexposure experiment, $1 6 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate was stored in a $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ Tefon (FEP) bottle, incubated in duplicate. Tis resulted in 20 bottles (2 per station). Te $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate was exposed to UV light from 280 to $4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ from the bottom of the bottle, through transmitting UVT Plexiglas (Fig. S5). Tis experimental setup allowed for $> 8 0 \%$ of light to pass through the Plexiglas sheet, with measured UV exposure in the lab approximately $6 0 \%$ of that observed in the feld, representing values approximately equivalent to average in situ exposure in the frst optical depth (see “Supplementary Methods”). Media was photoexposed for 7 days $( 1 6 8 \pm 1 \mathrm { h } )$ ). Bottles were evenly spaced along the length of each fuorescent bulb in two rows (A and B duplicate rows) initially. Samples were mixed (inverted twice) and bottles were rotated daily to ensure approximately uniform incident light exposure across bottles, as light intensity along the length of each fuorescent light bulb varied up to $1 1 \%$ at the ends of the bulbs $( < 2 \%$ variability along the middle 8 bottles; Fig. S5). Afer sub-sampling this media for optical analysis, the remaining media was used for the photobleaching and microbial degradation $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB) experiment.

During this initial 7-day period, $1 6 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate used for the PB (only light) treatment was stored for 1 week at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in duplicate $2 5 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ Tefon bottles for each station, and then exposed to light for 24 days, following the same light-exposure protocol as above. PB samples were measured for change at 1, 3, 14 and 24 days, with the 7 day timepoint provided from the frst photoincubation experiment. $3 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of water was removed from the Tefon bottle for absorption and fuorescence at each timepoint without re-fltering prior to analysis, with DOC additionally measured at the 24 day timepoint.

To examine microbial degradation, we conducted 24-day incubations of photoexposed DOM with the $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ inoculum, as well as non-photoexposed DOM with $< 0 . 7$ and $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ inocula. Tese incubations began afer the initial 7-day period (samples stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ), to allow for all 24-day incubations to occur coincidentally. Six bottles, per station, were used: (1) four $4 8 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ amber glass bottles with $4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of non-exposed $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate, two of them inoculated with $1 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $< 0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial inoculum and two inoculated with $1 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial inoculum $( ^ { \infty } 0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m } ^ { \mathfrak { n } }$ and $^ { \mathrm { { e } } } 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { { m } } ^ { \mathrm { { \prime } } }$ treatments, respectively), (2) one $4 8 0 \mathrm { m L }$ amber glass bottle with $2 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of previously photoexposed $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate, inoculated with $0 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial inoculum and (3) one $2 5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ clear glass bottle with $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of control incubation media (i.e., non-photoexposed $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate) without microbial inoculum addition, used as the experimental control. All incubations were conducted at $1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to mimic average temperatures observed in situ $( \sim 9 \mathrm { - } 1 1 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for all river and plume stations, and $8 . 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at St 12). Bottles were aerated and mixed daily by briefy removing the cap $( < 1 0 ~ s )$ , then closing and inverting the bottle twice. MD and $3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ -afer-PB samples were measured for optical change at 3, 10 and 24 days, and change in DOC at 24 days. $6 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of water was removed from sample bottles at each timepoint and the water was fltered through a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ disc flter prior to analysis for all samples, including the control.

Samples for analyzing microbial cell counts and biomass were collected on all dark incubations, with $5 \mathrm { m L }$ of sample extracted from each bottle at 1, 3, 10 and 24 days. Samples were preserved with $2 \%$ glutaradlehyde and stored at $4 ~ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until analysis on a Guava easyCyte fow cytometer. Prior to sample collection, a shared use Guava easyCyte 5 fow cytometer was identifed for use; however, upon return from the feld, the instrument was unexpectedly at sea. Upon instrument return, initial samples collected were approximately 6 weeks old. $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of sample was stained with SYBR Green I dye and cell counts were reported within the InCyte sofware, based on detection of green DNA fuorescence. As noted in the literature, the quality of the samples were likely comprised and cell counts were deemed unreliable63. Nevertheless, sample quality was deemed sufcient to indicate the presence or absence of bacterial cells. All control bottles $( 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate) displayed evidence of signifcant microbial biomass (cell counts an order of magnitude greater than SYBR-stained deionized water controls), as found in prior studies64. From this, we considered the control bottles as a $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ MD treatment. Tis also indicated that some degree of microbial activity was present in all PB experiments consistent with rapid growth of bacteria even under UV exposure in a previous study using the same incubation design64. It is noted that the microbes present in the $< 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ fltrate are also expected to be present in the $< 0 . 7$ and $< 3 . 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microbial inocula (Fig. S6).

Optical and chemical analysis. CDOM absorption was measured using a Cary 300 dual-beam spectrophotometer in a $5 \mathrm { c m }$ pathlength quartz cuvette65. Cuvettes were cleaned before analysis and rinsed with MilliQ then sample water between samples. Measurements were baseline-corrected using MilliQ water, and MilliQ blanks were subtracted from individual scans. Triplicate measurements were performed for each sample, with individual scans assessed as outliers and all quality-controlled scans then used to estimate an average absorbance for the sample66. Absorption was measured from 220 to $7 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (1-nm bandwidth and interval). Absorbance values were converted to CDOM (Napierian) absorption coefcients $( \mathsf { a } _ { \mathrm { C D O M } } ( \lambda ) )$ ) following:

$$
a _ { C D O M } ( \lambda ) = 2 . 3 0 3 \frac { A ( \lambda ) } { l }
$$

where $\mathrm { A } ( \lambda )$ is the measured absorbance and $l$ is the pathlength in meters (here, $0 . 0 5 ) ^ { 6 7 }$ . Once converted to absorption, spectra were modeled using a nonlinear ftting routine to compute spectral slope from 275–295 and $3 5 0 { \mathrm { - } } 4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $S _ { 2 7 5 : 2 9 5 }$ and $\mathrm { S } _ { 3 5 0 : 4 0 0 } .$ , respectively):

$$
a _ { C D O M } ( \lambda ) = a _ { C D O M } ( \lambda _ { 0 } ) e ^ { - S ( \lambda - \lambda _ { 0 } ) }
$$

where $\lambda$ is wavelength $( \mathrm { n m } )$ ), the reference wavelength $\left( \lambda _ { 0 } \right)$ is the average wavelength for the spectral range being ft (285 or $3 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ ) and S is the spectral slope $( \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 } ) ^ { 6 6 }$ . Dissolved organic carbon was measured on a Shimadzu TOC-V Total Organic Carbon analyzer. A few samples exhibited larger DOC values at day 24 than at day 0 and were excluded from analysis (e.g., St 12 in the $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ treatment, Table 1). Te specifc UV absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ $\mathrm { ( S U V A _ { 2 5 4 } ) }$ ) values were determined by dividing CDOM absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ by measured DOC concentration and are reported in units of liter per milligram carbon per meter68.

CDOM fuorescence was measured on a Horiba Aqualog Spectrofuorometer (Jobin Yvon Horiba) using a 1-cm quartz cuvette. On each day of sample analysis, a reference was run for quinine sulfate fuorescence and a blank was run using a sterile MilliQ water cuvette. Samples were analyzed at $5 \mathrm { n m }$ increments of excitation from 240 to $6 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ with an integration time of 1 s. Excitation emission matrices (EEMs) from the Aqualog represented a ratio of emission detector output to that of the reference detector (S/R). Within the Aqualog sofware, both the reference signal and emission detector signal had dark signals subtracted and spectral corrections applied. EEMs outputted from the Aqualog were loaded into a Matlab structure and aligned for input into the drEEM toolbox, where corrections were made for the inner flter efect based on absorbance coefcients of aligned samples run on the Cary spectrophotometer. Sample EEMs had fuorescence signal from MilliQ water subtracted, based on blank EEMs that had been run on the same day. Tese MilliQ water EEMs were also used to calculate Raman peaks, to then convert sample EEMs to fuorescence in Raman Units.

PARAFAC. An initial EEMs dataset including all incubation DOM samples $\left( \mathrm { n } = 2 8 5 \right)$ ), other DOM samples from the Yukon River and Norton Sound sampling in 2019 $\left( \mathrm { n } = 2 6 \right)$ and DOM samples from the Elson Lagoon and local freshwater systems near Barrow, AK $\left( \mathrm { n } = 2 8 \right)$ ) were run through the exploratory data analysis functions within the drEEM toolbox to identify outliers and the appropriate number of fuorescent components to model using Parallel Factor Analysis (PARAFAC)69–71. Of the initial 339 EEMs, 33 were removed as outliers, including 26 incubation EEMs accounted for primarily by Station 12 samples (highest salinity and lowest fuorescent signal). A six component model that explained $> 9 9 . 9 \%$ of total variation was identifed as the best ft using split half analysis to validate the model (Fig. S7). All components co-varied independently. PARAFAC modeling of EEMs from all feld and incubation samples $\left( \mathrm { n } = 3 0 6 \right)$ ) produced six components (C1-C6), with St. 12 excluded from analysis due to many EEMs removed for PARAFAC validation. We published our modeled components to the OpenFluor database44 and compared to published components on OpenFluor.

Percent change in absorption and fuorescent components over time, in Raman units (R.U.), was calculated as

$$
\Delta C ( x ) = 1 0 0 \cdot \left( \frac { C ( x ) _ { i + t } - C ( x ) _ { i } } { C ( x ) _ { i } } \right)
$$

where $C ( { \boldsymbol { x } } ) _ { i }$ represents the initial fuorescent component loading (day 0), $C ( x ) _ { i + t }$ represents the fuorescent component loading at time $t$ (days), and $\varDelta C ( x )$ is percent change in PARAFAC component at time $t .$ . Percent changes were related to the initial component fuorescence to ensure percent changes were comparable across time points. Component changes largely followed a normal distribution across sites and time points; thus, outliers were identifed using Grubb’s test for outliers, which removes one outlier per iteration based on a failure to adhere to a normal distribution. In practice, this removed any component changes greater than $\pm 8 0 \%$ between individual timepoints.

Percent contribution of each component to total fuorescence was calculated as the fuorescence maxima (component loading in R.U.) divided by the sum of fuorescence maxima for all components, e.g.,

$$
C _ { i } \% = \frac { C _ { i } F _ { m a x } } { \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { 6 } C _ { i } F _ { m a x } } * 1 0 0 
$$

where $\mathrm { C _ { i } }$ represents one of the six ftted components and $\mathrm { F _ { m a x } }$ is derived from the component loading output of PARAFAC.
