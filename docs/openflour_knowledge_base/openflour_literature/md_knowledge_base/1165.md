# Submarine mud volcanoes as a source of chromophoric dissolved organic matter to the deep waters of the Gulf of Cádiz

Valentina Amaral1,2\*, Cristina Romera‑Castillo3 & Jesús Forja1

Seafoor structures related to the emission of diferent fuids, such as submarine mud volcanoes (MVs), have been recently reported to largely contribute with dissolved organic matter (DOM) into the oceans. Submarine MVs are common structures in the Gulf of Cádiz. However, little is known about the biogeochemical processes that occur in these peculiar environments, especially those involving DOM. Here, we report DOM characterization in the sediment pore water of three MVs of the Gulf of Cádiz. Estimated benthic fuxes of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and chromophoric DOM (CDOM) were higher than in other marine sediments with an average of $0 . 1 1 \pm 0 . 0 4 \ : \mathrm { m m o l } \ : \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ : \mathrm { d } ^ { - 1 }$ for DOC and ranging between 0.11 and $2 . 8 6 \mathsf { m } ^ { - 1 } \mathsf { L } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { d } ^ { - 1 }$ , for CDOM. Protein-like components represented $\sim 7 0 \%$ of the total fuorescent DOM (FDOM). We found that deep fuids migration from MVs (cold seeps) and anaerobic production via sulfate-reducing bacteria represent a source of DOC and FDOM to the overlying water column. Our results also indicate that fuorescent components can have many diverse sources not captured by common classifcations. Overall, MVs act as a source of DOC, CDOM, and FDOM to the deep waters of the Gulf of Cádiz, providing energy to the microbial communities living there.

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is one of the Earth’s major carbon reservoirs and the largest ocean pool of reduced carbon $( 6 6 2 \mathrm { P g } \mathrm { C } ) ^ { 1 }$ . Te fraction of DOM that absorbs light, referred to as chromophoric DOM (CDOM), and its fuorescent sub-fraction (FDOM) are ubiquitous constituents of the ocean DOM pool, afecting the optical properties of the water column2,3 . Te major sources of marine DOM are photosynthesis from the ocean surface and terrestrial inputs, however, marine sediments are also considered to be an important source of DOM to the overlying seawater $( \sim 3 5 0 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { C y } ^ { - 1 } ) ^ { 4 }$ , comparable to that estimated for rivers $( \sim \bar { 2 } 5 0 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { C y } ^ { - 1 } ) ^ { 5 }$ . Marine sediments are also a source of CDOM, but there is comparatively little information about the benthic fux of CDOM and the nature of these compounds6 . Recent studies suggest that seafoor features related to large emissions of diferent fuids can act as sources of DOM and CDOM into the oceans7–9 therefore, having a signifcant infuence on the chemistry of the oceans10.

Seafoor fuids can derive from microbiological and geological processes and they are classifed in hydrothermal $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ up to $4 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ) and cold seeps $( < 6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ . Hydrothermal vents have been described as sinks of DOC, removing $1 . 4 \pm 0 . 7 \times 1 0 ^ { 4 }$ tons $\mathrm { C y r ^ { - 1 } }$ from the deep-ocean11,12. However, they have been also found to act as a source of CDOM to the seawater9 . Cold seeps comprise brines, methane and other hydrocarbons and are mainly controlled by tectonics13, but there is little information regarding the behavior of DOM from these fuids. Submarine cold seepage could result in a variety of seafoor structures such as mud volcanoes (MVs).

Submarine MVs are broadly distributed on Earth continental shelves, insular slopes and abyssal parts of inland seas $( 1 0 ^ { 3 } – 1 0 ^ { 5 } ) ^ { 1 4 }$ . MVs are cone-shaped structures built up by mud breccia containing gases (mainly methane), saline water, mud, and, occasionally, oil rising from deep pressurized sources through controlled conduits15,16. Tey can be associated with the presence of chemosynthetic biological communities17,18. A recent study in the South China Sea suggests that MVs are an important source of DOC to the deep waters through benthic fuxes, and these could represent up to $2 4 \%$ of the total annual fux from the largest river that drains into this sea19. Moreover, Retelleti et al.20 also measured a high concentration of DOC and FDOM in pore water from MVs ( $1 { - } 6 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ and 0.01–0.06 RU, respectively). Te authors found that fuid migrating from deep sediments through MVs could be an important source of altered FDOM to the overlying water column, with a total fuorescence of up to eight times higher than sediments without MVs. Tey suggest that these structures may play a signifcant role in providing FDOM to the ocean. However, as far as we know, no studies have estimated benthic fuxes of CDOM and FDOM from MVs yet, and their role in the deep ocean is poorly understood.

Te Gulf of Cádiz is a tectonically active area where large felds of submarine MVs, associated with tectonic compression due to the convergence of the African and the Eurasian plates21, have been recently discovered17,22,23. Its geological features, the geochemistry and emission of fuids, and the benthic fauna associated with these fuids have been widely studied16,17,24,25. However, no studies have focused on the dynamic and difusive benthic fux of DOM from these MVs.

Tis work aims to quantify and characterize DOM in the pore water of three MVs located in the Gulf of Cádiz (Fig. 1), and to determine the possible sources of the diferent fractions of DOM. To do so, we measured DOC concentration and DOM optical properties (absorbance and fuorescence). We also estimated difusive benthic fuxes of DOC, CDOM, and FDOM across the sediment–water interface at these MVs to better understand their efect on the concentration and distribution of DOM into the deep water of the Gulf of Cádiz.

# Discussion

Te fuorescent components found in the pore water model have been described for several aquatic environments (Table S2). Our results support the hypothesis that similar components may have diferent sources6,30. In the water column above the MVs, C1 and C2 showed the usual positive relationship between humic-like components from similar sources $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 8 5 , p { < } 0 . 0 0 1 , \mathrm { n } { = } 3 7 ,$ ), and mixing processes explained their distribution, in agreement with previous studies in this zone27. Te same occurs with the protein-like components, where C3 and C4 were positively related $\mathrm { \Delta } R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 6 0$ , $\textstyle P ^ { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ , $\mathtt { n } = 3 7$ ), and probably associated with biological activity27. Conversely, diferent behaviors were observed in pore water samples from the MVs. In AN, humic-like C1 and C2 were negatively related $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 7 1$ , $\textstyle P ^ { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ , $\mathrm { n } = 5 8$ ), and no relationship was observed between protein-like C3 and C4, indicating diferences in their sources and sinks. On the other hand, all fuorescent components in PI and PT, showed a positive relationship among them $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } > 0 . 5 0 $ , $\textstyle P ^ { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ , Table S3). Terefore, the processes that produce and remove FDOM also difer between MVs.

One possible source could be the production of thermogenic DOM together with the production of methane. More than $2 . 4 \%$ of DOM compounds in the ocean are thermogenic7 , and a previous work suggests that the FDOM coming from the deep sediments of MVs fuids was mainly thermogenic20. According to the authors, the lower DOC-normalized fuorescence in the MVs, compared to those found in a reference site (without MVs), may be due to the thermal quenching of the fuorophores. Based on stable carbon isotope composition of dissolved methane in the column water above the MVs studied here, Sierra et al.31 found that methane emissions in AN and PT are of thermogenic origin, while in PI, it seems to be of biogeochemical origin. Unfortunately, we did not measure DOM composition of other pore water sediments in the zone to use as a reference site, and similar values of DOC-normalized fuorescence were observed between AN, PT and PI. Tus, a thermogenic origin of FDOM in this work cannot be confrmed.

However, the diferences in the DOM sources between MVs could be related to diferences in their activity stage, hence, with the intensity of the fuids moving upward from the deeper sediments. MVs alternate between massive mudfow extrusion and dormant/sleeping periods17. According to Mazzini et al.18, most of the MVs are currently in the latter, generally characterized by gas and water seepage of variable intensity. Te $\mathrm { M g : C l }$ ratio indicated a higher fraction of MVs fuid in AN than in the other MVs (Fig. 4) and Sierra et al.31 found an increase of methane with depth in the water column above AN $\mathrm { ^ { \prime } 1 0 0 { - } 1 2 5 \ n m o l { L } ^ { - 1 } }$ ) and PT ( $\mathrm { 1 0 { - } 1 5 \ n m o l { L } ^ { - 1 } }$ ), whereas in PI this trend was not observed. Tis is in agreement with a previous work analyzing benthic fauna associated with seepage of MVs fuids, suggesting locally elevated gas fuxes that reach the seafoor surface, and higher activity in AN than $\mathrm { P I } ^ { 1 7 }$ . Additionally, habitats with structures created by the escaping gas were also found in $\mathrm { P T } ^ { 3 2 }$ , and elevated methane fuxes have also been found in relatively active MVs in the Gulf of $\mathrm { C a d i z ^ { 1 6 , 3 3 } }$ . Te level of extrusion activity between the MVs studied here is $\mathrm { A N > P T > P I }$ and it could be the reason for the existence of diferent sources of DOM in the MVs. For example, in AN, DOC, C2 and $\mathrm { C 4 }$ exhibited a negative relationship with the $\mathrm { M g : C l }$ ratio $\mathrm { \mathrm { ~ R } } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 5 1$ , 0.79, 0.45, respectively, $\textstyle P ^ { < 0 . 0 0 1 }$ , ${ \mathfrak { n } } = 2 3$ , Fig. 5), indicating an increase with the increasing mud volcano fuids. In PT, DOC and C2 also showed an inverse relationship with this ratio, weaker than in AN, but signifcant $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 4 4$ and 0.51, respectively, $\hbar { < } 0 . 0 1$ , $\mathrm { n } = 1 2$ , Fig. 5), while no relationships were observed in PI $( p > 0 . 0 1 )$ . Our results indicate that cold seeps from MVs could be a source of DOC and FDOM to shallower sediments and, eventually, to the overlying water column.

We found a net production of DOC, CDOM, and FDOM to the overlying water column in the three MVs (Table 3). However, as previously discussed for PI, and given the $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 }$ of the relationship between DOM and the $\mathrm { M g : C l }$ ratio found in AN and PT, there might be other factors and sources implicated in the fnal distribution of DOM. In sediments, there is generally a net production of DOM as a result of organic matter degradation processes4 . Sulfate is one of the main electron acceptors available for the oxidation of organic matter in marine sediments; hence sulfate reduction is an important diagenetic process below the oxic zone34. Several studies in marine sediments found that FDOM could be produced via anaerobic degradation of particulate organic matter through sulfate reduction or oxidation of methane35–37. Luek et al.30 found that the production of FDOM was directly related to sulfate-reducing bacteria in incubations of coastal sediments. Te decomposition of organic matter by sulfate reduction consumes sulfate and releases ammonium34. Both sulfate and ammonium were measured in pore water samples from the three MVs and are described elsewhere (Jiménez-López et al. submitted). Accordingly, in AN, the increase of DOC concentration, C2 and C4 was accompanied by the increase in ammonium (Fig. 6A–C, $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 2 8$ , 0.75 and 0.79, respectively, $\hbar { < } 0 . 0 1$ , ${ \mathfrak { n } } = 2 3$ ) concurrent with the decrease of sulfate (Fig. 6D–F, $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 3 9$ , 0.89 and 0.59, respectively, $\hbar { < } 0 . 0 1$ , ${ \mathfrak { n } } = 2 3$ ). Moreover, in AN, these fuorescent components were related to a decrease in DOM humifcation and with an increase of freshly released DOM (Table S3). In PT, only C2 exhibited a strong relationship with ammonium and sulfate, with the same trends as in AN (Fig. $\mathrm { 6 G , H }$ , $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 9 1 $ and 0.87, respectively, $\hbar { < } 0 . 0 1$ , ${ \bf n } = 1 2$ ), whereas no relationship was observed in PI $( p > 0 . 0 1 )$ . Tese results suggest that DOC concentration, C2 and C4 could also be related to the activity of sulfate-reducing bacteria in MVs sediments.

Additionally, AN is characterized by the presence of large microbial mats in its summit, associated with cold seep and extremophiles conditions in its anoxic sediments, while chemosynthetic-based communities have also been found in $\mathrm { P T } ^ { 1 7 , 3 8 }$ . Te biological release of DOC has been previously reported in seep environment characterized by the presence of microbial mats in the Hydrate Ridge39. Te deep DOC in fuid seepage may stimulate the microbial community to release autochthonous organic matter and enhance heterotrophy in seep environments39. Based on BIX values, Retelleti et al.20 suggested that MVs have more abundance of DOC from biological sources than sediments without MVs, which has been attributed to the direct release of DOC from microorganisms. Tis could explain the highest BIX values observed in AN $\mathrm { > } 1$ up to 3), which corresponds to the presence of DOM recently released40. Anaerobic production of DOM via sulfate-reducing bacteria might also be a source of DOM in AN and, to a lesser extent, in PT, providing energy to the microbial communities living there. Tese processes were also suggested as one of the responsible of FDOM composition in other $\mathrm { M V } s ^ { 2 0 }$ .

On the other hand, deep cold seep fuids and biological activity appear to be a minor source of DOM in PI, probably linked to the lower seepage activity of this mud volcano, as previously mentioned. Although PI is located in the same MVs feld than AN (Table S1), it is strongly infuenced by bottom currents from Mediterranean waters32 and organisms not associated with fuid emissions, but typical in these waters, such as dense crinoid beds, had been observed in its summits17. We hypothesized that the net increase of DOM observed in PI (Table 3) could be due to abiotic processes such as condensation, fragmentation, or physical dissolution of POM to $\mathrm { D O M } ^ { 3 , 4 1 }$ . For example, abiotic condensation of FDOM could be responsible of refractory organic matter3 . Tis could explain the negative relationship between FDOM components with BIX observed in PI, but not observed in the other MVs (Table S3), as well as the relatively less variable DOM profles in pore water (Table 1, Figs. 2 and 3).

Our results agree with previous works that suggest that similar fuorescent components may have diferent sources6,30. Humic-like C2 was frst proposed as a component of marine origin29, but further studies indicate that this component is observed in almost all aquatic environments (Table S2), and its signal was then attributed to microbial humic-like fuorescence3 . In the water column of the Gulf of Cádiz, C2 was described from both autochthonous and allochthonous sources27, while in this work it was related to cold seeps from MVs and bacterial sulfate-reduction. Several works indicate that ‘humic-like’ DOM is also produced in the absence of terrestrial sources or humifcation processes3,30,42. Our results support the hypothesis that spectral regions originally defned as “humic-like” can have many diverse sources not considered by the traditional classifcation29. On the other hand, the distribution of C1 might be explained by deposition from the water column and/or solubilization, given the similar distribution observed between MVs $\left( p > 0 . 0 5 \right)$ , while the net increase observed might be due to abiotic processes. Another possibility could be C1 production by solubilization of the terrestrial organic matter that has settled.

Finally, C3 was described as a mixture of PAH and protein-like components related to anthropogenic pollution in the water column of the Gulf of Cádiz27. Although MVs could also be a source of $\mathrm { P A } \bar { \mathrm { H } } ^ { 1 8 }$ , a unique source cannot be distinguished using PARAFAC. Spectral fuorescence overlapping between fuorophores with low excitation wavelength such as $\mathrm { P A H } ^ { 4 3 }$ and signals from proteins44 might result in a mixture of fuorophores. Moreover, Sharma et al.45 found a similar component to our C3 in soil organic matter and described it as a mixture of terrestrial humic-like and tryptophan fuorescence (peak-T)29 with almost no biological production, degradation or absorption. Accordingly, C3 was positively related to the terrestrial C1 in the three MVs (Table S3) and also with the peak-T ( $\mathrm { R } ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 6 3$ , $\hbar { < } 0 . 0 1$ , $\mathrm { n } = 6 0$ ) suggesting that C3 could be a mixture of fuorophores with diferent sources.

Continental margin sediments may be responsible for a signifcant fraction of DOM delivered to the marine environment6,35,46. Te benthic fuxes of DOC were in the range of those estimated in other MVs and most continental slopes, and lower than those from estuarine sediments (Table 2). Te highest estimated benthic fux of DOC was observed in PT, which could be due to the higher DOC concentrations observed here (Table 1). We can conclude that cold seep from MVs acts as a source of DOC to the deep waters, probably minimal compared to other oceanic sources (e.g. $2 5 0 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 }$ tons $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { y } } ^ { - 1 }$ from rivers).

Although no information of difusive benthic fux of CDOM and FDOM from MVs was found, our data was compared with those from marine sediments and hydrothermal vents. Benthic fux of CDOM (as $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $a _ { 3 5 0 } \mathrm { . }$ ) showed higher values than those reported for marine sediments, whereas the fuxes from the humic-like components were in the same range (Table 2). Among the fuorescent components, protein-like estimated benthic fuxes from AN were one order of magnitude higher than those in PI and PT and also in marine sediments. Our results suggest that MVs could act as a source of CDOM and FDOM to the deep waters of the Gulf of Cádiz. Noteworthy, benthic fuxes from a reference site without mud volcanoes in this zone will improve our fndings.

# Conclusions

Cold seeps from MVs and biological activity via anaerobic sulfate-reducing bacteria represent a source of DOC, CDOM, and FDOM to the deep waters of the Gulf of Cádiz, providing energy and resources to the deep heterotrophic microbial communities. However, further studies are needed to better understand the role of mud volcanoes in DOM cycling in the deep water of the Gulf of Cádiz (e.g. stable isotopes, FT-ICR-MS, amino acid analysis).

# Methods

Site description. Te Gulf of Cádiz is located between the SW continental margin of the Iberian Peninsula and the NW margin of Africa (Fig. 1). It is strongly infuenced by the exchange of Atlantic and Mediterranean water masses through the Strait of Gibraltar and it is characterized by the presence of several areas of hydrocarbon-rich fuid venting structures (e.g., MVs, pockmarks, carbonate chimneys and crusts, mud mounds and diapiric ridges) $^ { 1 7 , 2 2 , 4 7 , 4 8 }$ .

Te study was carried out in three MVs: Anastasya and Pipoca, situated in the Guadalquivir Diapiric Ridge Field, and St. Petersburg, in the deeper Tasyo feld, located between 457 and $8 6 0 \mathrm { m }$ depth (Table S1). Recent work found that bottom waters at these MVs exhibit high methane concentrations, especially in $\mathrm { A N } ^ { 3 1 }$ . Based on cold seep fauna at the summit of these MVs and the characteristic of the mud breccia sediments (e.g., gas bubbles, smell of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ ), AN seems to be the more active one among the three MVs studied here17.

Sampling and analytical methods. Two cruises were carried out onboard R/V Angeles Alvariño and Ramón Margalef during June and December 2016. Seawater samples from the water column were collected from $5 \mathrm { m }$ to the summit of each MV $\left( \mathrm { n } = 4 4 \right)$ , and pore water samples were collected from gravity cores inside each MV. Te 44 seawater samples were taken using Niskin bottles (10 L) mounted on a rosette-sampler coupled to a Seabird CTD $9 1 1 +$ to measure temperature and salinity. Details regarding the measurements of physicochemical variables are described elsewhere27. For measuring DOC concentration, absorbance and fuorescence, seawater was fltered using precombusted flters (Whatman GF/F, nominal pore size $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $4 5 0 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } , 4 \mathrm { h }$ ). Both optical analyses were performed on board with the same settings described below. Samples for DOC concentration were frozen in acid-clean HDPE bottles until analysis.

Two $1 \textrm { m }$ long gravity cores of $9 . 5 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ inner diameter were collected from inside each mud volcano. Subsamples from each core were taken onboard. Sections of $1 \mathrm { c m }$ thickness were carefully sliced at diferent depths $_ { \sim 0 . 5 }$ to $4 5 \mathrm { { c m } }$ below the seafoor) and stored frozen until analysis. Sediments and pore water samples were treated in an inert atmosphere using $\Nu _ { 2 }$ to minimize exposure to oxygen. Pore water samples were collected by centrifugation $( \sim 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { g }$ of sediment, $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , $1 0 . 0 0 0 \mathrm { G }$ and $1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ SIGMA-18KS, $\mathrm { n } = 6 0$ ) and then fltered (Millipore HPF $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ ). Combusted GF/F flters signifcantly increase their retention capacity due to compaction of the borosilicate microfbers49, hence no signifcant diferences associated with using diferent flters for pore water and seawater samples are expected in this work. Even though centrifugation could have an efect on DOM composition, it would be minimal compared to other techniques when analyzing $\mathrm { F D O M } ^ { 5 0 , 5 1 }$ . It does not require pretreatment and it is currently the most widely used method for collecting pore water from sediments for DOC and FDOM analyses (see references Table 2). Magnesium $( \mathrm { M g } )$ , chloride (Cl) and sulphate concentrations were measured in pore water samples by ionic chromatograph (Metrohm 881/882, Compact IC pro, plus, $\mathrm { C V } = 1 . 7 8 \pm 0 . 2 6 \% )$ , and ammonium was measured using a segmented fow auto-analyser (Skalar, San Plus) (Jiménez-López et al. submitted). For DOC analysis, pore water was diluted in MilliQ and stored frozen until analysis using a Multi N/C 3100 Analytik Jena analyzer (instrument variability $= 4 3 \pm 1 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ , Hansell CRM Program). UV–visible absorption spectra were immediately scanned from 250 to $8 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ using a spectrometer (JASCO-V750) using a $1 \mathrm { c m }$ path length quartz cuvette. Te estimated detection limit of the spectrophotometer for quantifying CDOM absorption is 0.0015 absorbance units or $0 . 0 3 \mathrm { m } ^ { 1 }$ (1 cm cuvette), and the noise is 0.00004 absorbance units (specifcations JASCO-750). A blank was measured every six samples to detect and correct instrument drif. We calculated the absorption coefcient at 254 and $3 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and the spectral slope $\mathrm { S } _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } { } ^ { 5 2 , 5 3 }$ . Excitation-emission matrixes (EEMs) at 250–450/300–560 nm were immediately obtained using a spectrofuorometer with an accessory for temperature control (JASCO FP-8300, EHC-813). Prior to PARAFAC modeling, EEMs from seawater and pore water samples were standardized (e.g. blank subtraction, instrument correction factor, Raman and Rayleigh scatter band were trimmed, and inner flter efect was corrected), and normalized to Raman Unit (RU) using the drEEM 0.2 toolbox for Matlab54. Fluorescent components were validated using split-half validation and random-initialization analysis54. We also calculated the humifcation (HIX) and freshness index (BIX) following Huguet et al.40. Further details for the calculation of spectral indexes, EEMs standardization, and PARAFAC analysis are described in a previous work27.

Assuming difusive transport across the sediment–water interface, we estimated the benthic fuxes of DOC, CDOM (as $a _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $a _ { 3 5 0 } \mathrm { , }$ ), and FDOM components from the MVs using Fick’s frst law of difusion following Burdige et al.35 and Hung et al. 19.

Statistical analysis. Two ways ANOVA and the post hoc Tukey test were performed to assess diferences in DOM (factor 1: MVs and factor 2: cruises). A signifcance level of 0.05 was selected.
