The spatial and vertical distribution of colored dissolved organic matter (CDOM) in the North Pacific Ocean, Bering, Chukchi and Beaufort Seas was investigated with respect to water circulation. Four fluorescent components were identified in 546 samples using parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC): terrestrially-derived humic-like (C1), UV humic-like (C2), microbially derived humic-like (C3) and proteinaceous/tryptophan-like (C4) components. Deep water upwelling upon contact with the Aleutian Islands influenced optical properties of CDOM in Bering Sea surface water, where higher absorption coefficient at $3 5 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ $\left( \mathsf { a } _ { 3 5 5 } \right)$ , fluorescence intensities of all CDOM components and lower spectral slope $( S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } )$ were found compared to surface waters of the Gulf of Alaska $( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 1 )$ ). Further north, a marked increase in bioprocessed material (C4) was found in surface waters in Chukchi Sea. This finding was attributed to elevated biological activity in surface Chukchi water. Humic-like C1–C3 showed considerable dilution with increasing latitude from coastal to offshore regions in surface Beaufort Sea, suggesting a terrestrial predominance. This contrasts well with protein-like C4 where no significant gradient was found in Beaufort Sea surface water, supporting in situ generation. Finally, the vertical decrease of protein-like C4 relative to humic-like components C1–3 suggests that humic-like CDOM is produced at depth as a byproduct of remineralization.

# 1. Introduction

The distribution and dynamics of dissolved organic matter (DOM) in marine aquatic environments have received a great deal of attention over the past several decades. This attention has been bolstered by a warming climate, which has been proposed to thaw northern circumpolar soils and permafrost (Gruber et al., 2004). In this fashion, otherwise frozen organic matter may be mobilized to proximal Arctic rivers for subsequent export to marine coastal margins (Peterson et al., 2002, 2006). A climate induced flux in Arctic riverine DOM is expected to influence oceanic carbon cycling, particularly in coastal regions, and thus the global carbon cycle (Raymond et al., 2007). A fraction of DOM, generally described as colored or chromophoric DOM (CDOM), makes up a part of aquatic DOM. CDOM absorbs light within the ultra violet (UV) and visible regions of the electromagnetic spectrum. As such, CDOM has the potential to modulate the extent of photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) penetration throughout the water column and thus, the euphotic zone (Foden et al., 2008). Compression or expansion of the euphotic zone, and the capacity of aquatic micro-organisms to catalyze solar energy, can influence marine ecosystem structure (Urtizberea et al., 2013).

In addition to UV/visible absorption, CDOM can be monitored by excitation–emission matrix spectroscopy (EEM; Coble, 1996). EEM datasets are extensive in nature, each typically containing $> 3 0 0 0$ fluorescence intensities, which may be sufficiently informative for the differentiation of specific CDOM sources (Coble, 1996). Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) is now ubiquitous in investigations where the identity and source of chemically distinct CDOM components are of interest (e.g. Guéguen et al., 2012; Murphy et al., 2008; Stedmon et al., 2003; Walker et al., 2009).

Documenting concentration, composition and distribution of DOM can be critical in advancing our understanding of the carbon cycle. Previous studies have linked DOM quality and denitrification rates (Barnes et al., 2012). This is particularly pertinent to the western Arctic Ocean because of the influence of DOM-rich river inflows (i.e. Mackenzie River; Guéguen et al., 2005) and nitrification/denitrification processes occurring on the shelves (e.g. Chang and Devol, 2009; Hirota et al., 2009). A change in DOM composition also significantly affects metal distribution and bioavailability (Chen et al., 2013; Nakayama et al., 2011; Nishimura et al., 2012). For example, humic-type fluorescence (excitation/emission $3 2 0 / 4 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ) was positively correlated to dissolved iron concentration in the Arctic Ocean (Nakayama et al., 2011; Nishimura et al., 2012), suggesting that humic-type DOM are important ligands complexing with Fe(III) in marine waters.

In this study, we applied PARAFAC to EEMs to characterize CDOM in water samples collected from the North Pacific Ocean, the Bering,

Chukchi and Beaufort Seas as part of the Canada/US Joint Ocean Ice Study (JOIS). North Pacific seawater is driven north beyond the Bering Strait and east over the Chukchi Shelf into Beaufort Sea by the Alaska Coastal Current (Stabeno et al., 2004). Pacific waters dominate the top surface layer in Beaufort and Chukchi Seas, underlain by Atlantic-derived deep water (Swift et al., 1998). Acquisition of CDOM fluorescence and absorption measurements aims to trace and determine the fate of optically distinct fractions of CDOM during their transport throughout marine environments. Of particular interest is discriminating the optical signature of riverine CDOM and developing a spatial assessment of its mixing/transformation. The spatial distribution of CDOM components in this study region promises to provide insight as to the effects of climate change induced modifications in organic carbon loading to the North Pacific and Arctic Oceans.

# 2. Methods

# 2.1. Sampling stations

A total of 546 samples were collected in the North Pacific, and Bering, Chukchi and Beaufort Seas in summer/fall 2010 as part of the Canada's Three Oceans (July 8–21, 2010; SWL) and the Canada/US Joint Ocean Ice Study programs (September 15–October 15, 2010; LSSL) in Niskin bottles mounted on a rosette together with a conductivity– temperature–depth profiler (Fig. 1). Seawater samples were collected at water depths between 5 and $1 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ at all SWL stations, and at two depths (i.e. $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ and Sp 33.1) at all LSSL stations except at two LSSL stations (140.01 W/77.98 N and $1 5 0 . 0 4 \mathrm { ~ W } / 7 9 . 0 0 \mathrm { ~ N } _ { \cdot }$ ) where seawater samples were collected at water depths ranging from 5 and 3000– $3 6 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ . The samples were immediately filtered using pre-combusted ( $4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 4 \mathrm { h } }$ ) glass fiber filters (GF/F, Whatman) and stored in the dark at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in pre-combusted amber glass vials. Surface seawater samples were also taken from the underway system ( $\sim 1 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ }$ deep) while the ship was steaming.

# 2.2. CDOM UV/visible absorption

Room temperature CDOM samples were pipetted into a $1 0 – \mathrm { c m }$ quartz cuvette before loading into a Shimadzu UV 2550 spectrophotometer and scanning against a Milli-Q water reference from 250 to

$7 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . A slit width of $0 . 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ was applied, with an absorbance acquisition interval of $1 \mathrm { n m }$ . The cuvette was rinsed with Milli-Q water between sample absorbance readings to minimize cross contamination. Absorbance (A) was converted to absorption coefficient (a; $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ according to the equation: $\mathsf { a } = 2 . 3 0 3 \mathsf { A } / \mathsf { l } ,$ where l is the path-length of the optical cell in meters. Absorption coefficients at $3 5 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ $\left( \mathsf { a } _ { 3 5 5 } \right)$ were of particular interest given that this wavelength corresponds to that of terrestrial CDOM quantification (Blough et al., 1993). The precision of the measurements made with the 10-cm cell was better than $0 . 0 5 \%$ at $3 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . The spectral slope was determined from 275 to $2 9 5 \mathrm { n m }$ $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 } .$ ) (Helms et al., 2008).

# 2.3. Discrete CDOM fluorescence

Excitation/emission matrix spectra (EEMs) were obtained in signalto-reference ratio mode over excitation wavelengths of $2 5 0 \mathrm { - } 5 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and emission wavelengths of 300–600 through successive scans with a Fluoromax-4 Jobin Yvon spectrofluorometer (Guéguen et al., 2011). Excitation and emission slit widths and sampling intervals were set to $5 \mathrm { n m }$ . Blank Milli-Q EEMs were measured intermittently during sample analyses and were subtracted as a means of correcting for background signal and scatter. All EEMs were normalized to Raman units (r.u.) by dividing the signal at all $\mathrm { E x / E m }$ pairs by the integrated area under the Raman water peak at $\mathrm { E x } = 3 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ using a Milli-Q blank EEM (Lawaetz and Stedmon, 2009). A relative standard deviation (RSD) of $1 . 3 5 \%$ was associated with Raman areas determined over the 4 months of analysis.

# 2.4. PARAFAC modeling

Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was used to decompose the 546 EEMs into independent fluorescent components. The analysis was carried out in Matlab R2011b (MathWorks) using the DOMFluor toolbox (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). The model was constrained to nonnegative values and run for three to ten components. The appropriate number of components was determined by split-half and Tucker congruence coefficient analyses (Stedmon and Bro, 2008). A PARAFAC model was derived for each cruise (one for SWL and one for LSSL) as well as with all samples $( \mathrm { S W L } + \mathrm { L S S L } ,$ ) measured in this study. For statistical tests of variance among CDOM component proportions the distribution was normalized by using the arcsine method (PawlowskyGlahn and Egozcue, 2006).

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Water column structure

The North Pacific Ocean, Bering, Chukchi and Beaufort waters were classified based on their respective potential temperature (θ) and practical salinity $[ S _ { \mathrm { p } }$ ; Fig. 2). In the Pacific/Bering sector (SWL cruise), three water masses were distinguished. North Pacific Surface Water (NPSW) was found at depths ${ \sim } 0 { - } 3 0 \mathrm { m }$ spanning a warm, fresh, windmixed surficial layer (Emerson and Stump, 2010; Stabeno et al., 2012). The North Pacific Halocline (NPH) was identified as a layer of rapidly increasing salinity from $\sim 1 0 0$ to $2 0 0 \ \mathrm { m }$ , similar to that previously depicted in the Subarctic Pacific Ocean (Dodimead, 1961; Fujishima et al., 2001). A bottom layer where salinity gradually plateaued in progressively colder waters from $\sim 5 0 0$ to $1 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ was described as North Pacific Intermediate Water (NPIW) with a potential density of \~26.8 (Talley, 1993).

In Beaufort Sea (LSSL cruise), four water masses were identified. Beaufort Surface Water (BSW) constitutes a warm, surface mixed layer confined to depths from 0 to $3 0 \mathrm { m }$ (McLaughlin et al., 2004) and corresponding to $ { \mathrm { S } } _ { \mathrm { p } } < 3 0$ . Beaufort Halocline (BH) was characterized by a potential temperature minimum at $\sim 1 5 0 \mathrm { m }$ and $\mathsf { S } _ { \mathsf { p } }$ centered around $\sim 3 3 . 1$ (Coachman and Barnes, 1961). The underlying warm $( \Theta \sim 0 . 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ salty $( \mathsf { S _ { p } } 3 4 . 7 5  – 3 4 . 8 5 )$ Atlantic waters below $\sim 3 5 0 \mathrm { m }$ are described as the Beaufort Atlantic Layer (BAL) (Rudels et al., 1996) followed by the Beaufort Deep Water (BDW) below $8 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ and $S _ { \mathrm { p } }$ within 34.85–35.0 (Swift et al., 1998).

# 3.2. PARAFAC modeling

PARAFAC modeling was first performed on EEM datasets for individual cruises (SWL and LSSL). Seawater samples taken during SWL and LSSL cruises encompass marine environments with unique regimes of biological productivity, freshwater sources and, perhaps most importantly, water mass structure. Seawater samples taken during the SWL cruise comprise Pacific-derived water masses, a fraction of which are eventually transported north beyond the Bering Strait into the Beaufort Sea where they contribute to BSW and BH formation. These Pacific-derived water masses are underlain, however, by those of Atlantic origin. These considerable distinctions in seawater samples collected during SWL and LSSL cruises led to preliminary trials where PARAFAC models were constrained to separate cruises. Three component models were validated for each dataset (Table 1). The excitation and emission characteristics of the components from each model were very similar (Fig. 3). As a result the datasets were combined and modeled together.

Upon combining EEM datasets from the two cruises $( { \mathrm { S W L } } + { \mathrm { L S S L } }$ ; $\mathrm { n } = 5 4 6$ ), a 4-component model was found with residual fluorescence intensities typically low and featureless for all samples $( < 1 0 \% )$ . A 3-component model with very similar components to those modeled for individual cruises was also validated for the cumulative EEM dataset, although the 4-component model had a sum of squared error that was $\sim 2 8 \%$ lower. Inclusion of additional fluorescence data for the PARAFAC modeling allowed this fourth component to become identified. Similarly, a study of estuarine DOM with a limited number of samples $\mathrm { \Phi } _ { \mathrm { { n } } } = 9 0$ ) derived five components (Stedmon et al., 2003) while the follow up study on the same catchment with an extensive dataset $\mathit { l } \mathrm { n } = 1 2 7 6$ ) revealed eight components (Stedmon and Markager, 2005). Larger datasets may result in models with a larger number of components that could not be separated from data contained in fewer samples. Therefore, only results from the combined dataset $( { \mathrm { S W L } } + { \mathrm { L S S L } } )$ were used for further data analysis in this study. Contour plots of PARAFAC components identified in this four-component model as well as their spectral loadings are presented in Fig. 3.

The four fluorescent components (Fig. 3) found in the cumulative dataset $( \mathrm { S W L + L S S L }$ ; 546 EEMs) have been previously identified. C1 (Fig. 3A) showed a primary excitation maximum at $< 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ followed by a broad, less intense excitation peak at $3 7 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and an emission maximum at $4 7 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . C1 spectral features resembled conventional humic-like peaks A and C (Coble, 1996) which have been proposed to be representative of humic-like materials of terrestrial origin (Guo et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2011). Similar fluorophores have been reported as particularly prevalent in low salinity surface waters in coastal environments (Yamashita et al., 2010), although this terrestrially derived CDOM component has also been observed in the open ocean, including the Arctic Ocean (Guéguen et al., 2012). C1 has the longest emission maximum wavelength among all components identified, suggesting an elevated degree of aromaticity (Stedmon et al., 2003).

Component C2 (Fig. 3B) showed excitation maxima at $2 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 3 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , with an emission maximum at $4 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , whereas C3 (Fig. 3C) displayed excitation maxima at $< 2 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 0 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and an emission maximum at $4 0 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . C2 and C3 present similar spectral properties to peaks A and M, respectively (Coble, 1996). These humic-like components have been found in both fresh and marine waters (Fellman et al., 2009; Murphy et al., 2008; Pifer et al., 2011; Singh et al., 2010; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Stedmon et al., 2003). Recent biological activity has been correlated with these humic-like materials (Shimotori et al., 2012; Yamashita et al., 2010), which could include potential microbial reprocessing (Stedmon et al., 2007) and/or phytoplankton degradation (McIntyre and Guéguen, 2012; Romera-Castillo et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2011).

Component C4 (Fig. 3D) showed an excitation maximum at $2 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and an emission maximum at $3 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The spectral features of C4 are similar in comparison to the traditional tryptophan-like T peak (Coble, 1996), which has been associated with amino acids free or bound in proteins (Yamashita and Tanoue, 2003). Tryptophan-like fluorescence has been linked to recent biological production (Coble et al., 1998)

and has been commonly found in both fresh and marine environments (Fellman et al., 2009; Guéguen et al., 2012; Kowalczuk et al., 2009; Murphy et al., 2008; Stedmon and Markager, 2005; Yamashita et al., 2011).

# 3.3. CDOM distribution in surface waters

CDOM absorption at $3 5 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ $\left( { \mathsf { a } } _ { 3 5 5 } \right)$ ) and spectral slope from 275 to $2 9 5 \mathrm { n m }$ (S275–295) were assessed for surface water samples (Fig. 4). The highest a355 values $\left( > 0 . 5 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } \right) ,$ ) associated with lower $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ $( 0 . 0 1 9 - 0 . 0 2 4 \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ ) were observed in surface waters in close proximity to the Aleutian Islands and the Mackenzie River delta, which is indicative of higher molecular weight terrestrial DOM (Helms et al., 2008; Guéguen and Cuss, 2011) of elevated aromaticity (Sulzberger and Durisch-Kaiser, 2009). On the other hand, the highest $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ values $( 0 . 0 2 7 - 0 . 0 3 5 \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) coupled with lower $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ values $( < 0 . 2 0 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 } ) .$ were found in the open ocean waters of the Gulf of Alaska and Beaufort Sea. This is expected to primarily be the result of increased distance from terrestrial sources, and dilution of allochthonous CDOM (Granskog, 2012) with a background marine CDOM signal. Photodegradation can cause $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ to decrease, but the low sun angle at high latitudes coupled with sea ice cover reduces sunlight penetration in surface waters. Osburn et al. (2009) estimated that around $1 0 \%$ absorption was lost upon photo-irradiation, suggesting that sunlight exposure does not substantially degrade CDOM on Arctic shelves.

Latitudinal distribution of CDOM PARAFAC components in surface waters is summarized in Fig. 5. Fluorescence intensity of humic-like C1–C3 was comparable to earlier work (Guéguen et al., 2012; Heller et al., 2013; Jørgensen et al., 2011; Walker et al., 2009). Average fluorescence intensity for tryptophan-like C4 ranged from $0 . 0 0 4 \pm$ 0.005 r.u. in Gulf of Alaska to $0 . 0 2 2 \pm 0 . 0 0 6$ r.u. in coastal Beaufort Sea $( 7 0 - 7 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } )$ . Similar ranges have been found previously in surface ocean waters (Heller et al., 2013; Jørgensen et al., 2011). Bering Sea surface waters had higher $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ , fluorescence intensities of all CDOM components, $C 3 \%$ and $C 4 \%$ and lower $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ and terrestrial humic-like $C 1 \%$ $\scriptstyle \mathbf { p < } 0 . 0 1$ ) when compared to those of the Gulf of Alaska. Surface waters of the Bering Sea are largely composed of nutrient-rich deep waters which have upwelled upon incidence with the Aleutian Islands (Swan et al., 2009). This has been reported to result in the deep basin of the Bering Sea having approximately twice as much net primary productivity when compared to the Gulf of Alaska (Chen et al., 2004). This biological activity could account for comparatively higher proportions of the microbially-derived C3 and protein-like C4 in Bering Sea. In fact, Bering Sea sampling stations were rarely beyond the shelf break and were likely enriched in concentrations of all CDOM components by freshwater inflow to coastal Gulf of Alaska Shelf transported by the Alaska Coastal Current north beyond the Aleutian Islands (Stabeno et al., 2004). North of the Bering Strait, the Chukchi Shelf showed a decrease in $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ and $C 3 \%$ whereas $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ , C1 and C2 fluorescence intensities as well as $C 1 \%$ and $C 4 \%$ increased $\left( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 1 0 \right)$ . The surface C2 intensity averaged $0 . 0 2 3 6 \pm 0 . 0 0 5 0$ r.u. in Chukchi Sea (Fig. 5A). Nakayama et al. (2011) and Nishimura et al. (2012) reported similar values in Bering and Chukchi Seas (0.0177–0.0450 and 0.0150–0.0288 r.u. respectively, assuming $\mathrm { Q S U } _ { 3 2 0 / 4 2 0 } = 0 . 0 1 1 5$ ; Heller et al., 2013). High chlorophyll-a concentrations have been found in surface waters proximal to the Yukon River discharge and Bering Strait towards the end of the polar summer (Wang et al., 2005).

# 3.4. CDOM component depth distribution in the North Pacific and Arctic Oceans

Vertical profiles of physical and chemical parameters (Fig. 2) led to the identification of distinct water masses constituting the water column for SWL and LSSL cruises. The a355 as well as fluorescence intensities of humic-like C1–C3 and $C 2 \%$ were higher in BH when compared to NPH $\cdot \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5$ ; Fig. 6). Indeed, vertical profiles showcase distinct CDOM optical characteristics in BH with pronounced maxima of $\mathtt { a } _ { 3 5 5 }$ (Fig. 7B) and humic-like fluorescence (Fig. 7C) at $\sim 1 5 0 \mathrm { m }$ when compared to NPH seawaters of the Gulf of Alaska, Bering and Chukchi Seas (Fig. 7D). In the BH layer, the average C2 intensity was similar to that of humic-type fluorescence (Nakayama et al., 2011; Nishimura et al., 2012). The more pronounced humic-like character in BH has been previously linked to interactions with DOM-rich bottom sediments on Beaufort Shelves during sea ice formation (Guéguen et al., 2007).

Similarly, the θ-Sp plot (Fig. 2) discriminates the warm Atlanticderived BAL $\mathrm { \cdot } \Theta \sim 0 . 5 \ \mathrm { \^ { \circ } C } ,$ Sp \~ 34.8) and cold BDW $\Theta < 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ Sp \~ 34.9). Despite their common origin in Norwegian, Icelandic and Greenlandic

Seas, BDW is formed by a fraction of this Atlantic water sinking after transport through a complex conduit of ridges and becoming entrained in the deep Canada Basin (Swift et al., 1998). Humic-like CDOM fluorescence in deep seawater has been correlated with apparent oxygen utilization associated with microbial activity and remineralization of particulate matter as it sinks towards the ocean floor (Jørgensen et al., 2011). This is consistent with the finding that C4 was 1.4 times lower in BDW relative to BAL $\cdot \mathrm { p } < 0 . 1 5$ ; Fig. 6) as humic-like CDOM components are replenished by sinking particles, whereas protein-like C4 is depleted by slow acting degradation processes. This decrease in C4 from $\sim 3 5 0$ to $3 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ was also shown for a spectrally similar component resembling pure tryptophan identified by Jørgensen et al., 2011 for North Atlantic seawater. Fluorescence intensities for these proteinaceous PARAFAC components were comparable within this window of the vertical profiles, ranging from ${ \sim } 0 . 0 0 2$ to $0 . 0 1 0 \ \mathrm { \ r . u . }$ Conversely, terrestrially-derived humic-like C1 was much less subject to change throughout this depth range, with an average fluorescence intensity of 0.015 r.u. with R.S.D. of only $6 . 1 \%$ Jørgensen et al., 2011 identified a PARAFAC component very similar to C1 (also annotated C1), which had a similarly stable fluorescence intensity within this depth range, although the average fluorescence intensity was ${ \sim } 0 . 0 1 2$ r.u. Indeed, terrestrially derived organic material has been suggested to represent a fraction of refractory CDOM in deep Atlantic seawater (Nelson and Siegel, 2013). BDW is considerably older than BAL as it includes waters isolated below ${ \sim } 2 2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ for $\sim 4 5 0$ years (Schlosser et al., 1997), which is expected to contribute to this difference in CDOM fluorescent properties being quantifiable.

NPIW is much younger $_ { < 2 0 }$ years since ventilation; Hansell et al., 2002) than these Beaufort deep waters, serving as an intermediate layer ${ < } 2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m }$ ventilated by wind-driven mechanisms (Talley, 2008). The fluorescence intensities and proportions of C1 in NPIW were higher when compared to both BAL and BDW, with $C 4 \%$ being lower for NPIW relative to BAL $( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 5 )$ . Considerable terrestrial CDOM contribution to the NPIW has been found and attributed to freshwater discharge from the Amur River into the Okhotsk Sea included in NPIW formation (Yamashita et al., 2010). This terrigenous signal and DOC-enriched waters are expected to be carried into the Gulf of Alaska via the Oyashio Front, providing a probable explanation for these findings (Hansell et al., 2002). This is supported in part by a decrease in $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ with depth throughout NPIW (Fig. 7A), although this reinforces the aforementioned mechanism for resuspension of humic-like CDOM as settling particulate organic matter dissolves (Jørgensen et al., 2011).

# 4. Conclusions

This study highlights the spatial and vertical distribution of CDOM optical properties in North Pacific Ocean, and Bering, Chukchi and Beaufort Seas. Absorbance and fluorescence properties of CDOM in surface waters were shown to be strongly affected by proximity to terrestrial sources. This dependence was particularly evident for coastal CDOM which was indicative of significantly higher a355 and lower $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ when compared to that that of offshore regions, which goes in hand with terrestrially-derived CDOM of greater molecular weight being more prevalent. This was supported by the disproportionate depletion of humic-like PARAFAC components C1–3 compared to protein-like C4 with increasing latitude in Beaufort Sea surface water. Upwelling and biological activity were also evidenced as environmental features which were influential with respect to CDOM optical properties in surface waters. The age and source of deep water masses also appeared to be instrumental in determining the composition of CDOM components, given distinct behavior of humic and protein-like CDOM components in deep seawater.
