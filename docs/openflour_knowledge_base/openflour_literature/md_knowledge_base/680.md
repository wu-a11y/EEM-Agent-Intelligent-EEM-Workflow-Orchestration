# ABSTRACT

A paired-watershed approach was used to compare the quality and fluxes of dissolved organic matter (DOM) during stormflow and baseflow in two lowland tropical rainforest streams located in northeastern Costa Rica. The Arboleda stream received regional groundwater (RGW) flow, whereas the Taconazo stream did not. DOM quality was assessed with absorbance and fluorescence and stable carbon isotope $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C - D O C } )$ values. RGW DOM lacked detectable fluorescence and had specific ultraviolet absorption $\left( \mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } \right)$ and absorbance slope ratio $( S _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ values consistent with low aromaticity and low molecular weight material, respectively. We attributed these properties to microbial degradation and sorption of humic DOM to mineral surfaces during transport through bedrock. $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were lower and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values higher in the Arboleda stream during baseflow compared to the Taconazo stream, presumably due to dilution by RGW. However, no significant difference in SUVA254 or $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ occurred between the streams during stormflow. $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ was negatively correlated to $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC $( r ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 6 1$ , $\mathrm { p } { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ ), demonstrating a strong linkage between stream DOM characteristics and the relative amounts of RGW flow and local watershed runoff containing soil and throughfall C sources. Mean DOC export from the Taconazo stream during the study period was $2 . 6 2 { \pm } 0 . 3 9 \ \mathrm { g } \mathrm { C } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ , consistent with other tropical streams, yet mean DOC export from the Arboleda stream was $1 3 . 7 9 { \scriptstyle \pm 2 . 0 7 \mathrm { ~ g ~ C ~ m ~ } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 } }$ , one of the highest exports reported and demonstrating a substantial impact of old regional groundwater from outside the watershed boundary can have on surface water carbon cycling.

# 1. INTRODUCTION

Dissolved organic matter (DOM) plays a key role in aquatic food webs, influences the availability of dissolved nutrients and metals, and affects the optical properties and overall biogeochemistry of aquatic ecosystems [Findlay and Sinsabaugh, 2003]. Rivers and streams transport or store about $2 \mathrm { P g }$ of terrestrial organic carbon (C) annually and at least half of the C inputs to fluvial systems are outgassed to the atmosphere before reaching the oceans [Cole et al., 2007; Aufdenkampe et al., 2011]. About $50 \%$ of the total organic carbon exported from watersheds by rivers to the oceans is in the form of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) [Schlesinger and Melack, 1981; Raymond and Bauer, 2001a]. With a major active role in the global inland carbon cycle, the quality of DOM is as important to study as its quantity [Cole et al., 2007; Spencer et al., 2012].

DOM quality in streams is often studied via bulk properties such as chromophoric DOM (CDOM) content and its stable isotopic composition, both of which provide information on its sources and biogeochemical cycling [Findlay and Sinsabaugh, 2003]. CDOM specific ultraviolet (UV) absorbance at $2 5 4 ~ \mathrm { \ n m }$ $\mathrm { ( S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } )$ ) is related to its aromaticity [Weishaar et al., 2003], while CDOM spectral slope ratio $( S _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ is related to photodegradation and molecular weight [Helms et al., 2008]. Metrics of CDOM quality derived from excitation-emission matrices of its fluorescence (EEMs) include biological and humification indices (BIX and HIX, respectively), which correspond to DOM produced recently by microorganisms (BIX) and DOM that contains recalcitrant humic substances (HIX). Chemometric techniques have been used to analyze EEM fluorescence and determine distinct signatures (“components”) that provide information on the chemical composition of DOM [Fellman et al., 2010]. Stable carbon isotope values of DOM $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C - D O C } )$ indicate its sources and often distinguish between $\mathrm { C } _ { 3 }$ and C4 plant sources as well as between fresher plant leachate and humic substances [Lloret et al., 2016].

There are multiple sources of DOM to streams: soil organic matter, plant litter, in-situ primary production, throughfall, and stemflow [Mulholland, 2003; Inamdar et al., 2011; Lloret et al., 2011]. These sources may reach streams via surface runoff and groundwater flowpaths. Previous studies have shown that precipitation events lead to greater DOC concentration and flux during stormflow compared to baseflow conditions, and DOC sources to fluvial systems shift from groundwater to surface water during such events after different regions of a watershed are activated [McGlynn and McDonnell, 2003; Raymond and Saiers, 2010; Inamdar et al., 2011]. This shift in water source from groundwater to surface water also changes DOM sources to streams. Rain events tend to change the quality of the stream water DOM, due in part to water movement overland or through shallow soil during storms [McGlynn and McDonnell, 2003; Raymond and Saiers, 2010]. DOC concentrations tend to increase on the rising limb of a hydrograph and decrease on the falling limb, likely because water-soluble soil organic matter is depleted after a certain amount of flushing from rain via hydrologic throughflow [Hornberger et al., 1994].

DOM exported during storms is of a higher molecular weight and greater aromaticity than that observed during baseflow, which is dominated by groundwater inputs [Hood et al., 2006; Lloret et al., 2011; 2016]. Recent work indicates that this high molecular weight, aromatic material originates from fresh plant litter as well as labile substrates washed from plant surfaces via throughfall and stemflow [Inamdar et al. 2011; Lloret et al. 2016]. By contrast, groundwater inputs tend to be lower in DOM concentration with lower aromaticity and lower molecular weight and indicative of its biogeochemical degradation [Chen et al. 2010; Inamdar et al. 2011; Inamdar et al. 2012]. The residence time of DOM in groundwater may contribute to these characteristics [Genereux et al. 2013]. Considering the importance of storms in exporting large amounts of DOC from watersheds, it is also important to understand how the quality of the DOM exported from watersheds differs between stormflow and baseflow [Raymond et al. 2016].

In general, tropical ecosystems are understudied in the inland carbon cycle. In particular, uncertainties in DOM quality and quantity in tropical ecosystems are larger than in temperate or boreal ecosystems, despite tropical ecosystems having a disproportionately greater role in downstream transport of C [Aufdenkampe et al., 2011; Raymond et al., 2013]. DOC and CDOM concentrations in tropical streams tend to increase during storm events and during wet seasons [Newbold et al., 1995; Johnson et al., 2006; Spencer et al., 2010; Bass et al., 2011]. However, only a few studies have examined DOM quality in addition to quantity in tropical streams and rivers under contrasting flow regimes, and fewer still have considered the influence of groundwater on DOM quality [Spencer et al., 2010; Genereux et al., 2013; Ganong et al., 2015]. Closing this knowledge gap is an essential task as hydrology, vegetation, and biological activity are expected to change with the increasing climate variability, particularly in the tropics, where rainfall is substantial and the frequency and duration of droughts and storms are changing [O’Gorman et al., 2012].

The aim of this study was to determine differences in DOM quality between baseflow and stormflow in two adjacent lowland tropical rainforest streams and to assess the influence of groundwater, throughfall, and soil sources of DOM to the streams. We used a pairedwatershed approach to study the temporal variability of DOM quantity and quality in two streams, the Arboleda and the Taconazo, draining adjacent watersheds located at La Selva Biological Station in Costa Rica. The streams share similar soils and vegetation but differ in their water sources. Only young, shallow, local groundwater discharges into the Taconazo, whereas the Arboleda stream receives both local groundwater and much older regional groundwater (RGW) [Genereux et al., 1997; 2002; 2005; Solomon et al., 2010]. DOM was characterized on samples collected approximately weekly over approximately 2 years (January 2012-March 2014), with higher resolution during three specific rain events in June 2013. We analyzed DOC concentration, CDOM absorption and fluorescence, and $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC values along with stream discharge to determine: 1) sources of DOM to the two tropical streams; 2) the temporal variability of DOM quality and quantity between stormflow and baseflow in the two adjacent watersheds; and 3) the influence of RGW inputs on stream DOM. Results were placed in context of prior work on stream DOM quality and export in temperate and tropical ecosystems.

# 2. STUDY SITE

This study was conducted at La Selva Biological Station (LSBS), a field research station of the Organization for Tropical Studies (OTS). LSBS is a 1536 ha research preserve in northern Costa Rica (Fig. S1). The geology of the site is mainly volcanic rocks of Quaternary age and intermediate andesitic to basaltic composition [Genereux and Jordan, 2006; Genereux et al., 2009]. Average annual rainfall at La Selva from 1963 to 2013 was $4 3 2 8 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , with February, March, and April as the driest months and July, November, and December as the wettest (online meteorological data at http://www.ots.ac.cr/meteoro/default.php?pestacion $^ { = 2 }$ ). From 1982 to 2013, the mean air temperature was $2 5 . 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ .

The two streams studied at LSBS were the Arboleda and the Taconazo (Fig. S1), adjacent streams whose watersheds are identical or nearly so with regard to many characteristics, including vegetation, relief, soil type, rainfall, temperature, and evapotranspiration [Genereux et al., 2005]. However, the Arboleda has much higher discharge and inorganic solute concentrations than the Taconazo, a product of a high influx of old RGW which contributes to the Arboleda but not to the Taconazo (Table S1) [Genereux et al., 2002; 2005; 2009]. This regional groundwater is recharged upslope of La Selva in the Cordillera Central and enters the Arboleda watershed as interbasin groundwater flow that passes beneath the topographic boundary of the watershed. RGW accounts for about $34 \%$ of the annual discharge of the Arboleda but $0 \%$ of the Taconazo discharge [Genereux et al., 2005]. Stream discharge was measured at a sharp-crested V-notch weir on each watershed; watershed areas upstream of the weirs are 46.1 ha for the Arboleda and 27.9 ha for the Taconazo [Genereux et al., 2005; Genereux et al., 2013].

# 3. METHODS

# 3.1. Water Sample Collection and Processing

Water samples for the analysis of DOC concentration and isotopic composition as well as optical properties of the DOM were collected approximately weekly in the Arboleda and Taconazo streams from January 2012 through March 2014 (Table S2). Stream water samples were collected from each stream at a V-notch weir installed in each stream, in precleaned 1-L high density polyethylene bottles. The bottles were rinsed with stream water three times before filling. Samples were also taken at Guácimo Spring located approximately 1 km southeast of LSBS; this spring water is representative of old RGW at the site [Genereux et al., 2002, 2005, 2009]. Guacimo Spring water samples were collected from a small concrete cistern at the discharge point of the spring (through which there is a continuous strong flow of spring water), using a pre-cleaned syringe and Teflon tubing [Genereux et al., 2005]. A 3-way valve was used to ensure sample flowed through the tubing only and did not enter the syringe. The apparatus was flushed with well spring water prior to collection. After field collection, water samples were transferred to pre-cleaned $1 2 5 \mathrm { - m L }$ polycarbonate bottles, frozen and transported from Costa Rica to the United States and stored at ${ } - 2 0 \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ upon arrival

at North Carolina State University.

Using Teledyne ISCO 3700 samplers deployed near the weirs, we sampled storm events at high frequency in the Arboleda and the Taconazo in June 2013. Samplers were started manually at the beginning of each precipitation event. Hourly stream samples were collected (before, during, and after rainfall) for 12 hours on three days: June 13, June 14, and June 25 (Table S2). Discharge data from V-notch weirs were taken every 15 minutes in both the Arboleda and the Taconazo (details on discharge measurements can be found in Genereux et al., [2005]). Some dates do not have recorded data due to instrument malfunction or flooding events at the weirs; in these cases the watershed hydrologic model TOPMODEL was used to estimate stream discharge, using precipitation data from a tipping-bucket rain gauge that is within $1 . 5 \mathrm { k m }$ of both weirs [Zanon et al., 2014].

Stream samples were classified as either baseflow or stormflow, based on visual examination of stream discharge hydrographs and precipitation data. Measured precipitation occurring within five hours of sample collection was used to characterize discharge values as stormflow. Discharge values ${ > } 1 7 7 \mathrm { ~ L ~ s } ^ { - 1 }$ in the Arboleda were classified as stormflow; for the Taconazo, discharge values ${ > } 1 . 9 \mathrm { ~ L ~ s ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ were classified as stormflow. In some cases, samples collected at discharges above these values were classified as baseflow if no precipitation was measured within five hours of sample collection.

Potential stream DOM source samples (precipitation, throughfall, and soil leachate) were collected in January 2016. Precipitation was collected in pre-cleaned, round and shallow 7-L plastic containers deployed for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ in an open area with no trees within a $1 5 \mathrm { - m }$ radius. Accumulated precipitation collected during the $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ period was $1 1 . 7 \ \mathrm { m m }$ . Throughfall also was collected during the $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ period in similar 7-L containers which were deployed randomly under the canopy of forested areas in the Arboleda and Taconazo watersheds. After collection, samples were transferred into HDPE dark 2-L bottles and transported immediately

to the laboratory facilities at LSBS.

Soil samples from the top $4 \ \mathrm { c m }$ were collected from the Arboleda and Taconazo watersheds (1 sample each) and from near the LSBS laboratory $( \mathrm { N } = 3 )$ ) using cleaned $5 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ polypropylene centrifuge tubes to core and collect the sample. The samples were immediately transported to the laboratory at LSBS, where $1 5 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ of soil was transferred into clean $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ polypropylene centrifuge tubes to which $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of ultrapure water was added. The soils were agitated on a shaker table for 24 hours to disaggregate soil particles and facilitate extraction of OM into water and then centrifuged. The supernatant (“soil leachate”) was decanted. Precipitation, throughfall, and soil leachate samples were filtered in the laboratory using precombusted $4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; 6 hours) $0 . 7 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ nominal pore size glass fiber filters (Whatman GF/F). Each DOM source sample was stored frozen then sent to NC State University for measurements of DOM optical and chemical properties.

3.2. Dissolved Organic Carbon, Stable Carbon Isotope, and Chromophoric Dissolved Organic Matter Analyses

Prior to analysis, samples were thawed to room temperature and each sample was mixed via vortexer. No precipitates were observed in the soil leachate DOM after thawing and mixing. A vacuum pump (Barnant Company) was used to filter the unfiltered stream samples through pre-combusted $0 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ GF/F filters into a cleaned $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ glass Erlenmeyer flask within 24 hours of thawing. Absorbance and fluorescence were measured on filtrates within one week. After optical measurements, the remaining filtrate was transferred to a precombusted glass vial and acidified with $8 5 \%$ phosphoric acid to a $\mathsf { p H }$ of 2 for DOC and stable carbon isotope $\mathrm { 8 ^ { 1 3 } C - D O C ) }$ analysis.

DOC concentration ([DOC]) was measured using an OI Analytical Aurora Model 1030 Total Organic Carbon Analyzer while carbon stable isotopes were measured on a Delta

V Isotope Ratio Mass Spectrometer [Osburn and St-Jean, 2007]. Reference carbon dioxide pulses were sent through the system before analysis until an acceptable standard deviation $_ { ( < 0 . 0 5 ) }$ was observed. A standard curve for [DOC] was created consisting of six solutions of IAEA-600 caffeine with [DOC] values ranging from $0 . 2 5 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } ~ }$ to $2 5 \mathrm { \ m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ . DOC measurements using this technique over this range had a standard deviation of $0 . 0 3 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ and a method detection limit (MDL) of seven (7) $0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ standards was $0 . 0 9 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ . Carbon stable isotope ratios from the IRMS were blank-corrected and then calibrated to the Vienna Pee Dee Belemnite (VPDB) scale $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { \mathrm { V P D B } } )$ with caffeine solutions $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { \mathrm { V P D B } } = .$ $2 7 . 7 7 { \pm } 0 . 0 4 \% 0 )$ and solutions of IAEA C6 sucrose $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C _ { \mathrm { V P D B } } = - 1 0 . 4 5 { \pm } 0 . 0 3 \% ) }$ . Samples and standards were blank-corrected and a linear regression of the area of the curve of the caffeine standards was created to relate area under the curve to [DOC]. Reproducibility of this method is $\pm 0 . 4 \text{‰}$

CDOM absorbance spectra of the samples were measured from 200 to $8 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ on a Varian Cary 300UV spectrophotometer in quartz cells that were either 1-cm or $1 0 \mathrm { - c m }$ in length, depending on the CDOM concentration in the sample. Samples were diluted if necessary to keep the absorbance $< 0 . 4$ in 1-cm cells; samples in $1 0 { \mathrm { - c m } }$ cells did not require dilution. Blank-corrected absorbance values were converted to Napierian units using the following equation:

$$
\begin{array} { r } { \mathsf { a } ( \lambda ) = \frac { A _ { ( \lambda , \mathsf { s a m p l e } ) } - A _ { ( \lambda , \mathsf { b l a n k } ) } } { \mathsf { L } } \mathbf { x } \ : 2 . 3 0 3 } \end{array}
$$

where $a$ is the absorption coefficient at wavelength $\gimel$ , A is the absorbance, and $\mathrm { L }$ is the pathlength of the quartz cell, in meters [Osburn and Morris, 2003]. The value 2.303 converts the decadal absorption to Naperian absorption. CDOM was quantified as Napierian absorption at $2 5 4 \ \mathrm { n m } \ ( \mathrm { a } _ { 2 5 4 } )$ . The ratio of decadal $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ to DOC concentration, i.e., the specific UV absorption $\left( \mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 } \right)$ ), was computed and used to estimate the relative amount of the aromatic groups in DOM [Weishaar et al., 2003]. Spectral slopes $( S )$ were computed over 275-295 nm and $3 5 0 – 4 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ via linear regression of natural-log transformed absorption spectra. Slope ratio $( S _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ values were calculated as $S _ { 2 7 5 - 2 9 5 }$ divided by $S _ { 3 5 0 - 4 0 0 }$ .

Fluorescence excitation-emission spectra were measured on a Varian Eclipse spectrofluorometer, sampled from 240 to $4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ in the excitation mode $\left( \operatorname { E x } \right)$ and 300 to 600 nm in the emission mode (Em). These samples were corrected by subtracting an ultrapure lab water blank. Samples were also corrected for the inner filtering effect and to the spectrofluorometer‟s water Raman signal before a final normalization to quinine sulfate units (QSU). Corrections of absorbance and fluorescence spectra were done with in-house processing scripts using MATLAB 2014a (Mathworks). Parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) was used to quantify and characterize DOM in this study. Fluorescence emission spectra for each sample were processed into an EEM, and matrices were then compiled into an array and processed with the DOMFluor toolbox [Stedmon and Bro, 2008; Murphy et al., 2013] in MATLAB. PARAFAC models containing three to six components were fit to the data array and validated using split-half analysis, random starts, and inspection of any residuals. Maximum fluorescence values (FMax) for each component are reported in QSU and percentages of each component (e.g. $\% { \bf C l - } \% { \bf C } 6$ ) express the abundance of each component in a sample relative to the total abundance of all six components.

# 3.3. Statistical tests

Assumptions for parametric statics were not met by most of the results, thus nonparametric tests were used. Non-parametric rank correlations between variables were made using Kendall‟s tau (τ) statistic. For univariate data, ANOVA on ranks (Kruskal-Wallis test) with Dunn‟s post-hoc test were undertaken to determine differences between streams and between stormflow and baseflow. PARAFAC results are multivariate data that often are intercorrelated and require non-parametric techniques for making comparisons [Murphy et al., 2014]. Therefore, permutation MANOVA (PERMANOVA) tests were conducted on PARAFAC results based on Euclidean distances [Anderson, 2001].

3.4. Calculation of DOC export fluxes

Export fluxes of DOC were calculated by the flow-weighted mean method from Littlewood, [1992] and Birgand et al., [2010]. This method also has been used in prior studies of DOC fluxes in the Arboleda and Taconazo watersheds (Genereux, et al., [2013]; Ganong et al., [2015]). The flow-weighted mean is an averaging method in which the annual export flux is calculated as the product of the annual stream discharge volume and the flowweighted mean concentration of the solute in the stream water [Littlewood, 1992]: $\begin{array} { r } { F l u x = K V \frac { \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { n } C i Q i } { \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { n } Q i } } \end{array}$

(2)

where V is the annual cumulative stream water discharge (in L), $\mathrm { C } i$ and $\mathbf { Q } i$ are instantaneous [DOC] and stream discharge values $( \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ and $\mathrm { ~ L ~ s ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively), and K is a conversion factor to correct for different units.

# 4. RESULTS

4.1. Stream DOM compared between watersheds and flow conditions

Flow values $( \mathrm { L ~ s } ^ { - 1 } )$ varied greatly between baseflow and stormflow for both streams (Supporting Information). Minimum and maximum baseflow values of $1 4 4 . 7 \mathrm { L ~ s } ^ { - 1 }$ and 182.9 L s -1, respectively, were measured in the Arboleda. In the Taconazo, minimum baseflow was $0 . 2 \mathrm { ~ L ~ s ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ and maximum baseflow was $1 9 . 7 \mathrm { L } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ . Maximum observed stormflow was $9 4 1 . 0 \mathrm { L }$ $\overline { { \textbf { s } } } ^ { - 1 }$ in the Arboleda and $3 2 5 . 9 \mathrm { L ~ s ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ in the Taconazo).

There were clear differences in [DOC] and CDOM concentrations (quantified as $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ ) between the streams, and between baseflow and stormflow (Table 1 and Fig. 1). DOM data were generally not normally distributed, hence median, minimum, and maximum values are reported (Table 1). [DOC] in the Arboleda at baseflow (median $= 0 . 8 3 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ ) was roughly $6 6 \%$ that in the Taconazo (median $= 1 . 2 6 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ ). [DOC] at baseflow was significantly lower in the Taconazo $( \mathbf { P } { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ , Table S3). However, during stormflow, [DOC] was not significantly different between the two streams $( \mathrm { P } { > } 0 . 0 5$ , Table S3). Within each stream, median [DOC] was significantly higher at stormflow than at baseflow $\mathrm { P } { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ for the Arboleda, $\mathrm { P } { < } 0 . 0 5$ for the Taconazo; Table S3), and stormflow median in the Arboleda (2.04 $\mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ ) was not significantly different from that in the Taconazo $( 2 . 2 5 \mathrm { m g L ^ { - 1 } } )$ .   
In general, a254 followed similar trends to [DOC]. Median a254 values during stormflow in the Arboleda and the Taconazo streams were higher $( 9 . 7 1 \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ and $9 . 3 9 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively) than at baseflow $( 2 . 2 3 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ in the Arboleda and $6 . 2 5 \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ in the Taconazo) (Table 1). Median stormflow $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values in the Arboleda increased by 4-fold from baseflow to stormflow, compared to 1.5-fold increase for the Taconazo (Table 1). Baseflow vs. stormflow differences in $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ were statistically significant in each stream $\mathrm { P } { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ in each case, Table

S3).

Measured $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC values in the Taconazo and Arboleda streams spanned nearly the entire range of values observed for most freshwater ecosystems: -22 to $- 3 6 \text{‰}$ [Raymond and Bauer, 2001]. Median values for both streams at baseflow were about $- 2 7 \text{‰}$ , indicative of C3 terrestrial vegetation (Table 1). The median value at stormflow was slightly enriched in the Arboleda $( \delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { - D O C } = - 2 6 . 6 9 \% )$ compared to the Taconazo $( \ S ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { - D O C } = - 2 7 . 1 5 \% )$ ). No significant differences were observed between baseflow and stormflow for either stream, due in part to the relatively large range of values measured in each stream.

CDOM quality however did vary significantly between the streams (Fig. 1 and Table S3). SUVA254 values generally increase with a greater aromatic content of DOM and typically range from $1 { - } 4 \ \mathrm { L } \ \mathrm { m g } \ \mathrm { C } ^ { - 1 } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 1 }$ in stream and rivers [Spencer et al., 2012]. For the Arboleda, the median $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ value during baseflow was on the lower end of this range $( 1 . 3 6 \mathrm { L } \operatorname* { m g } \mathrm { C } ^ { 1 } \operatorname { m } ^ { - 1 } )$ and increased significantly during stormflow to $2 . 0 3 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g ~ C ^ { - 1 } \ m ^ { - 1 } }$ (Table S3). In the Taconazo, median $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values were 2.12 and $2 . 4 5 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g ~ C ^ { - 1 } ~ m ^ { - 1 } }$ at baseflow and stormflow, respectively, and there was no significant difference in $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values between the Taconazo at baseflow and stormflow. Median $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ was significantly different between the Arboleda and the Taconazo at baseflow but not at stormflow (Table S3).

Slope ratios $( S _ { \mathrm { R } } )$ are considered proxies for the molecular weight of DOM, with high values corresponding to low molecular weight DOM and low values corresponding to high molecular weight DOM [Helms et al., 2008]. $S _ { \mathrm { R } } { < } 1$ indicate CDOM of high molecular weight and aromaticity [e.g., Spencer et al., 2012]. $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ was generally higher in the Arboleda than the Taconazo [Genereux et al., 2013]. In the Arboleda, median $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ was significantly higher at baseflow (1.05) than stormflow (0.91) (Table S3); in the Taconazo, median $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ was similar between baseflow and stormflow (Table 1 and Fig. 1) and significantly lower than $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ in the Arboleda (Table S3).

A six-component (C1-C6) PARAFAC model was fit to the DOM fluorescence measurements from the Arboleda and the Taconazo (Fig. S2). Each component was compared to previously published models in the OpenFluor database, using a criterion of $9 5 \%$ similarity to indicate a match (Table S4) [Murphy et al., 2014b]. In general, C1-C4 exhibited excitation and emission spectra consistent with humic substances from soils and freshwaters [Senesi, 1990]. C1 matched with 10 models and exhibited a terrestrial, humic-like signal consistent with photoreactive material found in lakes, rivers, and coastal waters. C2 matched with 13 models from productive ecosystems such as estuaries but also European lakes and streams and tropical rivers, and it shared spectral properties common to microbial humic-like substances [Kothawala et al., 2014; Osburn et al., 2016; Lambert et al., 2016]. C3 exhibited the longest excitation and emission spectral features, often associated with fulvic acids extracted from soils and matched with 17 models, including a wide variety of humicrich freshwaters, estuaries, and coastal waters, including tropical rivers [Yamashita et al., 2010; Chen et al., 2010; Osburn et al., 2016]. C4 had primary excitation ${ < } 2 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , secondary excitation at $3 6 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , and emission at $4 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ . While no statistical match was found in OpenFluor, its primary Ex/Em corresponds to a degradation component that has been produced photochemically [Stedmon et al., 2007]. Components 5 and 6 exhibited fluorescence that often is attributed to protein-like substances but also shares characteristics with phenolic material from lignin and tannin [Hernes et al., 2009; Stubbins et al., 2014; Wünsch et al., 2016]. C5 matched with 14 models ranging from the open ocean to lakes, rivers, and estuaries [e.g., Yamashita et al., 2010; Osburn and Stedmon, 2011]. The spectral features of C6 matched with two models of mostly coastal waters [e.g., Kowalczuk et al., 2009].

Fluorescence intensity of components (FMax values) for the Arboleda and Taconazo streams at baseflow and stormflow exhibited similar patterns (Table 1 and Fig. 2). Compared to the baseflow samples, the stormflow samples in both streams tended to have greater fluorescence intensity. # 5. DISCUSSION

5.1. RGW Drives Differences in Baseflow Stream DOM in Adjacent Watersheds

The major distinction between stream DOM quantity and quality in these adjacent watersheds occurred at baseflow, when the effects of RGW on Arboleda stream water DOM quantity and quality were greatest (Table S3 and Fig. 1). RGW exerted an extreme effect on DOM in the Arboleda via dilution with seemingly heavily degraded, less aromatic, lower molecular weight DOM. At baseflow, [DOC] in Arboleda stream was about $6 6 \%$ of that in the Taconazo stream, and far more similar to [DOC] in RGW than to that in the Taconazo (Table 1, Table 3). Values of [DOC] in RGW were roughly $50 \%$ and $30 \%$ of those in the Arboleda and Taconazo streams at baseflow, respectively, and consistent with DOC concentrations reported in Ganong et al. [2015]. These results indicate that input of RGW to the Arboleda stream dilutes CDOM and DOC from other sources such as local groundwater, precipitation, and overland flow [Genereux et al., 2013]. By contrast, the Taconazo stream is fed only by local groundwater (no RGW), leading to smaller differences in DOM quality between stormflow and baseflow in the Taconazo than in the Arboleda, despite significant increases in Taconazo [DOC] and $\mathtt { a } _ { 2 5 4 }$ during high discharge events (Tables 1 and S2).

In terms of DOM quality, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ in the Arboleda stream during baseflow conditions was significantly lower than in the Taconazo stream, and more comparable to $\mathrm { \Delta R G W \ S U V { A } _ { 2 5 4 } }$ values. Median $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values in Arboleda at baseflow $( 1 . 0 7 \pm 0 . 1 ) $ were greater than median $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values of the Taconazo during baseflow (0.77). $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values $< 1$ typically are interpreted to indicate high molecular weight DOM, and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values tend to increase its molecular weight decreases [Helms et al., 2008]. Together, these results indicate that local groundwater sources of DOM to the Arboleda stream are diluted by RGW DOM at baseflow, lowering the stream DOM‟s overall aromaticity and molecular weight.

Scant data exist in the literature for optical properties of DOM in groundwater, especially for old groundwater in regional aquifer systems. In our study, RGW SUVA254 values $( 0 . 4 9 { \pm } 0 . 2 0 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g ~ C ^ { - 1 } ~ m ^ { - 1 } } )$ were much lower than other published values for shallow and deep groundwater [range: 0.8 to $3 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g ~ C ^ { - 1 } \ m ^ { - 1 } }$ ; Chen et al., 2010; Chapelle et al., 2011; Shen et al., 2015]. Mean $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ $( 7 . 2 9 { \pm } 2 . 8 9 )$ likewise is much higher than the stream waters we sampled as well as reported values for local groundwater in the Florida Everglades (1.22±0.18 [Chen et al., 2010]) and the southeast US coastal plain ( $0 . 7 9 { \scriptstyle \pm 0 . 0 4 }$ , Shen et al. [2015]). Also, RGW samples exhibited no measurable fluorescence, indicating any soilderived DOM in RGW was heavily altered. RGW fluorescence was essentially similar to that of our laboratory water used as a blank, water that has been extensively purified to remove DOM. A PARAFAC fluorescence model of deep groundwater $( { > } 6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { ~ m ~ } )$ taken from a borehole in the Canadian Shield (Ontario, Canada) showed substantially reduced intensity compared to shallow groundwater, especially in humic-like components matching C1 and C4 from the La Selva model in this study [Caron et al., 2010]. While the residence time in that study was not reported, the presence of fluorescent DOM, albeit at low intensities, suggests RGW feeding streams at La Selva was far more altered than bedrock groundwater analyzed by Caron et al., [2010]. Altogether, these results strongly suggest that RGW's residence time of about 3000 years in the regional volcanic bedrock aquifer system and upslope of La Selva [Genereux et al., 2009] affords ample time to remove DOM‟s fluorescence properties as well as produce DOM exhibiting $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values ${ < } 1 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g \ : C ^ { - 1 } \ : m ^ { - 1 } }$ and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values ${ > } 5$ .

$\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values of RGW approximate heavily photobleached oligotrophic ocean seawater (0.82 to $0 . 4 1 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g ~ C ^ { - 1 } ~ m ^ { - 1 } }$ and 2.1 to 8.9, respectively; Helms et al., [2013]). Mean age of DOM in the ocean is on the order of 4000 years, a timescale similar to the age of RGW discharging to the Arboleda and other streams at La Selva [Hansell, 2013]. Extensive degradation of DOM in the oceans via photochemistry and microbial degradation operate to produce weakly fluorescing DOM having $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ values similar in magnitude to the RGW in our study. While RGW is obviously not photodegraded by sunlight, it is subject to sorption reactions in soils, especially to iron hydroxides, which can remove aromatic and carboxyl moieties [Kaiser et al., 1996; Kaiser et al., 1997; Kalbitz et al., 2000; Fielder and Kalbitz, 2003]. These sorption reactions can mimic degradation in terms of DOM quality [e.g., Hernes et al., 2007]. The similarity in quality of altered DOM in such markedly different aquatic ecosystems (ocean, RGW) is fascinating, and suggests that a variety of DOM transformations converge to a common state in aquatic ecosystems and underscores the “recalcitrant” nature of old oceanic and groundwater DOM in such dilute concentrations [Arrieta et al., 2014].

Despite marked differences at baseflow, during stormflow we observed that the DOM quantity and quality in the streams became nearly indistinguishable. This result suggested the influence of terrestrial DOM pulses in storm runoff dominated both by DOM leached from soils and DOM in throughfall leached from canopy vegetation in the watersheds (see section below). Nonparametric MANOVA on all 6 PARAFAC components indicated no significant differences in FMax between the Taconazo at stormflow and the Arboleda at stormflow. Similarly, $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ results showed that DOM in the Arboleda and the Taconazo became more similar during stormflow, reflecting sources with the same degree of aromaticity to both streams. In contrast to $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and fluorescent properties, during stormflow the median $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ value for the Arboleda (0.93) was still much larger than the median $S _ { \mathrm { R } }$ value of the Taconazo (0.78) and indicated the influence of RGW in the Arboleda, even during stormflow.

The similarity between streams during stormflow was a clear result of the influence of terrestrial carbon sources reaching the stream through transient storm runoff flowpaths. The distribution of $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } .$ -DOC values plotted against $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values represents a gradient of flowpaths to the streams, each containing different sources of DOM (Fig. 4). Soil leachate and throughfall dominate in storm runoff and increase in prominence during storms [Inamdar et al. 2011]. This influence of soil leachate and throughfall DOM in the streams was evident when comparing the distribution of relative FMax values for the six PARAFAC components (Fig. 2).

Both soil leachate and throughfall sources of DOM could be enriched in fresh plant litter, which exhibits a range of $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values (e.g., $^ { - 2 2 }$ to $- 3 0 \text{‰}$ ) in tropical ecosystems, based on differences among plant species common to La Selva and effects of season and photosynthetic recycling of $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ in the canopy [Tieszen, 1991; Ting et al., 1985; da Silveira et al., 1989; Hammond et al., 2005]. Throughfall had proportionally greater aromatic DOM and more depleted $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values than did the soil leachate (Fig. 4). Enrichment of soil DOM $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values of up to $4 \text{‰}$ relative to plant litter DOM could be due to adsorption of $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -enriched carboxyl moieties onto soil inorganic particles [Lloret et al., 2016]. Notably, the high $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values $( > 2 . 0 \mathrm { ~ L ~ m g ~ C ^ { - 1 } ~ m ^ { - 1 } } )$ and $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC values $< - 2 8 \text{‰}$ (Table 3) found in throughfall are consistent with a recent study of throughfall from trees in the coastal southeastern US, in which high resolution mass spectrometry was used to quantify aromaticity [Stubbins et al., 2017]. Mixing of multiple sources of DOM – including throughfall, litter leachate and shallow soil water – can produce highly aromatic DOM (high $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values) [Inamdar et al., 2011]. Therefore, the heterogeneity of plant types and seasonal differences might partly explain the underlying variability in the storm runoff $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } .$ - DOC values, and suggests that these $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values reflect combinations of fresh plant-derived DOC in throughfall and soil DOM leached from the surrounding watershed.

$\mathrm { \ S U V { A } } _ { 2 5 4 }$ values and $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values thus provided a framework to explain the variability of DOM sources to the study streams, summarizing both the effects of RGW and of soil and throughfall sources on stream DOM (Fig. 4). Mean stormflow values for each stream plotted between the soil DOM and throughfall DOM values, demonstrating the effect of storms in mobilizing these terrestrial sources of DOM into each stream. Also interesting was that mean Arboleda values at baseflow were closer to $\mathrm { S U V A } _ { 2 5 4 }$ and $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ values for precipitation and Guacimo Spring, both representative of DOM present in the RGW. Thus the observed range in the stream samples results from variable mixtures of terrestrial sources of DOM (soil, throughfall) as well as microbial degradation of DOM in RGW, especially in this warm tropical environment. Interestingly, the stormflow mean $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC values for both streams was roughly $1 \text{‰}$ higher than baseflow mean values, which may be due to the difference in riparian wetland vs. hillslope soil sources of DOM as observed in the Kervidy-Naizin

# 5.3. DOC Export in Tropical Streams and the Role of RGW

The Taconazo DOC export values fell within a range seen in other systems, from small forested temperate and tropical streams to large rivers $( 0 . 7 4 \mathrm { ~ g ~ C ~ m ~ } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ to $9 . 4 0 \mathrm { ~ g } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 2 }$ yr-1, Table 4). Estimates of the Taconazo stream‟s DOC export in this study also are similar to those calculated in 2003 $( 3 . 9 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { y } \mathrm { r } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Ganong et al., [2015]) and 2006 $( 4 . 0 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { y } \mathrm { r } ^ { - 1 }$ Genereux et al., [2013]) (Table 5). However, the Arboleda stream‟s DOC export calculated in this study was lower than in 2006 [Genereux et al., 2013]. Both 2012 and 2013 were dry years with annual stream water discharge totals much lower than in either previous study, especially for the Taconazo watershed, which exported only about $50 \%$ of the water exported in 2002-2003 or 2006 (Table 5).

DOC export for the Arboleda stream was much greater than that for the Taconazo and for many streams in other studies, exceeding the comparatively large DOC exports from the tropical Q. Sonadora in Puerto Rico $( 7 . 4 3 \textrm { g C m } ^ { - 2 } \textrm { y r } ^ { - 1 } )$ and the Penobscot River in the northeastern US $( 7 . 6 5 \ \mathrm { g \ C \ m ^ { - 2 } \ y r ^ { - 1 } ) }$ [Aitkenhead and McDowell, 2000; Spencer et al., 2013]. The DOC export of the Arboleda stream also was greater than several small streams on the tropical Guadeloupe Island (French West Indies) (range: 1.6 to $5 . 7 \mathrm { ~ g ~ C ~ m ~ } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { ~ y r } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Table 4) [Lloret et al., 2011]. Exports of DOC for the Arboleda stream were much greater than for large tropical rivers such as the Amazon, Congo, Mekong, or Orinoco Rivers, all which had reported DOC export values $< 5 . 0 \mathrm { ~ g ~ C ~ m ~ } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { ~ y r ~ } ^ { - 1 }$ (Table 4). However, the Taconazo stream‟s export value were in line with these larger systems.

Storms exert an important control on DOC exports, representing about $30 \mathrm { - } 7 0 \%$ of annual exports from catchments [Hinton et al. 1998; Raymond and Saiers 2010]. On Guadeloupe Island, for example, about $60 \%$ of DOC export occurred during flash flood events [Lloret et al., 2011]. The results of our storm data from June 2013 indicate that for the Taconazo, each storm exported a higher fraction of the annual watershed DOC export (0.6 to $4 . 6 \%$ than did the same storm in the Arboleda (0.4 to $1 . 4 \%$ ) (Table 6), because in the Arboleda such exports are superimposed on a very large and constant background (baseflow) export of DOC due to the presence of RGW. This result illustrates the potential importance of deeper hydrogeological systems and long-scale interbasin groundwater flowpaths to exports of C from surface watersheds beyond the importance of storm events [Genereux et al., 2013].

# 6. CONCLUSIONS

DOM dynamics in the Taconazo and Arboleda watersheds suggests that DOC export in these tropical ecosystems is variable and driven by rain events, similar to temperate ecosystems [Raymond and Saiers, 2010; Bass et al., 2011; Spencer et al., 2012]. Recent work at La Selva has shown that input of regional groundwater (RGW) originating from outside the topographic boundaries of the small lowland study watersheds adds complexity to this dynamic, driving higher export of DOC even with comparatively lower DOC concentrations [Genereux et al., 2013; Ganong et al., 2015]. Here we have demonstrated that discharge of RGW strongly influenced the rainforest stream DOM chemistry, including the quality of the DOM exported.

A major finding of this study is that the DOM in RGW bears striking similarity to oligotrophic oceanic DOM; there is a lack of measureable fluorescence, yet CDOM absorption spectral features resembled photobleached surface seawater. This finding implies that substantial degradation – possibly by sorption reactions and/or subsurface microbes – can exert a major control on DOM in deep groundwater systems. The possibilities for diverse modes of DOM processing in RGW systems and the ocean – which can operate on similar timescales of thousands of years – to achieve qualitatively similar altered DOM demand further investigation.

Stormflow exerted clear changes on the quality of DOM in streams by mobilizing more aromatic DOM and increasing the concentration of CDOM in streams. Terrestrial sources of DOM to the Arboleda and Taconazo at stormflow and baseflow were very similar, as indicated by a wide range of $\delta ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -DOC values in both streams showing no statistically significant difference between flow regime or between streams. However, fluorescence results indicated that the DOC in runoff to the streams during storms is comprised primarily of material leached from fresh plant litter which is mobilized in throughfall (and possibly stemflow) as well from organic-rich surface soils. Future work should investigate how storms in tropical watersheds influence the relative proportions of throughfall and stemflow to soil sources of DOM to streams, which could be an important dynamic in inland stream carbon cycling. Results from studies of lignin, an aromatic biopolymer of terrestrial vegetation considered to be recalcitrant to microbial degradation, in upstream waters of the Amazon River suggest a very rapid degradation of terrestrial DOM leading to a baseline stable state [Ward et al., 2015]. This result is consistent with the baseline DOM properties of the Arboleda and Taconazo reflecting partially-degraded DOM and being markedly different from throughfall or soil-leached DOM. The transfer of canopy carbon into stream networks where it likely is rapidly metabolized is being realized as an important flux between terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems [Stubbins et al., 2017]. Throughfall DOM transported to streams may therefore be important during the “hot moments” of storm events, representing poorly quantified C fluxes that warrant further study [McClain et al., 2003; Cole et al., 2007].

DOC export results from the Arboleda and the Taconazo watersheds showed that discharge of RGW into the Arboleda increased DOC export per unit watershed area by a factor of 3 to 6 (Table 5). In stream ecosystems with sufficient canopy in their watersheds, the connection of carbon biogeochemistry to forest dynamics and regional hydrogeology may be stronger than previously realized when considering the effect of DOM inputs from throughfall during storm runoff in addition to RGW discharge. Both DOM sources and flowpaths to streams clearly need consideration in future work because both exert strong controls on DOM quality as well as quantity.
